## Dating Vintage Clothing

### Era Identification Guide

```
Pre-1930s:
  - No zippers (hooks, snaps, buttons only)
  - Hand-finished seams and details
  - Natural fibers (silk, cotton, wool, linen)
  - Metal hook-and-eye closures
  - Very few surviving pieces (fragile)

1930s-1940s:
  - Metal zippers appear (teeth are visible metal)
  - Bias-cut silhouettes (1930s)
  - Shoulder pads begin (1940s)
  - Rayon/viscose becomes common
  - War-era: limited fabric, shorter hemlines, utility styling

1950s:
  - Metal zippers standard (may have brand like "Talon")
  - Full skirts, nipped waists
  - Nylon and early synthetics appear
  - Union labels common (ILGWU, ACWA)
  - No care labels yet

1960s:
  - Polyester appears mid-decade
  - Shorter hemlines, mod styling
  - Paper and metal zippers
  - Early size standardization
  - Psychedelic prints, bold colors

1971+: Care labels required in US
  Presence of care label = 1971 or later

1970s:
  - Nylon zippers become common
  - Polyester widespread ("leisure suit" era)
  - Earth tones, wide collars, bell bottoms
  - RN/WPL numbers on labels (searchable in FTC database)

1980s:
  - Power shoulders, bold colors, excess
  - Designer labels proliferate
  - Spandex/Lycra appears in clothing
  - Country of origin labels (Made in USA common until mid-80s)

1990s:
  - Minimalism vs grunge
  - "Made in China" becomes very common
  - Modern fiber blends
  - UPC barcodes on tags (late 80s-90s)
```

### Authentication Tips

```
Check for:
  - Consistent era-appropriate construction details
  - Label style matching the claimed period
  - Fabric and fiber consistent with the era
  - Hardware (zippers, buttons, snaps) matching the period
  - Wear patterns consistent with genuine age
  - Seam construction (serged/overlocked edges = likely post-1950s)

Red flags for fakes:
  - Modern synthetic fibers in allegedly pre-1960s pieces
  - Screen-printed labels in pre-1960s pieces
  - Perfect condition with no signs of age
  - "Vintage reproduction" labels (not actual vintage)
  - Inconsistent construction details across the garment
```

## Cleaning Methods

### Assessment Before Cleaning

```
ALWAYS test before cleaning any vintage piece:

1. Fiber identification
   - Burn test (tiny thread from inside seam):
     Cotton: Burns steadily, ash is gray, smells like paper
     Silk: Burns slowly, bead of ash, smells like hair
     Wool: Burns slowly, bead of ash, smells like hair
     Polyester: Melts and drips, hard plastic bead, chemical smell
     Rayon: Burns quickly like cotton, no bead

2. Colorfastness test
   - Dampen a white cotton swab
   - Press against an inconspicuous area for 30 seconds
   - Check for color transfer
   - Test EVERY color in the garment separately

3. Fabric strength test
   - Gently tug a seam or fold
   - If fabric tears easily, it's too fragile for wet cleaning
   - Dry cleaning or conservation cleaning only
```

### Cleaning Methods by Fabric

```
SILK:
  - Hand wash in cool water (never hot)
  - Use pH-neutral soap (Orvus paste or Soak wash)
  - Do NOT wring or twist
  - Roll in a clean towel to absorb excess water
  - Lay flat to dry on a clean towel
  - Steam (not iron) to remove wrinkles
  - NEVER use chlorine bleach

COTTON:
  - Hand wash in lukewarm water
  - Gentle agitation (no scrubbing delicate pieces)
  - OxiClean (oxygen bleach) safe for white cotton
  - Line dry or lay flat (dryer can shrink vintage cotton)
  - Iron while slightly damp

WOOL:
  - Hand wash in cool water with wool wash (Eucalan, Soak)
  - Do NOT agitate (felting risk)
  - Gently squeeze water out (never wring)
  - Reshape and lay flat to dry
  - Block to original measurements
  - Steam to freshen between washes

POLYESTER / SYNTHETICS (1960s+):
  - Most durable, easiest to clean
  - Hand or machine wash cool, gentle cycle
  - Air dry (polyester can melt at high heat)

RAYON / VISCOSE:
  - Very fragile when wet -- handle with extreme care
  - Hand wash in cool water or dry clean
  - Do NOT wring or twist (distorts shape)
  - Lay flat to dry, reshape while damp
```

### Stain Removal

```
GENERAL RULES:
  - Treat stains as soon as possible
  - Always test on an inconspicuous area first
  - Work from the outside of the stain inward
  - Blot, don't rub (rubbing spreads and damages fiber)
  - Use the mildest treatment first, escalate if needed

Common vintage stain treatments:

Yellowing (age, storage):
  - Soak in OxiClean solution (1 scoop per gallon, warm water)
  - Soak 2-4 hours, checking periodically
  - For stubborn yellowing: sunlight bleaching (wet garment in direct sun)
  - Works best on cotton and linen

Rust stains:
  - Lemon juice + salt, applied to stain, laid in sun
  - Or: commercial rust remover (Iron Out, Whink)
  - Test carefully -- rust removers can damage some fibers

Mildew/musty odor:
  - Air in direct sunlight for several hours
  - Soak in vinegar solution (1 cup per gallon of water)
  - Baking soda paste for stubborn mold (leave 30 min, brush off)
  - If mold is extensive, professional treatment recommended

Perspiration stains:
  - Soak in enzyme cleaner (BioKleen Bac-Out)
  - Or: paste of baking soda and water, leave 30 minutes
  - OxiClean soak for white fabrics

Ink stains:
  - Rubbing alcohol on cotton swab, blot carefully
  - Hairspray (older formulas) -- test first
  - May not be fully removable on vintage fabric
```

## Repair Techniques

### Common Repairs

```
Small holes and moth damage:
  Hand repair with matching thread
  - Weave thread across the hole using darning technique
  - For invisible repair: use thread pulled from an interior seam
  - Swiss darning (duplicate stitch) for knit fabrics
  - Iron-on patches for hidden areas (inside pockets, linings)

Torn seams:
  - Re-stitch by hand following original stitch holes
  - Use thread matching original weight and color
  - Backstitch for strength, slip stitch for invisible repair
  - Reinforce stress points (underarm, crotch) with extra stitching

Missing buttons:
  - Source period-appropriate buttons (vintage button dealers, eBay)
  - If one is missing from a set: move a button from a less visible
    position and replace that one with a close match
  - Or replace entire button set with matching vintage buttons

Zipper replacement:
  - Use era-appropriate zipper (metal for pre-1970, nylon for later)
  - Vintage zipper sources: eBay, Etsy, specialty haberdashers
  - Metal zipper cleaning: rub with wax candle to smooth operation
  - Do not replace with modern zipper on high-value pieces

Elastic replacement:
  - Common in 1940s-70s garments (waistbands, cuffs)
  - Old elastic crumbles -- always replace if degraded
  - Use similar width and weight elastic
  - Sew new elastic into existing casing
```

## Storage

### Proper Storage Guidelines

```
Environment:
  Temperature: 65-70F (stable, no extremes)
  Humidity: 45-55% RH (too dry = brittle, too damp = mold)
  Light: Dark or very low light (prevents fading)
  Air: Clean, circulating (not sealed airtight)

Hanging:
  - Use padded hangers (not wire)
  - Heavy items (beaded, sequined): fold and store flat
  - Knits: NEVER hang (stretches out of shape)
  - Silk and delicate fabrics: fold with acid-free tissue

Folding and boxing:
  - Fold loosely with acid-free tissue between layers
  - Stuff sleeves and bodice with tissue to prevent creases
  - Store in acid-free boxes (not cardboard or plastic bins)
  - Or wrap in unbleached muslin (breathable)
  - Re-fold periodically (every 6-12 months) to prevent permanent creases

NEVER use:
  - Plastic bags or sealed containers (trap moisture, off-gas chemicals)
  - Colored tissue paper (dye transfer)
  - Newspaper (acidic, ink transfer)
  - Cedar or moth balls directly touching fabric (chemical damage)
  - Wire hangers (distort shape, rust)
  - Regular cardboard boxes (acidic)

Moth prevention:
  - Lavender sachets (natural repellent)
  - Cedar blocks in the closet (not touching garments)
  - Regular inspection (catch damage early)
  - Clean garments before storing (moths attracted to body oils/food)
  - Freeze treatment for suspected moth eggs (bag, freeze 72 hours)
```

## Selling Vintage

### Valuation Factors

```
What increases value:
  + Designer/known brand label
  + Excellent condition (minimal flaws)
  + Desirable era (currently: 1960s-1990s most popular)
  + Rare print or fabric
  + Small/medium size (most in-demand)
  + Complete set (matching pieces)
  + Original tags (deadstock/NWT = premium)

What decreases value:
  - Stains that can't be removed
  - Alterations (especially visible ones)
  - Missing components (belts, buttons, tags)
  - Damage to fabric (tears, moth holes, fading)
  - Odor (smoke, must, perfume)
  - Very large or very small sizes (less demand)
  - Common/mass-produced pieces

Pricing research:
  - Check sold listings on eBay (not asking prices)
  - Etsy sold listings for comparable items
  - 1stDibs for high-end designer vintage
  - Instagram vintage sellers for current market
```
