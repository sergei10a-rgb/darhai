## Leather Types and Grades

### Leather Tanning Methods

```
VEGETABLE TANNED (VEG-TAN):
  Process: Tanned with natural tannins from bark and plants
  Characteristics: Stiff, moldable, develops patina over time
  Color: Natural tan, darkens with age and sun
  Best for: Tooling, carving, belts, wallets, holsters, saddlery
  Thickness range: 2oz-14oz common
  Can be: Dyed, tooled, carved, molded, wet-formed
  Price: $8-$15/sq ft (domestic), $4-$8 (imported)

CHROME TANNED:
  Process: Tanned with chromium salts
  Characteristics: Soft, supple, vibrant colors, no patina
  Color: Any color, colorfast
  Best for: Garments, upholstery, bags, soft goods
  Less suitable for: Tooling, carving, wet-forming
  Price: $5-$12/sq ft

COMBINATION TANNED (CHROME RE-TAN):
  Process: Chrome tanned then re-tanned with veg or other agents
  Characteristics: Medium stiffness, some toolability
  Best for: Bags, wallets, accessories
  Price: $6-$15/sq ft

BRAIN TANNED / BUCKSKIN:
  Traditional process using animal brains
  Very soft, supple, stretchy
  Best for: Historical, traditional crafts, pouches
```

### Leather Grades

```
FULL GRAIN:
  The complete top surface of the hide, unaltered
  Shows natural marks, scars, texture
  Most durable, develops best patina
  Highest quality and price

TOP GRAIN:
  Top surface has been lightly sanded or buffed
  More uniform appearance
  Still durable, less character than full grain
  Good quality at moderate price

GENUINE LEATHER:
  Despite the name, this is a LOWER grade
  Layers beneath the top grain
  Less durable, less attractive
  Budget-friendly but inferior

BONDED LEATHER:
  Ground-up leather scraps bonded with adhesive
  NOT suitable for leathercraft
  Will delaminate and fall apart
  Avoid entirely

LEATHER WEIGHT SYSTEM:
  Measured in ounces per square foot
  1 oz = approximately 1/64" (0.4mm) thickness

  | Ounces | mm      | Common Uses                    |
  |--------|---------|--------------------------------|
  | 2-3 oz | 0.8-1.2 | Linings, thin wallets, wraps   |
  | 4-5 oz | 1.6-2.0 | Wallets, small accessories     |
  | 6-7 oz | 2.4-2.8 | Bags, journal covers           |
  | 8-9 oz | 3.2-3.6 | Belts, holsters, thick bags    |
  | 10-12oz| 4.0-4.8 | Heavy belts, armor, saddlery   |
  | 13-14oz| 5.2-5.6 | Sole leather, heavy duty       |
```

## Cutting Techniques

### Cutting Best Practices

```
LAYOUT:
  1. Plan pieces to minimize waste
  2. Place largest pieces first, fill gaps with smaller pieces
  3. Grain direction matters: Stretch goes across the hide
     (spine runs head to tail; stretch is side to side)
  4. Avoid belly for structural pieces (too stretchy)
  5. Mark with scratch awl (fine lines) or silver pen (visible, removable)

CUTTING TOOLS BY USE:
  Straight lines: Metal ruler + rotary cutter or utility knife
  Curves: Round knife or sharp utility knife (slow, controlled cuts)
  Thick leather: Multiple light passes, NOT one deep cut
  Detail work: #11 X-Acto blade for tight curves

CUTTING RULES:
  1. Always cut on a cutting mat or punching board
  2. Keep blades extremely sharp (change frequently)
  3. Cut away from your body
  4. Use a metal straight edge (NOT plastic or wood, blade will cut into them)
  5. Hold ruler firmly; use cork-backed ruler for grip
  6. For thick leather, make scoring pass first, then deeper cuts
  7. Cut with the good side (grain side) up
  8. Let the blade do the work; don't force it
```

## Stitching (Saddle Stitch)

### The Saddle Stitch Method

```
WHY SADDLE STITCH:
  Hand saddle stitch is STRONGER than machine stitching
  If one stitch breaks, the rest hold (unlike machine lockstitch)
  Creates a distinctive angled stitch appearance
  The hallmark of quality leatherwork

PREPARATION:
  1. Mark stitch line with wing divider (set to 3-5mm from edge)
  2. Score groove along stitch line (optional, sinks thread below surface)
  3. Punch holes with stitching chisels/pricking irons
     - Place first chisel on the line, strike with mallet
     - Overlap last hole of previous punch for consistent spacing
     - Punch through both layers when possible (alignment)

STITCHING PROCESS:
  1. Cut thread 3.5-4x the length of the seam
  2. Thread a needle on EACH end of the thread
  3. Lock needles by passing through the thread twice
  4. Push first needle through the first hole
  5. Center the thread (equal length on each side)
  6. Insert right needle through next hole, pull through
  7. Insert left needle through SAME hole, pull through
     (pass behind the right needle's thread, consistent direction)
  8. Pull both threads tight (consistent tension)
  9. Repeat for every hole
  10. Backstitch 2-3 holes at the end to lock
  11. Cut threads close, melt ends with lighter (polyester) or
      leave hidden (linen thread)

THREAD CHOICES:
  Polyester (Ritza Tiger, Coats): Strong, consistent, many colors
  Linen: Traditional, natural, waxes well
  Nylon: Strong but stretches
  Thickness: 0.6mm (fine), 0.8mm (standard), 1.0mm (heavy)
  Wax: Pre-waxed is convenient; beeswax your own for more control

STITCH SPACING:
  3mm: Fine work, wallets, watch straps
  3.85mm: General purpose, most projects
  5mm: Heavy work, belts, bags
  Rule: Spacing should be roughly 1.5x the thread thickness
```

## Hardware Installation

### Common Hardware Types

```
RIVETS:
  Single cap (one finished side): For layered construction
  Double cap (both sides finished): For visible both sides
  Sizes: 6mm, 8mm, 10mm cap diameter; various post lengths
  Installation: Anvil on flat surface, rivet setter on top, strike with mallet
  Tip: Post length should be 2-3mm longer than combined leather thickness

SNAPS (LINE 20, LINE 24):
  Components: Cap, socket (female side); post, stud (male side)
  Line 20: Standard size for most accessories
  Line 24: Larger, heavier duty
  Installation: Snap setter tool + anvil, strike with mallet
  Tip: Reinforce thin leather with interfacing or extra layer

CHICAGO SCREWS (SCREW POSTS):
  Two-piece: male screw post + female screw back
  Removable (great for items that need disassembly)
  Apply threadlocker to prevent loosening

D-RINGS AND O-RINGS:
  Attached by folding leather around ring and riveting or stitching
  Sizes: 3/4", 1", 1.25", 1.5" common

BUCKLES:
  Roller buckle: Standard belt buckle
  Center bar buckle: Belt and strap buckle
  Heel bar buckle: Sandals, watchbands
  Installation: Fold leather around bar, stitch or rivet closed
  Punch holes for prong at calculated intervals
  Hole spacing: 1" apart for belts

SAM BROWNE STUDS/BUTTONS:
  Screw-on attachment point
  Used for bag closures, strap ends
  Clean, professional appearance

MAGNETIC CLOSURES:
  Hidden magnets sandwiched between leather layers
  Clean appearance, easy open/close
  Strengths: Various (choose appropriate for application)
```

## Dyeing and Finishing

### Dyeing Process

```
DYE TYPES:
  Spirit/Alcohol-based dye (Fiebing's):
    Penetrates deep, vibrant colors
    Dries fast, can be streaky if not careful
    Apply with wool dauber, sponge, or airbrush
    Best for: Veg-tan leather

  Water-based dye (Eco-Flo):
    Easier to use, more forgiving
    Less color depth than spirit dyes
    Easier cleanup
    Best for: Beginners

  Oil-based dye (Fiebing's Pro):
    Deep color, professional results
    Longer working time
    Best for: Large areas, even coverage

DYEING PROCESS:
  1. Ensure leather is clean and free of oils
  2. Dampen leather slightly with water (opens pores)
  3. Apply dye in smooth, circular motions
  4. Work in small sections for consistent coverage
  5. Apply multiple light coats rather than one heavy coat
  6. Let dry completely between coats (30-60 minutes)
  7. Buff with clean cloth when dry
  8. Apply finish coat (resolene, sealant) to protect color

TIPS:
  - Wear gloves (dye stains everything)
  - Work in ventilated area
  - Test on scrap piece first
  - Light colors are harder to get even than dark
  - You can always go darker, never lighter
  - Antique finish: Apply dark dye, wipe off quickly (stays in tooled impressions)
```

### Edge Finishing

```
EDGE FINISHING PROCESS:
  1. Trim edges flush if needed
  2. Bevel edges with edge beveler (both top and bottom)
  3. Sand edges (start at 220, progress to 400, 600)
  4. Apply edge finish compound (Tokonole, gum tragacanth, or beeswax)
  5. Burnish with wood slicker or canvas
     - Apply firm pressure, rub back and forth rapidly
     - Heat from friction melts fibers together
     - Repeat compound + burnish 2-3 times for glass-like edge
  6. For dark leather: Apply edge paint (Uniters edge paint)
     - Apply thin coat with edge roller or fine brush
     - Let dry, sand lightly, apply second coat
     - Produces clean, consistent colored edge

EDGE QUALITY LEVELS:
  Basic: Beveled and sanded (adequate for practice)
  Good: Beveled, sanded, burnished with compound (standard for sales)
  Excellent: Multiple rounds of sand, compound, burnish (professional)
  Premium: Edge paint, multiple coats, sanded smooth (luxury goods)
```

## Pattern Making

### Pattern Development Process

```
TOOLS:
  Cardstock or manila folder (for templates)
  Ruler and T-square
  Compass
  Pencil and eraser
  X-Acto knife
  Hole punch for stitch holes (optional)

PROCESS:
  1. Sketch the design with dimensions
  2. Draw full-size pattern on cardstock
  3. Add seam allowances (typically included in pattern)
  4. Mark stitch lines, fold lines, hardware locations
  5. Cut out pattern pieces
  6. Test with cheap leather or heavy fabric
  7. Adjust and iterate
  8. Create final pattern in durable material

PATTERN ELEMENTS TO MARK:
  - Cut line (outer edge)
  - Stitch line (where stitching goes)
  - Fold lines
  - Hardware placement (snaps, rivets, D-rings)
  - Grain direction arrow
  - Part name and piece count
  - Leather weight/thickness specification
  - "Cut 2" or "Cut 1 on fold" notation

DIGITAL PATTERN TOOLS:
  Adobe Illustrator / Inkscape (free): Vector-based precision
  Affinity Designer: Affordable alternative
  Print at 100% scale on tiled pages, tape together
  Always verify printed scale with a ruler
```

## Project Progression

### Beginner to Advanced

```
LEVEL 1 - FIRST PROJECTS (1-3oz veg-tan):

  2. Card holder (2-3 hours)

  3. Simple belt (3-4 hours)

  4. Bifold wallet (6-8 hours)

  6. Watch strap (3-4 hours)

LEVEL 3 - INTERMEDIATE:
  7. Tote bag (10-15 hours)
     Skills: Large pieces, handles, gussets, reinforcement

      Skills: Carving, stamping, dyeing, antiquing
  12. Western saddle components (50+ hours)
      Skills: Master-level tooling, complex construction
```

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Using dull blades (rough cuts, jagged edges, dangerous)
2. Not testing dye on scrap leather first (colors vary significantly by leather)
3. Skipping edge finishing (unfinished edges scream "amateur")
4. Punching stitch holes from both sides separately instead of together (misalignment)
5. Inconsistent stitch tension (every stitch should have the same pull)
6. Using too-thick thread for thin leather (overpowers the piece visually)
7. Not accounting for leather thickness in pattern dimensions
8. Buying cheap leather to save money (results will be disappointing and discouraging)
9. Gluing pieces before test-fitting (contact cement is permanent)
10. Rushing the saddle stitch (slow, consistent stitching produces the best results)
