## Questions to Ask First

Before starting the design process:

1. What board games do you enjoy playing? What makes them fun for you?
2. Do you have a game idea already, or are you starting from scratch?
3. What is your design experience? (First game / Have designed casually / Published designer)
4. What is your goal? (Personal project / Publish through Kickstarter / Pitch to publishers)
5. What is your budget for prototyping and development? ($0-50 / $50-200 / $200-1,000 / $1,000+)
6. How much time can you commit to design and playtesting? (Weekends / Several hours a week / Full-time)
7. What player count and play time are you targeting?
8. Do you have a playtest group, or do you need to find one?
9. What type of game interests you? (Strategy, party, cooperative, competitive, narrative)
10. Are you comfortable with graphic design, or will you need help with visual production?

## Game Mechanics Taxonomy

### Core Mechanic Categories

**Action Selection / Action Points**
- Players choose from available actions each turn
- May have limited action points to spend
- Examples: Pandemic (action selection), Terraforming Mars (card-driven actions)

**Worker Placement**
- Players place limited workers on shared action spaces
- Once placed, that space is blocked from other players
- Creates tension through scarcity
- Examples: Agricola, Lords of Waterdeep, Viticulture

**Deck Building**
- Players start with weak decks and buy/gain better cards
- Core loop: Play cards > Buy cards > Shuffle > Draw > Repeat
- Examples: Dominion (originator), Star Realms, Clank!

**Area Control / Area Majority**
- Players compete for dominance in regions of the map
- Scoring based on who has the most presence
- Examples: El Grande, Root, Blood Rage

**Engine Building**
- Players construct increasingly powerful combinations
- Early actions enable bigger future turns
- Satisfying exponential growth feeling
- Examples: Wingspan, Terraforming Mars, Splendor

**Drafting**
- Players select from shared pool or passing hand of options
- Simultaneous decision-making reduces downtime
- Examples: 7 Wonders, Azul, Sushi Go

**Tile Placement**
- Players add tiles to a shared or personal map
- Spatial puzzle element
- Examples: Carcassonne, Cascadia, Isle of Cats

**Auction / Bidding**
- Players bid resources for items, actions, or privileges
- Tests valuation skills
- Examples: Ra, Power Grid, The Estates

**Push Your Luck**
- Players choose between taking current rewards or risking them for more
- Generates excitement and tension
- Examples: Quacks of Quedlinburg, Can't Stop, Incan Gold

**Cooperative**
- All players work together against the game system
- Win or lose as a team
- Requires careful tuning of difficulty
- Examples: Pandemic, Spirit Island, The Crew

**Social Deduction**
- Hidden roles, players must identify who is on which team
- Lying, deduction, and persuasion
- Examples: Werewolf, The Resistance, Blood on the Clocktower

**Roll and Write**
- Players roll dice and record results on personal sheets
- Low component cost, high accessibility
- Examples: Yahtzee, Welcome To, Cartographers

### Combining Mechanics
Most successful games combine 2-4 mechanics. The combination creates uniqueness:
- Worker placement + engine building = Agricola
- Deck building + dungeon crawl = Clank!
- Area control + asymmetric powers = Root
- Drafting + pattern building = Azul

## Theme Integration

### Theme-First vs Mechanics-First Design
**Theme-first**: Start with a setting or story, find mechanics that express it
- Strength: Cohesive, immersive experience
- Risk: Mechanics may feel forced to fit theme

**Mechanics-first**: Start with an interesting mechanism, apply theme later
- Strength: Mechanically tight and balanced
- Risk: Theme may feel "pasted on"

**Best approach**: Iterate between both. Let theme suggest mechanics and mechanics suggest theme.

### Making Theme and Mechanics Reinforce Each Other
- Actions should feel like what the theme describes (in a farming game, planting should feel like investing in future harvest)
- Resource names should be thematic (not just "red cubes" but "iron ore")
- Scoring should reflect thematic success (winning conditions match the story)
- Card abilities should make narrative sense
- Player roles/powers should fit their character

### Theme Considerations
- Overdone themes: Zombies, generic fantasy, space trading (you need a unique angle)
- Underserved themes: Many historical periods, professions, natural phenomena, non-Western settings
- Theme affects audience: Family themes expand market, dark themes narrow it
- Cultural sensitivity: Research thoroughly if using real cultures or historical events

## Prototyping

### Paper Prototype (Start Here)
1. Index cards for cards
2. Poker chips or coins for tokens
3. Printed grid or hand-drawn board
4. Dice, meeples, or cubes from other games
5. Write game text by hand or print on labels
6. Use card sleeves with playing cards as backing for durability

### Digital Prototyping Tools
- **Google Slides/PowerPoint**: Quick card and board layout
- **Canva**: Free design tool, good for cards and boards
- **nanDECK**: Specialized card design software (free, steep learning curve)
- **Component.studio**: Browser-based game component design
- **Tabletop Simulator** ($20): Digital playtesting platform
- **Tabletopia** (free tier): Browser-based digital playtesting
- **BoardGameArena**: If your game fits existing frameworks

### Print-and-Play Prototype
1. Design cards in software (8.5x11 with 9 cards per sheet)
2. Print on cardstock or regular paper in card sleeves
3. Board: Print on multiple sheets and tape together
4. Laminate reusable components
5. Use blank dice with stickers for custom dice

### Upgrade Path for Prototypes
1. **V1 - Ugly prototype**: Hand-written, functional only (first 5-10 playtests)
2. **V2 - Clean prototype**: Printed, readable, consistent formatting (playtesting with strangers)
3. **V3 - Near-final prototype**: Professional-looking, final art direction sample (publisher pitches or Kickstarter preview)
4. **Production prototype**: Final art, manufactured sample (pre-Kickstarter or publisher submission)

## Playtesting Methodology

### Types of Playtesting

**Solo playtesting** (Designer plays all positions):
- First 10+ sessions should be solo
- Test if the core loop is fun
- Find obvious broken combinations
- Verify game ends in target time frame
- Identify decision points that feel meaningful vs automatic

**Friendly playtesting** (Friends and family):
- First exposure to other humans
- Watch their faces, not the board (confusion, boredom, delight)
- They will be too nice - look for what they DO, not what they SAY
- Observe: Where do they hesitate? Where do they make mistakes?
- 10-20 sessions at this stage

**Blind playtesting** (Strangers play with only the rulebook):
- The real test: Can people learn and play without you explaining?
- Do NOT intervene when they get confused - note what confused them
- Rules problems become immediately apparent
- This stage reveals the truth about your game
- 5-10 sessions minimum

**Development playtesting** (Experienced gamers testing for balance):
- Power gamers try to break the game
- Test edge cases and dominant strategies
- Competitive players find exploits you never imagined
- Critical for balance tuning

### Playtesting Feedback Methods

**Observation Protocol**:
- Note player facial expressions and body language
- Track decision time per turn (long pauses = analysis paralysis or confusion)
- Track game length
- Note which parts generate excitement and which generate frustration
- Do not explain or defend design choices during play

**Post-Game Questions** (Ask In This Order):
1. What was the most fun part?
2. What was the least fun part?
3. Was there any point where you were confused?
4. Did you feel like your decisions mattered?
5. Would you play again? Why or why not?
6. How did the game length feel?
7. Is there anything you would change?

**Avoid asking**: "Did you like it?" (too vague, people will just say yes)

### Iteration Cycle
1. Playtest
2. Collect feedback and observations
3. Identify the biggest problem
4. Change ONE thing to address it
5. Playtest again
6. Repeat (expect 50-200+ iterations for a publishable game)

## Balance Iteration

### Common Balance Problems
- **Dominant strategy**: One approach always wins (test by forcing different strategies)
- **Runaway leader**: Winner snowballs advantage (add catch-up mechanics or diminishing returns)
- **Kingmaker**: Losing player decides who wins (reduce player-targeted attacks)
- **Analysis paralysis**: Too many options per turn (limit choices, add time pressure)
- **First/last player advantage**: Turn order creates unfair edge (rotate start player, balance resources)
- **Dead turns**: Some turns have no meaningful decisions (ensure every turn matters)

### Balancing Techniques
- **Cost curves**: More powerful effects cost proportionally more
- **Opportunity cost**: Choosing one path means giving up another
- **Diminishing returns**: Concentrated strategies become less efficient
- **Variable setup**: Randomize elements so no single strategy dominates every game
- **Point salad**: Multiple paths to victory so players do not all compete for the same resources
- **Spreadsheet modeling**: Calculate expected values for cards, actions, and strategies
- **Data tracking**: Record scores, strategies, and win rates across many playtests

## Rulebook Writing

### Rulebook Structure
1. **Cover**: Game name, player count, age range, play time
2. **Component list**: Everything in the box with images
3. **Game overview**: 2-3 sentence description of what players do and how they win
4. **Setup**: Step-by-step with diagram showing table layout
5. **Turn structure**: What happens on each turn, in order
6. **Actions available**: Detailed description of each action
7. **End game conditions**: When and how the game ends
8. **Scoring**: How to calculate final score
9. **Glossary/reference**: Key terms and symbols
10. **Quick reference card**: Separate player aid with turn summary

### Rulebook Writing Tips
- Use consistent terminology throughout (if you call it a "region" on page 2, do not call it an "area" on page 5)
- Numbered steps for procedures
- Bold key terms on first use
- Include examples for complex rules
- Use diagrams and illustrations liberally
- Test the rulebook with blind playtesters (the true test)
- Read other published rulebooks for formatting inspiration
- Have someone who has never played read it and explain the game back to you
- The rulebook is a teaching tool, not a legal document - prioritize clarity

## Art Direction

### Art Roles
- **Illustrator**: Creates game art (box art, card illustrations, character art)
- **Graphic designer**: Creates layout, iconography, card templates, board design, UX
- **Note**: These are different skills. Do not assume one person can do both.

### Finding Artists
- BoardGameGeek artist forums
- DeviantArt and ArtStation portfolios
- Instagram (search game art hashtags)
- Game design communities (Facebook groups, Discord servers)
- Conventions (networking at Gen Con, Essen, PAX Unplugged)

### Art Budget Expectations
- Card illustration: $50-300+ per unique card image
- Game board: $500-3,000+
- Box art: $500-2,000+
- Graphic design (full game): $2,000-10,000+
- Icon design: $20-100 per icon
- Total art budget for a card game: $3,000-15,000
- Total art budget for a board game: $10,000-50,000+

### Working with Artists
- Provide clear art briefs (description, mood, reference images, dimensions, file format)
- Agree on scope, timeline, payment schedule, and rights before starting
- Milestone payments (30% upfront, 30% at sketch approval, 40% at final delivery)
- Get written contract specifying rights transfer or licensing
- Allow creative freedom within your brief (they are the expert)

## Kickstarter Launch

### Pre-Launch (3-6 Months Before)
1. Build an email list (landing page with signup, at least 1,000 emails for a strong launch)
2. Establish social media presence (Instagram, BGG, Facebook groups)
3. Send review copies to content creators (3-6 months before launch)
4. Create promotional video (show gameplay, tell the story)
5. Get manufacturing quotes (Panda Game Manufacturing, LongPack Games, WinGo Games)
6. Set realistic funding goal (enough to cover manufacturing + fulfillment for minimum viable order)

### Campaign Structure
- **Pledge levels**: Keep simple (1-2 core tiers, maybe a deluxe tier)
- **Stretch goals**: Plan in advance, pre-negotiated with manufacturer
- **Campaign length**: 15-30 days (shorter creates urgency)
- **Funding goal**: Cover manufacturing at minimum order + fulfillment + Kickstarter fees (10%)
- **Pricing**: Research comparable games, include shipping estimate

### Fulfillment
- Use a fulfillment partner (Quartermaster Logistics, Fulfillrite, Shipnaked)
- International shipping is expensive and complex - price accurately
- Customs and VAT for EU shipments
- Budget 6-12 months from campaign end to delivery
- Communicate delays transparently (backers are forgiving if you are honest)

### Common Kickstarter Mistakes
- Underestimating manufacturing and shipping costs
- Setting funding goal too high or too low
- No audience built before launch
- Poor video quality
- Unclear value proposition (why should I back THIS game?)
- Overpromising stretch goals
- Not budgeting for Kickstarter and payment processing fees (8-10% total)

## Publisher Pitching

### Finding Publishers
- Research publishers who make games similar to yours in complexity, theme, and player count
- Attend conventions with publisher speed-dating events
- Check publisher websites for submission guidelines
- Many publishers accept unsolicited submissions

### Pitch Package
1. **Sell sheet** (1 page): Game name, tagline, player count, time, age range, elevator pitch (2-3 sentences), key features, photos of prototype, your contact info
2. **Rulebook**: Clean, complete, tested
3. **Prototype**: Playable, functional (does not need final art)
4. **Video pitch** (optional): 2-3 minute demonstration

### The Pitch Meeting (Convention or Virtual)
- 5-minute pitch: Explain the game in 2 minutes, play a demo turn in 3 minutes
- Lead with the hook: What makes this game unique and exciting?
- Know your comparable games ("It is like Wingspan meets Ticket to Ride")
- Be ready to leave the prototype for evaluation
- Follow up within 1 week with thank you and digital materials

### Publisher Deal Terms (Typical)
- Royalty: 4-8% of wholesale price (not MSRP)
- Advance: Sometimes $1,000-5,000 (paid against future royalties)
- Publisher handles: Manufacturing, art, marketing, distribution, fulfillment
- Designer provides: Final game design, rulebook, playtesting support
- Contract length: 3-7 years or until out of print
- Always consult a lawyer before signing

## Safety and Inclusivity Considerations

- Consider content warnings if your game includes mature themes
- Use the X-Card or similar safety tools for social deduction or role-playing games
- Ensure representation in art and characters is thoughtful and respectful
- Test accessibility: Color-blind friendly design (use symbols AND colors), readable font sizes, component sizes
- Price point affects accessibility - consider offering print-and-play versions

## Progression Path

1. **Phase 1**: Play 20+ different games, analyze what makes them work, design first prototype
2. **Phase 2**: Playtest 20+ times, iterate on core mechanics, find playtest group
3. **Phase 3**: Blind playtest, write complete rulebook, polish prototype
4. **Phase 4**: Decide path: Kickstarter or publisher pitch, prepare materials accordingly
5. **Phase 5**: Launch or pitch, navigate production/publication process
6. **Phase 6**: Published game, gather player feedback, start next design
