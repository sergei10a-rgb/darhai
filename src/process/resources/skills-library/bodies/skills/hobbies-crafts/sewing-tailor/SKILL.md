## Machine Selection

### Sewing Machine Types

```
MECHANICAL SEWING MACHINE:
  How it works: Manual stitch selection via dials/knobs
  Stitch options: 10-30 stitches typically
  Cost: $100-$400
  Pros: Simple, reliable, easy to maintain, affordable
  Cons: Fewer stitch options, no automatic features
  Best for: Beginners, basic sewing, garment construction
  Recommended: Singer Heavy Duty 4423, Janome HD3000

COMPUTERIZED SEWING MACHINE:
  How it works: Electronic stitch selection, digital display
  Stitch options: 50-600+ stitches
  Cost: $200-$1,500
  Pros: More stitches, automatic buttonholes, precise
  Cons: More expensive, repair can be complex
  Best for: Intermediate to advanced, decorative sewing
  Recommended: Brother CS7000X, Janome MC9850

SERGER (OVERLOCK):
  How it works: Trims, sews, and finishes seam edges simultaneously
  Uses 3-4 threads
  Cost: $200-$1,000
  Pros: Professional edge finishing, stretchy seams for knits
  Cons: Does not replace a regular sewing machine
  Best for: Finishing seams, sewing knit fabrics
  Recommended: Brother 1034D, Juki MO-654DE

COVERSTITCH MACHINE:
  Purpose: Creates professional hems on knit fabrics
  Cost: $300-$800
  Best for: Active wear, t-shirt hems, professional knit finishing
  Buy after: You're comfortable with serger and regular machine
```

### Essential Sewing Tools

```
CUTTING:
  [ ] Fabric scissors (8" dressmaker shears, ONLY for fabric)
  [ ] Rotary cutter (45mm) and self-healing cutting mat
  [ ] Paper scissors (for cutting patterns)
  [ ] Thread snips or small scissors
  [ ] Seam ripper (your best friend)

MEASURING:
  [ ] Flexible measuring tape (60")
  [ ] Clear quilting ruler (6x24")
  [ ] Seam gauge (small ruler with sliding marker)

MARKING:
  [ ] Fabric chalk or chalk wheel
  [ ] Water-soluble marking pen
  [ ] Tailor's wax pencils

PINNING AND HOLDING:
  [ ] Straight pins (glass head, sharp)
  [ ] Sewing clips (Clover Wonder Clips)
  [ ] Pin cushion
  [ ] Hand sewing needles (assorted)
  [ ] Machine needles (universal 80/12, ballpoint for knits)

PRESSING:
  [ ] Iron (with steam)
  [ ] Ironing board
  [ ] Pressing cloth (muslin or organza)
  [ ] Tailor's ham (for pressing curved seams)
  [ ] Sleeve board (for narrow areas)

Rule: Pressing is NOT the same as ironing.
Press: Lift and place iron, press down, lift. No sliding.
Iron: Slide iron across fabric. Used for wrinkle removal only.
Pressing after every seam is critical for professional results.
```

### Fabric Guide

```
WOVEN FABRICS (Do not stretch, fray at edges):
  | Fabric        | Weight    | Best For                      |
  |---------------|-----------|-------------------------------|
  | Cotton        | Light-Med | Beginners, quilting, dresses  |
  | Linen         | Med       | Summer garments, home textiles|
  | Denim         | Heavy     | Jeans, jackets, bags          |
  | Chambray      | Light     | Shirts, light jackets         |
  | Flannel       | Medium    | Pajamas, baby items           |
  | Chiffon       | Sheer     | Overlays, scarves (advanced)  |
  | Silk          | Light     | Formal wear (advanced)        |
  | Canvas        | Heavy     | Bags, upholstery, outdoor     |
  | Corduroy      | Med-Heavy | Pants, jackets, fall wear     |
  | Twill         | Medium    | Pants, workwear, jackets      |

KNIT FABRICS (Stretch, do not fray):
  | Fabric        | Stretch   | Best For                      |
  |---------------|-----------|-------------------------------|
  | Jersey        | 25-50%    | T-shirts, dresses, baby items |
  | Interlock     | 20-30%    | Structured knit garments      |
  | French terry  | 25-50%    | Sweatshirts, loungewear       |
  | Rib knit      | 50-100%  | Cuffs, neckbands, fitted tops |
  | Spandex/Lycra | 75-100%+ | Activewear, swimwear          |
  | Sweater knit  | 30-50%   | Cardigans, sweaters           |

FABRIC CARE SYMBOLS:
  Always check the bolt end or label for washing instructions
  Pre-wash fabric before cutting (shrinkage prevention)
  Pre-wash the same way you'll wash the finished garment
```

### Fabric Selection Tips

```
BEGINNER-FRIENDLY FABRICS:
  1. Quilting cotton (doesn't stretch, easy to handle)
  2. Flannel (slightly fuzzy, stays in place)
  3. Chambray (light denim feel, well-behaved)
  4. Medium-weight linen (forgiving, beautiful drape)

AVOID AS A BEGINNER:
  - Silk and satin (slippery, every mistake shows)
  - Chiffon and sheers (hard to handle, seams visible)
  - Stretchy knits without a walking foot or serger
  - Very thick materials like upholstery fabric
  - Velvet (shifts, shows marks)

FABRIC MATH:
  Most fabric comes in 44-45" or 54-60" widths
  Pattern envelope lists yardage needed for each size
  Add 10-15% extra for matching prints, mistakes, or shrinkage
  "With nap" yardage = all pieces cut in same direction (velvet, corduroy)
```

## Pattern Reading

### Understanding a Sewing Pattern

```
PATTERN ENVELOPE:
  Front: Photo/illustration of finished garment
  Back:
  - Line drawings of all included views/variations
  - Size chart (compare YOUR measurements to pattern, not RTW sizes)
  - Fabric suggestions
  - Yardage requirements by size and view
  - Notions needed (zipper, buttons, elastic, etc.)

PATTERN PIECES:
  Grain line arrow: Must align with fabric selvage (parallel)
  "Place on fold": Pattern edge goes on fabric fold (creates symmetric piece)
  Notches: Alignment marks (match single to single, double to double)
  Dots/circles: Match points for construction
  Darts: Triangular markings for shaping (bust, waist, back)
  Cutting line: Outer solid line (cut here)
  Stitching line: Inner dashed line (sew here)
  Seam allowance: Space between cutting and stitching line
    Commercial patterns: Usually 5/8" (15mm)
    Indie patterns: Often 3/8" or 1/2" (check instructions)

TAKING MEASUREMENTS:
  Bust: Around fullest part of chest, over bra
  Waist: Natural waist, narrowest point (bend to the side, crease is your waist)
  Hips: Around fullest part of hips/seat (usually 7-9" below waist)
  Back waist length: Nape of neck to natural waist
  Compare to pattern size chart, NOT to ready-to-wear sizes
  Choose size based on largest measurement, then adjust others
```

### Pattern Adjustments

```
COMMON ADJUSTMENTS:
  Full bust adjustment (FBA): Add width at bust for larger cup sizes
  Small bust adjustment (SBA): Remove width for smaller cup sizes
  Lengthen/shorten: Cut and spread or overlap at adjustment lines
  Wide/narrow shoulders: Adjust at shoulder seam
  Grading between sizes: Use one size at bust, blend to another at hips

EASE:
  Wearing ease: Extra room for comfortable movement (built into patterns)
  Design ease: Extra room for the style (oversized, fitted, etc.)
  Negative ease: Pattern is smaller than body (stretchy fabrics only)
```

## Basic Stitches

### Machine Stitches

```
STRAIGHT STITCH:
  Most-used stitch for woven fabrics
  Length: 2.5mm (standard), 3.0-4.0mm (basting/gathering)
  Backstitch at start and end to secure

ZIGZAG STITCH:
  Use: Stretch seams, raw edge finishing, applique
  Width: Narrow for seam finishing, wide for applique
  Length: 1.0-2.0mm depending on application

BASTING STITCH:
  Longest straight stitch (4.0-5.0mm)
  No backstitching (temporary, meant to be removed)
  Use: Testing fit, gathering, holding pieces for permanent sewing

BUTTONHOLE:
  Most machines have a buttonhole foot and automatic function
  Always test on scrap fabric first
  Use interfacing behind buttonhole area for stability

OVERLOCK/OVERCASTING STITCH:
  Built-in stitch that mimics serger
  Use: Finishing raw edges on woven fabrics
  Not a substitute for a true serger on knits
```

### Hand Stitches

```
SLIP STITCH (LADDER STITCH):
  Use: Invisible closing of openings, hems, linings
  Technique: Alternate tiny stitches between folded edges

BACKSTITCH:
  Use: Strong seam by hand, repairs
  Technique: Forward one stitch, back into previous stitch hole

WHIP STITCH:
  Use: Closing openings, attaching patches, quick repairs
  Technique: Diagonal stitches over the edge

BLIND HEM STITCH:
  Use: Invisible hems on dress pants, skirts
  Technique: Catch 1-2 threads of outer fabric, larger stitch in hem fold

BASTING:
  Use: Temporary hold for fitting, ease adjustment
  Technique: Long running stitches, easy to remove

BLANKET STITCH:
  Use: Decorative edges, felt crafts, applique
  Technique: Loop stitch along the edge
```

## Garment Construction

### Construction Order (Standard Garment)

```
GENERAL SEQUENCE:
  1. Prepare pattern pieces and cut fabric
  2. Transfer all markings to fabric
  3. Sew darts (if any)
  4. Sew all structural seams (shoulder seams, side seams)
  5. Insert sleeves (set-in) or attach bodice to skirt
  6. Sew secondary seams (inseam for pants, sleeve seams)
  7. Apply facings or neckline finish
  8. Install closures (zipper, buttons)
  9. Hem garment
  10. Final pressing

PRESSING RULES:
  Press every seam BEFORE crossing it with another seam
  Press seams open for flat construction
  Press seams to one side for strength (toward darker fabric)
  Press darts toward center front/back (bust darts downward)
  Use a pressing cloth on delicate or synthetic fabrics
```

### Seam Finishing Methods

```
METHOD            | TOOLS NEEDED    | BEST FOR
------------------|-----------------|---------------------------
Pinked edges      | Pinking shears  | Quick, low-fray fabrics
Zigzag stitch     | Sewing machine  | Most woven fabrics
Overlock stitch   | Machine/serger  | All fabrics
French seam       | Sewing machine  | Sheer/lightweight fabrics
Flat-felled seam  | Sewing machine  | Jeans, sportswear
Bound edge        | Bias tape       | Unlined jackets, couture
Hong Kong finish  | Bias strips     | High-end, unlined garments
Serged edge       | Serger          | Fastest, most professional
```

## Alterations

### Common Alterations

```
HEMMING PANTS:
  1. Try on pants with intended shoes
  2. Pin desired length (fold fabric under)
  3. Mark all around with chalk at pin level
  4. Remove, measure hem allowance (1-1.5")
  5. Cut excess, leaving hem allowance
  6. Finish raw edge (serge or zigzag)
  7. Fold up and press
  8. Stitch: Blind hem by hand (invisible) or machine blind hem

TAKING IN A WAIST:
  1. Turn garment inside out, try on
  2. Pin excess evenly at side seams and center back
  3. Mark new seam line with chalk
  4. Remove original stitching in the area
  5. Sew new seam following marked line
  6. Try on, adjust if needed
  7. Trim excess seam allowance
  8. Press seams

SHORTENING SLEEVES:
  1. Try on, determine desired length
  2. Mark at desired length
  3. Open existing hem
  4. Re-mark cutting line with seam allowance
  5. Cut, finish raw edge
  6. Fold new hem, press, stitch
  For cuffed sleeves: Remove cuff, shorten sleeve, reattach cuff

TAKING IN SIDES (SHIRTS/DRESSES):
  1. Turn inside out, try on
  2. Pin along side seam for desired fit
  3. Mark new seamline (taper from armhole to hem)
  4. Sew new seam line
  5. Try on before cutting excess
  6. Trim and finish seam
```

## Hemming Methods

```
TURNED HEM:
  Fold raw edge under 1/4", press
  Fold again 1/2"-1.5", press
  Topstitch close to inner fold
  Best for: Casual garments, home textiles

BLIND HEM (HAND):
  Fold and press desired hem width
  Fold hem back, leaving 1/4" of finished edge visible
  Take tiny stitch through outer fabric (1-2 threads only)
  Take larger stitch through hem fold
  Repeat, keeping stitches loose
  Best for: Dress pants, skirts, formal wear

BLIND HEM (MACHINE):
  Use blind hem presser foot
  Fold as for hand blind hem
  Machine catches outer fabric with left swing of stitch
  Best for: Faster blind hems, less visible than regular topstitch

ROLLED HEM:
  Use rolled hem presser foot
  Creates narrow, clean hem (1/8"-1/4")
  Best for: Lightweight fabrics, scarves, ruffles

BIAS TAPE HEM:
  Enclose raw edge with bias tape
  Fold and stitch
  Best for: Curved hems, decorative finish
```

## Zipper Installation

### Zipper Types and Methods

```
CENTERED ZIPPER:
  Application: Center back of dresses, skirts, pillows
  Method:
  1. Sew seam, leaving opening for zipper
  2. Baste opening closed
  3. Press seam open
  4. Center zipper face-down over seam (teeth on seamline)
  5. Pin or tape in place
  6. Using zipper foot, stitch from bottom up on each side
  7. Remove basting stitches

INVISIBLE ZIPPER:
  Application: Side seams of dresses, skirts (invisible from outside)
  Method:
  1. Open zipper, press coils flat with iron (carefully)
  2. Pin right side of zipper to right side of fabric
  3. Using invisible zipper foot, stitch close to coils
  4. Repeat for other side
  5. Close zipper, stitch remainder of seam below zipper
  Tool: Invisible zipper foot (essential, not optional)

LAPPED ZIPPER:
  Application: Side seams where one side overlaps
  One side sits flush, other side covers zipper teeth
  Used in skirts and pants

EXPOSED ZIPPER:
  Application: Decorative, sporty garments, bags
  Zipper is visible by design
  Topstitch zipper tape in place from right side

SEPARATING ZIPPER:
  Application: Jackets, hoodies (opens completely at bottom)
  More complex to install; follow pattern instructions carefully
```

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Not pre-washing fabric (shrinkage after construction ruins the garment)
2. Cutting with dull scissors or not following grain line
3. Skipping pressing between steps (pressing is 50% of professional results)
4. Using the wrong needle for the fabric type
5. Not backstitching at seam starts and ends (seams come apart)
6. Pulling or pushing fabric through the machine (let the feed dogs do it)
7. Ignoring seam allowances or assuming they're always 5/8"
8. Not testing stitch length and tension on scrap fabric first
9. skipping to change your machine needle regularly (every 8-12 hours of sewing)
10. Trying to cut corners on fitting (fit as you go, not at the end)
