## The Cold Weather System

```
WINTER CAMPING RULE 1: Stay dry. Moisture kills.

Three sources of dangerous moisture:
1. SWEAT: The primary enemy. Overdressing while active soaks insulation.
2. CONDENSATION: Breath and body heat meeting cold air inside shelter or bag.
3. PRECIPITATION: Rain, snow, sleet getting into your layers.

WINTER CAMPING RULE 2: Eat and drink constantly.
Your body burns 4,000-6,000 calories per day in cold weather.
Dehydration sneaks up because you do not feel thirsty in cold air.

WINTER CAMPING RULE 3: Make camp before you are tired.
Winter camp setup takes 2-3x longer than summer.
Start setting up with 2+ hours of daylight remaining.
```

## The Layering System

### Upper Body

```
LAYER 1: BASE LAYER (moisture management)
Material: Merino wool or synthetic (polyester, Capilene)
Purpose:  Wicks sweat away from skin
Weight:   Lightweight for high activity, midweight for moderate
NEVER:    Cotton - it absorbs moisture and loses all insulation value

LAYER 2: INSULATING MID-LAYER (warmth)
Material: Fleece, down, or synthetic fill
Options:
  Active: Lightweight fleece or active insulation (breathable)
  Moderate: Midweight fleece or light puffy
  Camp: Heavy fleece or expedition-weight puffy
Strategy: Multiple thin layers beat one thick layer (versatility)

LAYER 3: SHELL (wind and precipitation protection)
Material: Hardshell (waterproof-breathable) or softshell
Purpose:  Blocks wind and sheds snow/rain
Key:      Must be breathable enough for your activity level
          Pit zips are essential for ventilation during exertion

LAYER 4: INSULATED JACKET (belay parka / camp layer)
Material: Expedition-weight down or synthetic puffy
Purpose:  Worn ONLY when stopped - camp, breaks, sleeping
Key:      Size to fit over all other layers
          Keep accessible at top of pack for quick warmth
```

### Lower Body and Extremities

```
LOWER BODY:
- Layer 1: Base layer tights (merino or synthetic)
- Layer 2: Softshell or fleece pants (for active travel)
- Layer 3: Hardshell pants (wind/snow protection, side zips)
- Insulated pants: For camp only
- Gaiters: Essential in deep snow

HEAD (loses significant heat, carry multiple options):
- Thin beanie (active)
- Heavy insulated hat (camp/sleeping)
- Balaclava (wind, extreme cold)
- Buff/neck gaiter (versatile layer)

HANDS (the hardest to keep warm, use layered system):
- Liner gloves (thin, dexterous, keep on most of the time)
- Insulated gloves (moderate activity)
- Expedition mittens (rest stops, extreme cold)
- Mittens are warmer than gloves (fingers share heat)
- Carry chemical hand warmers as backup

FEET (cold feet end trips, critical system):
- Midweight wool socks (NOT cotton, not too tight)
- Insulated winter boots (rated to expected temperatures)
- Size boots to accommodate thick socks without compression
- Compression = reduced circulation = cold feet
- Camp booties (insulated, for around camp)
```

## Sleep System

```
SLEEPING BAG SELECTION:
Rating:  Choose a bag rated 10-15F below your expected low temperature
Type:    Down (lighter, compresses better, fails when wet)
         Synthetic (heavier, bulkier, insulates when damp)
Shape:   Mummy (warmest for weight), semi-rectangular (more room)

SLEEPING PAD:
R-Value: The critical spec. Higher = more insulation from ground.
  Summer:    R-value 2-3
  3-Season:  R-value 3-4
  Winter:    R-value 5+ (minimum)
  Extreme:   R-value 7+

DOUBLE PAD STRATEGY (recommended for winter):
  Bottom: Closed-cell foam pad (R-value 2-3, doubles as sit pad)
  Top:    Inflatable insulated pad (R-value 4-5)
  Combined: R-value 6-8 (values add together)
  Benefit: Foam pad is insurance if inflatable pad pops

SLEEP WARM TIPS:
1. Eat a high-calorie snack before bed (fuel for heat generation)
2. Do light exercises before getting in bag (warm up your core)
3. Pee before bed (body wastes energy keeping urine warm)
4. Wear dry base layers (change out of sweaty hiking layers)
5. Put next day base layers in the bag (warm to put on)
6. Use a sleeping bag liner (+5-15F boost)
7. Hot water bottle (Nalgene with hot water in the footbox)
8. Cinch hood so only nose and mouth are exposed
9. Breathe OUTSIDE the bag (condensation destroys insulation)
10. If cold in the night: eat a snack, do sit-ups in the bag
```

## Shelter Systems

```
SHELTER OPTIONS:
Type              | Weight  | Warmth | Setup Time | Skill Level
------------------+---------+--------+------------+------------
4-Season Tent     | 5-8 lbs | Good   | 15-30 min  | Beginner
Hot Tent + Stove  | 10+ lbs | Best   | 30-45 min  | Intermediate
Snow Cave         | 0 lbs   | Best   | 1-3 hours  | Advanced
Quinzhee          | 0 lbs   | Good   | 2-4 hours  | Intermediate
Tarp + Bivy       | 2-3 lbs | Fair   | 10-20 min  | Advanced

CAMP SITE SELECTION:
- Protected from wind (tree line, terrain features)
- Avoid avalanche runout zones and overhead hazards
- Flat or slightly concave spot (stamp out a platform)
- Not in a drainage (cold air pools in low spots)
- Access to water if possible (or plan to melt snow)
- Consider sun exposure (morning sun warms camp faster)

SNOW PLATFORM PREPARATION:
1. Stamp down snow with skis/snowshoes in tent footprint
2. Let the platform set up (harden) for 15-30 minutes
3. Check for level, adjust as needed
4. Set up tent on hardened platform
5. Build wind wall if exposed (snow blocks on windward side)
6. Dig a vestibule trench for gear storage and entry
```

## Water and Meal Planning

```
WINTER HYDRATION:
- You may not feel thirsty (cold suppresses thirst)
- Water sources freeze
- Target: 3-4 liters per day minimum

WATER STRATEGIES:
1. Insulated water bottle in sleeve, turned upside down
2. Hot water thermos (stays warm 6-8 hours)
3. Snow melting: start with water in pot, add snow gradually
   10 liters of snow = approximately 1 liter of water
   Budget extra stove fuel for this

CALORIC NEEDS: 4,000-6,000 cal/day (50-100% more than summer)

HIGH-CALORIE WINTER FOODS:
Food              | Cal/oz | Notes
------------------+--------+-------------------------------
Olive oil         | 240    | Add to everything
Butter            | 200    | Solid in cold, easy to carry
Nuts/nut butter   | 170    | Dense calories, good fats
Hard cheese       | 110    | Does not freeze solid
Chocolate         | 150    | Morale food and calories
Salami            | 120    | Protein and fat, cold-stable

MEAL STRATEGY:
Breakfast: Quick hot meal + hot drink (fast to make)
Lunch:     Graze throughout the day (no long stops to cool down)
Dinner:    Biggest meal, hot, calorie-dense
Snacks:    Keep accessible, eat every 1-2 hours while moving

STOVE NOTES:
- Canister stoves lose pressure below 20F (warm canister in jacket)
- Liquid fuel stoves work in all temperatures (recommended)
- Budget 50% more fuel than summer for snow melting
```

## Cold Weather Safety

### Hypothermia

```
RECOGNITION (progressive stages):
Mild:    Shivering, cold extremities, clumsiness, poor decisions
         The umbles - stumbles, mumbles, fumbles, grumbles
Moderate: Violent shivering then shivering stops, confusion, drowsiness
Severe:  Unconscious, weak pulse, shallow breathing - evacuate immediately

PREVENTION:
1. Stay dry (moisture management is prevention number one)
2. Eat and drink constantly (fuel for heat production)
3. Monitor yourself AND partners (buddy checks every hour)
4. Change wet layers immediately
5. Recognize early signs and act immediately

TREATMENT (mild to moderate):
1. Get out of wind and wet
2. Remove wet clothing, replace with dry
3. Insulate from ground
4. Add layers and external heat sources
5. Feed warm sweet drinks and high-calorie food
6. Monitor continuously
```

### Frostbite and Avalanche Awareness

```
FROSTBITE PREVENTION:
- Appropriate insulation for conditions
- Windproof outer layers on exposed skin
- Keep moving fingers and toes
- Monitor sensation (numbness is a warning sign)
- Buddy checks on faces
- Stay hydrated (dehydration reduces circulation)

FROSTBITE TREATMENT:
- Protect from further cold exposure
- Do NOT rub frozen tissue
- Do NOT rewarm if there is risk of refreezing
- Rapid rewarming in warm water (100-108F / 37-42C)
- Evacuate for medical care

AVALANCHE AWARENESS (if above treeline or on steep slopes):
- Take a formal avalanche course (not optional for this terrain)
- Essential gear: beacon, probe, shovel (all three required)
- Red flags: recent avalanche activity, cracking/collapsing snow,
  heavy recent snowfall, wind-loaded slopes, rapid warming
- Avoid slopes between 30-45 degrees (prime avalanche angle)
```

## Winter Camping Gear Checklist

```
ESSENTIAL GEAR:
- 4-season tent or winter shelter
- Sleeping bag (rated 10-15F below expected low)
- Sleeping pads (R-value 5+ combined)
- Complete layering system (base, mid, shell, insulated)
- Extra dry base layers (minimum 1 spare set)
- Winter boots, gaiters, camp booties
- Glove/mitten system (liner + insulated + expedition)
- Head system (beanie + balaclava + neck gaiter)
- Stove and extra fuel (50% more than summer)
- Insulated water bottles and/or thermos
- High-calorie food (4,000-6,000 cal/day)
- Headlamp + spare batteries
- Fire starting kit (lighter + backup + tinder)
- Emergency shelter (bivy sack or space blanket)
- First aid kit
- Sunglasses AND goggles (snow blindness is real)
- Sunscreen (snow reflects UV intensely)
- Chemical hand/toe warmers
- Repair kit (duct tape, cord, spare parts)
```

## Trip Planning Protocol

```
BEFORE EVERY WINTER TRIP:
1. Check weather forecast from multiple sources
2. Check avalanche forecast if applicable
3. File detailed trip plan with emergency contact
4. Set turnaround time and conditions (and honor them)
5. Pack for conditions one level worse than forecast
6. Ensure everyone in group has adequate gear and skills
7. Establish communication plan (satellite messenger if remote)
8. Know nearest evacuation routes and emergency services

DECISION FRAMEWORK IN THE FIELD:
If conditions deteriorate beyond plan: execute contingency
If anyone shows hypothermia signs: stop, treat, reassess
If gear fails critically: retreat to safety
If avalanche risk exceeds comfort: change route or turn back
The mountain will be there next time.
```
