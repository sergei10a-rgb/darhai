# Appreciative Inquiry Facilitator

## When to Use

**Use this skill when:**
- A user wants to design or facilitate an Appreciative Inquiry (AI) summit, workshop, or team session and needs help structuring the 4-D cycle (Discover, Dream, Design, Destiny)
- A user needs to write paired interview protocols, including question design calibrated to elicit genuine peak-experience stories rather than generic positive answers
- A user is trying to craft Provocative Propositions (also called Design Statements or Possibility Propositions) that are grounded in the positive core and written in affirming present tense
- An organization or community is planning a large-scale participatory change process and needs help sequencing the summit agenda, forming table groups, and creating a harvest plan
- A coach, OD practitioner, HR leader, or community organizer wants to shift a deficit-focused conversation (performance reviews, strategic planning, post-conflict reconciliation) into a strengths-based inquiry
- A facilitator needs to adapt AI methodology for small groups (under 20 people), virtual environments, or time-constrained settings (60--90 minutes)
- A user is preparing facilitator guides, participant workbooks, or post-summit action-tracking frameworks for an AI engagement
- A team wants to embed appreciative practices into recurring rituals -- staff meetings, one-on-ones, onboarding, quarterly reviews

**Do NOT use when:**
- The user needs a root-cause analysis, failure mode investigation, or structured problem-solving process -- use a Systems Thinking or 5-Whys methodology skill instead
- The organization is in an acute crisis requiring immediate triage (safety incidents, financial insolvency, legal emergencies) where problem diagnosis is genuinely unavoidable before forward movement
- The user wants a traditional strategic planning process with SWOT analysis, gap assessment, and remediation roadmaps -- use a Strategic Planning skill instead
- The user needs individual performance management, disciplinary documentation, or corrective action planning -- these require a different HR methodology
- The request is about building survey instruments for statistical benchmarking or voice-of-customer quantitative research -- use a Research Design skill
- The user needs conflict mediation where one party denies wrongdoing and legal or HR investigation is required before dialogue
- The context involves trauma processing, grief work, or clinical mental health intervention -- refer to trauma-informed facilitation or therapeutic frameworks
- The user needs a training curriculum on AI theory rather than facilitation guidance -- use an Instructional Design skill

## Process

### Step 1: Establish the Affirmative Topic Choice

The single most important design decision in any AI process is choosing the right affirmative topic. Everything downstream -- interview questions, Dream exercises, Provocative Propositions -- is shaped by this choice.

- Reframe the presenting concern into its positive opposite: "High turnover" becomes "Magnetic culture that attracts and retains extraordinary people"; "Poor interdepartmental communication" becomes "Seamless cross-functional collaboration that multiplies impact"
- The affirmative topic must be genuinely desired by participants, not just by leadership. Topics imposed from above generate compliance, not energy
- A well-formed affirmative topic is specific enough to be actionable but broad enough to invite diverse stories. "Employee wellness" is too vague; "The conditions under which our people do their best work and feel most energized" is well-formed
- Test the topic by asking: "If we fully achieved this, would it address the underlying concern?" If yes, the reframe is valid
- For multi-topic summits (3+ days, 100+ people), identify 3--5 affirmative topics that form a coherent thematic cluster
- Involve a small steering committee (6--12 people representing different levels and functions) to co-create and validate the affirmative topics before finalizing them
- Document the original presenting problem and its affirmative reframe side-by-side. This helps skeptics see that AI is not ignoring real challenges -- it is approaching them from a generative angle

### Step 2: Design the Paired Interview Protocol

The paired interview is the engine of the Discover phase. It generates the raw material -- stories, values, strengths, wishes -- that every subsequent phase depends on.

- Write 3--5 interview questions per topic. The first question should always invite a peak-experience story: "Tell me about a time when you felt most proud, most engaged, or most alive in this work. Walk me through what happened, who was involved, and what made it exceptional."
- Follow the story question with a values question: "Without being modest, what do you most value about yourself that you brought to that experience?"
- Include a life-giving factors question: "What do you think are the core strengths of this organization that made that peak experience possible?"
- End with a wishes or forward-looking question: "Looking forward, what are your deepest hopes or three wishes for this organization?"
- Each interview should run 20--25 minutes per direction (45--50 minutes total for the pair). For time-constrained settings, reduce to one question per direction with 8--10 minutes each
- Instruct interviewers to listen deeply and take notes on specific words, images, and metaphors the storyteller uses -- these become the raw language for Provocative Propositions
- Pair people who do not normally work together. Cross-functional and cross-hierarchical pairing generates richer data and breaks down silos as a side effect
- Provide printed interview guides. Do not rely on participants to remember questions. The guide should include space to record key quotes, themes, and standout phrases

### Step 3: Facilitate the Discover Phase and Map the Positive Core

The Discover phase moves from individual stories (pairs) to collective themes (tables) to organizational patterns (whole room). Each level of aggregation extracts higher-order meaning.

- After paired interviews, bring pairs into table groups of 6--8. Each person shares the most compelling story or insight from their partner (not their own) -- this creates generosity and cross-pollination
- Give table groups 20--25 minutes to identify 3--5 themes that emerged across all the stories at their table. Themes should be expressed as vivid, evocative phrases, not abstract nouns. "The time people stepped up without being asked" is better than "initiative"
- Have each table report 2--3 themes to the whole room. Use a visual harvest wall, sticky notes, or a projected document to capture everything in real time
- As themes accumulate, cluster them into a Positive Core Map: a visual diagram showing the organization's deepest strengths, organized into 4--6 categories (e.g., People & Relationships, Processes & Practices, Leadership & Culture, Impact & Purpose, Innovation & Learning)
- The Positive Core Map becomes the foundation document for the entire summit. Post it visibly and refer back to it throughout the Design and Destiny phases
- Plan for a dedicated harvest team of 2--3 people whose sole job is to capture outputs, quotes, and themes in real time. Do not rely on the lead facilitator to do this
- Allocate 2.5--3.5 hours for the full Discover phase in a summit setting. Rushing it produces thin material for Design

### Step 4: Facilitate the Dream Phase

The Dream phase expands possibility thinking. Its job is to stretch the group from "what has been" to "what could be" -- not incrementally, but aspirationally.

- Open the Dream phase by sharing the most evocative stories and themes from Discover. This reconnects participants to the energy generated during interviews and provides the launching pad for imagination
- Use a creative visioning exercise as the primary Dream method for groups of 20 or more. Give table groups 35--45 minutes to create a 4--5 minute skit, news broadcast from the future, documentary trailer, illustrated magazine cover, or visual mural showing the organization at its absolute best 3--5 years from now
- Brief the groups carefully: "This is not a presentation of current reality. It is a creative performance of your preferred future as if it has fully arrived. Be specific. Show, don't tell."
- For more analytical or introverted groups, offer an alternative: a guided visualization (10 minutes) followed by individual journaling (5 minutes), partner sharing (5 minutes), and table synthesis (10 minutes)
- Each table then distills its Dream into 2--3 "Dream Statements" using the frame: "We are an organization/community where... We are known for... The impact we create is..."
- Facilitate a whole-group convergence: post all Dream statements and conduct a dot-vote or structured discussion to identify the 4--6 Dream Themes that carry the most energy and resonance across the full group
- Dream Themes become the design architecture for the next phase. They should feel both inspiring and grounded -- connected visibly to the Positive Core, not invented from nothing

### Step 5: Craft Provocative Propositions

Provocative Propositions (PPs) are the most technically demanding output of any AI process. They are design statements written in the present tense that declare the desired future as if it already exists.

- Each PP must satisfy four criteria simultaneously. It must be (1) grounded -- rooted in documented examples from the Discover phase; (2) desired -- reflecting what participants genuinely want, not what leaders prescribed; (3) provocative -- stretching beyond current reality in a way that challenges the status quo; and (4) positive -- written in affirming, declarative language with no deficit framing
- The standard PP structure: "[Organization/team] is a place where [specific desired condition]. We [observable behavior or practice that makes it real]. As a result, [impact on key stakeholders]."
- Form Design Teams of 5--8 people who self-select around each Dream Theme. Each team writes 2--3 PPs for their theme
- Give Design Teams 45--60 minutes to draft their PPs. Circulate as a facilitator and offer coaching on these common errors: future tense ("will be" instead of "is"), deficit framing ("no longer struggles with" instead of a positive statement), vagueness ("is a great place to work" instead of describing specific practices), and disconnection from the Positive Core (invented aspirations with no evidence base)
- Conduct a Gallery Walk: post all PPs on walls around the room. Give participants sticky dots (3 per person) to vote for the PPs that resonate most strongly. Give them sticky notes to add suggestions or enhancements
- Refine the top-voted PPs in a brief whole-group session. A complete AI process typically yields 8--15 final PPs across all design themes
- PPs are organizational commitments, not individual goals. They describe the system, not the person. They are the equivalent of a constitutional charter for the change initiative

### Step 6: Facilitate the Destiny Phase

The Destiny phase (sometimes called Deliver) converts aspirational design into self-organized, committed action. The key principle is that action must be self-chosen, not assigned.

- Open Destiny by reading the final PPs aloud. Ask the group: "Which of these propositions calls to you most strongly? Where do you see yourself contributing?"
- Use Open Space Technology or Innovation Fair format: propositions are posted as "stations" around the room. Participants physically move to the station that most interests them and self-organize into Innovation Teams of 4--8 people per proposition
- Each Innovation Team completes an Action Charter with five fields: (1) the PP they own, (2) the first three concrete actions they will take within 30 days, (3) the owner or champion for each action, (4) resources or support needed, (5) how they will know they are making progress
- Have each team present a 2-minute "commitment statement" to the full group. Public commitment dramatically increases follow-through compared to private planning
- Each participant writes a personal commitment card: "I personally commit to [specific action] by [specific date] to contribute to [PP title]." These are signed and shared with a partner who serves as an accountability buddy
- Design a post-summit rhythm: Innovation Teams should meet every 2 weeks for the first 90 days, report progress monthly to the broader organization, and reconvene for a 90-Day Celebration event to share stories of progress and recalibrate
- Appoint an AI Stewardship Team of 3--5 people (not the facilitation team) who sustain the process after the summit ends -- tracking action charters, circulating stories, and integrating AI practices into organizational routines

### Step 7: Design the Facilitation Architecture and Logistics

The physical and temporal design of an AI event is as important as the content. Poor logistics undermine even the best process design.

- **Room setup:** Round tables of 6--8 people. Never rows or classroom style. Tables should be close enough for energy but spread enough for movement. Assign seats using a pre-designed seating chart that ensures cross-functional and cross-hierarchical mixing
- **Table composition:** For groups of 50+, rotate table assignments at each phase transition (after Discover, after Dream) so each person builds relationships with 18--24 different people across a 2-day summit
- **Participant ratio:** One facilitator per 50--75 participants for plenary facilitation; one table coach per 4--5 tables for small-group support
- **Time allocations for a 2-day summit:** Day 1 Discover (3.5 hours active), Dream kickoff (1 hour). Day 2 Dream completion (2 hours), Design (3 hours), Destiny (3 hours). Total active process time: 12.5--13 hours over two days
- **Documentation:** Assign a harvest team. Use large sticky notes, chart paper, and digital photography. Consolidate all outputs into a Summit Report delivered to participants within 5 business days
- **Energy management:** Plan a physical energizer or movement break every 75--90 minutes. AI summits are cognitively and emotionally intense. Music during table work, creative expression during Dream, and celebration during Destiny all serve energy management functions
- **Virtual adaptation:** For virtual summits, use breakout rooms of 5--6 for interviews and table work. Use a shared digital whiteboard (Mural, Miro, or equivalent) for positive core mapping and PP gallery walks. Reduce session length to 3 hours maximum per day and build in more frequent breaks

## Rules

1. **Never reframe a topic so positively that it becomes dishonest.** If an organization has had a serious safety incident, "Radical safety excellence" is a valid affirmative topic. "We are already safe" is not. The inquiry must be grounded in real evidence from the Positive Core, not wishful thinking.

2. **The first interview question must always invite a specific story, not an opinion.** "What do you think about our culture?" produces abstractions. "Tell me about a time when you were most proud to work here -- walk me through what happened" produces narrative evidence. Stories are the data of Appreciative Inquiry.

3. **Provocative Propositions must be written in present tense, active voice, and without deficit language.** Past tense ("we have created"), future tense ("we will become"), and negation ("we no longer struggle with") all weaken the design statement. Present tense declarative is the only correct form.

4. **Do not allow leadership to pre-write Provocative Propositions before the summit.** PPs that are not generated by participants carry no motivational energy and signal that the AI process is theater. Leadership can suggest affirmative topics during pre-summit design; participants must generate the propositions themselves.

5. **Cross-functional and cross-hierarchical pairing is mandatory, not optional.** Pairing people who already work together closely produces redundant themes and misses the connective tissue function of the Discover phase. The seating plan and interview pairing strategy must be designed in advance.

6. **A summit with no harvest team is a summit that will be forgotten.** Real-time documentation by dedicated harvesters -- not the lead facilitator, not participants -- is a non-negotiable structural requirement for any event with more than 20 people.

7. **Do not skip the positive core mapping step.** Moving directly from interview sharing to Dream without synthesizing a Positive Core Map causes the Dream phase to float free of evidence. PPs drafted without a visible Positive Core Map are typically disconnected from organizational reality and fail the "grounded" criterion.

8. **Action commitments in the Destiny phase must be self-chosen and specific.** "I will support the wellness initiative" is not a commitment. "I will bring the Walking Meeting practice to my team's Monday standup starting October 14" is a commitment. Facilitate specificity by asking: "What exactly will you do? By when? How will you or someone else know you did it?"

9. **Virtual AI facilitation requires smaller breakout groups and more structured prompts.** Groups larger than 5--6 in a virtual breakout room produce dominated conversations and passive participation. Every virtual small-group exercise needs a printed or displayed facilitation prompt, a designated timekeeper within the group, and a note-taker who will paste outputs into a shared document before the group returns to plenary.

10. **Appreciative Inquiry is not positive thinking, toxic positivity, or the suppression of legitimate concerns.** If a participant raises a real problem during an AI session, acknowledge it directly: "That is a real challenge. Let me ask: when has this challenge been at its least severe? What was different then?" This redirects toward inquiry without dismissing the concern. Facilitators who reflexively shut down negative comments damage trust and undermine the process.

## Edge Cases

### When the Group Has Experienced Recent Trauma or Loss

Organizations that have recently gone through layoffs, a leader's death, a public scandal, or a serious safety event require a modified opening. Do not jump directly into peak-experience stories.

- Open with a brief, facilitator-led acknowledgment of the difficulty: "Before we look forward, let's take a moment to honor what has been hard." Allow 5--10 minutes of structured sharing using a grief-to-growth frame: "What have we learned about ourselves and each other through this difficulty?"
- Then transition: "Even in difficult times, there are moments of grace, resilience, and people showing up for each other. Tell me about one of those moments." This creates a bridge from the pain to the Discover phase without bypassing it
- Do not attempt to facilitate the full 4-D cycle in a single session when trauma is recent. Consider a two-stage design: a 90-minute "healing and honoring" session followed by a full AI summit 4--6 weeks later

### When Leadership Wants Predetermined Outcomes

This is the most common AI process failure mode. A sponsor who says "We want the summit to produce buy-in for the reorganization we have already decided on" is not describing an AI process -- they are describing a communication exercise.

- Facilitate an explicit conversation with the sponsor before accepting the engagement: "Appreciative Inquiry works because participants genuinely influence the outcome. If the outcome is already decided, I recommend a different approach -- a structured town hall or change communication campaign. Would you like to talk through the difference?"
- If the sponsor genuinely wants AI but also has non-negotiable constraints (a budget ceiling, a regulatory requirement, a structural change that cannot be reversed), those constraints should be disclosed to participants at the start of the summit as "given conditions" within which the inquiry will operate. Hidden constraints discovered by participants mid-summit destroy trust instantly
- Document this conversation and the sponsor's commitments in writing before proceeding

### When the Group Is Highly Skeptical or Cynical

In organizations with a history of failed change initiatives, participant cynicism about "another workshop" is common and legitimate.

- Name it directly in the opening: "Some of you may be thinking 'here we go again.' That skepticism is earned, and it is welcome data. One of our goals today is to figure out what makes change actually stick in this organization -- and that requires your honest experience, including the times change has failed."
- Use the cynicism as inquiry material: "Tell me about a time when a change initiative at this organization actually worked. What made it different?" This both honors the skepticism and redirects it toward generative analysis
- Keep early exercises low-stakes and brief. Do not open with a 45-minute paired interview when trust is low. Start with a 5-minute pair share on a single question to demonstrate that AI conversations feel different from typical workshops
- Debrief the exercise explicitly: "How was that different from what you expected?" This metacognitive reflection accelerates trust-building

### When the Group Is Very Small (Under 10 People)

The standard AI summit format assumes mass participation for diversity of perspective. With very small groups, the architecture must change.

- Conduct paired interviews as normal, but instead of table-group aggregation, bring all pairs into a single whole-group sharing circle. Every person shares their partner's story -- no table layer needed
- The Positive Core Map is created by the whole group together on a single large sheet of paper or whiteboard
- Dream phase: skip the creative performance exercise (too theatrical for 6 people) and use guided visualization followed by individual journaling and whole-group sharing
- Design phase: the whole group writes PPs together rather than in separate design teams. Aim for 3--5 PPs total
- Destiny phase: each person owns one or more commitments. No need for formal Innovation Teams -- create a shared accountability document instead
- Total time for a small-group AI process: 3--4 hours is sufficient for a meaningful full-cycle experience

### When Operating Across Multiple Languages or Cultures

AI was developed primarily in Western organizational contexts. Several of its conventions require cultural calibration.

- "Without being modest, what do you value about yourself?" is deeply uncomfortable in high-context, collective cultures (many East Asian, Southeast Asian, and Indigenous contexts) where individual self-promotion violates social norms. Reframe as: "What strengths do your colleagues, family, or community see in you?" or "What did your contribution make possible for the people around you?"
- The peak-experience question assumes participants have experienced moments of exceptional engagement. In organizations where workers have experienced exploitation or marginalization, modify to: "Tell me about a time when this work felt most meaningful to you, or when you saw the organization at its best."
- For multilingual summits, provide interview guides in all participant languages. For large multilingual groups, create language-specific table clusters for small-group work and bring a translator into plenary for whole-group sharing. Do not rely on participants to self-translate during emotionally rich exercises -- cognitive load kills depth
- In cultures with strong power distance, participants may be reluctant to share critical wishes or dissatisfactions even within the AI frame. Use anonymous written input (sticky notes without names) as an alternative to verbal sharing during the wishes question

### When the 4-D Cycle Must Be Compressed Into a Single Hour

Full AI requires 2--3 days for a summit or 3--4 hours for a meaningful workshop. When a team has only 60--90 minutes, a stripped-down but structurally complete micro-AI can still be effective.

- 5 minutes: Appreciative check-in -- "Share one thing that went well this week" (whole group round-robin)
- 10 minutes per direction: Single paired interview question -- "Tell me about a recent moment when our team was at its best. What were we doing? What made it work?" (20 minutes total)
- 15 minutes: Whole-group positive core harvest -- "What themes do you notice across our stories? What are our core strengths?"
- 10 minutes: Quick Dream -- "If we fully amplified these strengths over the next 6 months, what would be different? What would others notice about us?"
- 10 minutes: One PP per table (or for the whole group) -- written together using the present-tense format
- 10 minutes: Personal commitments -- one commitment per person, stated aloud or written on an index card
- This micro-format does not replace a full AI process but builds AI literacy and demonstrates the methodology in action. It is appropriate for ongoing team meetings as a recurring practice

### When Provocative Propositions Are Too Vague After Multiple Revision Rounds

Sometimes Design Teams produce PPs that remain abstract and inspiring-sounding but operationally empty ("We are an organization that values every person"). This is the most common PP failure mode.

- Ask the team: "If a new employee joined this organization tomorrow, what would they see, hear, or experience within the first week that would show them this proposition is real?" Force behavioral specificity
- Ask: "Which element of our Positive Core Map directly supports this proposition? Can you name the specific stories that prove this is achievable?" If the team cannot, the proposition is not grounded and needs rewriting
- Ask: "What is provocative about this -- what does it challenge or stretch beyond your current state?" If nothing about the statement is uncomfortable, it is not provocative enough
- A useful diagnostic: read the PP aloud and ask the group to rate it on a scale of 1--5 for (a) groundedness, (b) desire, (c) provocation, and (d) clarity. Any dimension scoring below 3 needs targeted revision. Do this rating exercise in 3 minutes -- it surfaces disagreements quickly and efficiently

### Provocative Propositions (Draft Examples for Design Teams)

**Dream Theme 1: Belonging and Psychological Safety**

PP 1: "[Hospital] is a place where every nurse -- new graduate or
20-year veteran -- feels genuinely welcomed and backed by their team.
We have established unit-level buddy systems, weekly team check-ins that
open with appreciative reflection, and a culture where asking for help
is a sign of professional maturity. As a result, new nurses reach full
clinical confidence 30% faster and choose to renew their contracts at
exceptional rates."

PP 2: "Our nursing units are communities where conflict is addressed
with directness and care, not avoidance. Charge nurses are equipped
with strengths-based facilitation skills and practice them weekly.
As a result, nurses describe their team as the primary reason they
stay at this hospital."

**Dream Theme 2: Career Visibility and Growth**

PP 3: "Every nurse at [Hospital] has a visible, individualized growth
pathway that connects their clinical strengths to the roles they want
to hold in 2 and 5 years. Our Clinical Ladder program is known in
the region as the most transparent and accessible in healthcare.
As a result, internal promotion fills 70% of senior nursing vacancies
and nurses cite career opportunity as a top reason for choosing to stay."

**Dream Theme 3: Workload Sustainability**

PP 4: "[Hospital] is an organization where nursing workload is
continuously calibrated to ensure every nurse can deliver the quality
of care they trained for. Our charge nurses have real-time staffing
tools, our float pool is robustly supported, and 'asking for coverage'
is never a career risk. As a result, nurse-reported burnout scores
are below the 20th percentile nationally and our nurses are physically
and emotionally present for their patients."

**Grounding note:** PP 3 is grounded in Positive Core themes of
"exceptional peer mentorship" and "charge nurses who remember every
nurse's name and goals." PP 4 is grounded in documented peak experiences
of "the shift when we were fully staffed and the care felt like what
we trained to do." All PPs are reachable because examples exist --
they need amplification, not invention.

### Facilitation Notes and Cautions

- **Anticipated resistance:** Physicians and some administrators attending
  the summit may default to problem-focused language ("the real issue is
  scheduling"). Acknowledge their expertise and redirect: "That is
  important context. Let me ask -- when scheduling has worked well for
  nursing, what was in place?" Do not shut down the concern; redirect
  it into inquiry.

- **Night-shift representation:** Ensure 25--30% of participants are
  night-shift nurses. If they are underrepresented, their stories and
  conditions will be absent from the Positive Core. Consider compensating
  night-shift attendees for their schedule disruption.

- **Leadership visibility:** The CNO and at least 2 nursing directors
  should participate as full participants, not observers. They should
  sit at mixed tables and be subject to the same interview process.
  Leaders who sit at the back or leave for calls model disengagement
  and undermine the whole-system premise of AI.

- **Energy management:** Nursing staff are often exhausted before they
  arrive. Open Day 2 with a genuine energizer (not a forced fun exercise).
  Recommended: a 10-minute "best patient story" rapid-share circle --
  this reconnects nurses to their purpose and generates immediate energy
  for the Dream phase.

- **Post-summit cynicism prevention:** At the close of Day 2, be explicit:
  "Within 5 business days, you will receive the full summit report.
  Within 30 days, every Innovation Team will share their first update.
  If 90 days pass and you have not heard progress, that is a signal
  to hold us accountable." Managing expectations for follow-through
  is the single most important closing intervention.

### Post-Summit Sustainability Plan

- **AI Stewardship Team:** 5-person team including 1 CNO representative,
  2 bedside nurses (from different units), 1 charge nurse, and 1 HR
  partner. Meets monthly. Owns the 90-day rhythm and annual
  re-engagement.

- **Stories to circulate:** Monthly "Choosing to Stay" story feature
  in the nursing newsletter. Format: 150-word story from a nurse
  describing a moment that reflects one of the Provocative Propositions.
  Collected by the Stewardship Team via a simple submission form.

- **Embedded practices:** Start every unit staff meeting with a
  2-minute appreciative check-in: "What went well on our unit since
  we last met?" Orientation for new nurses includes a
  "Positive Core Story" session where a veteran nurse shares a
  peak-experience story from the summit.

- **90-Day Celebration:** Half-day event (3 hours) for all summit
  participants. Each Innovation Team shares one story of progress
  (not a status report -- a story). Celebrate specific people by name.
  Introduce 2--3 new PPs if momentum calls for expansion.

- **Annual rhythm:** Each September, conduct a 3-hour AI mini-summit
  (new employee cohort + returning staff) to refresh the positive core,
  check progress on propositions, and generate new initiatives.
  The annual revisit signals that AI is a cultural practice, not a
  one-time event.
