# Recommendation Letter

## When to Use

**Use this skill when:**
- A user asks to write a recommendation letter for a student applying to undergraduate, graduate, or professional school (MBA, law, medical)
- A user needs a reference letter for a job applicant, particularly for senior or competitive roles where a letter carries weight
- A user needs to write a letter supporting a colleague or student for a fellowship, grant, award, or competitive program (NSF, Fulbright, Rhodes, MacArthur, internal company awards)
- A user needs a letter endorsing someone for a promotion, tenure review, professional certification, or board membership
- A user is a candidate who needs help drafting a recommendation letter to hand to their recommender as a starting point
- A user needs a letter of support for a visa application, research partnership, or collaborative grant (these follow professional recommendation conventions, not housing reference conventions)
- A user is writing a character reference for a professional licensing board (bar admission, medical licensure, CPA)

**Do NOT use this skill when:**
- The user wants to write their own cover letter or personal statement -- use career writing skills, which follow a first-person narrative structure entirely different from third-party endorsement
- The user needs a personal character reference for a court proceeding or legal matter -- these have specific legal conventions and risk profiles that require legal guidance
- The user needs a landlord reference or housing reference -- these focus on financial reliability and tenancy behavior, not professional competence
- The user wants to write a LinkedIn recommendation -- these are 100-300 word public endorsements with a conversational tone and no formal structure; they are a different genre entirely
- The user needs a performance review or annual evaluation -- these are internal documents with HR-specific formats and legal constraints
- The user wants to write a letter declining to recommend someone -- declining communications are brief, tactful, and follow a different logic
- The user is writing a testimonial for a product or service -- commercial endorsements have different rhetorical goals and no credentialing structure

## Process

### Step 1: Conduct a Structured Intake Before Writing a Single Word

The single biggest failure mode in recommendation letters is writing from insufficient information. Before drafting, collect all of the following:

- **Relationship architecture:** How does the recommender know the candidate? What was the power dynamic (advisor-advisee, supervisor-employee, collaborator, instructor-student)? Duration -- months or years? Intensity -- daily contact, occasional project, classroom-only?
- **Purpose and opportunity:** What specifically is the letter for? A letter for a PhD program in materials science requires completely different emphasis than one for a product management role. Get the exact program name, company, fellowship, or award.
- **Target audience:** Who reads this letter and what do they care about? Admissions committees at research universities care about intellectual independence and research potential. Hiring managers at growth-stage companies care about execution, ownership, and speed. Fellowship panels care about distinction and originality. Misaligning emphasis is one of the most common ways letters fail.
- **The candidate's submitted materials:** What is the candidate saying about themselves in their resume, personal statement, or application? The letter must add new information -- not restate what the committee already knows. If the candidate's resume says "led a team of 12," the letter must explain what their leadership actually looked like in practice.
- **Concrete examples:** Collect at minimum three specific incidents, projects, or behaviors with enough detail to be vivid. "She is a strong communicator" is useless. "When our executive team rejected her proposal in a Q2 review meeting, she asked three clarifying questions, revised the proposal overnight, and secured approval at the next meeting" is evidence.
- **Comparative ranking:** Every strong letter includes a percentile or comparative statement. Ask directly: "In the context of everyone you've supervised or taught in this capacity, how does this person rank?" Force a specific answer -- "top 10%," "the best in five years," "one of three students I'd call exceptional in my career." Vague enthusiasm without ranking is a yellow flag for experienced readers.
- **Candidate's self-assessment:** If the candidate is providing input, ask them what qualities they most want emphasized and which specific projects they want highlighted. This is not ghostwriting deception -- it is collaborative accuracy. The recommender will verify and personalize everything.

If the user cannot provide specific examples, do not proceed directly to drafting. Ask probing questions: "Can you describe a moment when they handled a difficult situation unusually well?" or "What did they do that surprised you -- something beyond what you'd expect at their level?"

---

### Step 2: Diagnose the Letter's Strategic Function

Not all recommendation letters play the same role. Before structuring the letter, identify which strategic function it must perform:

- **Confirmatory function:** The candidate is already strong on paper; the letter confirms that the record is real and the person behind it is as good as they appear. This letter should establish the recommender's credibility and validate specific accomplishments.
- **Revelatory function:** The candidate's paper record doesn't fully capture who they are. The letter must reveal qualities -- resilience, leadership under pressure, intellectual range -- that transcripts and resumes cannot show. This letter does more narrative work.
- **Comparative function:** The opportunity is extremely competitive and the committee is choosing among many strong candidates. The letter must differentiate -- explain not just that the candidate is excellent, but what makes them unusual among excellent people.
- **Explanatory function:** The candidate has something in their record that needs context -- a gap, a lower grade period, a career pivot. The letter explains the context credibly and honestly without being defensive.

Most letters blend these functions, but identifying the primary function shapes the structure. A revelatory letter leads with a story. A confirmatory letter leads with credentials and comparison.

---

### Step 3: Map Competencies to Evidence Using a Grid

Before writing prose, build a competency-evidence map. This prevents the common failure of writing in abstract praise.

Create a mental (or literal) two-column structure:

| Competency to Highlight | Specific Evidence |
|---|---|
| Independent research aptitude | Identified a flaw in the existing methodology unprompted, proposed a correction, validated it in three months without supervision |
| Communication across audiences | Translated technical findings to a non-specialist clinical audience; clinician described it as "the first time I could evaluate a computational claim myself" |
| Leadership under pressure | When the launch timeline compressed by six weeks, reorganized team task allocation, held daily standups, delivered on the revised date |

Rank these rows by relevance to the specific opportunity. The highest-ranked competency gets the most space -- typically a full paragraph of 120-180 words. The second gets 90-130 words. The third, if included, gets 60-90 words or is folded into the closing.

Never list more than three core competencies. Readers remember the first two claims strongly. A letter that claims seven strengths is remembered as claiming zero -- the evidence gets diluted.

---

### Step 4: Draft Using the Proven Five-Paragraph Architecture

The recommendation letter has a settled structural logic developed over decades of institutional reading. Deviation requires justification. Follow this structure:

**Paragraph 1 -- Opening credentialing and headline (60-90 words):**
- Identify who the recommender is in one sentence -- enough to establish credibility, not a biography
- State the relationship: capacity, duration, intensity
- Deliver the overall recommendation in explicit terms -- "without reservation," "my strongest recommendation," "among the most capable individuals I have supervised." The opening recommendation should be unambiguous. Committees read dozens of letters; they need the signal immediately.
- Include the comparative ranking here or anchor it by promising it will follow.

**Paragraph 2 -- Core competency with primary evidence (120-180 words):**
- State the quality being demonstrated
- Narrate the specific incident or project with enough detail to be vivid and verifiable
- Include concrete specifics: numbers, timelines, outcomes, scale ("team of 8," "three months ahead of schedule," "manuscript accepted to Nature Methods")
- End with the recommender's interpretive assessment -- what does this incident mean? What does it reveal about the candidate's potential?

**Paragraph 3 -- Second competency with supporting evidence (90-130 words):**
- Introduce a distinct quality -- not a repetition or variation of Paragraph 2
- Evidence can be briefer here, but must still be specific
- If possible, show how Paragraph 2 and Paragraph 3 qualities work together -- they compound into a portrait, not a list

**Paragraph 4 (optional) -- Third quality or character dimension (60-90 words):**
- Use this paragraph when there is a meaningful personal quality that the institutional record cannot show: intellectual humility, generosity as a mentor, ethical judgment under pressure
- This paragraph is most valuable in academic letters for programs where collegiality and community contribution matter
- Skip this paragraph for most business-context letters -- keep them tighter

**Paragraph 5 -- Closing with comparative ranking, restatement, and contact offer (60-90 words):**
- State the comparative ranking explicitly if not in Paragraph 1: "Of the 47 master's students I have supervised in fifteen years, Maya ranks in the top three."
- Restate the recommendation with a forward-looking frame: not just "she is excellent" but "she will excel in your program because..."
- Offer explicitly to discuss further, and provide direct contact information (email and phone). This signals genuine confidence -- a lukewarm recommender offers no such invitation.

Total target length: 400-700 words. One page is standard and sufficient for most purposes. Two pages is appropriate only for academic tenure letters or senior executive recommendations with genuinely extensive evidence to present.

---

### Step 5: Apply the Four Quality Tests

After a complete draft, run each of these tests before delivering the letter:

**The Swap Test:** Read the letter and replace the candidate's name with a generic placeholder. Could this letter apply to any competent person in the field? If yes, the specificity is insufficient. Every paragraph should be falsifiable -- it should only be true of this specific person.

**The Evidence Test:** For every claim made in the letter, ask: what is the specific incident that proves this? Unsupported adjectives ("brilliant," "exceptional," "tireless") carry negative credibility with experienced readers because they signal that the recommender is performing enthusiasm rather than reporting evidence.

**The Credibility Calibration Test:** Read the letter's claims against what the recommender could plausibly know. A professor who had a student in one seminar cannot credibly assess their "leadership under pressure." A direct supervisor for three years can. Overclaiming undermines the entire letter. The letter's credibility range equals the recommender's observational access.

**The Lukewarmness Scan:** Read specifically for phrases that function as faint praise: "adequate," "solid," "met expectations," "I believe she has potential," "he should do well," "a pleasant person to work with." In the grammar of recommendation letters, these phrases function as red flags. Committees have learned to read between lines. If any of these appear, rewrite them -- or, if the recommender genuinely cannot say more, have an honest conversation about whether a letter from a different recommender would serve the candidate better.

---

### Step 6: Calibrate Tone and Register to the Institutional Context

Academic letters and professional letters have different conventions. Match the register:

**Academic context (graduate school, fellowships, academic positions):**
- Tone is formal and analytical. The recommender is an authority speaking to peer authorities.
- Language foregrounds intellectual qualities: "rigorous," "independent," "original," "methodologically sophisticated"
- Evidence privileges research outputs: papers, conference presentations, thesis quality, seminar performance
- Comparative ranking is explicitly expected and read carefully
- Letters from dissertation advisors carry more weight than letters from tangential faculty
- Length can stretch to two pages for PhD or faculty position letters

**Professional / corporate context:**
- Tone is confident and direct. Readers are busy.
- Language foregrounds execution and impact: "delivered," "drove," "built," "solved," "led"
- Evidence privileges measurable outcomes: revenue, team size, timelines, business results
- Comparative ranking still valuable but framed in business terms ("one of the strongest analysts I've managed across four teams")
- One tight page is preferred. Two pages is rarely appropriate.

**Fellowship and award context:**
- The committee's question is: "Why this person over other excellent candidates?"
- The letter must answer the distinction question -- not just "she is excellent" but "she is exceptional in ways that are rare"
- Evidence should show the candidate operating at the edge of their field, not just performing competently within it
- The recommender's own stature in the field matters here -- a letter from a recognized leader in the field carries more weight than an identically worded letter from an unknown

---

### Step 7: Handle the Ghost-Writing Scenario With Precision

When the user is the candidate drafting a letter for their recommender, follow this approach:

- Write the letter in first person from the recommender's authentic perspective
- Include only examples the recommender personally witnessed or supervised -- do not invent examples the recommender cannot verify
- Flag every specific example so the recommender knows which claims require their verification
- Build in places where the recommender can add their own language -- a specific anecdote they remember, their own framing of the candidate's impact
- Frame the draft explicitly as a starting point: "Here is a draft for your recommender. Ask them to review every specific claim, personalize any examples they remember differently, and add their own closing in their voice."
- Do not write ghost-drafted letters that would require the recommender to claim personal knowledge they do not have

---

### Step 8: Final Formatting and Delivery Checks

- Use professional block-letter formatting: date, recipient address block (or "Dear Members of the Admissions Committee" for generic submission), formal salutation, body paragraphs, formal close, signature block
- Signature block must include: full name, title, department/organization, institution, email address, phone number -- this is not optional formality, it is a credibility signal
- If submitting electronically, the letter should still be formatted as a formal letter document (PDF), not a plain email
- Match the requested format: some institutions require letters on institutional letterhead; flag this requirement if mentioned
- Check whether the institution has a word or page limit -- some applications specify "no more than one page" or "no more than 500 words"
- Double-check the candidate's name spelling throughout -- one misspelling of the candidate's name damages the entire letter

## Rules

1. **Never write without at least two specific, concrete examples.** One example makes a letter plausible. Two examples make it credible. Zero examples, regardless of how enthusiastic the language is, read as fabricated -- experienced committee members recognize generic praise as a signal that the recommender barely knows the candidate.

2. **Every adjective must be earned by evidence.** "Brilliant," "exceptional," "extraordinary," "remarkable" -- these words only carry weight when the preceding paragraph has already proven them. If the letter claims "exceptional analytical thinking" and the evidence is "she completed all assignments on time," the adjective destroys the letter's credibility.

3. **The comparative ranking is not optional.** It is the single most informative signal in the letter because it gives the committee calibrated context. "Top student in five years" means something different from "top student this semester." A letter without a comparative ranking forces the committee to assume the worst.

4. **Never claim observational authority the recommender does not have.** A recommender who supervised a candidate for one month cannot credibly assess their leadership development over time. Scope claims to what the recommender actually witnessed. Overclaiming is caught and penalized more than appropriate epistemic humility.

5. **The letter must add information the candidate cannot provide about themselves.** If every claim in the letter is something the candidate already said in their personal statement or resume, the letter contributes nothing. The unique value of a recommendation is third-party perspective, credentialed assessment, and comparative ranking -- none of which the candidate can supply.

6. **Lukewarm language is disqualifying, not neutral.** In the grammar of recommendation letters, "I believe she has potential," "he is a pleasant colleague," and "she will likely succeed" are yellow-to-red flags. Committees have decoded this language for decades. If the recommender cannot write enthusiastically and specifically, the honest advice is to decline the request and help the candidate find a recommender who can.

7. **Tailor competencies to the specific opportunity, every time.** A letter that leads with "creative problem-solving" for a computational biology PhD is well-targeted. That same letter submitted for a role as a financial auditor is misaligned. Relevance is the multiplier on every quality you highlight -- an excellent trait that doesn't matter to the reader is wasted space.

8. **Do not include information the candidate has not consented to disclose.** Medical conditions, mental health history, family hardships, financial difficulties -- these may be powerful context, but including them without explicit candidate consent is a violation of trust and, depending on context, may implicate legal protections. The exception is when the candidate has specifically asked the recommender to contextualize a performance gap.

9. **Ghost-drafted letters must stay within the recommender's verified knowledge.** When a candidate drafts a letter for their recommender, the draft must only assert things the recommender can verify as true from their own observation. A fabricated example that the recommender adopts but cannot speak to under follow-up inquiry destroys both the letter's value and the recommender's credibility.

10. **Contact information in the closing is non-negotiable.** An offer to discuss further without contact information is meaningless. Full email and phone in the signature block signals genuine confidence and gives committees the option to verify. Omitting contact information reads -- correctly -- as a recommender who does not expect to be called.

## Edge Cases

### The Candidate Has Asked for a Letter But the Recommender Has Reservations

This is one of the most ethically complex scenarios. Help the user think through the framework:
- If the recommender has genuine reservations about the candidate's fit for the specific opportunity, the most honest and kind action is often to decline to write the letter. A lukewarm letter harms the candidate more than no letter.
- If the reservations are about one dimension but the recommender can genuinely endorse others, write a truthful letter that focuses only on the competencies the recommender can affirm without qualification, and does not address the areas of concern. A narrowly scoped honest letter is legitimate.
- If the user asks you to write a letter that misrepresents the recommender's actual assessment, that is fabrication. Decline the fabrication; help the user either write an honest limited letter or draft a graceful decline note for the recommender to send.

### The Recommender Barely Knows the Candidate

Some institutional cultures require a letter from a specific category of person (department chair, direct supervisor) even when that person had limited contact. Approach:
- Scope the letter explicitly and honestly: "While my direct supervisory contact with [Name] was limited to [specific context], I can speak specifically to what I observed during [precise context]."
- Make the specific evidence work harder -- two vivid observations from limited contact are more valuable than vague enthusiasm across claimed extensive interaction.
- Do not pad the letter with inferred qualities or secondhand reports ("my colleagues tell me she is excellent"). Secondhand praise is nearly worthless and signals the recommender's limited knowledge.
- A shorter, scoped, honest letter is always better than a padded, overclaiming one.

### The Candidate Has a Record Gap or Problematic Element

When the letter must address context for a weak grade period, academic interruption, or career gap:
- Only include this explanation if the candidate has explicitly asked for it and consented to the explanation being shared
- Frame context before assessment: explain the circumstance briefly (one to two sentences), then pivot immediately to performance evidence that demonstrates recovery, resilience, or sustained excellence outside the affected period
- Avoid language that sounds defensive ("despite these challenges") -- use language that sounds forward-looking ("what this period demonstrated was")
- Never speculate about causes you don't know. Only explain what you directly observed or what the candidate told you with their consent.

### Academic Recommendation for a Highly Competitive Research Program (PhD, Rhodes, NSF Graduate Fellowship)

These contexts have experienced, skeptical readers who have read thousands of letters. The bar is different:
- Superlatives must be earned and specific. "The best student I've taught" from a faculty member who has taught for 20 years means something; from someone in their second year, it means less.
- Research evidence is king: co-authorship, conference presentations, independent contributions to methodology, intellectual initiative are the currency. Class performance alone is insufficient evidence of research potential.
- The letter should demonstrate that the candidate can generate original questions, not just execute assigned tasks. This is the crucial distinction between an excellent coursework student and a viable PhD candidate.
- If the recommender is prominent in the field, a brief statement of the recommender's own research positioning helps the committee calibrate the evaluation. A letter from a National Academy member carries different weight than a letter from an adjunct instructor.

### Professional Letter for a Senior or Executive Role

At senior levels, committees and hiring managers are reading for leadership evidence that is specifically about scale, ambiguity, and organizational impact:
- Evidence must match seniority: managing a team of 2 is not leadership evidence for a VP-level letter. Evidence should show decisions made at organizational scope, stakeholders influenced without direct authority, or complex cross-functional leadership.
- Quantification matters more in business letters than academic ones: "$14M budget," "across 6 countries," "reduced churn by 18 points" -- these numbers give the letter specificity that words alone cannot.
- Avoid academic language in business letters. "Demonstrated strong theoretical grounding" is the wrong register. "Built the analytical framework the team still uses two years later" is right.
- One tight page is strongly preferred. Two pages risks reading as a recommender who could not edit -- itself a professional quality signal.

### International Candidates and Cross-Cultural Context

When writing a letter for a candidate whose educational or professional context is unfamiliar to the receiving institution:
- Contextualize institutional rankings or competitive context: "Tsinghua University's Computer Science program accepts roughly 200 students from a national applicant pool of over 1 million" is necessary context for an American reader unfamiliar with the Chinese Gaokao system.
- Grade scales differ internationally: a 7.8/10 GPA from a European institution may be equivalent to a 3.9/4.0 in the US system. If relevant, note this explicitly.
- Research or professional experiences may look different on paper but be substantively equivalent or superior. Help the recommender contextualize what the candidate's role meant in their specific institutional and national context.

### The User Is the Candidate Drafting for Their Recommender

This is entirely standard practice and ethically fine when handled correctly:
- Write in first person from the recommender's perspective, using only information the recommender actually has
- Build in a note at the top of the draft: "DRAFT FOR [RECOMMENDER NAME] -- Please review every specific claim for accuracy, add your own examples, and personalize the closing in your own voice."
- Flag any example that should be verified: "[Recommender: please confirm the specific timing/outcome of this project before keeping this paragraph]"
- The final letter must sound like the recommender, not like a template. Encourage the recommender to change language that doesn't feel natural to them.
- Remind the candidate that the recommender has the right to modify or decline to send anything in the draft -- the candidate is providing a service to the recommender, not committing the recommender to specific content.

## Example

**Input:** "I need to write a recommendation letter for my direct report, James Okafor, who is applying for a VP of Product role at a Series B fintech company. I've managed him for three years. He's been instrumental in our growth -- he took over a struggling product line and turned it around. He's also an unusually good communicator and has become the internal face of our product org for our board and investors. I want to make this letter strong."

**Output:**

---

October 14, 2024

Hiring Committee
[Company Name]

Dear Members of the Hiring Committee,

I write to give my strongest and most unqualified recommendation for James Okafor as your next Vice President of Product. I have been James's direct manager for three years at [Current Company], where I have overseen his work daily across multiple product lines and organizational inflection points. Among the eleven product leaders I have managed in my career, James is the one I would most want building something with me from scratch.

The clearest demonstration of James's product leadership came eighteen months ago when I asked him to take ownership of our payments reconciliation product -- a line that had missed revenue targets for two consecutive quarters, had a 34% user churn rate, and was facing an internal decision about discontinuation. Within six months, James had reduced churn to 11%, launched a revised onboarding flow that cut time-to-first-value from 14 days to 4, and returned the line to quarterly revenue growth that has sustained through today. What distinguished James's approach was not the execution alone but how he got there: he spent his first three weeks doing 40 customer interviews before writing a single line of his turnaround proposal. He showed the internal stakeholders who had written the product off exactly what customers needed and why the prior version had failed them. That combination -- rigorous customer discovery, clear diagnosis, and fast disciplined execution -- is rare in a single leader.

James has also taken on a communication role in our organization that far exceeds his formal title. Over the past year, he has presented our product strategy to our board and our Series C lead investors in four separate sessions. Our board chair told me directly after James's most recent presentation that "he made a complicated roadmap feel inevitable -- I understood not just what you're building but why in that sequence." I do not know many VP-level product leaders who can do that, let alone directors. James builds clarity out of complexity, and he does it without oversimplification -- he respects his audience's intelligence while making sure they leave with the right mental model.

James is also, more quietly, a builder of people. Two members of his product team have been promoted while working under him. Neither promotion was inevitable -- both required James to invest deliberately in their growth, sponsor them visibly, and structure their responsibilities to demonstrate readiness. He does this without being asked and without credit-seeking. In a VP role where building and scaling a team will be as important as building product, this quality matters.

Of the eleven product leaders I have managed, James ranks first in his ability to combine customer rigor, execution discipline, and organizational communication into a coherent leadership profile. He is ready for a VP role -- I would go further and say he has been operating at that level for the past 12 to 18 months. I recommend him without reservation, and I mean that precisely: I have no reservations. Please feel free to call me directly at [phone] or reach me at [email]. I will make time to speak with you.

Sincerely,

[Recommender Full Name]
[Title, e.g., Chief Product Officer]
[Company Name]
[Email address]
[Phone number]

---

**Why this letter works:**
- The comparative ranking ("eleven product leaders," "ranks first") is explicit and calibrated -- it is not hyperbolic but it is specific
- Both primary evidence blocks contain concrete numbers: 34% churn to 11%, 14 days to 4 days, two team promotions
- The turnaround story reveals a specific decision-making process (40 customer interviews before proposing anything) -- this is the kind of observable behavior that differentiates insight from luck
- The board communication paragraph includes a direct quote from a third party (the board chair), which adds a layer of external validation
- The closing restatement explicitly says "I have no reservations" and backs it up with "he has been operating at VP level for 12-18 months" -- a forward-looking calibration that helps the committee
- The tone is professional and direct throughout -- no academic register, no vague enthusiasm, no hedging
- Contact information is in the signature block with an explicit invitation to call
