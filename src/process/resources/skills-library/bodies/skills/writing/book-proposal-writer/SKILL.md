## Questions to Ask First

1. **What is the book about in one sentence?** (If this takes more than 15 seconds, the concept needs sharpening.)
2. **Who is the target reader?** (Be specific: demographic, psychographic, behavior.)
3. **Why are you the right person to write this book?** (Credential, experience, platform, perspective.)
4. **What books already exist on this topic?** (Have you read them? How is yours different?)
5. **Do you have a platform?** (Email list, social media, speaking, media, academic position, professional network.)
6. **What is your publishing goal?** (Big Five, mid-size, academic press, or hybrid?)
7. **Do you have an agent?** (Most major publishers require agented submissions.)
8. **How much of the book is written?** (Proposal can sell before the book is done --- that is the point.)
9. **What is your timeline for completing the proposal?** (Proposals typically take 4-8 weeks to write well.)

## The Non-Fiction Book Proposal: Overview

A standard non-fiction book proposal runs 30-75 pages and contains these sections:

1. **Title Page**
2. **Table of Contents (of the proposal)**
3. **Overview** (2-5 pages)
4. **Target Audience** (1-3 pages)
5. **Competitive Analysis** (2-4 pages)
6. **Author Bio and Platform** (1-3 pages)
7. **Marketing and Promotion Plan** (1-3 pages)
8. **Chapter Outline** (10-20 pages)
9. **Sample Chapters** (20-50 pages)

Total: 40-80 pages is typical.

## Section 1: Overview

### Purpose
The overview is the most important section of the proposal. It must sell the book's concept, demonstrate market need, and establish your authority --- all in 2-5 pages. Think of it as a pitch that an acquisitions editor could use to sell the book at an editorial meeting.

### Overview Structure
**Paragraph 1: The Hook**
Open with a compelling anecdote, startling statistic, or provocative question that illustrates why this book needs to exist. This paragraph should make the reader unable to stop reading.

**Paragraph 2-3: The Problem**
Define the problem your book solves. Be specific. Quantify if possible. Who has this problem and how does it affect them?

**Paragraph 4-5: The Solution**
Introduce your book as the answer. What is your unique approach, framework, or perspective? Why is your approach different from or better than what exists?

**Paragraph 6-7: The Book Description**
What will the reader experience? Describe the book's structure, tone, and approach. Is it prescriptive? Narrative? Research-based? Some combination? What will the reader be able to do or understand after reading?

**Paragraph 8: The Market Context**
Why now? What cultural moment, trend, or need makes this book timely? This paragraph connects your book to something bigger than itself.

**Paragraph 9: The Credentials**
Why you? One paragraph establishing your authority to write this book. (The full bio comes later; this is the elevator version.)

**Final paragraph: The Takeaway**
One powerful sentence or short paragraph that encapsulates the book's value proposition.

### Overview Template
```
[HOOK: Anecdote or statistic that illustrates the problem]

[PROBLEM: What is broken, misunderstood, or missing?]

[SOLUTION: What does your book offer? What is the framework or approach?]

[THE BOOK: What will readers experience? Structure, tone, scope.]

[MARKET: Why now? Cultural context and timing.]

[AUTHORITY: Why you? Credentials and perspective.]

[TAKEAWAY: The book's core promise in one powerful statement.]
```

## Section 2: Target Audience

### Purpose
Demonstrate that you know exactly who will buy this book and that there are enough of them to justify publication.

### Target Audience Framework
**Primary Audience:** The core reader. Describe them as specifically as possible.
```
WHO: [Demographic details: age, profession, education, life stage]
WHERE: [Where do they spend time? Online communities, events, organizations]
WHAT: [What is their current pain point or aspiration?]
WHY: [Why would they pick up this book specifically?]
HOW MANY: [Quantify the audience if possible --- industry size, membership numbers, search volume]
```

**Secondary Audience:** Adjacent readers who would also buy.
```
[Same framework as above, but briefer]
```

**Institutional/Bulk Buyers:** Organizations that might adopt the book.
- Academic courses
- Corporate training programs
- Professional associations
- Government agencies
- Book clubs

### Audience Evidence
Support your audience claims with:
- Relevant market statistics (industry reports, census data)
- Size of relevant communities (subreddits, Facebook groups, professional associations)
- Sales data of comparable books
- Google search volume for key terms
- Existing demand signals (your own platform engagement, speaking event attendance, course enrollment)

## Section 3: Competitive Analysis

### Purpose
Show that you understand the market, that there is room for your book, and that your book fills a gap the competition does not.

### What Competitive Analysis Is NOT
It is NOT trashing other books. It is a professional, respectful assessment that positions your book as complementary and differentiated.

### Competitive Title Template (Analyze 5-7 Books)
```
TITLE by AUTHOR (Publisher, Year) --- [Sales rank or notable achievement]

[2-3 sentences describing what this book does well and who it serves.]

[1-2 sentences describing what it does NOT cover, who it does NOT serve,
or how its approach differs from yours.]

[1 sentence on how your book fills this gap or serves a different audience.]
```

### Competitive Analysis Strategy
- Include books from the last 5 years (preferably 3)
- Include both successful and moderately successful titles
- Do NOT include mega-bestsellers as comps (your book will not be the next "Atomic Habits" --- be realistic)
- DO include books that sold well in your specific niche
- Show that you have read these books, not just scanned their Amazon pages

### Positioning Statement
After analyzing all competitive titles, write a positioning statement:
"[YOUR TITLE] fills the gap between [Book A]'s [strength] and [Book B]'s [strength], offering [your unique combination] for [your specific audience]."

## Section 4: Author Bio and Platform

### Purpose
Prove that you are credible to write this book AND capable of selling it.

### Bio Structure
**Paragraph 1: Core Credentials**
Your most relevant qualification for writing this book. Lead with the strongest credential.

**Paragraph 2: Professional Background**
Career experience relevant to the book's topic. Positions, organizations, achievements.

**Paragraph 3: Platform**
Your existing audience and reach. Be specific with numbers:
- Email list subscribers (and open rate if strong)
- Social media followers (by platform, with engagement metrics)
- Website traffic (monthly unique visitors)
- Podcast audience (downloads per episode)
- Speaking frequency (events per year, audience sizes)
- Media appearances (notable outlets)
- Previous publications (books, articles, columns)

**Paragraph 4: Personal Connection**
Why this topic matters to you personally. Adds human dimension to professional credentials.

### Platform Reality Check
For a major publisher (Big Five), you generally need:
- Email list: 10,000+ subscribers (25,000+ is strong)
- Social media: 50,000+ combined followers with real engagement
- Speaking: Regular engagements with audiences of 100+
- Media: Track record of being featured in relevant outlets
- Previous publications: Articles, columns, or prior books

For mid-size and independent publishers, the bar is lower but platform still matters.

**If your platform is small:** Emphasize growth trajectory, engagement quality, and institutional connections (university, company, organization) that provide built-in audience.

## Section 5: Marketing and Promotion Plan

### Purpose
Show that you will actively partner with the publisher to sell this book. Publishers want authors who are marketing partners, not passive creators.

### Marketing Plan Template
```
PRE-PUBLICATION:
- [Specific action: e.g., "Announce the book to my 15,000-person email
  list 3 months before publication"]
- [Specific action: e.g., "Write guest articles for [Publication 1],
  [Publication 2], and [Publication 3] with built-in audience of X"]
- [Specific action: "Coordinate with my speakers bureau to schedule
  12+ events in the 6 months surrounding publication"]

LAUNCH:
- [Specific action: e.g., "Host a virtual launch event through my
  existing community of X"]
- [Specific action: e.g., "Run a podcast tour targeting shows in
  [relevant category] with an aggregate audience of X"]

POST-PUBLICATION:
- [Specific action: e.g., "Develop a corporate workshop based on the
  book's framework, targeting my existing clients at [companies]"]
- [Specific action: e.g., "Produce a 12-part social media content
  series drawing from each chapter"]

ONGOING:
- [Specific action: e.g., "Feature the book in my weekly newsletter
  to X subscribers on an ongoing basis"]
```

### What Makes a Strong Marketing Plan
- Specific, not vague ("pitch to 20 podcasts" not "do podcast interviews")
- Quantified ("my newsletter reaches 15,000 readers" not "I have a newsletter")
- Realistic (do not promise what you cannot deliver)
- Shows publisher you understand the sales process
- Demonstrates existing relationships and infrastructure

## Section 6: Chapter Outline

### Purpose
Show the full scope and structure of the book. Each chapter summary should make the editor think "I want to read that chapter."

### Chapter Outline Format
```
CHAPTER [#]: [TITLE]
(Estimated length: X,000 words)

[1-2 paragraph summary that covers:]
- The central question or problem this chapter addresses
- The key ideas, frameworks, or arguments presented
- Stories, case studies, or examples included
- What the reader will take away

[Optional: List 3-5 key topics or subheadings within the chapter]
```

### Chapter Outline Best Practices
- Write each chapter summary as if it were its own mini-pitch
- Show variety: mix prescriptive content, narrative, research, and practical application
- Demonstrate logical progression from chapter to chapter
- Include introduction and conclusion summaries
- Total chapter outline section: 10-20 pages (1-2 pages per chapter)
- Show that you have thought about the reader's journey through the book

## Section 7: Sample Chapters

### Purpose
Prove you can write the book. The sample chapters are the writing audition.

### What to Include
- **Always include the Introduction.** This is where the reader meets the book; the editor needs to see it.
- **Include 1-2 body chapters.** Choose chapters that best showcase your writing, research, and unique perspective. They do not have to be consecutive.
- **Total sample chapter length:** 20-50 pages (roughly 8,000-15,000 words per chapter)

### Sample Chapter Quality
These chapters should be publication-ready:
- Fully researched and cited
- Polished prose (not draft quality)
- Strong opening hooks for each chapter
- Mix of narrative, analysis, and practical application
- Demonstrate your voice and authority

### What Not to Include as a Sample
- The weakest chapter (obviously)
- A chapter that is all theory with no stories or examples
- A chapter that requires context from previous chapters to understand

## Proposal Formatting

### Standard Formatting
- 12-point Times New Roman or similar serif font
- 1-inch margins
- Double-spaced body text (single-spaced is acceptable for the overview and chapter outline at some agencies)
- Clear section headers
- Page numbers
- Author name and book title in the header
- Professional, clean design (no fancy fonts, colors, or graphics unless the book is visual)

### Submission
- Follow the specific agent's or publisher's submission guidelines exactly
- PDF is the standard format unless specified otherwise
- Include a cover letter (essentially a brief query) with the proposal
- Do not send unsolicited proposals to editors (go through an agent)

## Common Proposal Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Vague overview | Editor cannot picture the book | Be specific: what, who, how, why now |
| "Everyone" is the audience | Signals market ignorance | Name the specific primary reader |
| Ignoring competition | Appears naive about the market | Analyze 5-7 books with genuine insight |
| Inflating platform | Editors verify claims | Be honest; emphasize quality and trajectory |
| Passive marketing plan | Publisher sees no sales partner | Commit to specific, quantified actions |
| Weak sample chapters | Writing does not match the pitch | Polish until publication-ready |
| Too long | Signals inability to be concise | Keep to 50-75 pages total |
| No urgency | "Why now?" is unanswered | Connect to cultural moment, trend, or growing need |

The proposal is your book's business case. Treat every section as an argument for why a publisher should invest five or six figures in bringing this book to market. Be specific, be honest, be compelling, and be professional. The proposal that demonstrates market savvy, strong writing, and a capable author will rise to the top of every editor's pile.
