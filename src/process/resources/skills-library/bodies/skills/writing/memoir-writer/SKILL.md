## Questions to Ask First

1. **What period or theme of your life does this memoir cover?** (Memoir is a slice, not the whole pie.)
2. **Why do you want to write this?** (Therapy, legacy, advocacy, art? All are valid, but the approach differs.)
3. **Who is your intended reader?** (Family only? General public? A specific community?)
4. **How much emotional distance do you have from the events?** (Can you write about this without being consumed by it?)
5. **Are the people in your story still living?** (Critical for ethical and legal planning.)
6. **Have you written about this before --- journaling, essays, blog posts?** (Identifies existing material.)
7. **What transformation does your memoir show?** (The "then me" vs the "now me.")
8. **Are there legal concerns --- NDAs, custody agreements, pending cases?** (Must address before writing.)
9. **What memoirs have you read and admired?** (Establishes stylistic aspirations.)

## The Fundamental Distinction: Autobiography vs Memoir

**Autobiography:** "This is what happened in my life." Chronological, comprehensive, factual record.
**Memoir:** "This is what it meant." Selective, thematic, emotionally true, shaped into narrative.

A memoir has a **narrative arc** --- a beginning, middle, and end that is not your birth, middle age, and death, but rather the arc of a specific transformation, conflict, or question.

## Phase 1: Finding Your Story

### The Memoir Is Not About What Happened
It is about what you understood, discovered, or became because of what happened. Every strong memoir answers one of these questions:
- How did I survive this?
- How did I become who I am?
- What did I learn that others need to know?
- How did my understanding of something fundamental change?

### Timeline Selection Methods

**Method 1: The Crucible**
Identify the period of greatest pressure and transformation. The memoir begins when the heat turns up and ends when you emerge changed. (Example: "Educated" by Tara Westover covers roughly ages 16-28.)

**Method 2: The Theme Thread**
Choose a theme and pull only the events that illuminate it, even if they span decades. (Example: "The Year of Magical Thinking" uses one year but threads in decades of marriage.)

**Method 3: The Quest**
Structure around a specific pursuit --- a journey, a search, a project, a question. (Example: "Wild" by Cheryl Strayed is framed by the PCT hike.)

**Method 4: The Dual Timeline**
Alternate between past and present, showing how then informs now. The past reveals what happened; the present reveals what it means.

### The Memory Inventory Exercise
Set a timer for 60 minutes. Write down every memory associated with your topic. Do not filter, do not elaborate --- just one line per memory. You will typically generate 50-150 entries. Then:
1. Star the ones that still carry emotional charge
2. Circle the ones that involve turning points
3. Underline the ones that reveal character (yours or others')
4. The memories with multiple marks are your core scenes

## Phase 2: Truth, Memory, and Ethics

### The Truth Spectrum in Memoir
- **Literal truth:** Exactly what happened, verifiable by evidence
- **Emotional truth:** How it felt, even if specific details are reconstructed
- **Narrative truth:** The story shaped from events to convey meaning

All published memoir operates in the space between literal and narrative truth. You are not a court reporter. You are a storyteller working with real material.

### What You Can Ethically Do
- Reconstruct dialogue that captures the essence of what was said
- Compress timelines (combine three similar arguments into one representative scene)
- Omit events that are not relevant to your narrative arc
- Change names and identifying details of private individuals (with disclosure)
- Write from your perspective, acknowledging it is your perspective

### What You Cannot Ethically Do
- Invent events that did not happen
- Attribute actions or words to real people that are fabricated
- Claim to be something you are not (false credentials, false identity)
- Present composite characters as single real people without disclosure
- Alter facts to make yourself look better at others' expense

### Protecting Others: The Ethical Framework
**Tier 1: Public figures** --- Fair game for factual reporting of public actions
**Tier 2: Willing participants** --- People who have agreed to be in your story (get this in writing)
**Tier 3: Peripheral characters** --- Change names and identifying details
**Tier 4: Vulnerable people** --- Children, those with mental illness, victims --- extra caution required
**Tier 5: People who would be harmed** --- Consider: Is including this person essential to the story? Can you tell the truth without causing disproportionate harm?

### The Phone Call Test
Before publishing, ask yourself: Can I defend every claim in this book if the person calls me? Not "will they like it" --- but "is it true and fair?"

### Legal Considerations
- Defamation: A false statement of fact that damages reputation. Truth is a defense.
- Invasion of privacy: Publishing private facts that are not newsworthy. Even true facts can be actionable.
- Right of publicity: Using someone's name/likeness for commercial purposes.
- **Always consult a media attorney before publication if your memoir includes accusations, criminal behavior, or potentially defamatory material.**

## Phase 3: Structure

### Structure 1: Chronological with Frame
The most common memoir structure. A framing device (present-day situation) bookends a chronological narrative of past events. The frame provides perspective and the "now me" voice.

### Structure 2: Thematic/Mosaic
Organized around themes rather than time. Each chapter explores a different facet of the central question. Works well for memoirs about identity, culture, or complex family dynamics.

### Structure 3: Braided
Two or more timelines interwoven, with connections between them becoming clear as the book progresses. Requires careful planning to maintain clarity.

### Structure 4: Hermit Crab
Borrows the structure of another form --- recipes, encyclopedia entries, field guides, letters --- and fills it with memoir content. (Example: "The Chronology of Water" by Lidia Yuknavitch.)

### Structure 5: Quest/Journey
A physical journey or concrete pursuit provides the spine. Inner transformation mirrors outer progress. Clear forward momentum built into the structure.

### The Scene vs Summary Decision
**Scene (show):** Moment-by-moment, with dialogue, sensory detail, and real-time action. Use for:
- Turning points
- Emotionally charged moments
- Revelations and discoveries
- Confrontations

**Summary (tell):** Compressed narration covering days, months, or years. Use for:
- Transitions between key events
- Background information
- Periods of stasis
- Context setting

A common memoir mistake is summarizing the dramatic moments and dramatizing the mundane ones. Reverse that.

## Phase 4: Voice and Emotional Distance

### The Two Voices of Memoir
Every memoir has two narrators:
1. **The experiencing self:** The person living through events, with limited understanding
2. **The narrating self:** The person writing now, with hindsight and reflection

The tension between these two voices is where memoir lives. The experiencing self provides immediacy; the narrating self provides meaning.

### Finding Your Voice
Your memoir voice is not your speaking voice or your journaling voice. It is a crafted literary voice that:
- Sounds like you at your most articulate
- Carries emotional authority without melodrama
- Can shift between close (in-scene) and distant (reflective)
- Has a relationship with the reader (confiding, teaching, sharing)

### Emotional Distance Calibration
- **Too close:** The writing reads like therapy journaling. Raw, unprocessed, focused on the writer's pain rather than the reader's experience. Symptom: extensive use of "I felt."
- **Too far:** The writing reads like clinical observation. Detached, analytical, emotionally flat. The reader cannot feel anything.
- **Right distance:** The writer can describe painful events with specificity and sensory detail, reflect on their meaning, and maintain enough control to craft the scene for the reader's experience.

### The "Two-Year Rule"
Many writing teachers advise waiting at least two years after events before writing memoir. This is not a rigid rule, but the principle is sound: you need enough distance to see pattern and meaning, not just pain.

If you must write about recent events, write a draft for yourself first. The publishable version comes after the emotional processing.

## Phase 5: Craft Techniques

### Sensory Grounding
Memory lives in the senses. Anchor every key scene in at least three sensory details:
- What did the room/place look/smell/sound like?
- What were you wearing/touching/tasting?
- What was the weather, the light, the temperature?

These details make reconstructed scenes feel real to the reader and often unlock deeper memories for the writer.

### Handling Gaps in Memory
Be transparent. Phrases like:
- "I do not remember exactly what she said, but the feeling was..."
- "The details have blurred, but what remains sharp is..."
- "My mother tells it differently. In her version..."

This honesty builds trust with the reader and is more compelling than false precision.

### Dialogue in Memoir
You will not remember exact words from decades ago. You can:
- Reconstruct dialogue that captures the essence and speech patterns
- Use indirect dialogue ("She told me I was being selfish")
- Note that you are reconstructing ("Something like: 'You'll never amount to anything'")
- Use documented sources (letters, emails, recordings) for precision

### The Reflection Passage
After dramatizing a key scene, the narrating self steps in to reflect. This is where meaning is made. Keep reflection passages:
- Brief (a few sentences to a paragraph, rarely more)
- Insightful (say something the reader could not conclude alone)
- Earned (only after the scene has done its emotional work)

## Phase 6: Revision for Memoir

### Pass 1: Arc and Structure
- Is the transformation clear from beginning to end?
- Does each chapter serve the central narrative question?
- Is there enough forward momentum? (Memoir is not just "and then, and then, and then")
- Are the stakes clear? What could be lost?

### Pass 2: Truth Audit
- Have you been honest, even when it is unflattering to you?
- Have you been fair to others, even those who hurt you?
- Are there places where you are performing victimhood or heroism rather than being truthful?
- Does every scene pass the "would I defend this as true?" test?

### Pass 3: Voice and Emotional Calibration
- Is the balance between experiencing self and narrating self working?
- Are there passages that are too raw (need more distance) or too detached (need more feeling)?
- Is the voice consistent throughout?

### Pass 4: Craft
- Line editing for prose quality
- Sensory detail check for key scenes
- Dialogue authenticity
- Pacing (memoir often needs tightening in the middle)

## Common Memoir Pitfalls

| Problem | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| "My life is not interesting enough" | Memoir is about meaning, not spectacle. Quiet lives contain universal truths. |
| Telling instead of showing | Dramatize key moments into full scenes with dialogue and sensory detail |
| Writing for revenge | The reader can always tell. Write to understand, not to punish. |
| Including everything | Ruthlessly cut anything that does not serve the central arc |
| Flat characters (other people) | Give others complexity. Even antagonists had reasons. |
| No reflection | Scenes without meaning are just anecdotes. Add the narrating self's insight. |
| Fear of hurting others | Address directly: change names, get consent, consult an attorney, or accept the consequences |

## Publishing Your Memoir

### Memoir in the Market
- Memoir is one of the strongest-selling non-fiction categories
- Platform matters less for memoir than for prescriptive non-fiction (the story is the sell)
- Agents want a completed manuscript for memoir (unlike other non-fiction, which sells on proposal)
- Comparative titles help agents and editors position your book: "In the vein of [published memoir]"

### The Memoir Query
Memoir queries read like fiction queries --- they sell the story, not the topic:
- Lead with the narrative hook (the situation, the conflict, the stakes)
- Show the arc (where does the narrator start and where do they end up?)
- Establish your unique voice in the query itself
- Include relevant credentials or platform, but the story comes first

### Memoir vs Personal Essay Collection
- A memoir has one continuous arc with a single narrative question
- An essay collection is a series of self-contained pieces unified by theme or voice
- Collections are harder to sell to publishers (lower commercial demand)
- Many memoirists start by publishing personal essays to build a platform and test material

## Resources for Memoir Writers

### Essential Reading on Craft
- "The Art of Memoir" by Mary Karr (the definitive craft book for memoirists)
- "Writing Hard Stories" by Melanie Brooks (interviews with memoirists about difficult material)
- "Handling the Truth" by Beth Kephart (the ethics and craft of memoir)
- "The Situation and the Story" by Vivian Gornick (the relationship between narrator and narrative)

### Workshop and Community
- Writing workshops specifically for memoir (Tin House, Bread Loaf, community programs)
- Memoir-focused critique groups (online and local)
- Sensitivity readers for material involving communities you are not part of
- Therapists who specialize in creative process (writing memoir can surface trauma)

## The Memoir Writer's Manifesto

You have the right to tell your story. You also have the responsibility to tell it with craft, honesty, and compassion --- for yourself and for the people in it. The best memoirs are not about settling scores or processing trauma (though both may happen). They are about offering the reader a window into a specific human experience and, through that window, a mirror for their own.

Write the book only you can write. Write it with the courage to be honest and the skill to make it art.
