# Fan Fiction

## When to Use

**Use this skill when:**
- The user asks to write fan fiction based on a specific existing fictional universe -- a novel series, film franchise, television show, video game, anime, comic book run, or tabletop RPG setting
- The user wants to continue a story from a specific point in canon: "write what happens after the finale," "fill in the time skip," or "continue from chapter X"
- The user wants to explore characters from an established franchise in a new situation -- a crisis, a quiet moment, an unexplored relationship dynamic
- The user asks for a "what if" scenario grounded in an existing world: "what if Character A had made a different choice at this specific moment"
- The user requests an AU (alternate universe), canon-divergent story, or missing scene that requires intimate knowledge of the source world
- The user wants to explore a ship (romantic pairing), a gen friendship dynamic, or a character study set within an existing universe
- The user wants to write a crossover between two or more existing fictional universes
- The user asks specifically for fan fiction tropes by name: hurt/comfort, fix-it fic, fluff, angst, slow burn, enemies-to-lovers within an existing fandom context

**Do NOT use this skill when:**
- The user wants original fiction with wholly original characters and world -- use `short-story-writing`
- The user wants to write a parody or comedic deconstruction of an existing work -- use `satire-writing`
- The user wants to design and develop new characters from scratch with no existing source material -- use `character-development`
- The user wants to outline a plot without writing prose -- use `plot-outline`
- The user wants a critical or analytical essay about a fandom or source text -- use `literary-analysis`
- The user wants to write original poetry inspired by a fandom theme -- use `poetry-writing`
- The user wants to create a new world inspired by but not set within an existing franchise -- use `worldbuilding`

---

## Process

### Step 1: Identify the Source Material, Canon Position, and Compliance Level

Before writing a single word of prose, establish the foundation. This step prevents the most common fan fiction failure: writing a version of a character that readers do not recognize.

- **Identify the fandom precisely:** Not just "Marvel" but "MCU post-Endgame" or "Earth-616 comics circa Jonathan Hickman's Avengers run." The same character behaves differently across canons. Ask the user to clarify if the answer is ambiguous.
- **Establish the canon timeline position:** Where in the characters' arcs does this story take place? "Mid-Season 2 of Breaking Bad" and "post-series Breaking Bad" require completely different versions of Walter White. Characters evolve -- their canon state at the story's start is a hard constraint.
- **Define the compliance level explicitly** -- these three categories have distinct reader contracts:
  - **Canon-compliant:** The story fits within established events without contradicting anything. Missing scenes, gap-fills, and side stories fall here.
  - **Canon-divergent:** The story splits from a specific, named decision point. Everything before that point is preserved; the story explores the alternate branch. Name the divergence point precisely: "diverges from the Battle of Blackwater in Game of Thrones S02E09."
  - **AU (Alternate Universe):** Character psychology is preserved but the world is restructured. The reader contract is different-world-same-souls. The most common AU types are: modern AU (contemporary real-world setting), coffee shop AU, college AU, historical AU, role reversal AU, and fandom fusion AU.
- **Gather the premise:** What is the core "what if," tension, or unexplored dynamic that drives this story? Good fan fiction has a specific premise, not just "characters interact."
- **Confirm tone:** Canon tone, or a deliberate register shift? Know the difference between "this story is darker than canon" and "this story explores a lighter side of a dark canon." Both are valid; they require different techniques.
- **Determine scope:** A single scene (500-1,500 words), a short story (2,000-5,000 words), or a chapter of a longer work (3,000-6,000 words)? Scope determines how much character development, plot, and world texture you can include.

---

### Step 2: Audit the Characters for Voice Markers

Character voice is the single most important quality signal in fan fiction. Readers who love these characters have spent hundreds of hours with them. They will notice within two sentences if a voice is wrong.

- **Identify 5-7 voice markers per major character.** Voice markers are specific, recurring attributes observable across multiple canon sources:
  - **Vocabulary register:** Does this character use formal language, street slang, technical jargon, archaic diction, understatement, hyperbole?
  - **Sentence length and rhythm:** Short, clipped sentences under stress (Geralt of Rivia, John Wick) vs. winding, self-interrupting internal monologue (Hamlet, Bojack Horseman)?
  - **Verbal tics and catchphrases:** Used sparingly -- once or twice per story, not every paragraph
  - **What this character avoids saying:** A repressed character talks around emotions. A prideful character never admits weakness directly. A traumatized character deflects with humor.
  - **Default emotional response mode:** Does this character lead with anger, deflection, sarcasm, warmth, calculation, self-deprecation?
  - **How they refer to other characters:** Nicknames, titles, first names, last names -- these encode relationship and power dynamics
  - **Physical habits under emotional stress:** These belong in action beats, not just dialogue
- **Map the relationship dynamics** between all characters in the scene. Fan fiction lives in the space between characters. Know the power differential, the history, the unsaid thing, the wound, and the warmth before writing.
- **Identify the character's arc position in this story.** Is this version of the character pre-trauma, mid-trauma, or post-trauma? Have they learned their lesson yet, or is this before the breakthrough? Their behavior must be consistent with their arc stage.
- **For supporting characters and OCs:** They must serve the story's purpose without flattening into tools. A new character who exists only to prompt the protagonist to deliver a speech is a paper character.

---

### Step 3: Audit the World Rules

Fan fiction that violates the internal rules of its universe feels like a counterfeit. Even casual readers notice when something is wrong with the world.

- **Hard rules:** Rules the world treats as inviolable. In the Avatar: The Last Airbender universe, only the Avatar can bend all four elements. In the Mistborn universe, Allomancy requires ingesting specific metals. These cannot be violated without triggering a canonical divergence.
- **Soft rules:** Social conventions, power structures, economic realities, political landscapes. These are the texture of a world. They can be bent with good reason.
- **Magic/technology power scale:** Where do the characters in this story sit on the power scale? Fan fiction that gives characters abilities far beyond their established power ceiling is called "powerscaling" and is a major authenticity failure.
- **Sensory and aesthetic details:** Fan fiction should deploy the specific sensory vocabulary of the source world. The Great Hall smells like candle wax and parchment and roast chicken. The Blade Runner rain is acid-yellow under the neon. These details signal that the author lives in this world.
- **Timeline continuity:** If you reference specific events, reference them accurately. Getting the order of canon events wrong breaks the contract with knowledgeable readers immediately.

---

### Step 4: Identify the Fan Fiction Trope and Genre Conventions

Fan fiction has developed a sophisticated, codified trope vocabulary over decades. These tropes are not shortcuts -- they are genre conventions that carry specific reader expectations. Knowing them allows for both meeting those expectations and subverting them intentionally.

- **Gen (general):** Friendship, adventure, character study, no romantic focus. Requires the same craft as any short story.
- **Hurt/Comfort (H/C):** One character is hurt (physically or emotionally); another provides comfort. The core dynamic is vulnerability and care. The trap: the hurt must feel real, not manufactured for the comfort payoff.
- **Slow burn:** A relationship develops over a long narrative arc with deliberate restraint. The tension is the point. Requires patience -- premature resolution deflates the story.
- **Enemies-to-lovers:** Characters who start in antagonism develop romantic feeling. The antagonism must be genuine and have real stakes. The transition must be earned through specific events, not softened for convenience.
- **Fix-it fic:** Rewrites a canonical outcome the fan community finds unsatisfying. Warning: fix-it fics that remove all difficulty lose the tension that made the original work matter. Good fix-it fics change the outcome but preserve the cost.
- **Character study / introspective piece:** A deep dive into one character's interiority. Requires the most accurate voice work. Often written in close third or first person.
- **Missing scene:** A scene that could have occurred between canonical events. Must be consistent with both what precedes and follows it in canon.
- **Fluff:** Low-stakes, emotionally warm stories. The trap is sentimentality -- warmth without specificity. Fluff works when the details are so specific and right that the warmth is earned.
- **Angst:** Emotional pain, grief, internal conflict. The trap is wallowing -- angst without movement or insight. Good angst goes somewhere.
- **Crossover:** Two or more distinct fictional universes share a narrative space. See Edge Cases for detailed handling.

---

### Step 5: Draft the Opening

The opening of fan fiction has a different contract than original fiction. The reader is already invested -- they do not need worldbuilding exposition or character introductions. They need immediate immersion.

- **Start in-scene, in media res, with characters already in motion.** Readers of this fandom know who these people are. Begin with an action, a thought, a line of dialogue, or a sensory detail that is immediately, distinctly right for this character and world.
- **Never open with a lore dump.** The statement "Hogwarts School of Witchcraft and Wizardry was located in Scotland" tells the reader nothing they don't already know and signals that the author does not trust the reader's familiarity.
- **The opening paragraph should do two things simultaneously:** Ground the reader in the specific canon world AND establish the emotional register of this particular story.
- **For AU openings:** The first few sentences must do the harder work of signaling "different world, same soul." A coffee shop AU of a space opera character should have their voice unmistakably present within the first exchange.
- **Avoid:** Dream sequences as openings, weather-as-atmosphere without narrative function, waking up (one of the most over-used fan fiction openers).

---

### Step 6: Draft the Body -- Voice, World, and Craft

This is the primary writing phase. Apply the following discipline throughout:

- **Dialogue as voice:** Every line of dialogue for every character must pass the blind test -- cover the character names and the attribution tags. You should be able to identify who is speaking from the content and rhythm alone. If you cannot, the voice is not specific enough.
- **Action beats over adverbs:** Instead of `he said nervously`, write the specific physical behavior that signals his nervousness. A character who twists their ring, who finds something to clean, who answers a question with a question -- these are character-specific physical tells, not generic emotion labels.
- **World texture through assumed familiarity:** Reference canon details the way a character who lives in this world would -- casually, without explanation. A character walking through Diagon Alley notices that Gringotts looks different since the break-in; they do not explain what Gringotts is.
- **Subtext over text:** Fan fiction readers are sophisticated. If a character is in love but won't say it, do not have the narrator say it for them. Show it in what the character notices, what they avoid, what they do not say.
- **Internal monologue calibration:** Match the interiority style to the source. A first-person narrator in a source work (Bridget Jones, Holden Caulfield) should have rich internal monologue. A character from an action-driven source with limited interiority should have spare, physical-focused narration.
- **Pacing:** Fan fiction scenes can linger in ways that canon cannot afford due to plot necessity. This is one of the gifts of fan fiction -- the quiet moment, the conversation that could not happen on screen because there was no time. Use this space.
- **Original characters (OCs):** If an OC is necessary, they should exist to create a situation that enables the canon characters to reveal themselves. The OC is a catalyst, not the subject.

---

### Step 7: Construct the Ending

Fan fiction endings serve a different purpose than original fiction endings. They are in conversation with an ongoing canonical story.

- **Emotional closure over plot closure:** Fan fiction rarely resolves entire story arcs -- it opens a window onto a moment. The ending should close the emotional arc of this specific scene or story, not the overarching canon narrative.
- **Leave the characters where they belong:** Unless this is fix-it fic with an explicit mandate to change canon outcomes, end with the characters in a state consistent with how canon continues. You cannot give characters growth they will not have achieved at this point in their arc.
- **The earned note:** The final beat should be specific, quiet, and true to the characters. A line of dialogue that is entirely this character -- not any character, this one. A specific detail that resonates backward through the whole story.
- **For AU stories:** The ending can be conclusive because the AU has its own complete story contract. The characters are yours within this alternate frame.
- **Avoid:** Cliffhanger endings that lead nowhere (unless this is explicitly Chapter 1 of an ongoing fic), endings that summarize the emotional content instead of embodying it, endings that undo the tension of the story to achieve comfort.

---

### Step 8: Quality Check Against Fan Fiction-Specific Failure Modes

Before finalizing, run the story against these specific failure patterns:

- **The transcript failure:** Characters speak in generic dialogue with no distinguishing voice. Fix: blind test every character's lines.
- **The lore dump failure:** Paragraphs explaining the world to readers who already know it. Fix: remove all explanatory exposition. Add sensory, in-world detail instead.
- **The OOC (out-of-character) failure:** A character behaves in a way that contradicts their established psychology without narrative justification. The most common variant: a reserved, emotionally closed character delivers a long, open speech about their feelings with no narrative reason for the emotional access. Fix: earn every deviation from established behavior with story causation.
- **The fix-it without cost failure:** A canonical bad outcome is reversed but all tension is removed. The character who died just... does not die, and everyone is fine. Fix: preserve the cost even when changing the outcome.
- **The Mary Sue / Gary Stu failure:** An original character who has no meaningful flaws, masters every challenge, and earns the admiration of all canon characters. Fix: give OCs genuine limitations and make them earn every success.
- **The wish fulfillment failure:** The story exists to deliver the author's preferred emotional outcome without the characters having earned it. Fix: the characters must struggle, want, fail, or sacrifice something. Fan fiction should challenge the characters it loves, not simply reward them.
- **The power scale failure:** Characters perform at power levels inconsistent with canon -- a character who struggled for a whole season to defeat one villain suddenly dispatches three with ease. Fix: calibrate every character action against their established capability ceiling.

## Rules

1. **NEVER write a character out of their established psychology without narrative causation.** If a character who never apologizes apologizes, something must have happened in this story to break that pattern. The deviation is the story.

2. **NEVER open with lore exposition.** Fan fiction readers already know the world. Opening paragraphs explaining the setting signal distrust of the reader and waste the most important real estate in the story.

3. **NEVER break canonical hard rules** -- magic systems, power ceilings, fundamental world laws -- without either (a) naming this as a canon-divergent or AU story, or (b) making the rule-break the plot of the story.

4. **NEVER write dialogue that could belong to any character.** Every line of dialogue for every named character must be attributable from the content alone. Generic dialogue is the most common quality failure in fan fiction.

5. **ALWAYS identify the canon compliance level before writing** and maintain it consistently throughout. Do not write a "canon-compliant" story that quietly contradicts established canon events.

6. **ALWAYS calibrate character behavior to their arc position.** A character at the beginning of their redemption arc behaves differently than a character at the end of it. The story must be set at a specific point, and the characters must behave accordingly.

7. **ALWAYS deploy specific, earned world details.** Not generic "fantasy castle" details but the specific details of this world -- the named places, objects, terms, and sensory texture that signal authentic knowledge of the source material.

8. **NEVER create original characters who solve the central problem of the story.** OCs are catalysts, mirrors, or foils for canon characters. The canon characters must drive their own story.

9. **For fix-it fic:** Changing a canonical outcome does not mean removing all cost. If a character survives who canonically died, something else must bear the weight of that altered reality. A fix-it fic that fixes everything cheapens the original stakes.

10. **For shipping/romantic fan fiction:** The emotional arc of the relationship must be earned within the story, regardless of what the reader hopes will happen. Two characters who are enemies in canon cannot become lovers in one scene without a credible arc of shift. The reader's desire for the pairing does not substitute for narrative causation.

11. **NEVER reproduce substantial passages of the original copyrighted source text.** Reference, quote sparingly (a line of canon dialogue as an anchor point), and reimagine -- do not transcribe.

12. **For crossovers:** Each character must maintain the voice and worldview of their own universe when encountering the other. A character who has never experienced magic will react to magic with the psychology their canon established. Do not flatten characters from different universes into a single generic register.

## Edge Cases

### The Crossover (Two or More Source Universes)

A crossover requires establishing which world provides the primary narrative frame. If Sherlock Holmes travels to the Star Trek universe, the Trek universe is the setting and Holmes's Victorian worldview is the point-of-view engine. Establish this frame clearly.

The narrative justification for the crossing must be specific -- dimensional rifts, time travel, magical transportation, shared dream-state -- and must be consistent with at least one of the two universes' established rules. Once the frame is set, each character's reaction to the other universe must be true to their psychology. Sherlock Holmes encountering a warp drive does not become excited by the wonder of space travel; he begins methodically applying the scientific method to understand it and becomes irritated when the crew will not give him access to engineering schematics.

Each character's voice remains their own. The crossover format enables one specific type of story that no single-fandom story can tell: the collision of two incompatible worldviews through characters who embody them.

### The AU (Alternate Universe)

The AU contract is different-world-same-soul. The reader is not there for the setting -- they are there to see how the character's core psychology expresses itself in an alien context. This means the character's fundamental Want (what they consciously pursue), Need (what they actually require to be whole), and Wound (the formative damage that drives the Need) must survive the transplant intact.

For a modern AU of a fantasy character: strip away the magic system, the political structures, the medieval setting. What remains is the psychology. A character who is defined by their exile and longing for belonging in a fantasy setting becomes in a modern AU someone who is estranged from their family and works to be accepted by a chosen community. The surface is different. The soul is the same.

The most common AU failure is retaining the surface (character names, aesthetic preferences, vague personality descriptors) without retaining the deep structure. If you could replace the character names with different names and the story would not change, the AU has failed.

### The Missing Scene

A missing scene is constrained on both ends by canon. You must research what happens in the scenes immediately before and after your insertion point. The missing scene must:
- Be consistent with every detail established in the preceding scene
- Not introduce information, developments, or emotional shifts that would contradict the following scene
- Fill a gap that the reader actually felt -- a conversation that was implied but not shown, the immediate emotional aftermath of a major event, a journey between locations

The trap: missing scenes that are so significant they would have changed the canonical events that follow. If your missing scene has Character A confess their feelings to Character B, but in the scene immediately following in canon Character A is clearly still suppressing those feelings, the missing scene fails the continuity test.

### The Villain-Centric or Sympathetic Villain Story

Writing a villain as the protagonist does not mean softening them. It means finding their internal logic -- the argument they make to themselves that justifies their actions -- and presenting it with enough craft that the reader understands (not necessarily agrees with) the reasoning.

The technique: find the moment the villain was still capable of a different choice and was not a villain yet. Understand what they wanted and what they believed the world owed them. A well-executed villain-centric story makes the reader uncomfortable because the internal logic is coherent, not because the villainy has been explained away. Do not grant the villain a tragic backstory and then treat that backstory as absolution. The backstory is context, not excuse.

The failure mode to avoid: the "misunderstood" villain whose villainy turns out to be entirely the fault of others, who has no genuine agency in their own evil, and whose crimes are minimized by the narrative.

### The Canon Character Death Fix-It

This is the most emotionally loaded type of fan fiction. The reader is usually writing from grief -- they experienced a canonical loss as genuinely painful and want an alternative. The skill in a fix-it is not removing the pain but redirecting it.

Techniques for a non-cheap fix-it:
- The character's survival must have consequences. Someone else bears a cost. The timeline changes in ways that introduce new difficulties.
- The surviving character carries the awareness of how close it was -- they are changed by the near-death, not simply restored to the pre-death state.
- The emotional weight of the original scene can be honored even as the outcome is changed -- the terror, the grief, the love that drove the scene.

A fix-it that simply removes the death and continues as if it never happened is wish fulfillment without craft. A fix-it that grapples with the cost of the alternate timeline is a genuine story.

### The Ship Fic for a Non-Canon Pairing

When writing a romantic relationship that does not exist in canon (called a "non-canon ship"), the story must build its case entirely from the canon characterization. The reader cannot be expected to accept the pairing just because the author desires it -- the story must earn the emotional connection.

The technique: find the genuine canonical basis for the relationship. What do these two characters actually share? Where does their dynamic create friction that could be romantic tension? What does each character see in the other that they cannot get from their established canonical relationships? Build from this foundation.

Common failure: writing the pairing as if the romantic relationship is already established at the opening of the story, when the entire story's purpose is to demonstrate how they got there. The pairing must be constructed in the reader's presence.

### The Fanfic for Obscure or Niche Source Material

When a user requests fan fiction for a source material the AI may have limited information about -- a small-press novel, a regional TV show, a very specific manga arc -- be transparent about this limitation. Ask the user to provide:
- Key voice samples (lines of dialogue, narrative passages) for major characters
- Core world rules relevant to the story
- The specific canon position of the story

Work from the user-provided material rather than generating from incomplete knowledge. A story with accurate voice from user-provided samples is far better than a confident story built on potentially incorrect assumptions.

## Example

**User Input:** "Write a fan fiction scene from The Last of Us. Set during the five-year time skip between Part I and Part II. Joel and Ellie are living in Jackson. It's a quiet evening -- Joel is teaching Ellie to play guitar. I want it to be canon-compliant and warm but with the specific quality of their relationship: the awkwardness under the warmth, the things they don't say. Not saccharine. Missing scene."

---

## Calluses

*Fandom: The Last of Us (Part I / Part II continuity)*
*Characters: Joel Miller, Ellie Williams*
*Canon: Compliant -- no divergence*
*Set: During the Jackson time skip, approximately Year 2-3 post-Part I*
*Trope/Genre: Missing scene, relationship study*
*Rating: G*

---

The guitar had belonged to someone before Jackson. Joel didn't know who. He'd found it in a house on the east end of town during one of the early clearing sweeps, leaning against a bedroom wall with a snapped E string and a water stain blooming across the body like a continent. He'd restrung it. It took a week to track down strings, three separate supply runs, and Maria thought he was spending his request credits on something embarrassing until she saw what he brought back.

He didn't tell Ellie about the guitar for two months after that.

He told himself it was because he was still relearning. His left hand had forgotten things. The muscle memory came back in patches, unreliably, and some nights he sat on the porch after Ellie had gone back to her own place and worked through chord changes until his fingertips ached in the good, specific way they used to ache when he was twenty-two and thought he might actually do something with this.

The real reason, he suspected, had more to do with not being ready to be watched.

"You're doing it again," Ellie said.

She was sitting cross-legged on the floor of his living room with the guitar in her lap, watching him watch her left hand. The fire in the woodstove was doing most of the work for light. Outside, Jackson had gone quiet in the way it did by nine o'clock -- the wall shifts changed, the families went inside, the generators hummed.

"Doing what," Joel said.

"The face. The -- " She made a face that was apparently his face. It involved her forehead.

"I don't look like that."

"You literally look like that right now."

He looked at the floor. "Your thumb's coming over the top of the neck again."

"I know."

"It's going to limit your reach."

"I know, I said I know." She adjusted her thumb. "Better?"

He studied her hand. "Better."

The chord she was working on was a G. It should have been straightforward -- three fingers, two strings requiring the stretch. On her hand it was a problem of proportion; her fingers were smaller than the exercise assumed, and the stretch to the high E was genuinely uncomfortable. He had spent twenty minutes when they started this trying to find a way to explain the discomfort would pass. He wasn't sure he'd found it.

She strummed. The G rang out -- mostly clean, a faint buzz from the middle finger not pressing firmly enough to the fret.

"Closer," he said.

"I heard the buzz."

"Your middle finger. You're letting up."

"It hurts."

"I know it hurts."

She looked up at him. There was a version of this look she gave him sometimes that he had learned, over the past year and a half, meant she was deciding whether to push on something or let it go. This version resolved in the direction of letting it go. She repositioned her middle finger, pressed harder, strummed again.

Clean.

Something in his chest did a thing he was choosing not to name.

"There you go," he said.

"There I go," she agreed. She strummed it again, unnecessary, clearly just because it had sounded right. He recognized the impulse.

He watched her work through the transition he'd given her -- G to C, C back to G, slow and deliberate. Her right hand was natural; that part had come easy. It was the left hand learning to hold the shape, his old friend the fretting hand, that was taking the time it needed to take.

"You had a band," she said.

It wasn't a question. Tommy had told her, probably. Tommy told her most things.

"A band's a strong word."

"What's the weak word?"

"A few guys who played at a bar on Thursday nights. Sometimes we got paid."

"What kind of music?"

He leaned back in the chair. "Country. Some rock."

She made a sound that was not quite a laugh but was headed in that direction. "Country."

"Don't."

"I'm not doing anything."

"You're doing a thing with your face."

"I learned it from someone." She looked back down at the guitar and started the transition again. G to C. The chord changes were tentative but honest. He could hear her thinking through each one. "What happened to them? The band?"

He thought about it. Danny Ruiz, who had driven a UPS truck during the day and played lead with the careful, measured style of someone who had learned everything from books. Leon Park, who had played bass and known every word to every song they'd ever covered and a lot they hadn't. "Life," he said. "Work. Kids. Same thing that always happens."

"That's a depressing answer."

"It's an honest answer."

She played the G again. Her calluses were coming in -- he'd checked without making it obvious, the way the pads of her fingers were changing, the skin thickening at the precise points where the strings pressed hardest. It took a few weeks. It hurt until it didn't. He remembered this.

"When do the calluses stop hurting?" she asked.

He looked at her fingers. "You're about halfway."

"Wonderful."

"You asked."

"I was hoping for a different answer."

He almost said something then. He got as far as knowing what he would have said, the shape of it, which was something about how she was doing well and how this part was almost over and how he thought she had -- something. An ear for it, maybe. The kind of patience that looked like impatience but was actually just concentration. He knew the difference.

He didn't say it.

She wouldn't have wanted him to. That was the thing about Ellie that he had needed longer to understand than he probably should have: she didn't want it said directly. She wanted to hear it some other way, or not at all, and if he miscalibrated she would deflect so completely that the original moment would become a joke, and then neither of them would have it anymore.

"Try the transition faster," he said instead.

She tried the transition faster. It fell apart at the C.

"Again."

She tried again. Better.

"Again."

"You know, you could also say encouraging things. That's a thing teachers do."

"You're getting better."

"Said like a man who has never once in his life been to therapy."

"Again."

She laughed -- a real one, short, and then she was playing again, and the fire in the woodstove was burning down, and outside Jackson was quiet, and Joel sat in his chair and listened to his guitar make sounds in someone else's hands and did not name what he felt about that either.

It was enough to be here. It was enough to have this.

That was all he was going to say about it.

* * *

She left around ten. He walked her to the door because the street was dark and he was who he was, and she said goodnight at the door the way she always did -- halfway gone before the word finished, already pulling her jacket around her, already somewhere else.

"Joel," she said, from the bottom step.

He waited.

"I want to learn a specific song. Not an exercise. A real song."

He looked at her. "Which one?"

She named it. He knew it -- everyone from a certain generation knew it. He hadn't thought about it in years.

"I can show you," he said.

"Yeah?" She was already two steps away, walking backward, which was a habit of hers he'd stopped commenting on.

"Next time," he said.

"Next time," she agreed, and turned, and walked back toward her place in the dark.

He stood on the porch for a minute. The air was cold and clean the way mountain air got in fall, and the stars above the wall were very clear, and somewhere on the east end of town a dog was barking at something.

He went back inside and picked up the guitar and played the song she'd named, all the way through, quietly, to make sure he still could.

He could.
