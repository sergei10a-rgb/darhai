## Questions to Ask First

Before developing a fundraising strategy:

1. What is your organization's annual budget and what percentage comes from fundraising?
2. What fundraising methods do you currently use?
3. How many donors do you have? What is your donor retention rate?
4. Do you have a donor database/CRM? Which one?
5. Who is responsible for fundraising? (ED only, development staff, board, volunteers)
6. What is your fundraising expense budget?
7. What is your organization's brand recognition in the community?
8. Do you have compelling stories and data to share with donors?
9. What is your board's involvement in fundraising currently?
10. What is your biggest fundraising challenge right now?

## Fundraising Fundamentals

### The Fundraising Pyramid
Most organizations raise money across these levels:

```
        /\          Planned Giving
       /  \         (Bequests, trusts)
      /----\        Major Gifts
     /      \       ($1,000-$100,000+)
    /--------\      Mid-Level Donors
   /          \     ($250-$999)
  /------------\    Annual Fund / Grassroots
 /              \   ($1-$249)
/________________\  Broad Base
```

Key principle: Most revenue comes from the top of the pyramid (80/20 rule - 80% of dollars from 20% of donors), but the base feeds the upper levels through donor cultivation and upgrades.

### Core Fundraising Metrics
- **Donor retention rate**: Percentage of last year's donors who give again (60-70% is good, industry average is 43%)
- **Donor acquisition cost**: What it costs to acquire a new donor
- **Average gift size**: Total donations / number of gifts
- **Lifetime donor value**: Average annual gift x average years of giving
- **ROI by channel**: Revenue generated / cost for each fundraising method
- **Fundraising efficiency ratio**: Fundraising cost / total funds raised (under 25% is strong)

## Annual Campaign

### What Is an Annual Campaign?
A systematic effort to secure unrestricted gifts from as many donors as possible each year. This is your fundraising backbone.

### Annual Campaign Components

**Direct Mail Appeal (2-4 times per year)**
- Year-end appeal (November): Largest response, 30%+ of annual giving happens in December
- Spring appeal (April-May): Program updates and spring giving
- Summer appeal (July-August): Mid-year bridge, often less competitive
- Letter should be 1-2 pages, personal, specific ask with suggested amounts

**Email Appeals**
- Monthly newsletter with embedded giving opportunity
- Targeted appeals around specific needs or milestones
- GivingTuesday campaign (Tuesday after Thanksgiving)
- Segmented messages for different donor levels

**Phone/Personal Outreach**
- Thank-you calls to recent donors (not asking for more)
- Personal ask calls to lapsed donors
- Board member reach-out to their networks

### Annual Campaign Calendar
| Month | Activity |
|-------|----------|
| January | Thank donors, update database, review year-end results |
| February | Plan spring appeal, identify major gift prospects |
| March | Spring appeal preparation, donor appreciation event |
| April | Mail spring appeal, major gift meetings |
| May | Spring appeal follow-up, event planning |
| June | Mid-year financial review, summer appeal prep |
| July | Summer appeal, GivingTuesday planning begins |
| August | Fall event preparation, year-end appeal writing |
| September | Major gift solicitations, event promotion |
| October | Fall event execution, year-end appeal finalization |
| November | Year-end appeal launch, GivingTuesday |
| December | Year-end push, matching gift opportunities, donor thanks |

## Major Gift Cultivation

### What Is a Major Gift?
Organization-specific: A gift significantly above your average that requires personal cultivation. For a small nonprofit, $500 may be major. For a hospital, $25,000+.

### The Cultivation Cycle
1. **Identify**: Research prospects using wealth screening, giving history, connection to mission
2. **Qualify**: Determine capacity (can they give?) and inclination (do they want to?)
3. **Cultivate**: Build relationship through 6-18 months of engagement
4. **Solicit**: Make the specific ask in a personal meeting
5. **Steward**: Thank, report impact, maintain relationship
6. **Repeat**: Re-engage for next gift (upgrade, renew, or special project)

### Cultivation Activities
- Personal meetings over coffee or lunch
- Facility tours and program visits
- Invitations to special events (not just fundraising events)
- Personalized updates on programs they care about
- Introductions to program staff and beneficiaries
- Board member involvement in cultivation (peer-to-peer)
- Handwritten notes from leadership

### Making the Ask
- Always ask in person, ideally face-to-face
- Be specific: "Would you consider a gift of $10,000 to fund our youth mentorship program?"
- After asking, be silent - let them respond
- If they say yes: Express gratitude, confirm details, follow up in writing
- If they need time: Set a specific follow-up date
- If they decline: Thank them graciously, ask if they can help in other ways
- Never apologize for asking - you are offering an opportunity to make impact

### Moves Management
Track each major gift prospect through a "moves" system:
- Assign a relationship manager (staff or board member)
- Plan 8-12 intentional "moves" (interactions) per year per prospect
- Document every interaction in your CRM
- Review prospect portfolio monthly
- Active portfolio: 15-25 prospects per relationship manager

## Crowdfunding

### Best Practices
- **Platform selection**: GoFundMe Charity (no platform fees for nonprofits), Classy, GiveLively (free)
- **Goal setting**: Achievable goal creates momentum (can always extend)
- **Compelling story**: Video (2-3 minutes) dramatically increases success
- **Launch strategy**: Secure 30% of goal before public launch from inner circle
- **Social proof**: Early donors encourage others
- **Updates**: Post progress updates every 2-3 days during campaign
- **Thank publicly**: Tag and thank donors on social media
- **Time-limited**: 30-45 day campaigns perform best (urgency drives action)

### Peer-to-Peer Fundraising
- Supporters create personal fundraising pages linked to your campaign
- Provide templates, suggested language, and images
- Works well for runs/walks, birthday fundraisers, challenge campaigns
- Facebook Fundraisers reach donors outside your usual network

## Event Fundraising

### Event Types and Expected ROI

**Gala / Dinner**
- Revenue: $10,000-$500,000+ depending on market
- Costs: Venue, catering, entertainment, printing (40-60% of revenue typical)
- Best for: Major donor cultivation, community visibility, sponsorship revenue
- Timeline: 6-12 months planning

**Auction (Live or Silent)**
- Can be standalone or part of gala
- Procurement is key: Donated items = higher profit margin
- Online auctions extend reach (GiveSmart, OneCause, Handbid)
- Experience items often outperform physical items

**Walk/Run/Ride**
- Revenue: $5,000-$200,000+
- Lower cost, higher participation, strong peer-to-peer potential
- Sponsorship is a major revenue component
- Requires significant volunteer coordination

**House Party / Salon**
- Low cost, intimate, personal
- Board member or supporter hosts in their home
- Best for cultivation and small-to-mid gift solicitation
- 15-30 attendees, informal program with mission moment

**Virtual Event**
- Lower overhead, broader geographic reach
- Requires strong tech setup and engagement strategy
- Works for: Auctions, panels, performances, giving challenges
- Hybrid (in-person + virtual) expanding since 2020

### Event Planning Essentials
1. Set clear revenue and attendance goals
2. Budget conservatively (include hidden costs: insurance, permits, AV, tips)
3. Secure event committee (6-10 dedicated volunteers)
4. Lock in date, venue, and headline attraction early
5. Launch sponsorship sales 4-6 months before event
6. Open ticket sales/registration 8-10 weeks before event
7. Promote through email, social media, board networks, local media
8. Plan program flow: Welcome, mission moment, fund-a-need, entertainment
9. Day-of logistics checklist and volunteer assignments
10. Post-event: Thank everyone, share photos, report impact, debrief

### Fund-a-Need (Paddle Raise)
- Live ask during event for specific program need
- Auctioneer or host calls out giving levels ($10,000 down to $50)
- Creates excitement and social proof
- Can raise 50%+ of event net revenue in 15 minutes
- Requires compelling program presentation right before the ask

## Corporate Sponsorship

### Sponsorship vs Donation
- **Donation**: Charitable gift with no quid pro quo (tax-deductible)
- **Sponsorship**: Business transaction - company provides funds in exchange for marketing value (may or may not be tax-deductible)

### Building Sponsorship Packages
Create tiered packages with clear deliverables:

**Title Sponsor** ($10,000+): Name in event title, logo on all materials, speaking opportunity, premium table, social media features, exclusive networking access

**Gold Sponsor** ($5,000): Logo on materials, social media mentions, premium seating, recognition from stage

**Silver Sponsor** ($2,500): Logo on select materials, social media mention, reserved seating

**Bronze Sponsor** ($1,000): Name listed in program, general seating

### Finding Corporate Sponsors
- Local businesses with community presence
- Companies whose customers overlap with your audience
- Businesses in related industries
- Companies with employee groups who volunteer with you
- Review corporate social responsibility reports

### Sponsorship Proposal Tips
- Lead with the marketing value, not the charitable mission
- Include audience demographics and reach numbers
- Show media coverage and social media metrics from past events
- Make it easy to say yes - clear packages, simple decision
- Follow up sponsorship with excellent fulfillment reporting

## Planned Giving

### What Is Planned Giving?
Gifts made as part of a donor's estate or financial plan. These are typically the largest gifts an organization receives.

### Types of Planned Gifts
- **Bequest**: Donor includes organization in their will (most common)
- **Beneficiary designation**: Life insurance, retirement account, bank account
- **Charitable remainder trust**: Provides income to donor, remainder to nonprofit
- **Charitable gift annuity**: Donor makes gift, receives fixed annual payment for life
- **Real estate gifts**: Donation of property

### Starting a Planned Giving Program
1. Add planned giving information to your website
2. Include "Remember us in your will" language in communications
3. Create a legacy society to recognize planned gift donors
4. Identify prospects: Loyal long-term donors, older donors, donors without children
5. Have conversations: "Have you considered including [org] in your estate plan?"
6. Consult an attorney: Planned giving has legal and tax implications

## Donor Stewardship

### The Thank-You Rule
- Thank within 48 hours of receiving a gift (aim for 24 hours)
- Thank 7 times before asking again (different touches, different formats)
- Personalize thanks for gifts above your major gift threshold

### Stewardship Touchpoints
1. **Immediate**: Automated email receipt (legal requirement for gifts over $250)
2. **Within 48 hours**: Personal thank-you letter from ED or board member
3. **Within 1 week**: Handwritten note or phone call for major gifts
4. **Monthly**: Newsletter or email update
5. **Quarterly**: Impact report showing their gift at work
6. **Annually**: Annual report, invitation to appreciation event
7. **Ongoing**: Birthday cards, anniversary of first gift, program updates

### Donor Recognition
- Public recognition (with permission): Annual report, website, wall display
- Naming opportunities for major gifts
- Special access or experiences
- Personal updates from leadership
- Invitation to exclusive events

### Lapsed Donor Recovery
- Define "lapsed" for your organization (typically 13-24 months since last gift)
- Recovery rates: Much higher than new donor acquisition rates
- Personalized outreach: "We miss you" letters, phone calls
- Ask why they stopped giving (survey)
- Make it easy to return: Simple online giving, suggested modest amount

## Donor Database (CRM) Selection

### Key Features to Evaluate
- Contact and gift tracking
- Acknowledgment letter generation
- Reporting and analytics
- Email integration
- Online giving integration
- Volunteer tracking
- Event management
- Cost and scalability

### Recommended Platforms by Size
- **Small (under 500 donors)**: Bloomerang, Little Green Light, Network for Good
- **Medium (500-5,000 donors)**: Bloomerang, DonorPerfect, Neon CRM
- **Large (5,000+ donors)**: Salesforce NPSP (free for qualifying nonprofits), Blackbaud Raiser's Edge

## Progression Path

1. **Phase 1**: Organize donor data, implement thank-you system, write first appeal
2. **Phase 2**: Launch annual campaign, build donor pipeline, plan first event
3. **Phase 3**: Begin major gift cultivation, diversify revenue streams
4. **Phase 4**: Add corporate sponsorships, implement planned giving program
5. **Phase 5**: Strategic multi-year fundraising plan, campaign feasibility
6. **Phase 6**: Capital campaign, endowment building, mentoring fundraising team
