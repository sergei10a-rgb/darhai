# Social Impact Measurer

**Use this skill when:**
- The user needs to measure and communicate social impact using logic models or theory of change frameworks
- The user wants help defining outcome indicators, designing data collection methods, or building evaluation frameworks
- The user needs to create impact reports for funders, stakeholders, or board members
- The user wants to demonstrate program effectiveness or compare intervention approaches

- The user needs fundraising strategy rather than impact measurement (use fundraising-strategist instead)

## Questions to Ask First

Before designing an impact measurement system:

1. What programs or services does your organization deliver?
2. What change are you trying to create in people's lives or in the community?
3. Who are your primary stakeholders (funders, board, community, government)?
4. What data do you currently collect? How?
5. What does your staff capacity for data collection look like?
6. Do funders require specific metrics or reporting formats?
7. What is your budget for evaluation? (Internal only / Can hire external evaluator)
8. How comfortable is your team with data and technology?
9. What decisions will impact data inform? (Program improvement, funding proposals, advocacy)
10. Have you measured impact before? What worked or didn't?

## Logic Models

### What Is a Logic Model?
A visual representation of how your program works - the logical connection between what you invest, what you do, and what changes as a result.

### Logic Model Components

```
INPUTS --> ACTIVITIES --> OUTPUTS --> OUTCOMES --> IMPACT
```

**Inputs** (Resources you invest):
- Staff time and expertise
- Funding
- Facilities and equipment
- Partnerships
- Volunteer time
- Curriculum or materials

**Activities** (What you do):
- Deliver workshops
- Provide counseling
- Distribute food
- Advocate for policy change
- Mentor youth
- Build housing

**Outputs** (Measurable products of activities):
- Number of workshops delivered
- Number of people served
- Meals distributed
- Policy briefs published
- Mentor-mentee matches made
- Units constructed

**Outcomes** (Changes in participants or community):
- Short-term (during program): Increased knowledge, changed attitudes, new skills
- Medium-term (after program): Changed behaviors, improved practices
- Long-term (sustained): Improved conditions, new status

**Impact** (Broad, long-term change):
- Systemic change in community conditions
- Population-level improvements
- Contribution to solving the root problem

### Logic Model Example: Youth Tutoring Program

| Component | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **Inputs** | 5 tutors, curriculum, classroom space, $50,000 budget, school partnership |
| **Activities** | Weekly 1-on-1 tutoring sessions, monthly parent workshops, teacher coordination |
| **Outputs** | 75 students tutored, 400 tutoring sessions delivered, 8 parent workshops held |
| **Short-term outcomes** | Students improve reading scores by 1 grade level, increased homework completion |
| **Medium-term outcomes** | Students pass standardized reading tests, improved school attendance |
| **Long-term outcomes** | Higher high school graduation rates, college enrollment |
| **Impact** | Reduced educational achievement gap in the community |

### Building Your Logic Model
1. Start with your intended impact (work backward)
2. What long-term outcomes must happen to achieve that impact?
3. What medium-term outcomes lead to long-term outcomes?
4. What short-term outcomes lead to medium-term outcomes?
5. What activities produce those short-term outcomes?
6. What inputs do those activities require?
7. Test the logic: Does each arrow make sense? Is there evidence for the connections?

## Theory of Change

### Logic Model vs Theory of Change
- **Logic model**: Shows what you do and what results you expect (the "what")
- **Theory of change**: Explains WHY your activities lead to outcomes (the "how" and "why")
- Theory of change includes assumptions, evidence base, and external factors

### Theory of Change Components
1. **Long-term goal**: The ultimate change you seek
2. **Preconditions**: What must be true for the goal to be achieved
3. **Interventions**: Your specific programs and activities
4. **Assumptions**: Beliefs about why your approach will work
5. **Evidence**: Research or experience supporting your assumptions
6. **External factors**: Things outside your control that affect outcomes
7. **Indicators**: How you will know if change is happening

### Writing Your Theory of Change Statement
Template: "IF we [intervention/activities], THEN [outcomes] will result, BECAUSE [evidence/rationale], ASSUMING [assumptions]."

Example: "IF we provide weekly one-on-one tutoring to struggling readers in grades 2-4, THEN their reading scores will improve by at least one grade level within one school year, BECAUSE research shows that individualized instruction at early ages has the highest impact on literacy development (National Reading Panel), ASSUMING students attend at least 80% of sessions and receive family support for homework."

## Outcome Indicators

### What Makes a Good Indicator?
- **Specific**: Clearly defined, everyone understands what is being measured
- **Measurable**: Can be quantified or categorized
- **Achievable**: Realistic target given resources and context
- **Relevant**: Directly connected to the outcome you are measuring
- **Time-bound**: Measured within a defined timeframe

### Types of Indicators

**Quantitative Indicators** (Numbers):
- Percentage of participants who achieve a benchmark
- Average change in score from pre-test to post-test
- Number of days employed in 6 months after program
- Reduction in emergency room visits

**Qualitative Indicators** (Descriptions):
- Participant descriptions of changed behavior
- Case studies documenting transformation
- Expert assessment of skill development
- Community member perceptions of change

### Common Indicator Categories

**Knowledge** (Do they know more?):
- Pre/post test scores
- Correct identification of concepts
- Ability to explain key information

**Attitude** (Do they think differently?):
- Survey responses on confidence, self-efficacy, motivation
- Likert scale ratings of beliefs and perspectives
- Expressed intentions and goals

**Behavior** (Do they act differently?):
- Observable behavior changes (attendance, participation)
- Self-reported behavior changes (eating habits, exercise)
- Verified behavior changes (employment records, academic transcripts)

**Condition** (Has their situation changed?):
- Income level, housing status, health outcomes
- Test scores, graduation rates, employment rates
- Measured environmental conditions (water quality, air quality)

### Setting Targets
- Use baseline data (where are participants starting?)
- Research comparable programs for benchmarks
- Set realistic but ambitious targets
- Distinguish between aspirational and expected targets
- Adjust based on actual results and learning

## Data Collection Methods

### Surveys
- **Pre/post surveys**: Measure change over time (same questions, beginning and end)
- **Satisfaction surveys**: How did participants experience the program?
- **Follow-up surveys**: Long-term outcomes after program completion
- **Tips**: Keep surveys short (10-15 minutes max), use validated instruments when available, pilot test before full deployment

### Interviews
- **Individual interviews**: Deep understanding of one person's experience (30-60 minutes)
- **Focus groups**: Group discussion revealing shared experiences (6-10 participants, 60-90 minutes)
- **Semi-structured**: Prepared questions with flexibility to follow interesting threads
- **Tips**: Record and transcribe (with consent), use open-ended questions, avoid leading

### Observation
- **Direct observation**: Watch and record behavior in program settings
- **Structured observation**: Use checklist of specific behaviors to look for
- **Tips**: Multiple observers increase reliability, document what you see, not what you interpret

### Administrative Data
- Attendance records
- Academic transcripts
- Employment records
- Health records (with consent/authorization)
- Case management notes
- Financial records

### Participatory Methods
- **Most Significant Change**: Collect stories of change, community selects most significant
- **Photovoice**: Participants photograph their experience, discuss in group
- **Community mapping**: Visual representation of community assets and challenges

### Data Collection Planning Template
For each indicator, document:
- What data will be collected
- How (survey, interview, observation, records)
- When (baseline, midpoint, endpoint, follow-up)
- By whom (staff, external evaluator, participants)
- From whom (all participants, sample, comparison group)
- Where data will be stored
- How privacy will be protected

## Impact Reporting

### Report Structure for Funders
1. **Executive summary**: Key findings in one page
2. **Program description**: Brief overview of activities and participants
3. **Methodology**: How you collected and analyzed data
4. **Findings**: Results organized by outcome area
5. **Stories**: 2-3 participant narratives illustrating impact
6. **Challenges and lessons learned**: Honest reflection
7. **Recommendations**: What you will do differently based on findings
8. **Data appendix**: Detailed tables and charts

### Reporting Best Practices
- Lead with outcomes, not just outputs (changed lives, not just numbers served)
- Compare to targets: Did you meet, exceed, or fall short of goals?
- Explain context: External factors that affected results
- Be honest about challenges: Funders respect transparency
- Use visuals: Charts, infographics, photos increase engagement
- Tell stories alongside data: Numbers inform, stories inspire
- Keep it concise: Busy readers appreciate brevity

### Visualizing Data Effectively
- **Bar charts**: Compare categories or time periods
- **Line charts**: Show trends over time
- **Pie charts**: Show parts of a whole (use sparingly, only for 3-5 categories)
- **Infographics**: Combine numbers, icons, and brief text for at-a-glance understanding
- **Dashboards**: Real-time or periodic visual summary of key metrics
- **Tools**: Canva (free infographics), Google Data Studio (dashboards), Excel/Google Sheets (charts)

## Storytelling with Data

### The Data-Story Connection
Data answers "how much?" Stories answer "what does it feel like?" You need both.

### Collecting Impact Stories
- Ask participants: "What is different in your life because of this program?"
- Capture stories regularly, not just at reporting time
- Get written consent for using stories publicly
- Include context: Where they started, what happened, where they are now
- Protect privacy: Use first names only, change identifying details if needed

### Story Structure for Impact
1. **The challenge**: What was the person facing before the program?
2. **The turning point**: How did the program make a difference?
3. **The outcome**: What changed in their life?
4. **The future**: What is possible now that was not before?

### Combining Data and Stories
"Of the 200 participants in our job training program, 78% secured employment within 90 days. Maria was one of them. After 18 months of unemployment following a layoff, she completed our 12-week program and landed a position as a medical office coordinator, increasing her family's income by 40%."

## Evaluation Frameworks

### Formative vs Summative Evaluation
- **Formative**: Conducted during program to improve it (Are we doing this well? What should we change?)
- **Summative**: Conducted at conclusion to assess overall impact (Did it work? Was it worth the investment?)

### Process Evaluation
- Was the program implemented as planned?
- Were target populations reached?
- Was the dosage sufficient? (Number of sessions, hours, intensity)
- What adjustments were made and why?
- What are participants' experiences of the program?

### Outcome Evaluation
- Did participants experience the expected changes?
- How much change occurred? (Effect size)
- Were changes sustained over time?
- Were there unintended outcomes (positive or negative)?

### Common Evaluation Frameworks
- **RE-AIM**: Reach, Effectiveness, Adoption, Implementation, Maintenance
- **Kirkpatrick Model**: Reaction, Learning, Behavior, Results (training programs)
- **Developmental Evaluation**: For innovative programs in complex environments
- **Collective Impact**: Shared measurement across multiple organizations
- **Empowerment Evaluation**: Community members lead their own evaluation

### When to Hire an External Evaluator
- Funder requires external evaluation
- Need rigorous design (comparison groups, statistical analysis)
- Want objective perspective on sensitive findings
- Organization lacks evaluation expertise
- Stakes are high (large program, significant funding decisions)

## Benchmarking

### Why Benchmark?
Comparing your results to similar programs provides context:
- Is a 60% success rate good or poor? Depends on what comparable programs achieve
- Benchmarks help set realistic targets
- Funders expect you to know how you compare

### Where to Find Benchmarks
- Published research on similar interventions
- National program networks that aggregate data (United Way, YMCA, Boys & Girls Club)
- Clearinghouses of evidence-based programs (SAMHSA, What Works Clearinghouse)
- State and federal agency reports
- Peer organizations willing to share data

## Progression Path

1. **Phase 1**: Build logic model, define 3-5 key indicators, start collecting basic data
2. **Phase 2**: Implement pre/post surveys, establish baseline, first impact report
3. **Phase 3**: Add qualitative methods (interviews, stories), compare to benchmarks
4. **Phase 4**: Develop data dashboards, integrate measurement into program management
5. **Phase 5**: External evaluation, publish findings, contribute to evidence base
6. **Phase 6**: Lead evaluation capacity building, develop theory of change, systems-level impact

## Edge Cases

- **Incomplete information:** Ask clarifying questions before proceeding rather than making assumptions
- **Conflicting requirements:** Identify trade-offs explicitly and present options with pros and cons
- **Scale mismatch:** Adapt recommendations to match the user's context (individual vs. team vs. organization)
- **Domain crossover:** When the request overlaps with other skill domains, address what falls within scope and reference specialized skills for the rest
