## Licensing Fundamentals

### Types of Licenses

```
LICENSE TYPES
==============

EXCLUSIVE LICENSE:
  Only the licensee can use the work for the specified purpose
  Higher royalty rates (exclusivity has premium value)
  Important: Limit exclusivity by category, territory, and duration
  Example: Exclusive for greeting cards in North America for 2 years

NON-EXCLUSIVE LICENSE:
  Artist can license the same work to multiple parties
  Lower individual rates but more total revenue potential
  More flexibility and less risk for the artist
  Example: Pattern available for home decor from multiple manufacturers

FLAT FEE (BUYOUT):
  One-time payment for specified usage rights
  No ongoing royalties
  Common for editorial, advertising, and small print runs
  Caution: Avoid full copyright transfers unless well-compensated

ROYALTY-BASED:
  Percentage of sales revenue paid to the artist
  Ongoing income tied to product success
  Usually with an advance against royalties
  Most common for consumer products and publishing

WORK-FOR-HIRE:
  All rights transfer to the commissioning party
  Artist has no further claim to the work
  Highest upfront payment, zero residual income
  Avoid unless the fee compensates fully for lost future revenue
```

## Contract Essentials

### Key Contract Terms

```
LICENSING CONTRACT CHECKLIST
==============================
Every licensing agreement should clearly define:

1. GRANT OF RIGHTS
   [ ] Specific artwork/designs covered
   [ ] Product categories permitted
   [ ] Territory (geographic limits)
   [ ] Duration (start and end dates)
   [ ] Exclusive or non-exclusive
   [ ] Channels (retail, online, wholesale)

2. ROYALTY TERMS
   [ ] Royalty percentage and basis (wholesale, retail, or net)
   [ ] Minimum royalty guarantee (annual minimum payment)
   [ ] Advance amount and terms
   [ ] Reporting frequency (quarterly is standard)
   [ ] Audit rights (you can inspect their sales records)
   [ ] Payment timeline (within 30-45 days of period end)

3. QUALITY CONTROL
   [ ] Approval rights on final product (you approve before manufacturing)
   [ ] Approval on marketing materials using your art
   [ ] Right to receive product samples
   [ ] Quality standards requirements

4. ARTIST PROTECTIONS
   [ ] Credit/attribution requirements
   [ ] Right to use for portfolio and self-promotion
   [ ] No sublicensing without written consent
   [ ] No modification of artwork without approval
   [ ] Indemnification (licensee covers legal claims on their products)
   [ ] Insurance requirements for the licensee

5. TERMINATION
   [ ] Termination conditions (breach, non-payment, etc.)
   [ ] Notice period for termination (60-90 days)
   [ ] Sell-off period (licensee can sell existing inventory, 3-6 months)
   [ ] All rights revert to artist upon termination
   [ ] Licensee must stop all new production immediately

6. ACCOUNTING AND REPORTING
   [ ] Quarterly royalty statements with unit detail
   [ ] Right to audit books (at your expense, but they pay if discrepancy > 5%)
   [ ] Definition of "net sales" (watch for excessive deductions)
   [ ] Currency and exchange rate terms
```

### Negotiation Strategies

```
LICENSING NEGOTIATION FRAMEWORK
=================================

BEFORE NEGOTIATING:
  1. Know your minimum acceptable terms (walk-away point)
  2. Research the licensee's size, distribution, and sales volume
  3. Understand the product category benchmarks
  4. Have alternatives (other interested licensees)
  5. Get everything in writing (email confirmations count)

NEGOTIATION TACTICS:
  Start high:
    If market rate is 5%, ask for 8%. Settle at 6%.
    You can always come down; you cannot go up.

  Trade, do not concede:
    "I can do a lower royalty if the advance is higher."
    "I will accept exclusivity if the minimum guarantee increases."
    "I can extend the term if we add a performance clause."

  Performance clauses:
    "If sales exceed $X, the royalty rate increases to Y%."
    "If minimum sales are not met in Year 1, exclusivity converts
     to non-exclusive."
    These protect you from a licensee sitting on your work.

  Territory negotiation:
    Limit territory to where the licensee actually sells.
    Do not grant worldwide rights if they only sell in the US.
    Each additional territory should increase the advance.

WHAT TO NEVER ACCEPT:
  x Full copyright transfer (unless compensation is extraordinary)
  x No advance or minimum guarantee
  x No termination clause
  x No approval rights on final product
  x Unlimited sublicensing rights
  x "Perpetual" license with no exit
  x Royalty based on "net profits" (too easily manipulated)
```

## Portfolio Preparation for Licensing

```
LICENSING PORTFOLIO STRATEGY
==============================

WHAT LICENSEES LOOK FOR:
  - Cohesive collections (not individual pieces)
  - Work that translates to products (repeats, colorways, coordinates)
  - Trend awareness balanced with timeless appeal
  - Versatility across product applications
  - Professional presentation

COLLECTION STRUCTURE:
  A licensing collection typically includes:
    - 3-5 hero patterns or illustrations
    - 2-3 coordinate/accent designs
    - 2-3 colorways per design
    - Blender patterns (small-scale supporting designs)
    - Spot illustrations or icons that complement the theme

PRESENTATION FORMAT:
  - High-resolution files (300 DPI minimum, CMYK for print)
  - Product mockups showing designs on real products
  - Collection overview sheet (all designs on one page)
  - Organized by theme or collection name
  - Professional lookbook or line sheet

FILE ORGANIZATION:
  Collection_Name/
  ├── Hero_Designs/
  │   ├── design_01_main.tiff
  │   ├── design_01_colorway_2.tiff
  │   └── design_01_colorway_3.tiff
  ├── Coordinates/
  │   ├── coordinate_01.tiff
  │   └── coordinate_02.tiff
  ├── Mockups/
  │   ├── pillow_mockup.jpg
  │   └── fabric_mockup.jpg
  ├── Linesheet.pdf
  └── Collection_Overview.pdf
```

## Working with Licensing Agents

```
LICENSING AGENT RELATIONSHIPS
================================

WHAT AN AGENT DOES:
  - Represents your work to manufacturers and brands
  - Negotiates deals on your behalf
  - Handles contract administration
  - Collects royalties and passes them through
  - Provides market intelligence and trend direction

TYPICAL AGENT TERMS:
  Commission: 40-50% of licensing revenue
  Contract length: 2-3 years initially
  Exclusivity: Usually exclusive representation
  Territory: May vary by region

WHEN TO USE AN AGENT:
  + You want to focus on creating, not selling
  + You are entering a new market and need connections
  + An agent has strong relationships with target licensees
  + You are generating enough volume to interest a good agent

WHEN TO SELF-REPRESENT:
  + Your licensing income is under $20,000/year
  + You enjoy the business side and have the time
  + You have direct relationships with licensees
  + You want to keep 100% of revenue

EVALUATING AN AGENT:
  [ ] What brands do they currently represent?
  [ ] What deals have they closed in the last year?
  [ ] Can you speak to their current artists?
  [ ] What markets and categories do they specialize in?
  [ ] How do they report and pay royalties?
  [ ] What happens to existing deals if you leave?
  [ ] Do they have a minimum portfolio size requirement?
```
