# Holiday Cooking

## When to Use

**Use this skill when:**
- A user asks how to plan, organize, or execute a Thanksgiving, Christmas, Easter, July 4th, or other major holiday meal
- A user needs help selecting cooking methods for a centerpiece protein (turkey, prime rib, glazed ham, roast lamb, whole fish)
- A user is managing dietary restrictions (gluten-free, dairy-free, vegan, kosher, halal) within a large holiday meal and needs substitution strategies or menu redesign
- A user is hosting 10+ people and needs buffet logistics, oven scheduling, borrowed equipment lists, or potluck coordination guidance
- A user needs make-ahead timelines, freezer strategies, or prep schedules to reduce day-of chaos
- A user is baking holiday cookies, cakes, or specialty breads and needs technique guidance, scaling math, or freeze-ahead planning
- A user is experiencing decision paralysis or stress overload and needs a structured, realistic plan to regain confidence
- A user wants to coordinate a potluck and needs sign-up categories, portion guidance, and allergen communication templates

**Do NOT use when:**
- The user needs a specific single recipe developed from scratch -- use a dedicated recipe generation skill instead
- The user is asking about food safety protocols for commercial kitchen settings -- refer to a food safety compliance skill
- The user needs professional catering pricing or event management logistics -- use an event planning or catering business skill
- The user is asking about non-food holiday planning (decorating, gift buying, travel) -- use a household or lifestyle planning skill
- The user has a severe food allergy and needs clinical-grade allergen guidance -- recommend consulting a registered dietitian
- The request is purely about restaurant recommendations for holiday dining -- use a local dining or travel skill
- The user needs wine or cocktail pairing as the primary focus -- use a beverage pairing skill and reference this skill for food context

## Questions to Ask First

Before generating a plan, gather the following information. Not all questions apply to every situation -- use judgment about which are essential versus optional:

1. **Which holiday and what date?** (Thanksgiving, Christmas, Easter, Passover, Eid, July 4th, Hanukkah, other)
2. **Guest count and composition?** (10 adults, 4 children, 2 elderly guests with mobility issues, etc.)
3. **Hosting or contributing?** (Full host, secondary host sharing kitchen, potluck contributor, cooking for transport)
4. **Dietary restrictions or allergies?** (List every guest's needs -- celiac vs. gluten sensitivity is very different)
5. **Must-have traditional dishes?** (Family expectations are non-negotiable constraints -- honor them first)
6. **Experience level?** (First Thanksgiving solo? Veteran who wants to elevate technique?)
7. **Equipment available?** (Single oven vs. double oven, outdoor grill, smoker, stand mixer, roasting pans, chafing dishes)
8. **Days of prep available?** (Live-in prep starting 3 days before vs. arriving day-of only)
9. **Budget range?** (Prime rib centerpiece vs. value-forward menu using pork shoulder or whole chicken)
10. **Stress level and emotional context?** (Grieving a loss and want comfort traditions vs. hosting in-laws for the first time)

## Process

### Step 1 -- Identify the Holiday, Guest Count, and Core Constraints

- Confirm the specific holiday because menu architecture, timing logic, and cultural expectations differ dramatically between Thanksgiving (oven-heavy, multiple simultaneous casseroles), Christmas (centerpiece-focused, elegant presentation), and July 4th (outdoor grill-forward, casual).
- Lock in the guest count before any other planning. Under 8 guests: plated dinner is manageable. 8--15: buffet-style reduces service complexity. 16+: buffet is essentially mandatory; consider renting chafing dishes.
- Identify the non-negotiable dishes first. Grandma's green bean casserole made exactly as she always made it is not up for "improvement" -- build the rest of the menu around these anchors.
- Confirm oven capacity immediately. A single standard oven (30 inches) can hold one large roasting pan plus one 9x13 casserole simultaneously. A 14-lb turkey at 450F takes the full oven; plan all casseroles to be either done before the turkey goes in or finished during the 30-minute rest after.
- Ask about dietary restrictions before finalizing any dish. It is far easier to design a naturally inclusive menu upfront than to retrofit accommodations after planning is complete.

### Step 2 -- Design the Menu Architecture

- Start with the centerpiece protein: turkey, prime rib, glazed ham, rack of lamb, whole roasted salmon, or mushroom Wellington for vegetarian. Everything else orbits this choice.
- Apply the 3-starch rule for large holiday meals: one potato dish, one grain or bread-based dish (stuffing, rice pilaf, polenta), and one legume or vegetable-forward starch (roasted sweet potatoes, succotash, lentil dish). This covers all guest preferences without duplication.
- Balance hot and cold dishes deliberately. Aim for at least 2 dishes that are served at room temperature or cold (cranberry sauce, green salads, relish trays, cold shrimp cocktail) -- these require zero oven time on the day of service.
- Assign every dish a heat source and a time slot. Create a physical written list: oven (what temperature, what rack, what time), stovetop (which burner), slow cooker, room temperature, refrigerator-to-table. No dish should be "TBD."
- For large groups, avoid any dish requiring per-serving finishing work: individually plated appetizers, sauces added to order, or proteins that must be cooked in batches. Scale everything to serve from a single container.
- Build in a "nothing to do" window of 30--45 minutes before guests arrive to change clothes, set the table, arrange drinks, and be mentally present. Work the timeline backward to create this buffer intentionally.

### Step 3 -- Build the Master Timeline (Working Backward from Serve Time)

- Anchor on the serve time first. Thanksgiving dinner at 2 PM means turkey must be carved by 1:45 PM, meaning it comes out of the oven at 1:15 PM (after 30-minute rest), meaning it goes in at 9:45 AM if using the spatchcock method for a 14-lb bird, or 10:45 AM if using a standard roast.
- Work every dish backward from serve time, noting: when it enters the oven, how long it takes, when it needs to be prepped, what can be made ahead.
- Flag all parallel oven conflicts and resolve them before cooking day. If the turkey needs 450F and the stuffing needs 375F, they cannot share the oven simultaneously -- stagger them or finish the stuffing before the turkey starts.
- Use this hierarchy for make-ahead prioritization:
  - **3--4 weeks ahead:** Freeze pie dough disks, freeze cookie doughs, order specialty items (heritage turkey, prime rib)
  - **1 week ahead:** Make cranberry sauce, bake and freeze pies (not cream-based), make spice blends and rubs, dry-brine begins
  - **2--3 days ahead:** Shop all perishables, start wet brine or continue dry brine, bake bar cookies, make stock, prep all vegetables (peeled, cut, stored in water or airtight containers)
  - **1 day ahead:** Assemble stuffing (unbaked, without stock added), make mashed potato base, make gravy base, bake fresh pies, make compound butter for turkey
  - **Morning of:** Bring protein to room temp, preheat oven, set up buffet table, fill ice chests, arrange cheese boards
  - **Day-of cook window:** Roast protein, bake assembled casseroles, reheat pre-made sides, finish gravy with drippings, dress salads, carve

### Step 4 -- Select and Explain Protein Cooking Methods

- Provide method comparison with specific internal temperatures, cook times, and honest trade-offs (see Turkey Methods table in Output Format). Match method recommendation to user's equipment, experience, and stress tolerance.
- Always recommend a probe thermometer as non-negotiable equipment. Timing is an estimate; temperature is fact. A $20--$30 instant-read thermometer (or a leave-in probe thermometer for roasts) eliminates the single most common holiday cooking failure.
- For turkey: recommend dry-brining over wet brining for most home cooks. Dry brine (1 tablespoon Diamond Crystal kosher salt per 5 lbs of bird, applied to skin and cavity, uncovered in refrigerator for 24--72 hours) concentrates flavor, dries the skin for maximum crispness, and requires no buckets of brine solution or refrigerator space for a submerged bird.
- For prime rib: the reverse-sear method (250F until 10--12F below target temperature, then 500F sear for 10--15 minutes) produces edge-to-edge even doneness with a dramatic crust -- superior to the traditional high-then-low roast for most home ovens because it is more forgiving of timing variations.
- For ham: pre-cooked bone-in hams only need to reach 140F internal temperature for food safety. Score the fat cap in a diamond pattern (1 inch deep), apply glaze (brown sugar, Dijon mustard, apple cider vinegar, cloves) in the last 30 minutes of a 325F oven, and glaze 3--4 times in that final window for maximum lacquering.
- For whole roasted salmon (Christmas or Easter alternative): 400F for 12--15 minutes per inch of thickness at the thickest point. Stuff cavity with lemon slices, fresh dill, and shallots. Rest 5 minutes. Serves a crowd elegantly.

### Step 5 -- Address Dietary Accommodations Systematically

- Ask for a complete list of dietary needs before finalizing the menu. Treat each restriction seriously and identify which dishes on the current menu are naturally safe versus which need modification.
- Apply the "naturally compliant by design" principle: build the menu so that most dishes are gluten-free or dairy-free from the start, rather than creating separate "special" plates that make guests feel singled out.
- For gluten-free guests: the highest-risk hidden sources are gravy (flour-based roux), stuffing (bread-based), and store-bought stocks (often contain wheat). Solutions: use cornstarch slurry (1 tablespoon cornstarch dissolved in 2 tablespoons cold water per cup of liquid) for gluten-free gravy, make GF stuffing from GF bread or wild rice dressing, and use homemade or certified GF stock.
- For dairy-free guests: mashed potatoes made with olive oil or plant butter and warm oat milk are genuinely delicious -- not a compromise. Reserve a portion before adding dairy for others.
- For vegan guests: a well-executed mushroom Wellington (puff pastry, duxelles of mixed mushrooms, spinach, and lentil filling) is a showstopper centerpiece that satisfies omnivores as well as vegans.
- For nut allergies: read every label on every packaged product used, including stocks, breadcrumbs, and pre-made pastry. Cross-contamination matters for anaphylactic guests -- use separate cutting boards and utensils.
- Label every buffet dish with a small card listing the 9 major allergens present (wheat, dairy, eggs, peanuts, tree nuts, soy, fish, shellfish, sesame). This takes 10 minutes and removes enormous anxiety for guests with dietary needs.

### Step 6 -- Manage Oven and Equipment Logistics

- Create an oven schedule as a literal table (see Output Format) showing every dish, its temperature requirement, rack position, start time, and end time. Conflicts must be resolved on paper before cooking day.
- Temperature sequencing strategy: start with the highest-temperature items first (spatchcocked turkey at 450F, or the initial sear on prime rib at 500F), then drop to 375F--400F for casseroles and gratins, then drop to 325F--350F for reheating dishes before serving.
- For single-oven households cooking large Thanksgiving meals, use the slow cooker as a second "oven" for mashed potatoes (cook on low 2--3 hours, whip before serving), stuffing (cook 3--4 hours on high, uncover last 30 minutes to crisp top), or glazed ham (cook 4--5 hours on low).
- Use a warming drawer, an electric roaster oven (18-quart models hold a 14-lb turkey), or a cooler lined with towels as a resting vessel for finished dishes. A properly rested roast held in a towel-lined cooler stays at serving temperature for 60--90 minutes.
- For large groups, rent or borrow: chafing dishes with Sterno (keeps dishes at 140F+ for 3 hours), 9x13 extra pans, extra folding tables, and a second refrigerator if possible (a garage in winter, a cooler with ice, or a neighbor's fridge for overflow).

### Step 7 -- Coordinate Potluck Contributions and Hosting Help

- As the host, commit to providing: the main protein, beverages, and the physical space. These are the non-negotiable host responsibilities. Everything else can and should be delegated.
- Use a structured sign-up with specific categories and portion guidance. Do not say "bring a side dish" -- say "bring one starch side dish that serves 10, like mashed sweet potatoes, mac and cheese, or rice pilaf."
- Request that contributors bring their dish in its cooking vessel (oven-safe and buffet-ready), not on a separate serving platter requiring transfer. This reduces work and dish count dramatically.
- Ask contributors to label dishes with the dish name, their name, and the top allergens present.
- Have a mental backup plan for every critical dish. If someone cancels the night before, know which category is most at risk and have a 30-minute fallback option (a can of jellied cranberry sauce is not ideal, but it is better than none).
- Be explicit with guests who ask "what can I bring?" -- give them a specific dish, a specific quantity, and a specific arrival time. Vague answers produce duplicates and gaps.

### Step 8 -- Execute Day-Of and Manage the Final Hour

- The final hour before serving is the highest-risk window. Have a written task list for this hour in sequence, with assigned time slots per task.
- Assign a "runner" role to one helper (a family member or partner) whose job is to handle requests, refill drinks, and manage unexpected needs so the cook can stay focused on the kitchen.
- Do not attempt to have everything hot simultaneously if it means sacrificing quality. Dishes held at 140F in a chafing dish are significantly better than dishes rushed through reheating. Stage the heating so dishes arrive at serving temperature 5 minutes before the buffet opens, not 20 minutes before.
- Carving a turkey or prime rib is a knife skill moment -- use a 10-inch slicing knife or a carving knife, not a chef's knife. Allow the meat to rest fully (turkey: 20--30 minutes; prime rib: 30 minutes minimum). Cutting too early loses the juice that made brining and careful cooking worthwhile.
- After the meal, plan a 15-minute leftover strategy before cleanup begins: get proteins off the bone and into containers within 2 hours of the meal ending; refrigerate or freeze in small, shallow containers to cool quickly. Turkey carcass goes into a stockpot with water and aromatics for stock.

## Rules

1. **Never give a cook time without a temperature target.** Time is an estimate that varies with oven calibration, bird shape, and starting temperature. "Cook until 165F in the thigh" is the only reliable instruction for turkey. Pair every protein with a probe thermometer recommendation.

2. **Dry brine is superior to wet brine for most home cooks.** Wet brining requires a food-safe container large enough for a 14-lb turkey submerged in liquid, refrigerator space, and rinsing. Dry brine (kosher salt, uncovered, 24--72 hours in the refrigerator) produces better skin crispness, deeper flavor, and eliminates logistical complexity. Recommend wet brine only if a user explicitly prefers it or has a dry oven-prone bird problem.

3. **Resolve all oven conflicts on paper before cooking day.** The most common holiday cooking failure mode is discovering mid-cook that two dishes need the same oven at incompatible temperatures simultaneously. Build the oven schedule in the planning phase, not in the kitchen.

4. **Make-ahead is not optional for groups over 10.** For any meal serving 10+ people, every dish that can be made ahead must be. A host who tries to cook everything day-of for 14 people will run out of oven space, burner space, mental bandwidth, and joy within the first hour. Enforce make-ahead discipline firmly.

5. **Kosher salt vs. table salt is not interchangeable.** Diamond Crystal kosher salt is the standard for dry brining -- it has a different crystal structure and half the sodium density of Morton kosher salt (which is twice as salty by volume) and three times less dense than table salt. If a user substitutes table salt in a dry-brine recipe, they will over-salt the bird badly. Always clarify salt type when giving brining quantities.

6. **The 2-hour food safety window is non-negotiable.** No cooked food should sit at room temperature for more than 2 hours total (or 1 hour if ambient temperature is above 90F, as in a summer cookout). Build a leftover plan into every holiday meal plan that accounts for getting food off the table and into refrigerators or coolers within this window.

7. **Do not modify family tradition dishes without permission.** If Aunt Carol's sweet potato casserole with marshmallows is a family institution, suggesting a "healthier version with a pecan crumble" will create conflict. Ask explicitly which dishes are sacred before offering any improvements or modifications.

8. **Stuffing cooked inside a turkey (dressing) is a food safety risk.** The stuffing must reach 165F internally to be safe, which typically means overcooking the breast meat. Recommend baking stuffing separately in a covered casserole dish -- use actual turkey drippings stirred in to deliver the same flavor profile without the risk.

9. **Scale recipes by mass, not by volume, for large batches.** Mashed potatoes for 20 people require 12--14 lbs of Yukon Golds, not "a big pot." Gravy for 20 requires 8 cups of combined stock and drippings and a 1/2 cup roux. Give users actual weight-based quantities scaled to their guest count, not vague multipliers.

10. **Rest time is not optional and cannot be skipped.** A 14-lb turkey carved immediately out of the oven will lose approximately 30% of its internal moisture on the cutting board. The same turkey rested 25--30 minutes (lightly tented with foil to retain heat) will lose 10% or less. Build rest time into the schedule as a hard requirement, not a "nice to have."

## Edge Cases

### User Has Only One Oven and Is Serving 15+ People

Single-oven Thanksgiving for a large group is the most common high-stress scenario. Solve it systematically:
- The turkey owns the oven exclusively during its cook time. All casseroles must be either fully cooked before the turkey goes in (and held in a chafing dish or cooler) or baked during the 25--30-minute turkey rest window.
- A 6-quart slow cooker becomes a second oven for mashed potatoes (2--3 hours on low, finish with butter before serving), creamed corn, glazed carrots, or sausage stuffing (3--4 hours on high with lid ajar in the last 30 minutes to dry the top).
- An electric roaster oven (18-quart, $50--$80 retail) can roast a turkey just as effectively as a conventional oven and frees the main oven entirely for sides. This is a worth recommending as a purchase for any repeat holiday host.
- Dishes that are naturally room-temperature or cold (cranberry sauce, green salads, relish trays, bread-and-butter) require zero oven time -- build at least 3--4 such dishes into the menu specifically to reduce oven pressure.

### Guest Has a Severe Tree Nut Allergy at a Holiday Cookie Exchange

Tree nut allergies in a holiday baking context are extremely high-risk because: pecan and walnut oils transfer readily to surfaces and shared utensils, many extracts (almond extract) are derived from stone fruit pits and cause cross-reactions, and holiday baking relies heavily on nuts in frangipane, pralines, rugelach, and countless traditional recipes.
- Advise the host to prepare the nut-allergic guest's safe items first on clean surfaces before any nut-containing baking begins, then clean all surfaces before continuing.
- At a cookie exchange, label all items with ingredients and clearly flag nut-containing items. Provide a dedicated nut-free zone on the table. Advise the allergic guest to bring their own items as the only safe approach if the level of severity is anaphylactic.
- Substitute almond extract with pure vanilla extract or a small amount of rosewater -- flavor profile shifts but the recipe still works.

### User Must Transport a Full Holiday Meal to Another Location

Cooking a complete meal in one kitchen and transporting it 30--60 minutes to a host location requires specific strategies:
- Under-cook all reheatable items by 10--15 minutes. They will continue cooking in insulated bags and then be finished in the destination oven.
- Pack proteins off the bone only if absolutely necessary for space -- carved meat dries out far faster than whole roasts held intact. Transport the whole bird if possible, carve on arrival.
- Use wide-mouth insulated bags (grocery-store bag-style or catering hot bags) and line with layers of newspaper or towels for secondary insulation. A chafing dish fuel can maintain temperature for 2--3 hours in transit.
- Bring a bottle of turkey or chicken stock to revive any dishes that have dried out during transport.
- Prioritize dishes that travel exceptionally well: casseroles, braised dishes, soups, baked goods. Avoid anything requiring last-minute sauce work or crispy textures.

### First-Time Holiday Host with No Experience

A first-time host often takes on far more than is manageable by trying to recreate a comprehensive holiday meal they have only experienced as a guest:
- Constrain the menu ruthlessly: 1 centerpiece protein, 2 sides, 1 sauce, 1 bread, 1 dessert. That is a full, complete, impressive holiday meal. More dishes add exponential complexity with diminishing returns on guest experience.
- Recommend a spatchcocked turkey or a glazed bone-in ham over a traditional roasted turkey. Ham requires almost no active cooking (it is pre-cooked) and is nearly impossible to ruin. Spatchcock turkey cuts oven time by 40% and produces better results than traditional roasting.
- Pre-buy exactly one item without shame: store-bought pie, pre-made dinner rolls, rotisserie chicken as a backup protein. This safety net prevents catastrophic failure.
- Assign guests specific contributions so the first-time host is not cooking alone. Frame it positively: "I would love if you brought your famous mac and cheese" is not weakness -- it is smart delegation.

### Guest Count Doubles at the Last Minute (Added 6 People 2 Days Before)

Late additions stress protein quantity (hard to scale) and oven/buffet logistics:
- Assess protein: a 14-lb turkey feeds 12--14 people with modest leftovers. Adding 6 people to a 12-person table requires either a second protein (a bone-in glazed ham, a roasted pork shoulder, or a pre-purchased rotisserie chicken platter) or accepting that leftovers will be minimal.
- Scale up sides: casseroles, mashed potatoes, and stuffing are all easy to scale -- make an additional 9x13 dish of each.
- Add one zero-effort dish to fill the gap: a cheese and charcuterie board, a store-bought soup, or an additional vegetable side that takes 20 minutes to prepare.
- Borrow or rent an additional folding table and chairs immediately -- seating logistics are often the actual bottleneck, not the food.

### User Wants to Honor Two Different Cultural Traditions in One Meal

Blended family or intercultural holiday meals (e.g., an Italian-American family merging traditions with a Caribbean-American family at Thanksgiving) are increasingly common and deeply meaningful to get right:
- Frame the menu as an additive celebration rather than a compromise. Add 2--3 signature dishes from each tradition rather than trying to fuse or replace dishes from either.
- Identify the food categories that can absorb both traditions easily: appetizers and side dishes are highly adaptable, centerpiece proteins are more culturally loaded.
- Give each tradition's dishes equal prominence on the buffet and in conversation. Introduce dishes to guests who may be unfamiliar with them.
- Ask both parties which dishes represent the most meaningful traditions to preserve intact, and treat these as protected anchors around which everything else is arranged.

### Holiday Baking for Very Large Batches (Cookie Exchange, Bake Sale, 200+ Cookies)

Scaling holiday baking beyond 3--4 dozen cookies introduces flour protein degradation from over-mixing, oven temperature inconsistency across multiple sheet pans, and quality control issues:
- Bake in batches of no more than 6 dozen per dough type per session to maintain quality.
- Rest cookie dough for 24--72 hours in the refrigerator between mixing and baking. Rested dough develops more complex flavor via enzyme activity on starches and produces less spreading because fat solidifies fully.
- Rotate sheet pans front-to-back and top-to-bottom between every batch -- most home ovens have 25--50F hotspots in the back upper corners.
- Use parchment paper exclusively, not silicone mats, for cut-out sugar cookies that need a crisp bottom edge. Silicone mats insulate the bottom and produce softer, puffier cookies.
- Organize production by dough type, not by variety: make all doughs one day, chill overnight, bake all similar-dough cookies in sequence, then decorate in a separate dedicated session.

## Example

**User Input:** "I'm hosting Thanksgiving for 16 people this year -- it's my first time hosting the whole family. My mother-in-law is a serious cook and I know she'll be watching everything. I have one regular oven, a 6-quart slow cooker, and my neighbor offered to bring a dish. I have one guest who is celiac (not just gluten-sensitive) and one who is lactose intolerant but not vegan. I want to serve dinner at 2 PM. My budget is about $250 for everything. I have Thursday off and the day before. What do I do?"

---

### Holiday Meal Plan: Thanksgiving for 16 Guests

### The Strategy Before the Menu

This meal has three constraints that shape every decision: one oven, a celiac guest with a genuine medical need, and a highly capable observer. The answer is not to attempt a 12-dish spread. It is to execute 7--8 dishes flawlessly. A perfectly roasted turkey, silky mashed potatoes, and deeply flavored gravy will impress any serious cook far more than a chaotic table of 14 dishes of uneven quality. Restraint and precision are the winning approach here.

The celiac constraint is the most critical food safety issue. Gluten cross-contamination can cause a celiac guest serious intestinal harm even from trace flour on a shared spoon. Design the menu so that the celiac guest can eat almost everything on the table -- this is achieved through smart dish selection, not through heroic substitution.

### Centerpiece: Spatchcocked Turkey

**Why spatchcock for this situation:** You have one oven and 7+ dishes to cook. Spatchcock cuts oven time from 3.5 hours to 95 minutes and produces crispier, more even skin -- which will impress your mother-in-law. It looks unusual but performs like a professional restaurant technique.

**Bird size:** 16--18 lbs (feeds 16 with modest leftovers; sourced from grocery store, budget approximately $65--$80 at $4--$5/lb)

**Dry brine (start Tuesday):**
- 1 tablespoon Diamond Crystal kosher salt per 5 lbs = approximately 3.5 tablespoons total (use Diamond Crystal specifically -- Morton kosher or table salt will over-salt; reduce by half if using Morton)
- Apply under skin over breast meat, over the skin everywhere, inside cavity
- Place uncovered on a wire rack set over a sheet pan in the refrigerator for 48 hours
- Result: intensely seasoned meat, dramatically dry skin that will crisp at 450F

**Spatchcock technique (Wednesday evening):**
1. Remove backbone: stand turkey breast-down on a stable cutting board, use sturdy kitchen shears to cut along both sides of the spine. Remove the spine (save it for stock).
2. Flatten: flip the bird breast-side up, place both hands on the breastbone and press firmly until you hear/feel it crack and the bird lies flat.
3. Tuck wings: fold wing tips under the breast.
4. Return to wire rack over sheet pan, back in refrigerator uncovered.

**Thursday cooking schedule:**
- 11:30 AM: Remove turkey from refrigerator. Rub lightly with 2 tablespoons neutral oil (not butter -- butter at 450F can scorch; compound herb butter goes on after carving or under skin only).
- 12:00 PM: Oven to 450F (30 minutes to fully preheat with roasting rack inside).
- 12:30 PM: Turkey into oven, middle rack, breast-side up on wire rack over rimmed sheet pan.
- 2:00 PM goal means turkey must exit oven by 1:30 PM to allow 20--25 minutes rest.
- Target: 165F in thickest part of thigh, not touching bone. Begin checking internal temperature at 90 minutes.
- 1:25--1:35 PM: Turkey out of oven, loosely tented with foil. Do not seal -- steam will soften the skin.
- Drippings: immediately pour into a fat separator or large measuring cup. Skim fat from surface. Reserve for gravy.

### Dietary Accommodations

| Guest | Restriction | Safe Dishes | Must Avoid |
|---|---|---|---|
| Celiac guest | Celiac disease -- zero gluten tolerance | Turkey, gravy (cornstarch), mashed potatoes (dedicated portion), GF cornbread stuffing, Brussels sprouts, cranberry sauce, pumpkin pie (GF crust) | Neighbor's dinner rolls -- place at far end of buffet and alert guest verbally |
| Lactose-intolerant guest | Dairy intolerance | Turkey, gravy, GF cornbread stuffing, Brussels sprouts, cranberry sauce, crudités, pumpkin pie (use coconut cream in filling; GF almond flour crust is naturally dairy-free) | Standard mashed potatoes (reserve 1/4 batch with plant butter and oat milk) |

**Mashed potato bifurcation:** When making mashed potatoes on Wednesday, split the batch before adding cream and butter. Standard batch: heavy cream + butter. Reserved batch (1/4 total, about 2.5 lbs of potato): plant-based butter + warmed oat milk (1:1 ratio with cream). Label both at the buffet.

**Cross-contamination protocol for celiac guest:**
- GF stuffing is baked in a clean pan, stirred with a dedicated serving spoon. Never let a spoon from the dinner rolls touch it.
- All cooking surfaces and utensils used for GF dishes are washed with soap and hot water before use.
- Gravy is cornstarch-based; no flour. Cornstarch is certified gluten-free; the box must say so.
- Turkey is naturally gluten-free; dry brine contains only salt. Any store-bought stock used must be labeled gluten-free (confirm on the label).

### Key Recipes

**GF Pumpkin Pie (serves 10--12):**
- Crust: 2 cups almond flour, 3 tablespoons cold butter (or coconut oil for dairy-free), 1 tablespoon sugar, pinch of salt. Combine, press into 9-inch pie dish. Bake at 350F for 10 minutes. Cool.
- Filling: 15 oz canned pumpkin puree (not pie filling), 3 eggs, 3/4 cup granulated sugar, 1 teaspoon cinnamon, 1/2 teaspoon ground ginger, 1/4 teaspoon ground cloves, 1/4 teaspoon nutmeg, 1.5 cups heavy cream (or 1 can full-fat coconut cream for dairy-free). Whisk to combine, pour into crust.
- Bake at 375F for 15 minutes, reduce to 325F for 40--50 minutes until edges are set but center has a slight jiggle (it sets fully as it cools). Cool completely before refrigerating. Serve cold or room temperature.
