## Questions to Ask the User First

1. **What's driving this?** (Moving, overwhelmed, life change, want more function, want more calm)
2. **Which spaces need help?** (Whole home, specific rooms, specific problem areas)
3. **What's your biggest pain point?** (Can't find things, too much stuff, no system, clutter returns)
4. **How would you describe your current situation?** (Mild clutter, moderate chaos, severely overwhelmed)
5. **Do you live alone or with others?** (Family members affect systems dramatically)
6. **Have you tried organizing before?** (What worked? What didn't stick?)
7. **How do you feel about getting rid of things?** (Easy, moderate difficulty, very hard)
8. **Any sentimental or emotional attachment challenges?** (Inherited items, grief-related, anxiety about waste)
9. **What's your timeline?** (This weekend, gradual over weeks, no rush)
10. **Budget for organizing supplies?** ($0 use what I have, $50-100, $100-500, open)
11. **Physical limitations?** (Mobility, lifting restrictions, energy levels)

## Organizing Methodologies

### Category-Based Decluttering

Many professional organizers recommend sorting by category rather than by room, because belongings of the same type are often scattered across multiple locations. This approach -- popularized by Marie Kondo's KonMari(TM) Method and adopted widely in the organizing profession -- ensures you see the full scope of what you own before making decisions.

**Recommended category order** (start with easiest decisions, build to hardest):
1. Clothing and accessories
2. Books and media
3. Papers and documents
4. General household items (kitchen, bathroom, garage, miscellaneous)
5. Sentimental items (last, because you have built decision-making confidence)

**For each category:**
- Gather ALL items of that type from every room
- Evaluate each item: Does it serve a clear purpose? Does it add value to my life? Would I choose to acquire this today?
- Decide: Keep, Donate, Sell, Trash, or Relocate
- Assign a permanent home to everything you keep

> For the complete KonMari(TM) Method including its specific principles and techniques, see Marie Kondo's books or visit konmari.com.

### The SPACE Method (Professional Organizers)
```
S.P.A.C.E. FRAMEWORK:

S - SORT
  Remove everything from the space.
  Group like items together.
  See what you actually have (duplicates, skipped items).

P - PURGE
  The "undecided" box gets a 30-day deadline.
  If you didn't need it in 30 days, let it go.

A - ASSIGN A HOME
  Every item needs a designated spot.
  Store items where you use them.
  Most-used items at most-accessible locations.

C - CONTAINERIZE
  Now (and only now) choose containers and systems.
  Measure before buying containers.
  Label everything (especially shared spaces).

E - EQUALIZE (maintain)
  Spend 5-10 minutes daily putting things back.
  Weekly mini-reset of each zone.
  One-in-one-out rule for new acquisitions.
```

### The Usefulness-and-Value Test

When deciding what to keep, ask two questions about each item:

1. **Is it useful?** Do I use this regularly? Does it serve a practical function in my current life?
2. **Is it valuable to me?** Does it hold personal significance, bring me genuine satisfaction, or enhance my daily experience?

If the answer to both is no, the item is a candidate for removal. This simple two-question test draws from the same psychological principles behind multiple decluttering methods: keep what serves your life, release what does not.

## Room-by-Room Organization Guide

### Kitchen Organization
```
KITCHEN ORGANIZATION PLAN:
DECLUTTER FIRST:
  [ ] Remove everything from cabinets and drawers (one section at a time)
  [ ] Discard: expired food, broken items, duplicates you never use
  [ ] Donate: specialty gadgets used less than 1x/year, extra dishes
  [ ] Relocate: items that don't belong in the kitchen
ZONE SYSTEM (organize by activity):
  ZONE 1: COOKING (near stove)
    - Pots, pans, cooking utensils, oils, spices
    - Pot lids: use lid organizer or rack
    - Utensil crock on counter near stove
  ZONE 2: PREP (near main counter space)
    - Cutting boards, knives, mixing bowls
    - Measuring cups and spoons
    - Food processor, blender, mixer
  ZONE 3: STORAGE (near fridge)
    - Food storage containers (match lids to bases, discard orphans)
    - Wraps, bags, foil
    - Pantry items
  ZONE 4: CLEANING (near sink)
    - Dish soap, sponges, cleaning supplies
    - Trash bags
    - Dish towels
  ZONE 5: SERVING (near dining area)
    - Plates, bowls, glasses, silverware
    - Serving dishes and platters
PANTRY ORGANIZATION:
  [ ] Group by category: baking, grains, canned, snacks, breakfast, etc.
  [ ] Decant into clear containers (optional but effective)
  [ ] Use turntables (lazy Susans) for deep cabinets
  [ ] Use risers/shelf organizers to see items behind other items
  [ ] FIFO principle: First In, First Out (new items to back)
  [ ] Label shelves, not just containers (helps everyone maintain)
REFRIGERATOR:
  Top shelf: Leftovers, drinks, ready-to-eat items
  Middle shelves: Dairy, eggs, packaged food
  Lower shelves: Raw meat, fish (prevents drip contamination)
  Crisper drawers: Produce (separate high/low humidity if available)
  Door: Condiments (NOT milk -- door is warmest spot)
  Clear bins: Group categories for easy access
COUNTER RULES:
  Counter-worthy items only: daily-use items (coffee maker, toaster, knife block)
  Everything else: in cabinets or stored
```

### Closet Organization
```
CLOSET ORGANIZATION PLAN:
STEP 1: EMPTY COMPLETELY
  Remove every item. Yes, every item.
  Clean the closet while empty.
STEP 2: SORT BY CATEGORY
  Group all like items:
    Dresses
    Outerwear
    Activewear
    Sleepwear
    Shoes
STEP 3: PURGE EACH CATEGORY
  For each item, ask:
  [ ] Does it fit RIGHT NOW? (Not "when I lose weight")
  [ ] Have I worn it in the past 12 months?
  [ ] Is it in good condition? (No stains, holes, pilling)
  [ ] Do I feel good wearing it?
  [ ] Does it match at least 2 other things I own?
  If no to 2+ questions: donate, sell, or discard.

STEP 4: ORGANIZE WHAT REMAINS
  Hanging:
    Order: jackets, dresses, blouses, pants, skirts
    Within each: light to dark (left to right)

  Folded (file folding = items stand upright):
    Drawers: fold items into rectangles, store upright like files
    Shelves: fold and stack neatly, or use shelf dividers

  Shoes:
    Seasonal rotation: off-season in bins

  Accessories:
    Jewelry: tray, wall organizer, or drawer insert
    Ties: rack or hanger

STEP 5: MAINTENANCE RULES
  Hanger trick: turn all hangers backward at start of season.
  After wearing, hang normally. At season end, anything still
  backward = you didn't wear it. Evaluate for donation.

  One-in-one-out: buy something new, something old goes.
  Seasonal swap: rotate warm/cold weather items (store off-season in bins)
```

### Garage Organization
```
GARAGE ORGANIZATION PLAN:

ZONES:
  Zone 1: AUTOMOTIVE (near garage door)
    Car supplies, maintenance items, car-washing supplies

  Zone 2: TOOLS (workbench area)
    Tool storage: pegboard, chest, wall-mounted
    Workbench with good lighting
    Hardware organization (labeled bins or drawer organizer)

  Zone 3: LAWN & GARDEN
    Long-handled tools: wall-mounted hooks or rack
    Potting supplies, seeds, gloves
    Hoses: wall-mounted reel

  Zone 4: SPORTS & RECREATION
    Ball storage: mesh bag or bin
    Bike storage: wall hooks or ceiling hoist
    Seasonal sports gear: labeled bins

  Zone 5: SEASONAL STORAGE
    Holiday decorations: labeled bins by holiday
    Seasonal items: clearly labeled, accessible

  Zone 6: HOUSEHOLD OVERFLOW

STORAGE SOLUTIONS:
  [ ] Wall-mounted shelving (2x4 and plywood or metal shelving units)
  [ ] Pegboard for tools (outline tools for easy return)
  [ ] Ceiling-mounted storage for seasonal items
  [ ] Clear bins with labels (NOT cardboard boxes -- pests, moisture)
  [ ] Heavy-duty hooks for bikes, ladders, hoses
  [ ] Workbench with drawers/cabinets underneath

GARAGE RULES:
  - Floor should be mostly clear (park cars, walk safely)
  - Store chemicals on high shelves (away from children, heat sources)
  - Never store propane tanks indoors
  - Label everything (you will skip what's in that bin)
  - Annual purge: review everything once per year
```

### Home Office / Paper Management
```
PAPER MANAGEMENT SYSTEM:

THE GOAL: Touch each paper once. Decide immediately.

PAPER DECISION FLOWCHART:
  Receive paper
  |
  Is it junk mail/catalog I didn't request?
  |-- YES --> Recycle immediately (don't even open)
  |-- NO -->
      |
      Does it require action?
      |-- YES --> Place in ACTION folder, schedule the action
      |-- NO -->
          |
          Must I keep it for legal/tax/reference?
          |-- YES --> File immediately in correct folder
          |-- NO --> Recycle/shred

FILING SYSTEM (physical):
  Use a simple filing cabinet or file box.
  Broad categories (NOT ultra-specific):
    [ ] Financial (bank, investments, retirement)
    [ ] Insurance (home, auto, health, life)
    [ ] Medical (records, EOBs, prescriptions)
    [ ] Home (mortgage, maintenance records, warranties)
    [ ] Auto (registration, maintenance, insurance)
    [ ] Tax (current year -- keep 7 years of returns)
    [ ] Legal (contracts, deeds, wills, IDs)
    [ ] Education (transcripts, certifications)
    [ ] Vital Documents (birth certificates, passports, SS cards)
        -- Store in fireproof safe or safe deposit box

WHAT TO KEEP AND FOR HOW LONG:
  Tax returns: 7 years
  Bank statements: 1 year (unless needed for tax)
  Pay stubs: until W-2 received, then verify and shred
  Medical records: indefinitely (or digital)
  Insurance policies: duration of policy + 1 year
  Utility bills: 1 year max
  Receipts (major purchases): warranty period
  Receipts (daily): shred monthly (unless needed for return/warranty)

GO DIGITAL:
  - Scan important documents (phone scanner app: Adobe Scan, free)
  - Store in cloud backup (Google Drive, Dropbox, iCloud)
  - Opt for paperless billing everywhere possible
  - Digital reduces physical storage needs by 90%+
```

## Digital Organization
```
DIGITAL ORGANIZATION SYSTEM:

EMAIL:
  Inbox Zero approach:
    For each email: Delete, Reply immediately (< 2 min),
    Archive, or Star/flag for action
  Folder structure: Keep it simple
    - Action Required
    - Waiting For Reply
    - Reference
    - Archive
  Unsubscribe from everything you don't read
  Process email 2-3x per day, not constantly

PHONE:
  Home screen: only daily-use apps (5-10 max)
  Delete apps unused for 30+ days
  Organize remaining into folders by category
  Turn off non-essential notifications
  Clean photos monthly (delete duplicates, blurry, screenshots)

COMPUTER FILES:
  Top-level folder structure:
    Documents/
      Financial/
      [Year]/
        [Month or Event]/
      [Project Name]/

  NAMING CONVENTION: YYYY-MM-DD_Description

  Cloud backup: Enable automatic backup of important folders
  Monthly cleanup: delete downloads folder, old files

PASSWORDS:
  Use a password manager (Bitwarden, 1Password, or similar)
  Unique password for every account
  Enable 2-factor authentication on critical accounts
  Store recovery codes in safe place
```

## Maintaining Organization

### Daily Habits (5-10 Minutes)
```
DAILY MAINTENANCE ROUTINE:

MORNING (5 min):
  [ ] Make bed (1 min -- biggest visual impact for least effort)
  [ ] Quick tidy of bathroom after getting ready
  [ ] Put breakfast dishes in dishwasher

EVENING (10 min):
  [ ] Kitchen reset: dishes done, counters wiped, sink empty
  [ ] Family 10-minute tidy (everyone picks up shared spaces)
  [ ] Process mail (sort immediately, recycle junk)
  [ ] Lay out tomorrow's essentials
  [ ] One load of laundry (start, switch, or fold/put away)

RULE: Never leave a room empty-handed.
Pick up one thing that doesn't belong and return it to its home.
```

### Weekly Reset (30-60 Minutes)
```
WEEKLY RESET CHECKLIST:

Pick one day (Sunday evening works well for many):

  [ ] Declutter all surfaces (counters, tables, desks)
  [ ] Process paper pile (decide, file, or recycle)
  [ ] Return all items to their assigned homes
  [ ] Quick clean of bathrooms
  [ ] Vacuum/sweep main living areas
  [ ] Plan meals and make grocery list
  [ ] Review calendar for the coming week
  [ ] Do one small organizing project (one drawer, one shelf)

THIS PREVENTS ORGANIZATION ENTROPY.
Without weekly maintenance, systems degrade within 2-3 weeks.
```

### The One-In-One-Out Rule
```
ONE-IN-ONE-OUT PRINCIPLE:

For every new item that enters your home,
one similar item must leave.

Buy a new shirt --> donate an old shirt
Receive a new kitchen gadget --> remove an old one
New toy enters --> old toy donated

EXCEPTIONS:
  - Consumables (food, toiletries, cleaning supplies)
  - Genuine needs being filled for the first time
  - Children's developmental needs (new stage = new items)

ADVANCED: One-in-two-out
```

## Decluttering Decision Frameworks

### For Difficult Items
```
DECISION HELPER FOR HARD ITEMS:

"But it was expensive..."
  The money is spent whether you keep it or not (sunk cost).
  Keeping an unused $200 item doesn't recover $200.
  Sell it and recover some value, or donate and recover the space.

"But I might need it someday..."
  If you haven't used it in 12 months, you likely won't.
  Could you borrow or re-buy for less than the cost of storing it?
  The 20/20 rule: If you can replace it for under $20 in under
  20 minutes, let it go.

"But someone gave it to me..."
  to store it forever. The person gave you a gift, not a
  storage obligation.

"But it was my [parent's/grandparent's]..."
  Keep the memory, not necessarily the object.
  Take a photo of the item before releasing it.
  Keep ONE representative item, not the entire collection.
  Repurpose: use the item instead of storing it, or incorporate it
  into your decor.

"But it might be worth something..."
  If you haven't sold it by now, will you?
  Set a deadline: list it for sale within 2 weeks, or donate.
  Most things are worth less than we think.

"But I don't know where to start..."
  Start with the easiest category (obvious trash and duplicates).
  Build momentum with quick wins.
  Save emotional items for last.
```

CLOSET:
  - Matching slim hangers (velvet or wood)
  - Shelf dividers
  - Hanging organizer for shoes or accessories
  - Clear shoe boxes (if you have the space)
  - Drawer organizers for folded items

BATHROOM:
  - Shower caddy or shelf
  - Under-sink tiered organizer
  - Drawer dividers for makeup/toiletries
  - Over-toilet shelving unit (if space is tight)

WHERE TO BUY:
  Budget: Dollar Tree, Target, Walmart, Amazon basics

RULE: Don't buy organizing supplies until you've decluttered.
