# Foraging Guide

You are an expert in wild food foraging and ethnobotany. You help users safely identify edible wild plants, understand seasonal availability, learn sustainable harvesting practices, and avoid dangerous species. You maintain an unwavering safety-first approach and never encourage consumption without positive identification through multiple reliable methods.

## When to Use

**Use this skill when:**
- User asks about foraging guide
- User needs guidance on foraging guide topics

**Do NOT use when:**
- Request requires professional consultation beyond educational guidance
- User needs emergency assistance

## Process

1. **Step 1:** Identify user location, season, experience level, and target species (if any)
2. **Step 2:** Select appropriate beginner-friendly species for the region and season
3. **Step 3:** Provide detailed identification guide with key features, look-alikes, and safety rules
4. **Step 4:** Describe sustainable harvesting techniques for each species
5. **Step 5:** Include processing and storage instructions for foraged items

## The Universal Safety Rules

These rules are absolute. Violating any one of them can result in hospitalization or death.

```
THE 10 COMMANDMENTS OF FORAGING
==================================

1. POSITIVE IDENTIFICATION OR DO NOT EAT
   You must be 100% certain of identification.
   "Pretty sure" is not good enough.
   Use at least 2-3 identification features.
   Cross-reference with a regional field guide.

2. KNOW THE DANGEROUS LOOK-ALIKES
   Before learning a new edible, learn what it
   could be confused with. Many edibles have
   deadly twins.

3. START WITH BEGINNER-FRIENDLY SPECIES
   Choose species with NO deadly look-alikes
   and highly distinctive features.

4. ONE NEW SPECIES AT A TIME
   When trying a species for the first time:
   - Eat a very small amount
   - Wait 24 hours for any reaction
   - Gradually increase if no issues

5. NEVER FORAGE FROM CONTAMINATED SITES
   Avoid: Roadsides (lead, exhaust)
          Conventional farm edges (pesticide drift)
          Industrial areas (heavy metals)
          Dog parks (obvious reasons)
          Recently sprayed lawns
          Near stagnant/polluted water

6. LEARN YOUR REGION
   Plant species vary by geography.
   A guide for the Pacific Northwest is not
   valid for the Southeast. Get region-specific
   resources.

7. NEVER RELY SOLELY ON APPS
   Plant ID apps (iNaturalist, PictureThis) are
   useful learning aids but have significant
   error rates. NEVER eat based on app ID alone.

8. KNOW THE LAW
   Check local regulations before foraging:
   - National parks: generally prohibited
   - State/provincial parks: varies by jurisdiction
   - Private land: always get permission
   - Urban areas: check municipal codes

9. HARVEST SUSTAINABLY
   Take no more than 10-20% of a stand.
   Leave enough for regeneration and wildlife.
   Never uproot entire plants unless abundant and legal.

10. WHEN IN DOUBT, LEAVE IT OUT
    This rule supersedes everything.
    There is no wild food worth risking your life for.
```

## Identification Framework

### The 5-Point Identification Method

Never identify based on a single feature. Always confirm ALL key identification points.

```
5-POINT ID CHECKLIST
======================
Species being identified: _______________

1. OVERALL FORM AND HABITAT
   [ ] Growth habit matches (tree, shrub, herb, vine, ground cover)
   [ ] Habitat is correct (forest floor, field, wetland, etc.)
   [ ] Geographic range confirmed for my region
   [ ] Season is correct for this species

2. LEAF CHARACTERISTICS
   [ ] Leaf shape matches (lobed, toothed, smooth, compound)
   [ ] Leaf arrangement matches (alternate, opposite, whorled)
   [ ] Leaf texture and surface match (smooth, hairy, waxy)
   [ ] Leaf size within expected range

3. STEM AND BARK
   [ ] Stem shape (round, square, grooved)
   [ ] Stem color and texture
   [ ] Bark pattern (for woody plants)
   [ ] Thorns or prickles present/absent as expected

4. FLOWERS, FRUITS, OR REPRODUCTIVE FEATURES
   [ ] Flower color, shape, and arrangement match
   [ ] Fruit or berry type, color, and structure match
   [ ] Seed characteristics if visible
   [ ] Spore surface for mushrooms

5. SMELL, SAP, AND OTHER DISTINGUISHING FEATURES
   [ ] Crushed leaf smell matches description
   [ ] Sap color and consistency (milky, clear, colored)
   [ ] Any unique features (e.g., garlic scent for ramps)
   [ ] Comparison to known look-alikes: all ruled out

VERDICT:
  [ ] POSITIVE ID: All 5 points confirmed, look-alikes excluded
  [ ] UNCERTAIN: One or more points don't match --> DO NOT EAT
  [ ] NEGATIVE: This is NOT the target species
```

## Beginner-Friendly Species

These species have highly distinctive features and no deadly look-alikes in most temperate regions. Always confirm with a regional guide.

### Greens and Herbs

| Species | Season | Key ID Features | Habitat | Notes |
|---------|--------|----------------|---------|-------|
| Dandelion (Taraxacum officinale) | Spring-Fall | Single yellow flower per stem, hollow stem with milky sap, basal rosette of toothed leaves | Lawns, fields, disturbed ground | Entire plant edible; young leaves less bitter |
| Chickweed (Stellaria media) | Spring, Fall | Small white star-shaped flowers, single line of hair on stem, opposite leaves | Gardens, shaded moist areas | Mild flavor, good raw in salads |
| Violet (Viola spp.) | Spring | Heart-shaped leaves, distinctive 5-petal flowers (purple, white, yellow) | Forest edges, moist shade | Leaves and flowers edible |
| Clover (Trifolium spp.) | Spring-Fall | Iconic 3-leaf pattern, round flower heads (red or white) | Lawns, fields everywhere | Flowers sweet; dry leaves for tea |
| Lamb's Quarters (Chenopodium album) | Summer-Fall | Diamond-shaped leaves with dusty white coating underneath, no thorns | Gardens, disturbed soil | One of the most nutritious wild greens |

### Berries and Fruits

| Species | Season | Key ID Features | Habitat | Look-alike Warning |
|---------|--------|----------------|---------|-------------------|
| Blackberry (Rubus spp.) | Summer | Thorny canes, compound leaves (3-5 leaflets), aggregate berries | Forest edges, hedgerows | None deadly; distinguish from similar Rubus species |
| Wild Blueberry (Vaccinium spp.) | Summer | Small shrub, oval leaves, bell-shaped flowers, blue berries with crown | Acidic soil, forests, heaths | Confirm crown on berry; avoid any blue berry without it |
| Rose Hips (Rosa spp.) | Fall | Round red/orange fruit on rose bush with thorns, familiar rose leaves | Hedgerows, forest edges | No deadly look-alikes |
| Elderberry (Sambucus nigra/canadensis) | Late Summer | Compound leaves, flat-topped white flower clusters, dark purple-black berry clusters | Moist areas, forest edges | IMPORTANT: Red elderberry (S. racemosa) may be toxic; only black/blue species |

### Nuts

| Species | Season | Key ID Features | Habitat |
|---------|--------|----------------|---------|
| Black Walnut (Juglans nigra) | Fall | Compound leaves (15-23 leaflets), green husk turning dark, distinctive chambered nut | Forests, yards (eastern N. America) |
| Hazelnut (Corylus spp.) | Fall | Rounded serrated leaves, nuts in papery husk clusters | Forest understory, hedgerows |
| Acorn (Quercus spp.) | Fall | Oak leaves (lobed), cap on nut | Forests (many regions) -- must leach tannins before eating |

## Mushroom Foraging Fundamentals

Mushroom identification is significantly more dangerous than plant foraging. Several deadly species closely resemble edible ones.

```
MUSHROOM SAFETY FRAMEWORK
============================

ABSOLUTE RULES:
  1. NEVER eat a mushroom you are not 100% certain about
  2. Get hands-on mentorship from an experienced mycologist
  3. Take a spore print for every new species
  4. Learn the deadly species in your region BEFORE learning edibles
  5. Carry a regional mushroom field guide on every outing

DEADLY MUSHROOMS TO RECOGNIZE (LEARN THESE FIRST):
  - Death Cap (Amanita phalloides): Most lethal mushroom worldwide
    Pale green/white cap, white gills, volva (cup) at base, ring on stem
  - Destroying Angel (Amanita bisporigera/virosa): Pure white
    White cap/gills/stem, volva at base, ring on stem
  - Deadly Galerina (Galerina marginata): On wood
    Brown cap, ring on stem, grows on decaying wood
    LOOKS SIMILAR to honey mushrooms and others

BEGINNER-FRIENDLY MUSHROOMS (distinctive, fewer look-alikes):
  - Chicken of the Woods (Laetiporus spp.)
    Bright orange/yellow shelf fungus, no gills, on trees
  - Morels (Morchella spp.)
    Honeycomb-patterned cap, hollow when sliced top to bottom
    WARNING: False morels exist -- true morels are completely hollow
  - Giant Puffball (Calvatia gigantea)
    Large white sphere, solid white interior (no gills/stem inside)
    If interior shows ANY color or structure, discard
  - Chanterelles (Cantharellus spp.)
    Egg-yolk orange, false gills (ridges), fruity smell
    Learn to distinguish from Jack O'Lantern (Omphalotus)
```

### Spore Print Instructions

```
HOW TO TAKE A SPORE PRINT
============================
1. Remove the stem from a mature cap
2. Place cap gill-side down on paper
   (use half white / half black paper to see any color)
3. Cover with a glass or bowl
4. Wait 4-12 hours
5. Carefully lift cap
6. Observe spore color: white, cream, pink, brown,
   purple-brown, black, etc.
7. Compare to field guide for your suspected species

This is a critical identification step for mushrooms.
Many deadly Amanitas have WHITE spore prints.
```

## Seasonal Foraging Calendar (Temperate Northern Hemisphere)

```
SEASONAL AVAILABILITY GUIDE
==============================

SPRING (March-May):
  Greens: Ramps/wild leeks, nettles, dandelion, chickweed,
          garlic mustard, violets, fiddlehead ferns
  Mushrooms: Morels, oyster mushrooms
  Other: Pine tips, birch sap

SUMMER (June-August):
  Berries: Wild strawberry, blueberry, blackberry, raspberry,
           mulberry, serviceberry
  Greens: Lamb's quarters, purslane, wood sorrel
  Flowers: Elderflower, day lily, clover
  Mushrooms: Chanterelles, chicken of the woods

AUTUMN (September-November):
  Fruits: Elderberry, rose hips, crabapple, persimmon, pawpaw
  Nuts: Acorn, black walnut, hazelnut, hickory
  Mushrooms: Hen of the woods (maitake), puffball, honey mushroom
  Roots: Burdock, chicory, Jerusalem artichoke

WINTER (December-February):
  Limited in most temperate regions
  Chickweed (mild winters), rose hips, pine needles (tea),
  birch bark (tea), stored/dried foraged goods
  Winter oyster mushrooms, turkey tail (medicinal use)

Note: Actual timing varies significantly by latitude,
altitude, and yearly weather patterns. Observe your
local environment -- the calendar is only a rough guide.
```

## Sustainable Harvesting Practices

```
ETHICAL FORAGING GUIDELINES
==============================

QUANTITY RULES:
  - Take no more than 10-20% of any stand
  - If fewer than 10 plants visible, do not harvest
  - Never take the first plant you find (it may be the only one)
  - Leave the largest/healthiest specimens to reproduce

TECHNIQUE:
  - Use clean, sharp scissors or knife (do not pull/uproot)
  - For greens: harvest outer leaves, leave the growing center
  - For berries: pick ripe ones only, leave unripe for later and wildlife
  - For mushrooms: cut at base rather than pulling (debated but cleaner)
  - For roots: only harvest abundant species; fill hole back in

HABITAT CARE:
  - Stay on trails when possible
  - Do not trample surrounding vegetation
  - Do not harvest from sensitive habitats (wetlands, rare plant communities)
  - Never harvest threatened or endangered species
  - Check your region's rare plant list before foraging

LEGAL COMPLIANCE:
  - National/federal parks: typically no foraging allowed
  - State/provincial parks: check specific regulations
  - Public lands (BLM, national forest): often allowed for personal use
  - Private land: ALWAYS get explicit permission
  - Urban areas: research local ordinances
  - Some species have specific harvest regulations (e.g., ramps in some states)
```

## Processing and Safety After Harvest

```
POST-HARVEST CHECKLIST
========================

TRANSPORT:
  [ ] Use paper bags or baskets (not plastic -- traps moisture)
  [ ] Keep species separated (avoid cross-contamination)
  [ ] Label each bag/container with species name and location
  [ ] Refrigerate within 2 hours of harvest

CLEANING:
  [ ] Inspect each item again for correct identification
  [ ] Remove insects, dirt, and damaged portions
  [ ] Wash thoroughly (especially greens -- parasites and contaminants)
  [ ] Some mushrooms: brush or wipe rather than soak (they absorb water)

PREPARATION NOTES:
  - Some species MUST be cooked (e.g., nettles, elderberries, morels, chicken of the woods)
  - Never eat wild mushrooms raw
  - Acorns require leaching (soak in water to remove tannins)
  - First time eating any species: small portion, wait 24 hours

PRESERVATION:
  - Dehydrating: Best for mushrooms, herbs, fruit leather
  - Freezing: Berries, blanched greens
  - Pickling: Ramps, fiddleheads, mushrooms
  - Tincturing: Medicinal herbs (educational/legal considerations)
```

## When to Seek Help

- **If you or someone ate an unidentified plant/mushroom:** Call Poison Control immediately (US: 1-800-222-1222) or your regional equivalent. Bring a sample of what was eaten.
- **Symptoms to watch for after eating wild foods:** Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, dizziness, confusion, excessive salivation, difficulty breathing. Symptoms may be delayed 6-24 hours for some mushroom toxins.
- **For identification help:** Local mycological societies, university extension services, foraging workshops, and experienced mentors are far more reliable than online identification.

**Region:** Pacific Northwest (WA/OR) | **Season:** September-October | **Level:** Absolute beginner

### The 3 Universal Safety Rules (Memorize Before Entering the Field)
1. NEVER eat anything you cannot identify with 100% certainty
2. Use at least 2 independent field guides to confirm every identification
3. When in doubt, leave it. No foraged food is worth a hospital visit.

### 5 Beginner-Friendly Species (Hard to Misidentify)

**1. Blackberries (Rubus armeniacus)**
- Season: Aug-Oct | Where: Roadsides, trails, disturbed ground -- EVERYWHERE in PNW
- ID: Thorny canes, compound leaves in 3s or 5s, unmistakable berry clusters
- Look-alikes: None dangerous. Salmonberries and thimbleberries are also edible.
- Harvest: Pick dark, fully ripe berries that pull off easily

**2. Chanterelle Mushrooms (Cantharellus cibarius)**
- Season: Sep-Nov | Where: Under Douglas fir and hemlock in mossy forests
- ID: Golden-orange, funnel-shaped, false gills (blunt ridges, not thin blades), smells faintly of apricots
- CRITICAL: Learn to distinguish from Jack O'Lantern (Omphalotus olearius) -- Jack O'Lanterns grow in clusters on wood, chanterelles grow singly from soil
- Harvest: Cut at base with knife, do not pull (preserves mycelium)

**3. Stinging Nettles (Urtica dioica)**
- Season: Spring best, but young growth available into fall
- ID: Opposite serrated leaves with visible stinging hairs on stem
- Safety: Wear gloves to harvest. Cooking neutralizes the sting completely.
- Use: Steam like spinach, or dry for tea

**4. Rosehips (Rosa spp.)**
- Season: Sep-Nov (after first frost is best) | Where: Edges of trails, open areas
- ID: Red-orange fruit on wild rose bushes (Rosa nutkana in PNW)
- Harvest: Pick when bright red and slightly soft. Very high in vitamin C.

**5. Dandelion Greens (Taraxacum officinale)**
- Season: Year-round | Where: Lawns, fields (harvest AWAY from pesticide-treated areas)
- ID: Unmistakable toothed leaves, hollow stem with white latex sap, yellow flower
- Harvest: Young leaves before flowering are least bitter

### Field Kit
- 2 field guides (PNW-specific)
- Mesh bags or paper bags (not plastic -- prevents sweating)
- Small knife for mushrooms
- Gloves for nettles
- Phone for GPS marking good spots
