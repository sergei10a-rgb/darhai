# New Pet Preparation

## When to Use

**Use this skill when:**
- A user is bringing home a puppy (under 6 months) or kitten (under 6 months) from a breeder, rescue, or shelter and wants to know what to buy, how to set up their home, and what to expect in the first week
- A user is adopting an adult dog or adult cat and needs a structured adjustment plan, supplies list, and home-safety overview specific to a pet with an unknown or known prior history
- A user is rehoming a dog or cat from a friend, family member, or online listing and needs to understand that even a seemingly "easy" transition requires proofing, scheduling, and a veterinary baseline exam
- A user asks what to buy before picking up their pet and needs a specific, age-calibrated, household-calibrated list rather than a generic "pet supplies" overview
- A user wants to know how to introduce a new dog or cat into a home that already has existing pets or young children
- A user asks how long adjustment takes for a newly adopted pet, what normal stress behaviors look like versus what needs veterinary attention, and how to tell the difference
- A user is adopting a senior dog or cat (7+ years) and needs modified expectations, a more comprehensive vet baseline protocol, and patience-focused scheduling advice

**Do NOT use when:**
- The user needs obedience training protocols, positive reinforcement technique breakdowns, or structured training programs -- use `dog-training-fundamentals`
- The user's pet is already home and they are asking about ongoing health monitoring, vaccination tracking, or chronic condition management -- use `pet-health-monitoring`
- The user wants detailed guidance on raw feeding, macronutrient ratios, homemade diets, or managing a pet's weight or allergies through food -- use `pet-nutrition-guide`
- The user's pet is a rabbit, guinea pig, hamster, ferret, bird, reptile, or fish -- these species have fundamentally different environmental, social, and physiological needs that are outside the scope of this skill
- The user is asking about a behavioral problem in a pet they have owned for more than a month -- adjustment period guidance does not apply; refer to species-specific behavioral resources
- The user is specifically asking about treating illness, wounds, or medical conditions in a new pet -- all health treatment decisions must come from a licensed veterinarian, not this skill

## Process

### Step 1: Gather the Pet Profile (Ask Before Producing a Plan)

Before generating any checklist or schedule, collect enough information to produce a calibrated output rather than a generic one. A kitten in a studio apartment with a toddler needs completely different guidance than an adult dog in a house with a yard.

- **Species:** Dog or cat -- this determines litter vs. elimination walks, scratching needs, containment strategy, and enrichment type
- **Age group:** Puppy/kitten (under 6 months), adolescent (6-18 months), adult (18 months to 7 years), senior (7+ years for dogs, 10+ years for cats) -- age governs vaccination urgency, energy level, chewing intensity, and adjustment expectations
- **Source:** Breeder, rescue/shelter, foster-to-adopt, owner rehome -- source determines what health documentation exists, whether the pet has had socialization, and what baseline vet work may already be done
- **Living situation:** House with fenced yard, house without fence, apartment above ground floor, apartment at ground level -- this governs elimination planning, exercise logistics, and safety-proofing priorities
- **Household composition:** Other pets (species and sex matter), children (with ages), adults who will interact with the pet regularly
- **Lifestyle:** Work schedule (hours at home vs. away), whether someone will be home during the day, budget for initial setup (not to suggest brands, but to flag whether cost-effective vs. premium options are relevant)

If the user has already provided this information conversationally, extract it without asking again. Fill in defaults where information is missing and state the assumptions clearly in the output.

### Step 2: Build the Supplies Checklist with Age and Household Calibration

Do not produce a flat list of generic items. Every item should be specified with size guidance, quantity, material preference, and reasoning. Distinguish between pre-arrival essentials and first-month additions.

**Dogs -- Pre-Arrival Essentials:**

- **Crate:** Wire crates with a divider panel are preferred for puppies because they allow you to start small (just enough room to stand, turn, lie down) and expand as the dog grows. A crate too large for a puppy creates space the dog will use as a bathroom corner. Adult dogs: size to the dog, not the breed standard -- the dog should stand without hunching and turn without bumping walls. Plastic airline crates work for dogs who prefer more enclosed dens.
- **Collar:** Flat buckle collar in nylon or leather, fitted so exactly two fingers slide snugly between the collar and the neck. For puppies, buy a small adjustable collar -- you will replace it within 8-12 weeks as they grow. Avoid choke chains, prong collars, and head halters in the first week -- those are training tools requiring professional guidance.
- **ID tag:** Engrave the dog's name and your mobile phone number at minimum. Have this ready before Day 1 -- more dogs escape during the first week in a new home than at any other time because they are disoriented and not yet bonded.
- **Leash:** A 6-foot flat leash in nylon or leather is the standard for controlled walking. Retractable leashes teach dogs to pull (the mechanism rewards forward pressure) and are not appropriate for a dog learning leash manners.
- **Bowls:** Stainless steel is preferred over plastic (plastic scratches and harbors bacteria; some dogs develop contact dermatitis from plastic bowls) and over ceramic (chips can harbor bacteria and lead paint in imported ceramics is a documented risk). Weighted base prevents tipping. Two bowls minimum: one for food, one for water.
- **Food:** Ask the source (breeder, rescue, foster) exactly what brand and formula the pet is eating. Buy that exact food for the first 10-14 days regardless of your long-term feeding plan. Transition to a new food over 7-10 days: Days 1-3: 75% old / 25% new; Days 4-6: 50/50; Days 7-9: 25% old / 75% new; Day 10+: new food only. GI upset (vomiting, diarrhea) in the first week is often diet-transition related, not illness.
- **Enzymatic cleaner:** This is not optional for puppies or any pet whose housetraining is uncertain. Regular household cleaners neutralize odor for humans but leave the specific protein compounds (uric acid crystals) that dogs and cats can detect. Uric acid crystals are not water-soluble and require enzymatic breakdown. An enzymatic cleaner must soak the area, not just be spritzed on top, and must air-dry to complete the chemical process.
- **Crate pad or bedding:** Machine-washable. For puppies, old towels from the source (if the rescue or breeder will provide them) carry familiar scent and reduce first-night distress significantly.
- **Chew toys (puppies):** Puppies from 8-20 weeks are in an intense teething phase -- the urge to chew is neurological, not misbehavior. Provide at minimum: one rubber teething toy (freezable rubber toys filled with kibble or wet food provide both chewing and cooling relief for inflamed gums), one rope toy, one stuffable hollow toy. Rotate every 3-4 days to maintain novelty.
- **Baby gates:** Two minimum. One for stairs (puppies and kittens should not use stairs freely until coordination is more developed -- around 12-16 weeks), one for a room to create a managed zone during early house training.
- **Poop bags:** A minimum of 60 bags before arrival for a puppy. Adult dogs: 30. Calculate roughly 2-3 bags per walk, 2-4 walks per day.

**Cats -- Pre-Arrival Essentials:**

- **Litter boxes:** The universal rule is one box per cat plus one additional. A single cat household needs two boxes. Place them in different locations -- not stacked side by side -- because a cat who is ambushed at one box needs access to another. Size matters: the box should be 1.5 times the length of the cat. Most commercially sold boxes are undersized for adult cats.
- **Litter type:** Unscented, clumping clay litter is the most accepted substrate across the widest range of cats. Scented litters are designed for human preference, not feline, and many cats will reject them. Do not switch litter types in the first month. Ask the source what litter the cat was using and replicate it.
- **Litter box placement:** Quiet, low-traffic, accessible locations. Not next to the washing machine (noise) or the food bowls (cats do not eliminate where they eat -- this is instinctive). Not in a closet with a swinging door that could block access.
- **Carrier:** Hard-shell or soft-sided carrier with a top-loading option -- top-loading carriers allow you to lower a reluctant cat in rather than forcing them in headfirst, which reduces carrier aversion dramatically. Leave the carrier in the safe room from Day 1 with a familiar-smelling blanket inside. The carrier should be a neutral or positive space, not something that only appears before a vet visit.
- **Scratching surfaces:** Provide both vertical and horizontal options before arrival. Cats scratch to deposit scent from paw glands, to shed claw sheaths, and to stretch. A cat that arrives without appropriate scratching outlets will find its own. Vertical posts must be tall enough for the cat to fully extend (minimum 28-32 inches for an adult cat). Place at least one scratching post near each sleeping area -- cats scratch upon waking.
- **Water:** Cats are evolutionarily adapted to meet hydration needs through prey rather than drinking. Many cats have low thirst drives and are chronically mildly dehydrated on dry food diets. Wide, shallow bowls prevent whisker contact with the bowl edge (whisker fatigue). Water fountains increase voluntary water intake in most cats and are worth considering in the first month, especially for cats on dry food diets.
- **Cat tree or perches:** Height is security for cats. A cat that cannot get above ground level in a stressful situation has no escape route by its own behavioral logic. A cat tree placed in the main living area provides scratching, sleeping, climbing, and a vantage point -- four needs in one piece of furniture.

### Step 3: Conduct Room-by-Room Home Safety Assessment

Safety-proofing is not optional and must be completed before the pet arrives. Walk through the following categories methodically.

**Toxins -- most common and most dangerous:**

Dogs are indiscriminate eaters and the risk of ingestion is higher; cats are generally more selective but can be poisoned by grooming toxins off their coat or paws. Know the difference:

| Toxin | Toxic to Dogs | Toxic to Cats | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chocolate (theobromine) | Yes -- dose-dependent; dark chocolate and baking chocolate most dangerous | Lower risk (cats rarely eat sweets) | As little as 1 oz dark chocolate per pound of body weight can cause seizures in dogs |
| Xylitol | Yes -- causes severe hypoglycemia and liver failure even in small amounts | Lower documented risk | Found in sugar-free gum, some peanut butter brands, dental products, vitamins, and many baked goods |
| Grapes and raisins | Yes -- even a single grape has caused renal failure in some dogs; mechanism unknown | Unknown; avoid | No safe dose has been established -- any ingestion warrants an emergency vet call |
| Onion, garlic, leeks | Yes -- causes hemolytic anemia; cooked and powdered forms are more concentrated than raw | Yes -- same mechanism, even higher sensitivity per body weight | Garlic powder in baby food used as a flavor enhancer is a documented poisoning source for cats |
| Lilies (all species) | Low risk | Highly toxic -- even pollen on fur groomed off can cause acute kidney failure | Easter lily, tiger lily, day lily, Asiatic lily: all fatal to cats. True lilies (not peace lily or calla lily, which cause irritation but are not acutely fatal) |
| Sago palm | Yes -- all parts, especially seeds; causes liver failure | Yes | Increasingly common as a houseplant and in landscaping in warm climates |
| Ibuprofen, acetaminophen, naproxen | Yes -- ibuprofen and naproxen cause GI ulcers and renal failure | Yes -- acetaminophen is rapidly fatal to cats even in a single pediatric dose | Keep all human medications in locked or child-proofed cabinets, not on counters |
| Rodenticides (rat/mouse poison) | Yes -- anticoagulant and neurotoxic types both dangerous | Yes | Second-generation anticoagulants (brodifacoum) accumulate in prey -- a cat that eats a poisoned mouse can be poisoned secondarily |

**Electrical and physical hazards:**
- Tuck or encase electrical cords in cord covers or cord organizers -- puppies and kittens chew cords and electrocution is a documented cause of death in young pets
- Secure recliners and rocking chairs (small pets can be crushed in the mechanisms -- verify before sitting or rocking)
- Remove rubber-backed rugs from wet areas (puppy paws slip and falls can injure young joints)
- Check that window screens are fastened securely -- not magnetically attached decorative screens but mechanically fastened frames

**Yard and exterior:**
- Walk the fence line looking for gaps larger than 3 inches at ground level (a 10-week puppy can compress its body and squeeze through openings that seem too small)
- Check gate latches -- install a secondary latch or carabiner clip, because dogs learn to push gate latches open by accident and then deliberately
- Identify and remove any mushrooms growing in the yard before the puppy's first outdoor time -- wild mushroom toxicity is highly variable and some species are rapidly fatal

### Step 4: Build the First-Week Schedule with Time-of-Day Specificity

The schedule must account for the age of the pet because developmental and physiological needs differ dramatically.

**Puppy elimination physiology baseline:** A puppy can approximately hold its bladder for one hour per month of age plus one (a 2-month-old puppy = approximately 3-hour maximum, but awake and active puppies need to go out more frequently than this ceiling suggests -- every 30-45 minutes when awake and playing). The maximum should never be relied on as a target -- it is the outer limit, not the goal.

**Kitten safe-room protocol:** A new kitten or cat should start in a single room with all resources (food, water, litter, scratching, sleeping, hiding options) and be allowed to expand access to the rest of the home only after showing confident behaviors: eating consistently, using the litter box reliably, approaching humans voluntarily, and exploring the room with tail up rather than crouched and low. This process takes 1-5 days for confident kittens and 1-4 weeks for shy or previously unsocialized kittens. Forcing access to more space before the cat is ready increases chronic stress and is a leading cause of inappropriate elimination problems.

**The 3-3-3 Rule:** A widely used framework in shelter and rescue communities. The numbers are approximations, not deadlines:
- First 3 days: the pet is overwhelmed, may not eat well, may hide, may not show its real personality. This is not a sign of a problem -- it is normal decompression.
- First 3 weeks: the pet begins learning the routine, showing more personality, testing boundaries. This is when behavioral patterns become more predictable.
- First 3 months: the pet feels genuinely at home. Some rescue pets, especially those with trauma histories, take longer at each stage.

**Day 1 specific protocols:**

Dogs:
- Arrive home during a calm, low-stimulus time. Not on a day when multiple family members or friends are gathering to "meet the puppy" -- this is highly stressful for an already-disoriented animal
- The first step after arriving is a leashed walk to the designated elimination area in the yard -- not inside first. The car ride is stressful and the bladder is likely full
- Introduce the inside of the house room by room, starting with one room, ideally the room where the crate will be located
- Introduce the crate with the door open, treats scattered inside, and a worn piece of your clothing (your scent reduces anxiety)

Cats:
- Carry the carrier directly to the prepared safe room and set it on the floor. Open the carrier door. Do not reach inside to remove the cat -- let the cat emerge on its own timeline
- Leave the room and give the cat 30-60 minutes of complete quiet before returning
- When returning, sit or lie on the floor at the cat's level -- do not approach; let the cat approach you

### Step 5: Plan and Prepare for the First Veterinary Visit

This visit is not optional and is not something to schedule "when you have time." The first vet visit serves as a health baseline and should occur within 5-7 days of arrival for all newly adopted pets.

**What to bring:**
- All adoption paperwork, surrender forms, and vaccination records
- A fresh fecal sample collected within 4-6 hours of the visit (seal in a zip-lock bag) -- this allows the vet to run a fecal flotation to check for intestinal parasites, which are extremely common in shelter and rescue animals regardless of health certificates
- A list of questions prepared in advance (use the checklist below)

**Vaccination schedule expectations by age:**
- Puppies under 16 weeks: core vaccines (distemper-parvovirus-adenovirus combination, commonly abbreviated as DHPP or DA2PP) are given every 3-4 weeks starting at 6-8 weeks of age, with the final booster at 14-16 weeks. Rabies: given at 12-16 weeks depending on state law. Until the puppy series is complete, avoid high-traffic dog areas (dog parks, pet store floors, unknown dogs) because immunity is not complete.
- Kittens under 16 weeks: core vaccines (FVRCP -- rhinotracheitis, calicivirus, panleukopenia) given every 3-4 weeks starting at 6-8 weeks. Rabies at 12-16 weeks. Feline leukemia (FeLV) vaccine for kittens with any outdoor access or exposure risk.
- Adult adoptees with unknown vaccine history: typically receive a single booster of core vaccines at the first visit, with boosters timed from that date forward
- Adult adoptees with documented vaccine history: review records with the vet to confirm timing of next due boosters

**Parasite prevention:**
- Heartworm (dogs): monthly preventatives require the dog to be confirmed heartworm-negative before starting. Dogs over 7 months who haven't been on prevention should be tested before being given preventatives because giving prevention to a heartworm-positive dog can cause a reaction.
- Flea and tick: topical or oral prevention should be discussed at the first visit. Most shelter animals have been treated, but the timing and product should be confirmed to avoid double-dosing within a safe interval.
- Intestinal parasites: roundworms and hookworms are so common in puppies and kittens that many veterinarians treat presumptively with pyrantel even before fecal results return. Giardia requires specific treatment and is not covered by standard dewormers.

**Spay/neuter timing:** Recommendations have evolved. Traditional guidance was 6 months for both species, but breed-specific research (particularly for large and giant breed dogs) now often recommends waiting until 12-24 months to allow growth plate closure. Discuss the specific pet's breed and size at the first vet visit rather than assuming a universal timeline.

### Step 6: Introduce the Pet to Other Household Members and Pets

Introductions are where many first-week plans break down. Rushed introductions cause fights, injuries, and long-term behavioral damage to inter-household relationships that can take months or years to repair.

**Children (under 8 years):**
- Supervise all interactions without exception for the first 4 weeks minimum
- Teach the child the two-second rule before any new pet: count two seconds before reaching toward the animal to allow the animal to sniff and orient
- Create a pet-only safe space (crate, cat tree, gated room) that the child is explicitly taught not to enter or disturb -- this gives the pet a reliable escape route and prevents the animal from feeling trapped, which is the most common precursor to defensive bites and scratches
- Puppies use their mouth to explore and play -- they do not know biting hurts. Children cannot reliably interpret the difference between a playing puppy nip and an aggressive bite, so adult supervision is not just recommended but physiologically necessary for the child's safety

**Dog to dog introductions:**
- First meeting: neutral territory only (a park, a neighbor's yard, anywhere that is not the home yard which the resident dog has scented-marked as territory)
- Both dogs on leash, both handlers present -- do not let dogs run together off-leash until they have had multiple successful on-leash interactions
- Read body language: loose wiggly posture, play bow, brief sniffing and disengaging = positive. Stiff posture, whale eye (whites of eyes visible), raised hackles, hard stare = separate immediately without drama and try again later
- Bring the dogs home together after the successful neutral meeting rather than introducing a new dog into a yard where the resident dog is already set up
- Feed separately for at minimum 2 weeks -- resource guarding between dogs who do not yet have an established relationship is a leading cause of bites

**Cat to cat introductions:**
- Day 1-3: complete separation. New cat in safe room. Resident cat has full house. Swap bedding between the two cats so scent exchange happens before visual contact.
- Day 4-7: feed both cats on opposite sides of the closed safe room door. This creates a positive association with the other cat's scent (food = good feeling = that smell is okay). Gradually move bowls closer to the door over several days.
- Days 7-14: crack the door with a door stopper allowing visual contact but not physical contact. Watch for hissing and posturing -- this is normal and does not mean the introduction has failed. It means the cats are still negotiating.
- Full access: only when both cats are eating normally, both are using their litter boxes, neither is showing sustained stress behaviors. This may take 2-6 weeks. Rushing this process is the leading cause of chronic inter-cat conflict in multi-cat households.

**Dog to cat introductions:**
- The cat must always have access to elevated space or a room the dog cannot enter. This is non-negotiable. A cat with no escape from a dog will eventually be forced into a defensive posture that can injure both animals.
- The dog must be behind a baby gate or on leash for all initial interactions -- never loose and uncontrolled around a new cat
- Reward the dog heavily for looking away from the cat (this is called an "auto-watch" or "look at that" incompatible behavior protocol -- it creates a positive association with the cat's presence while preventing fixation)
- Never leave them unsupervised until the dog is completely reliable with the cat in an observed context over at least 2-3 weeks

### Step 7: Identify Normal Stress Behaviors vs. Veterinary Red Flags

New owners frequently panic over normal adjustment behaviors and sometimes miss genuine medical emergencies. Provide clear criteria for each.

**Normal adjustment behaviors (do not require veterinary contact):**
- Not eating for 24-48 hours on arrival (stress suppresses appetite in dogs and cats -- longer than 48 hours without eating warrants a vet call)
- Hiding, especially in cats -- a cat that hides for the first 1-3 days is decompressing normally
- Loose stool for 2-3 days (diet transition plus stress causes GI disruption -- beyond 3 days or if there is blood, consult a vet)
- Whining or crying at night in a crate (puppy) for the first 3-5 nights -- separation distress is normal as the puppy adjusts to sleeping alone for the first time
- Not using the litter box for 12-24 hours in a new cat (especially if the cat is hiding) -- ensure the box is accessible and they know where it is; beyond 24 hours warrants attention

**Require veterinary attention -- call or visit same day:**
- Vomiting more than twice in 24 hours, or any vomiting accompanied by lethargy
- Diarrhea lasting more than 48 hours, or any diarrhea with blood (even a streak)
- Labored breathing, open-mouth breathing (in cats, any open-mouth breathing is an emergency)
- Straining to urinate or producing no urine (especially cats -- urinary blockage can be fatal within 24-48 hours)
- Neurological symptoms: trembling, circling, falling over, seizure activity
- Swollen abdomen (in dogs: bloat/GDV is a surgical emergency)
- Not eating for more than 48 hours in dogs, 24-36 hours in cats (cats are at particular risk for hepatic lipidosis if they stop eating for even 48-72 hours)
- Any suspected ingestion of a toxin -- call the ASPCA Poison Control or your emergency vet immediately, do not wait for symptoms

## Rules

1. **Always collect pet age, species, source, and household composition before generating the plan.** A flat checklist produced without this information will be generic and may contain irrelevant or even incorrect guidance (e.g., recommending off-leash socialization for a puppy whose vaccination series is incomplete).

2. **Always recommend continuing the source's food for a minimum of 10-14 days before transitioning.** GI upset in the first week is one of the most common triggers for panicked vet calls and emergency visits -- most are caused by abrupt diet changes, not illness. Provide the specific 7-10 day transition ratio (75/25, 50/50, 25/75, 100% new).

3. **Always recommend a fecal parasite exam at the first vet visit.** Intestinal parasites (roundworms, hookworms, giardia, coccidia) are present in a substantial proportion of rescue and shelter animals regardless of health certificates. Many are zoonotic (transmissible to humans), which makes this especially important in households with children.

4. **Never recommend a specific vaccination schedule without directing the user to confirm with their veterinarian.** Schedules vary by region (leptospirosis vaccine, for example, is strongly recommended in high-risk geographic areas and not universally given elsewhere), by individual health history, and by current protocols. Present the standard schedule as a baseline expectation, not a prescription.

5. **Always include the cats-need-escape-routes rule in any cat guidance.** Cats who feel cornered -- by a dog, a child, another cat, or an overwhelming space -- resort to defensive aggression or chronic stress. Every instruction for a cat in a multi-pet or child household must include the physical setup of at least one elevated or gated space the cat can access and other animals cannot.

6. **Never suggest introducing two dogs on the home resident dog's territory for their first meeting.** Territorial aggression during first dog-to-dog meetings at home is predictable and preventable -- neutral territory first is not a preference, it is the correct protocol.

7. **Always include the litter box minimum (one per cat plus one) and placement guidance (not adjacent to food, not in high-traffic/noisy areas).** Litter box avoidance is the leading reason cats are surrendered to shelters. Most litter box problems are environmental setup failures that are entirely preventable.

8. **Flag that cats should never go more than 24-36 hours without eating** -- not even during the adjustment period. Hepatic lipidosis (fatty liver disease) can develop within 48-72 hours of food refusal in cats, especially overweight cats, and it is a serious, expensive condition. If a cat is not eating within 36 hours of arrival, that is a veterinary consultation trigger, not a waiting game.

9. **Always distinguish between adjustment-period stress behaviors and medical emergencies.** Provide both the "normal, wait and watch" category and the "call the vet today" and "go to emergency care now" categories. New pet owners are statistically likely to be first-time owners who cannot accurately calibrate urgency. Under-informing them about red flags has real consequences.

10. **Never recommend crate use as punishment.** A crate that is used as a "time out" becomes a negative space, which undermines its function as a management tool for housetraining and as a safe den for the dog. All crate introduction guidance must be framed as gradual, positive, and treat-reinforced.

11. **Always recommend baby gates over free-roaming access for puppies in the first week,** regardless of how calm the puppy seems. House training is not about trust -- it is about building a reliable conditioned habit. Free-roaming access before the habit is established almost always results in accidents in hidden corners that are not found and cleaned promptly, which creates odor-based repeat elimination sites that are difficult to remediate.

12. **Always include the toxin table for the specific species,** not a combined list for both. Dogs and cats have meaningfully different toxicity profiles. Lilies are a life-threatening emergency for cats and a non-event for dogs. Xylitol is catastrophic for dogs and has minimal documented risk for cats. Conflating them with a generic "toxic foods" list obscures the level of urgency appropriately.

## Edge Cases

### Multi-Pet Household with an Existing Cat Introducing a New Dog (Adult Dog)

This is one of the highest-conflict introduction scenarios because the dynamic is reversed from what the dog may have learned previously. Key differentiators from the standard dog-to-dog or cat-to-cat protocol:

- The existing cat has ownership of the entire territory. The new dog is the intrusion. The cat's ability to access its normal resting spots, litter boxes, and food stations without the dog present must be maintained throughout the introduction period -- not just in a designated room but across the whole home.
- Install a baby gate with a cat door, or use a full-height gate with a shelf or step that the cat can jump over but the dog cannot. This allows the cat to move freely through the house while giving it the option to physically separate from the dog on demand.
- A dog with high prey drive is not necessarily disqualified from living with cats, but the introduction timeline is longer (months, not weeks) and should involve professional guidance from a certified applied animal behaviorist or veterinary behaviorist if the dog fixates, lunges, or cannot be redirected from staring at the cat.
- Monitor the cat's litter box use and food intake daily during introductions. A cat who stops eating or using the litter box is under intolerable stress levels, and the introduction is progressing too quickly regardless of what the behavior looks like from the outside.

### Apartment Dwelling with a Puppy and No Yard Access

Elimination planning is the primary challenge. The standard outdoor-based house training protocol assumes yard access. In a multi-floor apartment without private outdoor space, modify as follows:

- Identify the closest outdoor grass or gravel surface (ideally within a 1-2 minute walk) and plan all elimination trips there. Until the puppy's vaccination series is complete, avoid areas with unknown dog traffic (busy dog parks, highly trafficked sidewalks in urban areas) because parvovirus survives in soil and grass for months to years.
- For very young puppies (8-12 weeks), nighttime trips outdoors require putting on a leash and going all the way to the elimination surface. This is genuinely inconvenient and new owners must be prepared for it. Puppy pads used overnight are a reasonable management tool during this window, but the goal is always to transition to outdoor elimination before the puppy develops a surface preference for pads inside.
- Consider a balcony potty station (a sod patch or artificial turf with a collection tray) for overnight emergencies -- this is a genuine tool used by urban dog owners, not a permanent solution.
- Elevator buildings: the lobby and elevator are high-contamination surfaces for unvaccinated puppies. Carry the puppy until outside if possible, or keep it off the floor in these areas.

### Feral or Poorly-Socialized Kitten (Under-Socialized, 8-16 Weeks)

A kitten described as "shy" or "feral" from a rescue context is not the same as a hand-raised kitten who is nervous from transportation stress. Under-socialized kittens have a developmental window for socialization that closes around 7-9 weeks. Kittens adopted after this window with insufficient human contact require a different approach:

- Expect the safe-room phase to take 2-6 weeks rather than 1-5 days. Do not rush.
- Do not attempt to force handling. Sit in the room, move slowly, speak quietly, let the kitten approach on its own timeline. Wand toys are useful because they allow interactive engagement at a distance before the kitten is comfortable with direct contact.
- Scruffing and forced restraint for grooming or examination in the first weeks will set back progress significantly -- the stress response from being forcibly held can reset the trust-building clock.
- If the kitten is still completely non-interactive (no approach, no play, no eye contact from across the room) after 4 weeks, a veterinary check is warranted to rule out pain or illness masquerading as fear, and a referral to a cat behavior specialist may be appropriate.

### Senior Rescue Pet (Dog or Cat, 7+ Years)

Senior adopted animals are frequently given up for economic reasons, the death of an owner, or life changes -- not because they are difficult. Their preparation needs differ from younger animals in several concrete ways:

- **Veterinary baseline is more comprehensive:** The first vet visit for a senior pet should include a full blood panel (CBC, chemistry, thyroid for cats), urinalysis, blood pressure measurement, dental assessment, and joint evaluation. Budget $200-500 for this visit above a standard exam cost.
- **Adjustment period expectations are different in both directions:** Senior dogs and cats are often less reactive than young animals -- they have established personalities and habits that make adjustment shorter in some ways. However, they may also have acquired behaviors (house training lapses, resource guarding) that require patience to understand and modify.
- **Pain must be ruled out before attributing behavior to personality.** A senior dog who growls when approached on his bed, or a senior cat who stops jumping to his usual perch, may be experiencing arthritis pain. This is behavior that looks like "personality" but is actually a medical condition with treatment options.
- **Diet:** Senior pets should be fed age-appropriate formulas as confirmed by the veterinarian. Kidney disease (common in older cats), liver disease, and obesity are conditions that may already be present and that dietary choice significantly affects.

### Household with an Infant or Non-Walking Child (Under 18 Months)

The risk calculus changes when the child cannot yet understand instructions or move away from an animal independently:

- Any dog of any size must be treated as a supervision requirement at all times when the infant is in the same room. This is not a breed statement -- it is a developmental statement about the infant's inability to read or respond to animal body language.
- A crate for the dog serves a critical dual-purpose safety function in this household: it gives the dog a reliable retreat and it physically separates dog and infant during unsupervised moments (during a parent's shower, a phone call, etc.).
- Never place an infant on the floor in the same room as a new dog without an adult within arm's reach. Dogs at floor level with a small infant can knock, mouth, or step on an infant faster than a parent can cross a room.
- Cats and infants: the "cat stealing breath from a baby" myth is false, but cats may seek warmth and curl up near an infant's face during sleep in a crib. Keep the nursery door closed or use a crib net if the cat has access to the room.

### Newly Adopted Pet Who Was the Survivor of Animal Hoarding or Neglect

These pets require modified preparation because their behavioral baselines are not predictable from breed or age:

- Expect significant learned helplessness: animals from hoarding situations may show very little response to stimuli (no startle response, no play behavior, no appetite response to food) in the first days or even weeks. This is not a behavioral problem -- it is an adaptive shutdown response to chronic stress.
- Enrichment should be minimal and slow in the first two weeks. Overloading these animals with toys, social contact, and novel stimuli prolongs the recovery timeline. Predictability and routine are the most important therapeutic inputs.
- Veterinary care is urgent, not just recommended within a week. Animals from hoarding situations frequently have untreated infections, parasitic loads, dental disease, and malnutrition. A same-day or next-day vet visit is appropriate, not just within 5-7 days.
- Coordinate with the rescue organization if possible -- they may have specific behavioral notes, handling guidelines, or the name of a behavioral specialist who has already assessed the animal.

### Pre-Arrival Supplies Checklist

#### Essentials (Have Before Saturday)

**For the kitten's safe room:**
- [ ] **Litter box -- small, uncovered:** Standard adult boxes are too large for an 8-week kitten. Use a low-sided storage container or a small box with sides no higher than 3 inches so the kitten can climb in without difficulty. Fill with the same unscented clumping litter the foster home was using -- ask the rescue on pickup day.
- [ ] **Litter scoop**
- [ ] **Small stainless steel food bowl:** Wide and shallow -- kitten's whiskers should not contact the bowl sides during eating. Plastic bowls are not recommended (bacteria, possible contact dermatitis).
- [ ] **Stainless steel water bowl:** Wide and shallow. Separate from the food bowl -- cats in the wild do not drink near their prey, and some cats have aversion to drinking near their food station.
- [ ] **Kitten food -- same formula as foster home:** Contact the rescue before Saturday. Ask exactly what brand and formula. Buy enough for 2 weeks. At 8 weeks, a kitten needs calorie-dense, protein-rich kitten formula (not "all life stages" as a substitute -- kitten formula meets higher growth-phase nutritional requirements).
- [ ] **Hard-shell or soft-sided carrier with top-loading access:** Leave this in the safe room from Day 1 with a small piece of fleece or a worn t-shirt inside. The carrier must become a neutral space, not just the box that predicts vet visits.
- [ ] **Kitten-sized scratching post:** At least one vertical post and one horizontal pad. Place vertical post near the sleeping area -- kittens scratch upon waking. 8-week kittens are already beginning to develop scratching preferences; redirect from furniture now.
- [ ] **Hiding options:** A cardboard box on its side with a towel inside, a covered cat bed, or a paper bag with handles removed. An 8-week kitten in a new environment needs to be able to hide -- a kitten with no hiding option will experience higher acute stress. This is especially important given the 3-year-old in the household.
- [ ] **Interactive wand toy:** One wand toy for play sessions. Do not leave wand toys out unsupervised -- the string is a strangulation and ingestion hazard. All string, ribbon, and wand toys must be stored in a closed drawer or cabinet when you are not actively playing.
- [ ] **Solo toys:** 3-4 small toys -- crinkle balls, small stuffed mice, foil balls. These are safe for unsupervised play.
- [ ] **Enzymatic cleaner:** Even though kittens have a strong litter box instinct, accidents happen during transitions and stress. Have enzymatic cleaner stocked before Saturday.
- [ ] **Safe room gate or door plan:** Designate a room (bedroom, office, or bathroom if large enough) that can be closed or gated to serve as the kitten's exclusive space for Days 1-7+. The existing cat and the 3-year-old must not have access to this room during the initial phase.

**For the resident cat (new things he needs before kitten arrives):**
- [ ] **Second litter box in a new location:** You will need at least 3 total boxes for a 2-cat household (one per cat plus one). Add one now, in a location that the new kitten will not access during the safe-room phase, so the resident cat has options if he feels his current box is contaminated by kitten scent.
- [ ] **Feliway MultiCat diffuser:** Plug this in to the main living areas 3-5 days before the kitten arrives. Feliway is a synthetic analog of the feline facial pheromone associated with calm and familiarity -- clinical studies support moderate effectiveness in reducing inter-cat tension during introductions. This is a legitimate tool used by veterinary behaviorists, not a gimmick.

#### First-Month Additions (Acquire After Arrival)
- [ ] **Cat tree (medium height):** After the kitten has expanded access to more of the house, a cat tree in the main living area gives both cats vertical territory and reduces competition for single resting spots on the floor.
- [ ] **Puzzle feeders:** Introduce at 10-12 weeks when fine motor coordination allows. Both cats benefit from feeding enrichment.
- [ ] **Nail clippers:** Begin weekly nail trims starting at 8-10 weeks so the kitten habituates to handling before it is a fight.
- [ ] **Cat-specific toothbrush and enzyme toothpaste:** Start dental hygiene as early as 8-10 weeks -- this is the single most effective time to establish acceptance. Use only cat-specific enzymatic toothpaste (human toothpaste contains xylitol and fluoride, both toxic to cats).

### Home Safety Checklist

#### Immediate Hazards to Address Before Saturday

**Kitchen:**
- [ ] Secure trash can with a latching lid -- kittens and young cats tip open-top trash cans
- [ ] Move all onion, garlic, and their powdered forms to enclosed cabinets or high shelves
- [ ] Verify the peanut butter in the house does not contain xylitol (check the ingredient label -- some natural brands use it)
- [ ] Ensure rubber bands, twist ties, and bag clips are not left on counters or tables (high ingestion risk)

**Bathroom:**
- [ ] Toilet lids down -- an 8-week kitten can fall into a toilet and cannot climb out. This is a drowning risk.
- [ ] All medications, vitamins, supplements, and dental products in a closed cabinet. Acetaminophen (Tylenol) is rapidly fatal to cats -- even a single regular-strength tablet can kill an adult cat; a fragment is lethal to a kitten.
- [ ] Hair elastics, bobby pins, and hair ties off all surfaces and into a drawer -- one of the most common small-object foreign body ingestions in cats

**Living areas:**
- [ ] Check all window screens for secure fastening -- kittens can push through unlatched screens
- [ ] Secure or encase all electrical cords (lamp cords, phone chargers, TV cabling) -- 8-week kittens chew cords aggressively
- [ ] Remove lilies immediately if any are present in the household. This is the most important safety action for a home with a cat. All lily species -- Easter lily, tiger lily, Asiatic lily, day lily -- cause rapid and often irreversible acute kidney failure in cats. Even pollen transferred to fur and groomed off is toxic. Remove the plant entirely, dispose of it outside, and wipe all surfaces where pollen may have settled.
- [ ] Move pothos, philodendron, dieffenbachia, and peace lily plants out of reach or out of the house (these cause oral irritation and GI symptoms rather than acute organ failure, but are still toxic)
- [ ] Check under and behind appliances and furniture -- an 8-week kitten can fit into gaps as small as 1.5 inches. Temporarily block access behind the washer, dryer, refrigerator, and oven with towels or foam insulation until the kitten is large enough not to fit.
- [ ] Recliner and rocking chairs: post a note on both to check before sitting and to move slowly. Kittens are killed in recliner mechanisms with startling regularity.
