# Pet Behaviorist
## When to Use

**Use this skill when:**
- User asks about pet behaviorist
- User needs guidance on pet behaviorist topics
- User wants a structured approach to pet behaviorist

**Do NOT use when:**
- Request requires professional consultation beyond educational guidance

## Questions to Ask the User First

1. **What type of pet?** (Dog / Cat / Both / Other)
2. **What breed and age is your pet?**
3. **What behavioral concern are you experiencing?** (Anxiety / Aggression / Excessive barking/meowing / Destructiveness / House soiling / Fear / Compulsive behaviors / Multi-pet conflict)
4. **When did the behavior start?** (Always / Recently / After a specific event)
5. **Has your pet been examined by a vet recently?** (Rule out medical causes)
6. **What is your pet's daily routine?** (Exercise, feeding, alone time)
7. **What have you already tried?** (Training methods, medications, etc.)
8. **Is anyone in the household at risk?** (Children, elderly, other pets)
9. **What is your pet's history?** (Rescue, breeder, previous homes, known trauma)

## Reading Dog Body Language

### Emotional State Indicators
```
RELAXED / HAPPY:
- Soft, squinty eyes
- Mouth slightly open, tongue may be out
- Ears in natural position
- Tail at mid-level with broad wag
- Loose, wiggly body
- Play bow (front down, rear up)
- Soft facial muscles
ANXIOUS / STRESSED:
- Whale eye (showing whites of eyes)
- Lip licking (when not eating)
- Yawning (when not tired)
- Panting (when not hot or exercised)
- Ears pinned back
- Tail tucked between legs
- Pacing or inability to settle
- Shaking/trembling
- Drooling excessively
- Avoiding eye contact
- Turning head away
FEARFUL:
- Body low to ground / crouching
- Tail tucked tightly
- Ears flat against head
- Trembling
- Hiding behind owner or objects
- Freezing completely still
- Submissive urination
- Attempting to flee
AGGRESSIVE (Warning Signs - Escalation Order):
1. Stiffening of body (freeze)
2. Hard stare / fixed gaze
3. Raised hackles (piloerection)
4. Lips pulled back, showing teeth
5. Low, rumbling growl
6. Snapping (air bite)
7. Bite
communication that prevents escalation to biting.
```

### Dog Calming Signals

| Signal | Meaning | Context |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Lip licking | Stress, appeasement | Meeting new dogs/people |
| Yawning | Displacement behavior | Uncomfortable situations |
| Turning head away | "I mean no harm" | Direct approaches |
| Sniffing ground | Deflection, calming | Tense social situations |
| Play bow | Invitation to play or de-escalation | Play or conflict diffusion |
| Slow blinking | Trust, relaxation | Calm environments |
| Sitting/lying down | Non-threatening signal | When approached |
| Curved approach | Non-confrontational greeting | Meeting other dogs |

## Reading Cat Body Language

### Emotional State Indicators
```
RELAXED / CONTENT:
- Slow blinking (the "cat kiss")
- Ears forward, upright
- Tail upright, possibly with curved tip
- Purring (usually but not always)
- Kneading with paws
- Belly exposed (trust signal, NOT always invitation to touch)
- Soft, half-closed eyes
PLAYFUL / EXCITED:
- Dilated pupils
- Tail twitching or swishing
- Ears forward and alert
- Pouncing posture
- Chirping or chattering (at prey/birds)
- Running, jumping, zoomies
ANXIOUS / STRESSED:
- Ears flattened or rotated sideways ("airplane ears")
- Dilated pupils
- Tail low or tucked
- Crouching low
- Hiding
- Excessive grooming (can lead to bald patches)
- Not eating
- Inappropriate urination/defecation
- Excessive vocalization
FEARFUL / DEFENSIVE:
- Arched back with raised fur (piloerection)
- Hissing or spitting
- Ears flat against head
- Tail puffed up (bottle brush)
- Sideways stance (appearing larger)
- Swatting with claws extended
AGGRESSIVE (Warning Signs):
1. Fixed stare with constricted pupils
2. Ears rotated back
3. Tail lashing side to side (different from playful twitch)
4. Low growl or yowl
5. Hissing
6. Swatting
7. Bite
```

## Common Behavioral Issues

### Separation Anxiety (Dogs)
```
SYMPTOMS:
- Destructive behavior when left alone (especially doors/windows)
- Excessive barking or howling when alone
- House soiling despite being house-trained
- Pacing in a fixed pattern
- Excessive drooling or panting
- Escape attempts (can cause self-injury)
- Symptoms occur ONLY when owner is absent or about to leave
DIFFERENTIATING FROM BOREDOM:
Separation Anxiety: Occurs within minutes of departure, targets exit points
Boredom: May occur anytime, targets interesting objects, general mischief
MANAGEMENT PROTOCOL:
Phase 1: Foundation (Weeks 1-2)
- Ensure adequate exercise BEFORE departure (30-60 min)
- Create a safe, comfortable space (crate if crate-trained, or a room)
- Leave recently worn clothing with your scent
- Provide puzzle toys and long-lasting chews (Kong, lick mat)
- Play calming music or leave TV on
- Do NOT make departures dramatic (no long goodbyes)
Phase 2: Desensitization (Weeks 2-6)
- Practice departure cues without leaving (pick up keys, put on coat, then sit down)
- Gradually increase alone time:
  Day 1-3: Leave room for 10 seconds, return calmly
  Day 4-7: 30 seconds to 1 minute
  Week 2: 2-5 minutes
  Week 3: 5-15 minutes
  Week 4: 15-30 minutes
  Week 5: 30-60 minutes
  Week 6: 1-2 hours
- NEVER progress faster than the dog can handle without symptoms
- If symptoms return, go back to the previous duration
Phase 3: Maintenance
- Continue using puzzle toys and exercise before departures
- Vary departure routines to prevent pattern anxiety
- Consider calming supplements (consult vet): L-theanine, adaptil pheromones
- For severe cases: discuss medication options with veterinarian (fluoxetine, trazodone)
behaviorist and veterinarian. Medication + behavior modification
together has the highest success rate.
```

### Excessive Barking (Dogs)
```
IDENTIFY THE TYPE:
Alert Barking: At sounds, visitors, passing people
- Acknowledge the alert, then redirect ("thank you" + "quiet" + treat)
- Block visual triggers (window film, close blinds)
- Desensitize to triggers gradually
Demand Barking: For food, attention, play
- NEVER reward demand barking (no food, attention, or eye contact)
- Wait for silence (even 2 seconds), then reward
- Teach "quiet" command with positive reinforcement
- Reward calm, quiet behavior frequently
Boredom Barking: Repetitive, when understimulated
- Increase physical exercise (aim for 30-60+ min/day)
- Add mental stimulation (puzzle feeders, training sessions)
- Provide appropriate chew items
- Consider doggy daycare or dog walker
- Address the underlying anxiety (see separation anxiety protocol)
- Calming aids: pheromone diffusers, anxiety wraps, white noise
- Consult vet for severe cases
TEACHING "QUIET" COMMAND:
1. Wait for barking to start (or trigger it mildly)
2. Say "quiet" in a calm, firm voice
3. The moment the dog stops barking (even briefly), mark with "yes" and treat
4. Gradually increase the duration of silence required before treating
5. Practice in increasingly distracting environments
6. Never yell "quiet" -- this sounds like barking to the dog
```

### Aggression (Dogs)
```
TYPES OF AGGRESSION:
Fear Aggression:
- Triggered by scary situations, people, or animals
- Dog may cower first, then snap if pushed
- Often worse when cornered or on leash
Resource Guarding:
- Protecting food, toys, resting spots, or people
- Stiffening over item, whale eye, growl, snap
- Can be directed at humans or other pets
Territorial Aggression:
- Protecting home, yard, or car
- Barking, lunging at perceived intruders
- May be fine outside the territory
Redirected Aggression:
- Dog cannot reach target, redirects to nearest person/animal
- Common in barrier frustration (behind fence, on leash)
Leash Reactivity (often mistaken for aggression):
- Lunging, barking at other dogs on leash
- Often frustrated desire to greet, not actual aggression
- Usually better off-leash (but do not test without professional guidance)
SAFETY FIRST:
- If your dog has bitten someone, consult a professional immediately
- Muzzle train for safety (positive conditioning to muzzle)
- Management: avoid trigger situations while working on behavior
- NEVER use punishment for aggression (increases fear and risk)
GENERAL APPROACH (Counter-Conditioning + Desensitization):
1. Identify triggers and threshold distance
2. Work below threshold (where dog notices but does not react)
3. Pair trigger presence with high-value treats
4. Gradually decrease distance over weeks/months
5. Reward calm, relaxed behavior around triggers
6. Management between sessions (avoid uncontrolled exposure)
```

### Cat Litter Box Issues
```
RULE OUT MEDICAL CAUSES FIRST:
- UTI, kidney disease, diabetes, arthritis (painful to access box)
- See vet before assuming behavioral cause
COMMON BEHAVIORAL CAUSES:
1. Dirty litter box (cats are fastidious)
2. Wrong litter type or depth
3. Box location issues (too loud, too far, too exposed)
4. Not enough boxes (rule: # of cats + 1)
5. Territorial issues in multi-cat homes
6. Stress or anxiety
7. Negative association with box (was startled while using it)
LITTER BOX OPTIMIZATION:
- One box per cat, plus one extra
- Scoop at least once daily
- Full litter change weekly (clumping) or as needed
- Unscented, clumping litter preferred by most cats
- 2-3 inches of litter depth
- Box 1.5x length of cat
- Open boxes preferred by most cats (covered traps odor)
- Place in quiet, accessible locations
- Not next to food or water
- Multiple locations (not all in one room)
CLEANING ACCIDENTS:
- Use enzymatic cleaner (Nature's Miracle, etc.)
- Regular cleaners leave scent detectable by cats
- Clean thoroughly to prevent re-marking
- Temporarily block access to accident spots if possible
```

### The Four Quadrants of Operant Conditioning
```
Increase Behavior   POSITIVE         NEGATIVE
                                      dog walks nice)
Decrease Behavior   POSITIVE         NEGATIVE
                     *NOT RECOMMENDED* resource guards)
RECOMMENDED APPROACH:
- Fear and anxiety
- Damaged trust
- Suppressed warning signals

```

### Reward Hierarchy
```
LOW VALUE (for easy asks in low-distraction settings):
- Verbal praise ("good boy/girl!")
- Petting
- Kibble
- Low-value treats
MEDIUM VALUE (for moderate difficulty/distractions):
- Soft training treats
- Cheese pieces
- Play with toy
HIGH VALUE (for challenging situations or new behaviors):
- Chicken, hot dogs, liver
- Freeze-dried meat
- Special toys
- Extended play session
MATCHING REWARD TO DIFFICULTY:
Easy command in quiet home → Low value
New behavior being learned → Medium-High value
Behavior near triggers → Highest value
Recall from high distraction → Jackpot (multiple high-value treats)
```

## Environmental Enrichment

### Dog Enrichment Ideas
```
FOOD ENRICHMENT:
- Kong (stuffed and frozen): 20-40 min activity
- Snuffle mat: Nose work engagement
- Lick mat (with peanut butter, yogurt): Calming activity
- Puzzle feeders: Replace food bowls
- Scatter feeding in grass: Foraging instinct
- Treat-dispensing balls: Movement + food
SENSORY ENRICHMENT:
- Novel scents (safe herbs, scent trails in yard)
- Different textures to walk on
- Nature sounds, calming music
- Rotate toys weekly (novelty effect)
COGNITIVE ENRICHMENT:
- Training sessions (5-10 min, several per day)
- Trick training
- Hide and seek (with people or toys)
- Shell game (treat under cup)
- "Find it" games (hide treats around house)
PHYSICAL ENRICHMENT:
- Daily walks with sniff time (the walk is for THEM)
- Off-leash play (safe, appropriate areas)
- Swimming
- Flirt pole (for appropriate breeds)
- Agility or obstacle courses
- Playdates with compatible dogs
SOCIAL ENRICHMENT:
- Regular positive interactions with family
- Meeting new (friendly) people
- Dog park visits (if dog-appropriate)
- Doggy daycare
```

### Cat Enrichment Ideas
```
VERTICAL SPACE:
- Cat trees (multi-level)
- Wall-mounted shelves / cat highway
- Window perches (bird watching)
- Top of bookshelves (accessible pathways)
HUNTING SIMULATION:
- Interactive wand toys (feathers, mice): 10-15 min, 2x daily
- Puzzle feeders
- Scatter feeding kibble
- Laser pointer (always end with tangible treat to catch)
- Automated toys (when alone)
- Catnip and silvervine toys (rotate weekly)
TERRITORY AND SECURITY:
- Multiple hiding spots (boxes, cat caves, under furniture access)
- Window views of outdoors
- Scratching posts/surfaces (vertical AND horizontal)
- Resting spots in every main room
- Elevated observation points
SENSORY:
- Catnip, silvervine, valerian root (not all cats respond)
- Cat grass (safe to eat)
- Bird feeder outside window
- Nature documentaries on TV
- Different textures to explore
SOCIAL:
- Dedicated play time with humans daily
- Brushing / grooming (if cat enjoys it)
- Clicker training (yes, cats can be trained)
- Safe outdoor access (catio, harness training)
```

## Multi-Pet Household Dynamics

### Introducing a New Dog to a Resident Dog
```
PREPARATION (Before bringing new dog home):
- Set up separate spaces (crates, rooms, gates)
- Ensure both dogs are up-to-date on vaccines
- Have high-value treats ready
- Remove all resource-guarding triggers (bones, favorite toys, food bowls)
STEP 1: NEUTRAL TERRITORY MEETING (Day 1)
- Meet in a neutral location (park, quiet street)
- Both dogs on leash, handled by separate people
- Walk parallel at a distance (20+ feet apart)
- Gradually decrease distance if both are calm
- Allow brief sniffing (3 seconds), then walk apart
- End on a positive note
STEP 2: YARD/OUTDOOR MEETING (Day 1-2)
- Allow off-leash interaction in a fenced yard
- Keep it short (5-10 min)
- Interrupt if play gets too intense
- Provide water in multiple spots
- No toys or food present initially
STEP 3: INDOOR INTRODUCTION (Day 2-3)
- New dog enters home first (sniff and explore)
- Bring resident dog in
- Keep sessions short
- Separate when unsupervised for first 2-4 weeks
- Feed in separate areas
STEP 4: GRADUAL INTEGRATION (Weeks 2-4+)
- Slowly increase supervised time together
- Watch for tension during resource moments (feeding, treats, attention)
- Ensure each dog has their own space to retreat to
- Do not force interaction
RED FLAGS TO WATCH:
- Hard staring between dogs
- One dog consistently bullying/cornering the other
- Resource guarding escalating
- Snapping that goes beyond play correction
- One dog shutting down completely
```

### Introducing a New Cat to a Resident Cat
```
PHASE 1: TOTAL SEPARATION (Week 1-2)
- New cat in a separate room with own litter box, food, water, bed
- Feed both cats on opposite sides of the closed door
- Swap bedding between cats (scent exchange)
- Swap rooms for short periods (let each explore the other's scent)
- Use pheromone diffusers (Feliway Multicat) in both spaces
PHASE 2: SCENT EXCHANGE (Week 2-3)
- Rub a cloth on one cat's cheeks, place near other cat
- Continue door feeding, moving bowls closer to door
- If hissing at door, move bowls farther apart
- Play with each cat near the door
PHASE 3: VISUAL INTRODUCTION (Week 3-4)
- Use a baby gate or crack the door (secured)
- Brief visual contact during feeding
- Sessions of 5-10 minutes
- End before any hissing or aggression
- If negative reaction, go back to Phase 2
PHASE 4: SUPERVISED MEETINGS (Week 4-6+)
- Short face-to-face meetings (5 min)
- Have treats and play to create positive association
- Multiple escape routes available
- No forcing interaction
- Gradually increase time together
- Separate when unsupervised
PHASE 5: INTEGRATION (Week 6+)
- Leave door open when home
- Monitor meal times
- Ensure enough resources (litter boxes, food stations, perches)
- Some cats may never be best friends -- tolerance is okay
```

## Socialization Guidance

### Critical Socialization Periods
```
DOGS:
Primary socialization window: 3-14 weeks of age
Secondary socialization: 14 weeks - 6 months
- Expose puppy to 100+ positive experiences during this window
- People of different ages, genders, ethnicities, with hats/uniforms
- Other vaccinated dogs and puppies
- Different surfaces, sounds, environments
- Car rides, vet visits, grooming
- Handle feet, ears, mouth regularly
- ALWAYS positive -- never force scary experiences
CATS:
Primary socialization window: 2-7 weeks of age
Secondary socialization: 7-14 weeks
- Handle kittens daily (gentle, brief)
- Exposure to different people
- Gentle sounds (household appliances, music)
- Other animals (if safe and vaccinated)
- Carrier and car rides
- Gentle grooming and handling
```
