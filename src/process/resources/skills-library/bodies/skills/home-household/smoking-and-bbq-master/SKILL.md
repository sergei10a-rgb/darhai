## The Science of Smoke

```
WHAT CREATES FLAVOR:
- Smoke is a mixture of gases, water vapor, and microscopic particles
- KEY COMPOUNDS: syringol and guaiacol (the "smoky" flavors)
- THIN BLUE SMOKE: clean-burning, ideal - produces sweet, complex flavor
- WHITE BILLOWING SMOKE: incomplete combustion - produces bitter, acrid taste
- Rule: If you can smell pleasant campfire, you are on track.
        If it smells harsh or makes your eyes sting, too much smoke.

SMOKE RING SCIENCE:
- Pink ring beneath the bark on smoked meat
- Caused by nitric oxide and carbon monoxide reacting with myoglobin
- Happens in first 2-3 hours (before bark fully sets)
- Does NOT indicate flavor depth (it is cosmetic, but we love it)
- Maximized by: moist surface, longer exposure before wrapping

THE STALL:
- Internal temp plateaus at 150-170F for hours
- Caused by evaporative cooling (same as sweating)
- Options: Wait it out (purist), wrap in butcher paper or foil (Texas crutch)
- Wrapping speeds through the stall but softens the bark
```

## Wood Selection Guide

```
WOOD FLAVOR PROFILES:
Wood       | Intensity | Flavor Profile           | Best With
-----------+-----------+--------------------------+------------------
Hickory    | Strong    | Bold, bacon-like, sweet  | Pork, ribs, beef
Mesquite   | Very Strong| Earthy, aggressive      | Beef (use sparingly)
Oak (post) | Medium    | Clean, versatile, nutty  | Beef brisket, universal
Cherry     | Mild-Med  | Sweet, fruity, mahogany  | Pork, poultry, game
Apple      | Mild      | Sweet, subtle, delicate  | Poultry, pork, fish
Pecan      | Medium    | Nutty, sweet, mellow     | Everything (great blend)
Maple      | Mild      | Sweet, subtle            | Poultry, vegetables
Alder      | Mild      | Delicate, slightly sweet | Fish (traditional salmon)

BLENDING STRATEGY:
- Base wood (60-70%): Oak or hickory for backbone
- Flavor wood (30-40%): Cherry, apple, or pecan for complexity
- Classic: Oak + cherry for brisket, Hickory + apple for pork

WOOD FORM:
Logs:    Offset smokers, provides heat + smoke
Chunks:  Charcoal smokers, long burn, primary recommendation
Chips:   Gas/electric smokers, shorter burn, soak if desired
Pellets: Pellet grills, compressed sawdust, automated feed

NEVER USE: pine, cedar (unless planking), treated lumber,
plywood, painted wood, or any softwood (resinous, toxic)
```

## Temperature Control

```
THE FUNDAMENTAL SKILL: Maintaining steady temperature.

TARGET TEMPERATURES BY METHOD:
Method           | Pit Temp    | Time Factor | Best For
-----------------+-------------+-------------+------------------
Low and Slow     | 225-250F    | Long        | Brisket, pork butt
Hot and Fast     | 300-350F    | Medium      | Brisket, chicken
Standard BBQ     | 250-275F    | Standard    | Ribs, most cuts
High Heat Smoke  | 350-400F    | Short       | Poultry, wings

CONTROLLING TEMPERATURE:
Offset Smoker:
- More air = more heat (open intake vent)
- Less air = less heat (close intake vent)
- Exhaust vent stays 100% open (always - do not close it)
- Small, frequent adjustments beat large corrections
- Learn your smoker's vents: 1/4 turn = roughly 25F change

Kamado (Big Green Egg, etc.):
- Bottom vent controls air intake
- Top vent fine-tunes
- Very small adjustments needed (these are efficient)
- 225F: bottom vent barely cracked, top vent 1/4 open

Pellet Grill:
- Set temperature digitally
- Monitor actual vs set temp (can vary 15-25F)
- Cold weather: insulated blanket helps stability

Charcoal Kettle:
- Minion method: fill ring with unlit, light 10-15 on top
- Snake method: arrange briquettes in a C-shape, light one end
- Both provide 6-12 hours of consistent heat at 225-275F
```

## Essential Rub Formulas

```
ALL-PURPOSE BBQ RUB (makes ~1 cup):
- 1/4 cup paprika (or smoked paprika)
- 2 tbsp black pepper (coarse ground)
- 2 tbsp kosher salt
- 2 tbsp brown sugar
- 1 tbsp garlic powder
- 1 tbsp onion powder
- 1 tsp cayenne (adjust to preference)
- 1 tsp cumin

TEXAS-STYLE BRISKET RUB (dalmatian):
- 50% coarse black pepper (16-mesh)
- 50% kosher salt
That is it. Simplicity lets the beef and smoke shine.

MEMPHIS-STYLE PORK RUB:
- 1/4 cup paprika
- 2 tbsp garlic powder
- 2 tbsp mild chili powder
- 1 tbsp black pepper
- 1 tbsp kosher salt
- 1 tbsp onion powder
- 2 tsp dry mustard
- 2 tsp celery salt
- 1 tsp cayenne

APPLICATION:
1. Pat meat dry with paper towels
2. Apply thin coat of mustard or olive oil (binder - does not affect flavor)
3. Apply rub generously. More than you think. Press into surface.
4. For pork/brisket: apply night before, refrigerate uncovered
5. For ribs/chicken: 1-4 hours before is sufficient
```

## Smoking Times and Temperatures

```
CUT-BY-CUT GUIDE:

BEEF BRISKET (whole packer, 12-16 lbs):
  Pit temp: 250F (or 275-300F hot and fast)
  Target internal: 200-205F (probe tender)
  Time: 1-1.5 hours per pound at 250F
  Wrap: Optional at 165F internal (butcher paper preferred)
  Rest: Minimum 1 hour, ideally 2-4 hours in cooler

PORK BUTT / SHOULDER (8-10 lbs):
  Pit temp: 225-250F
  Target internal: 200-205F (should pull apart easily)
  Time: 1.5-2 hours per pound
  Wrap: Optional at 165F
  Rest: 1-2 hours, then pull

SPARE RIBS (3-2-1 method):
  Pit temp: 225-250F
  Phase 1: 3 hours unwrapped (smoke absorption)
  Phase 2: 2 hours wrapped in foil with liquid (tenderize)
  Phase 3: 1 hour unwrapped with sauce (set the bark)
  Done test: Bend test - pick up with tongs, meat cracks but does not fall off

BABY BACK RIBS (2-2-1 method):
  Same concept, shorter times (thinner rack)

WHOLE CHICKEN (4-5 lbs):
  Pit temp: 275-325F (higher temp for crispy skin)
  Target internal: 165F breast, 175F thigh
  Time: 2.5-3.5 hours
  Tip: Spatchcock (remove backbone) for even cooking

PORK BELLY (for burnt ends):
  Pit temp: 250F
  Phase 1: Smoke 3 hours until bark forms
  Phase 2: Cube, toss in sauce, smoke 1.5 hours in pan
  Target: Jiggly, caramelized, melt-in-mouth

SALMON:
  Pit temp: 180-225F
  Target internal: 140F
  Time: 1-2 hours
  Brine first: 4 hours in 4:1 water-to-salt solution with brown sugar

TURKEY (12-14 lbs):
  Pit temp: 275-300F
  Target internal: 165F breast, 175F thigh
  Time: 3-4 hours
  Brine recommended, spatchcock for best results
```

## Sauce Foundations

```
KANSAS CITY STYLE (sweet, thick, tomato-based):
- 2 cups ketchup
- 1/2 cup apple cider vinegar
- 1/2 cup brown sugar
- 2 tbsp molasses
- 1 tbsp Worcestershire
- 1 tsp each: garlic powder, onion powder, black pepper, smoked paprika
- Simmer 20 minutes. Adjust sweet/tang balance to taste.

CAROLINA VINEGAR SAUCE (Eastern NC, thin and tangy):
- 2 cups apple cider vinegar
- 2 tbsp brown sugar
- 1 tbsp red pepper flakes
- 1 tbsp kosher salt
- 1 tsp black pepper
- Mix. No cooking required. Let sit 24 hours.

MUSTARD SAUCE (South Carolina):
- 1 cup yellow mustard
- 1/2 cup apple cider vinegar
- 1/4 cup brown sugar
- 1 tbsp Worcestershire
- 1 tsp each: garlic powder, black pepper, cayenne
- Simmer 10 minutes.

ALABAMA WHITE SAUCE:
- 1 cup mayonnaise
- 1/2 cup apple cider vinegar
- 2 tbsp lemon juice
- 1 tbsp black pepper
- 1 tsp salt, 1 tsp cayenne
- Whisk together. Refrigerate overnight.
- Traditional with smoked chicken.

SAUCE APPLICATION RULE:
- Never sauce before the bark is set (sugar burns, bark gets mushy)
- Apply in last 30-60 minutes for glaze
- Or serve on the side and let people choose
```

## Troubleshooting

```
PROBLEM: Meat is dry
CAUSES:  Overcooked (went past target temp), not enough fat, no wrap
FIX:     Cook to temperature not time. Use a probe thermometer.
         Choose fattier cuts. Wrap during the stall. Rest properly.

PROBLEM: Bitter or acrid taste
CAUSES:  Dirty/thick smoke, creosote buildup, too much smoke wood
FIX:     Ensure thin blue smoke. Clean your smoker. Use less wood.
         Open the exhaust vent fully. Pre-burn wood to eliminate impurities.

PROBLEM: No smoke ring
CAUSES:  Wrapped too early, surface was dry, electric smoker
FIX:     Do not wrap before 3 hours. Spritz surface to keep moist.
         Electric smokers produce less NO (add a water pan and wood chunks).

PROBLEM: Bark is too soft or mushy
CAUSES:  Wrapped too tight, too much moisture in wrap, humidity too high
FIX:     Use butcher paper (breathes) instead of foil.
         Unwrap for the last hour to firm up bark.

PROBLEM: Temperature swings wildly
CAUSES:  Too much fuel, opening lid too often, wind exposure
FIX:     Make small vent adjustments. Wait 15 min between changes.
         Every time you open the lid: add 15-20 min to cook time.
         Use a windbreak. Get a good ambient probe thermometer.

PROBLEM: Tough brisket
CAUSES:  Not cooked long enough (most common), poor quality meat, sliced wrong
FIX:     Cook to probe-tender (200-205F AND the probe slides in like butter).
         Use Choice or Prime grade. Slice against the grain.
         The flat and point grains run different directions - identify the seam.
```

## Competition Tips for the Ambitious

```
TURN-IN BOX PRIORITIES (KCBS scoring):
1. Taste (most heavily weighted)
2. Tenderness
3. Appearance
4. Turn-in must look appetizing in a plain white Styrofoam box

COMPETITION TIMELINE (brisket example):
Day Before:  Trim, season, prep injections
11:00 PM:    Light fire, stabilize pit
12:00 AM:    Brisket on the pit at 250F
6:00 AM:     Check - should be around 160-170F, wrap if desired
10:00 AM:    Target 195-200F, begin checking probe tenderness
11:00 AM:    Pull when probe-tender, rest in cooler with towels
1:30 PM:     Slice for turn-in
2:00 PM:     Turn-in time

PRACTICE MAKES PITMASTER:
- Cook the same cut 5 times before trying to perfect a different one
- Take notes every cook: temps, times, wood, weather, results
- Adjust one variable at a time
```
