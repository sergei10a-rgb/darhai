## Questions to Ask the User First

1. **What is your USDA Hardiness Zone?** (Look up at planthardiness.ars.usda.gov by zip code)
2. **What type of gardening?** (Vegetable, flower, herb, lawn, indoor plants, container, all)
3. **How much space?** (Backyard, raised beds, containers/patio, balcony, windowsill)
4. **Sun exposure?** (Full sun 6+ hours, partial sun 3-6 hours, shade under 3 hours)
5. **Soil type?** (Clay, sandy, loamy, unknown, container mix)
6. **Experience level?** (First-time, beginner, intermediate, experienced)
7. **Watering situation?** (Hose access, irrigation system, hand watering only, rain-dependent)
8. **Budget?** (Minimal, moderate, willing to invest)
9. **Time commitment?** (Minutes per week, hours per week)
10. **Any specific goals?** (Feed the family, beautiful yard, pollinators, relax, etc.)
11. **Organic preference?** (Strictly organic, prefer organic, open to conventional)
12. **Any challenges?** (Deer, pests, poor soil, drought, short season)

```

  Good companions: Basil (repels pests, may improve flavor), carrots,

  Good companions: Basil, carrots, onions, spinach, tomatoes
  Bad companions: Fennel, kohlrabi
  Spacing: 18 inches apart

  Good companions: Beans, corn, peas, radishes, sunflowers
  Bad companions: Potatoes, aromatic herbs (sage, mint)
  Spacing: 12 inches on trellis, 36 inches on ground

BEANS:
  Good companions: Corn, squash, cucumbers, carrots, beets
  Bad companions: Onions, garlic, fennel

THE THREE SISTERS (Native American tradition):
  Corn + Beans + Squash
  Corn provides structure for beans to climb
  Beans fix nitrogen for corn and squash
  Squash shades soil, reducing weeds and moisture loss

MARIGOLDS:
  Plant throughout the garden -- repels many pests
  French marigolds are most effective for pest control
```

## Soil Preparation

### Soil Assessment
```
SOIL TEST GUIDE:

STEP 1: TEXTURE TEST (jar test)
  1. Fill a quart jar 1/3 with garden soil
  2. Fill with water, shake vigorously
  3. Let settle for 24 hours
  4. Bottom layer: Sand (settles in 1 minute)
  5. Middle layer: Silt (settles in 4-6 hours)
  6. Top layer: Clay (settles in 24 hours)
  Ideal: Roughly equal parts = loamy soil

STEP 2: pH TEST
  Home test kit: $10-15 at garden center
  Professional test (recommended): Contact county extension office ($15-25)
  Ideal range: 6.0-7.0 for most vegetables
  Too acidic (< 6.0): Add garden lime
  Too alkaline (> 7.5): Add sulfur or peat moss

STEP 3: DRAINAGE TEST
  1. Dig a hole 12" deep x 12" wide
  2. Fill with water, let drain completely
  3. Fill again and time the drainage
  Ideal: Drains 1-2 inches per hour
  Too fast (sandy): Add compost for water retention
  Too slow (clay): Add compost and consider raised beds

STEP 4: ORGANIC MATTER ASSESSMENT
  Dark, crumbly, earthy-smelling soil = good organic content
  Pale, hard, compacted soil = needs amendment
  Solution for all soil types: ADD COMPOST (the universal amendment)
```

### Soil Improvement
```
SOIL AMENDMENT PLAN:

FOR ALL SOIL TYPES:
  - Add 2-4 inches of compost to top of beds annually
  - Mulch 2-3 inches deep (retains moisture, builds soil)
  - Avoid walking on garden beds (compaction)
  - Rotate crops yearly (prevents nutrient depletion)

CLAY SOIL:
  Add: Compost (lots), coarse sand, gypsum
  Strategy: Raised beds work best over clay
  Never: Work clay soil when wet (creates concrete-like clumps)

SANDY SOIL:
  Add: Compost (lots), peat moss, coconut coir
  Strategy: Increase organic matter to hold water and nutrients
  Mulch: Extra important (moisture retention)

ROCKY/THIN SOIL:
  Strategy: Raised beds with imported soil mix
  Mix: 1/3 topsoil, 1/3 compost, 1/3 peat or coir

CONTAINER MIX (do not use garden soil in containers):
  Recipe: 1 part peat or coir + 1 part perlite + 1 part compost
  Or: Buy quality potting mix (not "potting soil" -- check for perlite)
```

## Organic Pest Management

### Integrated Pest Management (IPM) Approach
```
IPM HIERARCHY (try in this order):

LEVEL 1: PREVENTION (best approach)
  - Healthy soil = healthy plants = pest resistance
  - Choose disease-resistant varieties
  - Proper spacing for air circulation
  - Rotate crops annually
  - Remove plant debris at season end
  - Attract beneficial insects with flowers

LEVEL 2: PHYSICAL CONTROLS
  - Hand-pick large pests (hornworms, beetles)
  - Row covers to prevent insect access
  - Copper tape for slugs
  - Traps (yellow sticky traps for aphids, beer traps for slugs)
  - Water spray to knock off aphids
  - Barriers (collars for cutworms)

LEVEL 3: BIOLOGICAL CONTROLS
  - Attract or purchase beneficial insects:
    Ladybugs (eat aphids)
    Lacewings (eat many soft-bodied pests)
    Praying mantis (general predators)
    Parasitic wasps (caterpillar control)
  - Bt (Bacillus thuringiensis) for caterpillars
  - Nematodes for soil-dwelling pests

LEVEL 4: ORGANIC SPRAYS (last resort)
  - Neem oil (broad spectrum, use in evening to protect bees)
  - Insecticidal soap (aphids, mites, whiteflies)
  - Diatomaceous earth (crawling insects, apply when dry)
  - Pyrethrin (last resort, also kills beneficials)

Always start with Level 1 and work up.
```

### Common Garden Pest Identification

| Pest | Signs | Plants Affected | Organic Solution |
|------|-------|----------------|-----------------|
| Aphids | Sticky residue, curled leaves, clusters on stems | Nearly all | Water spray, ladybugs, insecticidal soap |
| Tomato hornworm | Large caterpillar, stripped leaves | Tomatoes, peppers | Hand-pick, Bt spray, attract parasitic wasps |
| Squash bug | Wilting leaves, brown eggs on leaf undersides | Squash, cucumbers | Hand-pick, trap boards, neem oil |
| Cabbage worm | Holes in leaves, green caterpillars | Brassicas | Row covers, Bt spray, hand-pick |
| Slugs/snails | Holes in leaves, slime trails | Lettuce, seedlings | Beer traps, copper barriers, hand-pick at night |
| Japanese beetles | Skeletonized leaves, metallic beetles | Many plants | Hand-pick, milky spore (lawn), neem oil |
| Deer | Browsed plants, hoof prints | Everything | Fencing (8+ ft), repellent spray, deer-resistant plants |

## Container Gardening

### Container Growing Guide
```
CONTAINER GARDENING ESSENTIALS:

CONTAINER SELECTION:
  Size matters -- bigger is almost always better:
  - Herbs: 6-8" pot minimum
  - Lettuce/greens: 6-8" deep, any width
  - Peppers: 5-gallon minimum (14" diameter)
  - Tomatoes: 10-gallon minimum (18" diameter)
  - Cucumbers: 5-gallon with trellis
  - Zucchini: 10-gallon minimum

  Material:
  - Plastic: lightweight, retains moisture, inexpensive
  - Terra cotta: attractive, breathes, dries faster, heavier
  - Fabric grow bags: excellent drainage and aeration, portable
  - Self-watering: best for consistent moisture, less daily attention

  CRITICAL: All containers must have drainage holes!

SOIL:
  Use quality potting MIX (not garden soil, not topsoil)
  Add slow-release fertilizer at planting
  Refresh or replace soil annually

WATERING:
  Containers dry out much faster than in-ground gardens
  Check daily (stick finger 1" into soil -- dry = water)
  Water until it drains from bottom
  Morning watering is ideal
  Consider self-watering containers or drip irrigation

FEEDING:
  Containers deplete nutrients quickly
  Fertilize every 1-2 weeks with liquid fertilizer
  Or use slow-release granular at planting (lasts 3-4 months)
  Signs of nutrient deficiency: yellowing leaves, poor growth

BEST CONTAINER VEGETABLES:
  - Cherry/patio tomatoes (determinate varieties)
  - Peppers (all types)
  - Lettuce and greens (any container)
  - Herbs (all types)
  - Radishes (shallow container is fine)
  - Bush beans
  - Strawberries
  - Dwarf cucumbers and squash
```

## Herb Garden Guide
```
ESSENTIAL HERB GARDEN:

CULINARY HERBS (start with these):
  Basil: Full sun, warm soil, pinch flowers for more leaves
  Cilantro: Cool weather, bolts in heat, succession plant
  Parsley: Part shade OK, biennial (lives 2 years)
  Rosemary: Full sun, well-drained, perennial in zones 7+
  Thyme: Full sun, well-drained, perennial, drought tolerant
  Mint: ANY condition (invasive -- keep in pot!)
  Chives: Full to part sun, perennial, easy
  Oregano: Full sun, perennial, spreads easily
  Sage: Full sun, perennial, drought tolerant
  Dill: Full sun, direct sow, attracts beneficial insects

GROWING TIPS:
  - Most herbs prefer well-drained soil (don't overwater)
  - Harvest regularly (promotes bushy growth)
  - Pinch flowers to keep leaf production going
  - Most herbs are happier slightly hungry than overfed
  - Windowsill herbs need 6+ hours of light (or grow light)

PRESERVATION:
  - Freezing: best for basil, cilantro, chives (chop, freeze in ice cube trays with oil)
  - Drying: best for rosemary, thyme, oregano, sage (hang bundles, air dry)
  - Herb butter: mix chopped herbs with softened butter, freeze
  - Herb salt: layer herbs and coarse salt, dry, grind
```

## Indoor Plant Care

### Houseplant Quick Reference
```
BEGINNER-FRIENDLY HOUSEPLANTS:

VERY LOW MAINTENANCE:
  Pothos (Epipremnum aureum)
    Light: Low to bright indirect
    Water: When top 1-2" dry (every 1-2 weeks)
    Skill: Nearly indestructible

  Snake Plant (Sansevieria)
    Light: Low to bright indirect
    Water: When completely dry (every 2-4 weeks)
    Skill: Thrives on neglect

  ZZ Plant (Zamioculcas zamiifolia)
    Light: Low to bright indirect
    Water: When completely dry (every 2-3 weeks)
    Skill: Extremely forgiving

LOW MAINTENANCE:
  Spider Plant (Chlorophytum)
    Light: Bright indirect
    Water: When top inch dry
    Bonus: Produces baby plants

  Peace Lily (Spathiphyllum)
    Light: Low to medium
    Water: When leaves start to droop (it tells you!)
    Bonus: Air purifying, flowers

  Rubber Plant (Ficus elastica)
    Light: Bright indirect
    Water: When top 1-2" dry

MODERATE CARE:
  Monstera deliciosa
    Light: Bright indirect (no direct sun)
    Water: When top 2" dry

  Fiddle Leaf Fig (Ficus lyrata)
    Light: Bright indirect to some direct
    Water: When top 2" dry, consistent schedule

INDOOR PLANT TROUBLESHOOTING:
  Yellow leaves: Usually overwatering
  Brown crispy edges: Underwatering or low humidity
  Leggy/stretching: Not enough light
  Dropping leaves: Stress (moved, temperature change, draft)
  Pests (tiny insects): Isolate, wipe with alcohol, neem oil spray
```

### Watering Schedules
```
INDOOR PLANT WATERING GUIDE:

THE GOLDEN RULE: Check the soil, don't follow a calendar.

CHECKING METHOD:
  1. Stick finger 1-2 inches into soil
  2. If dry = water  |  If moist = wait
  3. When watering, water thoroughly until it drains from bottom
  4. Empty saucer after 30 minutes (no sitting in water)

GENERAL FREQUENCY (varies by conditions):
  Succulents/cacti: Every 2-4 weeks
  Snake plant, ZZ, pothos: Every 1-2 weeks
  Tropical plants: Every 1 week
  Ferns: Every 3-5 days (keep evenly moist)
  Peace lily: When leaves droop slightly

FACTORS THAT INCREASE WATERING NEEDS:
  + More light
  + Higher temperature
  + Lower humidity
  + Smaller pot
  + Terracotta pot (breathes)
  + Active growing season (spring/summer)

FACTORS THAT DECREASE WATERING NEEDS:
  + Less light
  + Cooler temperature
  + Higher humidity
  + Larger pot
  + Plastic or glazed pot
  + Dormant season (fall/winter)
```

## Lawn Care Schedule
```
LAWN CARE ANNUAL PLAN:

SPRING:
  [ ] Rake out dead grass and thatch (when ground is firm)
  [ ] Soil test (every 2-3 years, spring is ideal)
  [ ] Apply pre-emergent herbicide (before soil temp reaches 55F)
      Organic: corn gluten meal
  [ ] First mow when grass is actively growing (highest setting)
  [ ] Begin regular watering if rain is insufficient
  [ ] Fertilize (light application, organic or slow-release)

SUMMER:
  [ ] Mow high (3-4 inches for cool-season grass, 2-3 for warm-season)
      Never remove more than 1/3 of grass height at once
  [ ] Water deeply but infrequently (1" per week including rain)
      Water early morning (6-10 AM) to reduce disease
  [ ] Leave clippings on lawn (free fertilizer)
  [ ] Spot-treat weeds as needed
  [ ] Reduce mowing frequency during heat/drought

FALL (most important season for cool-season lawns):
  [ ] Aerate (core aeration, especially if compacted)
  [ ] Overseed thin areas (after aeration)
  [ ] Fertilize (heaviest application of the year for cool-season)
  [ ] Continue mowing until growth stops
  [ ] Final mow slightly shorter than summer height
  [ ] Rake or mulch fallen leaves (don't let them smother grass)

WINTER:
  [ ] Service mower (sharpen blade, change oil, spark plug)
  [ ] Stay off frozen grass when possible
  [ ] Plan spring projects
  [ ] Order seed or sod if needed
```
