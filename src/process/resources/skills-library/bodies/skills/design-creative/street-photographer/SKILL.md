## Camera Settings for Street Work

### The Speed-First Approach

Street photography rewards readiness over perfection. Set your camera so you can react instantly.

| Setting | Recommended | Reasoning |
|---------|-------------|-----------|
| Mode | Aperture Priority or Manual | Control depth of field, let camera handle exposure shifts |
| Aperture | f/8 - f/11 (zone focus) or f/2.8 - f/4 (shallow) | Zone focusing for speed; wide open for subject isolation |
| ISO | Auto ISO 200-6400 | Let the camera adapt to light changes as you move |
| Shutter | Minimum 1/250 | Freezes walking pace; use 1/500 for running or gestures |
| Focus | Manual with zone focus OR continuous AF | Zone focus is faster; modern AF tracks well |
| Drive | Single shot or low burst | Decisive moments, not spray-and-pray |

### Zone Focusing Technique

Zone focusing is the street photographer's secret weapon. Pre-focus to a set distance so you never wait for autofocus.

1. Set lens to manual focus
2. Choose your working distance (typically 2-3 meters for 35mm, 3-5 meters for 50mm)
3. Set aperture to f/8 or f/11
4. Read the depth of field scale on your lens (or use a DOF calculator app)
5. Everything within the DOF range is acceptably sharp without focusing

**Quick Reference:**
- 35mm at f/8, focused at 3m: sharp from ~2m to 5m
- 50mm at f/8, focused at 3m: sharp from ~2.5m to 4m
- 28mm at f/11, focused at 3m: sharp from ~1.5m to infinity

### Lens Selection

| Lens | Character | Best For |
|------|-----------|----------|
| 28mm | Wide, environmental, immersive | Busy scenes, architecture + people, getting close |
| 35mm | Classic street focal length | Versatile, natural perspective, the standard |
| 50mm | Normal view, slight compression | Isolation from surroundings, portraits in context |
| 85-135mm | Telephoto compression | Candid distance work, layered compositions |

**The one-lens discipline:** Pick one focal length and shoot it exclusively for three months. You will learn to see in that frame.

## Candid Techniques

### The Invisible Photographer

The goal is to document life without altering it. Strategies for staying unnoticed:

**Body Language**
- Walk with purpose like you belong, not like you are hunting
- Camera at your side or chest, not constantly raised to your eye
- Avoid prolonged eye contact before shooting
- Dress to blend: no bright colors, no photographer vest
- Wear headphones (music off) to appear disengaged

**Shooting Methods**
- Hip shooting: Camera at waist, shoot without raising to eye
- Shoot-through: Use windows, fences, or crowds as natural blinds
- The walk-by: Raise camera as you pass, fire, keep walking naturally
- The wait: Find a good background and wait for someone to walk into it
- The pivot: Pretend to photograph something else, then redirect

**Timing**
- Anticipate the peak moment before it happens
- Watch for patterns: crosswalks, bus stops, cafe entrances
- Shoot as people enter a shaft of light or pass an interesting background
- The "decisive moment" is preparation meeting opportunity

### Approaching Subjects Directly

Some of the best street photography comes from engagement, not stealth.

1. Make eye contact and smile first
2. "I'm a photographer documenting life in this neighborhood - you caught my eye"
3. Ask if you can take a portrait; most people are flattered
4. Show them the image on your screen
5. Offer to send them the photo (carry small cards with your email/Instagram)
6. If they say no, thank them and move on immediately

### Working Specific Scenes

| Scene | Technique | What to Watch For |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|
| Markets | Slow walk, zone focus, hip shoot | Vendors interacting, hands exchanging money, color stacks |
| Transit | Sit and observe, use reflections | Waiting faces, reading, sleeping, window reflections |
| Crosswalks | Find elevated position, pre-focus | Patterns of movement, umbrellas, shadows |
| Cafes | Sit with a coffee, shoot from your table | Conversations, gestures, light through windows |
| Rain | Shoot through glass, use reflections | Puddle reflections, umbrellas, rushing figures |
| Night | Neon signs, storefronts, pools of light | Silhouettes, faces lit by phones, wet pavement glow |

## Composition in Chaotic Environments

### Controlling Chaos

Street scenes are visually noisy. Your job is to find order.

**Layering**: Build depth by including foreground, middle ground, and background elements. A railing in the foreground, your subject in the middle, a billboard behind.

**Framing**: Use doorways, arches, windows, and gaps between buildings to frame subjects within the scene.

**Juxtaposition**: Place contrasting elements together - old and young, advertising and reality, rich and poor, fast and still.

**Geometry**: Find leading lines (sidewalk edges, shadows, fences), triangles (three people), and repeating patterns (windows, market stalls).

**Negative space**: Use empty walls, sky, or pavement to give your subject room to breathe.

**Light as composition**: Let light define the image. A pool of sunlight on a shadowed street is your stage.

### The Background-First Method

Instead of chasing subjects, find a compelling background and wait.

1. Locate a wall, mural, light pattern, or geometric backdrop
2. Pre-focus to the distance a passerby would walk
3. Set your exposure for the scene
4. Wait. It may take 5 minutes or 45 minutes.
5. When the right person enters the frame, fire

This method produces cleaner compositions than reactive shooting.

## Legal Considerations

### General Principles (Varies by Jurisdiction)

**In most Western democracies:**
- Photographing in public spaces is generally legal
- No expectation of privacy in public areas
- Publication for editorial or art purposes is broadly protected
- Commercial use (advertising) of identifiable people requires model releases

**Key distinctions:**
| Use | Release Needed? | Notes |
|-----|-----------------|-------|
| Personal art/portfolio | No (in most jurisdictions) | Protected expression |
| Editorial/journalism | No | News, education, commentary |
| Fine art exhibition | Generally no | Protected expression |
| Fine art print sales | Gray area | Varies by jurisdiction |
| Commercial/advertising | Yes | Using someone's likeness to sell products |
| Stock photography | Yes | Broadly commercial |

### Jurisdiction-Specific Notes

**Research your local laws.** Some important variations:
- Some European countries have stricter personality rights (Germany, France)
- Some countries require consent before photographing (certain Middle Eastern countries)
- Private property that appears public (malls, transit stations) may restrict photography
- Photographing children raises heightened sensitivity everywhere
- Military installations, government buildings may have restrictions

### When Confronted

1. Stay calm and polite. Aggression escalates situations.
2. Know your rights but do not lecture people about them.
3. "I understand your concern. I'm working on a photography project about [topic]. Would you like me to delete your photo?" Offering the choice defuses most confrontations.
4. If someone is truly distressed, delete the photo. No image is worth causing harm.
5. If threatened, leave immediately. Personal safety first.
6. Never photograph someone in a vulnerable state (homeless, in distress, medical emergency) for shock value.

## Ethics Framework

### The Street Photographer's Code

| Principle | In Practice |
|-----------|-------------|
| Dignity first | Never photograph someone to mock, exploit, or demean them |
| Power awareness | You hold the camera; you hold the power. Use it responsibly |
| Context matters | A photo removed from context can misrepresent someone's reality |
| Vulnerability check | Would this person be harmed by publication? |
| Cultural sensitivity | Research norms before shooting in unfamiliar cultures |
| Children | Extra caution; many photographers avoid children entirely |
| Consent when possible | When you have time and it does not ruin the moment, ask |
| Delete when asked | Unless documenting genuine news, honor removal requests |

### The Publication Test

Before sharing a street photo, ask:
- Would I be comfortable if this were a photo of me?
- Does this image respect the humanity of the subject?
- Could this image cause harm to the person depicted?
- Am I reinforcing stereotypes or revealing truth?
- Is the story I am telling honest?

## Editing for Narrative

### Culling Street Work

Street photography has a very low keeper rate. Expect 1-5 strong images per session from hundreds of frames.

**First pass:** Flag anything with energy, emotion, or strong composition (be generous)
**Second pass:** Rate for technical quality - focus, exposure, framing
**Third pass:** Evaluate storytelling - does this image say something beyond the visual?
**Final selects:** Would this hold its own in a gallery or book? Keep only those.

### Processing Approach

**Black and White Conversion**
- Street photography has a strong B&W tradition
- Removes distracting color, emphasizes form and light
- High contrast: boost whites, deepen blacks, clarity +20 to +40
- Channel mixing: emphasize red channel for dramatic skies, blue for smooth skin

**Color Street Photography**
- Color should be intentional, not incidental
- Look for color harmony or deliberate color clash
- Mute background colors, let one dominant color pop
- Film simulation presets (Kodak Portra, Fuji Superia) add cohesive mood

**Universal Adjustments**
- Crop decisively: remove anything that does not serve the image
- Straighten horizons unless the tilt is intentional
- Dodge and burn to direct the viewer's eye to the subject
- Remove nothing: street photography ethics generally oppose cloning out elements

## Building a Storytelling Series

### From Singles to Stories

Individual street photos are powerful. Series are transformative.

**Thematic Series Ideas:**
- A single intersection documented over a year
- Workers: the hands, tools, and faces of labor
- Waiting: people in the space between activities
- Light and shadow: the same street in different seasons
- Transit: life in motion between destinations

### Series Construction

1. Shoot with a theme in mind for 3-6 months minimum
2. Cull ruthlessly: a series of 15-25 images is stronger than 50
3. Sequence matters: opening image hooks, closing image resonates
4. Vary scale: wide establishing shots, medium scenes, tight details
5. Create visual rhythm: alternate busy and quiet images
6. Include at least one "rest" image - simpler, contemplative, giving the viewer a pause
7. Edit for tonal consistency: the series should feel like one body of work

### Presentation Formats

| Format | Best For | Considerations |
|--------|----------|----------------|
| Instagram grid | Ongoing work, building audience | Consistent aspect ratio and tone |
| Photo book (self-published) | Complete series, personal milestone | Blurb, Magcloud, or offset printing |
| Exhibition prints | Gallery, cafe show | Mat and frame consistently |
| Photo essay (blog/magazine) | Story with text | Pair images with brief captions |
| Zine | DIY, punk aesthetic | Cheap to produce, trade with other photographers |

## Daily Practice Framework

### The One-Hour Walk

Structure a productive street session:

**Minutes 1-10:** Walk without shooting. Observe light direction, busy areas, interesting backgrounds.
**Minutes 10-40:** Active shooting. Work scenes, follow interesting characters, revisit strong backgrounds.
**Minutes 40-55:** Slow down. Look for quieter, more contemplative moments you missed earlier.
**Minutes 55-60:** Review on camera. Delete obvious misses. Note what worked for next time.

### Skill-Building Exercises

| Exercise | Rules | Purpose |
|----------|-------|---------|
| One lens, one hour | Single focal length, no zooming | Learn to see in one frame |
| 36 exposures | Shoot only 36 frames (one roll) | Forces deliberation |
| Color hunting | Only photograph scenes dominated by one color | Train color awareness |
| Shadow play | Only shoot when shadows are the primary subject | Learn to see light |
| Sit and wait | Pick one spot, do not move for 30 minutes | Practice patience |
| No faces | Make compelling images without showing a face | Focus on gesture and context |

## Gear Minimalism

### The Street Kit

Less gear means more freedom and less conspicuousness.

**Essential:**
- One camera body (smaller is better; mirrorless over DSLR for street)
- One prime lens (35mm is the classic recommendation)
- One spare battery
- One small memory card wallet

**Optional:**
- Wrist strap (faster than neck strap for hip shooting)
- Small flash for fill or creative night work
- Business cards with your contact info

**Leave at home:**
- Camera bag (use jacket pockets or a small crossbody)
- Tripod
- Multiple lenses
- Lens hood (adds bulk and draws attention)

The best street camera is the one you always have with you. Phone cameras are legitimate street photography tools.
