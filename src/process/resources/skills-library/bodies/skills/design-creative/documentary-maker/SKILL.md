## Questions to Ask First

Before advising on any documentary project, establish:

1. What is the subject of your documentary? Can you summarize it in one sentence?
2. What is your relationship to the subject? (personal connection, journalistic interest, advocacy, curiosity)
3. What documentary style are you drawn to? (observational/fly-on-the-wall, interview-driven, narrated essay, hybrid)
4. Who are your subjects or interview candidates? Do you have access to them?
5. What is your intended runtime? (short doc 5-20 min, medium 30-45 min, feature 60-120 min)
6. Where do you want this shown? (YouTube, festivals, broadcast, streaming platforms, educational)
7. What is your budget and timeline?
8. What equipment do you have or can you access?
9. Have you made a documentary before?
10. Are there any sensitive, legal, or ethical concerns with your subject matter?

## Research Phase

### Primary Research
- **Interviews (pre-production)**: Conduct informal phone or video calls with potential subjects before committing to film. These are not on-camera interviews -- they are research conversations.
- **Site visits**: Visit locations before bringing a camera. Note lighting conditions, ambient sound, visual interest, access restrictions.
- **Document collection**: Gather letters, photographs, records, newspapers, social media posts, court documents, anything relevant.
- **Expert consultation**: Identify 2-3 subject matter experts who can provide context and fact-checking, even if they do not appear on camera.

### Secondary Research
- Academic papers and books on the topic
- Existing documentaries and journalism on the same or adjacent subjects
- Historical archives (Library of Congress, Internet Archive, local libraries)
- Statistics and data from credible sources
- Online communities and forums where your subject matter is discussed

### Building a Research Document
Create a living document that grows throughout production:
- Timeline of key events
- Character/subject profiles with background details
- Key themes and contradictions you have discovered
- Questions that remain unanswered
- Potential narrative threads
- List of visual evidence and archival material available

## Story Structure for Documentaries

### Finding Your Throughline
A documentary needs a central question or tension that pulls the audience forward. This is not always apparent at the start. Common throughlines:
- **A quest**: Someone is trying to achieve something (win a competition, find a person, solve a problem)
- **A transformation**: Someone or something is changing over time
- **A mystery**: Something unknown is being investigated
- **A conflict**: Two forces are in opposition
- **An argument**: A thesis is being presented and supported

### Three-Act Structure Adapted for Docs
- **Act 1 (Setup, ~25%)**: Introduce the world, the subjects, the central question. Hook the audience within the first 2-3 minutes. Establish stakes.
- **Act 2 (Complications, ~50%)**: Deepen the story. Introduce complications, contradictions, subplots. Alternate between different subjects or perspectives. Build tension.
- **Act 3 (Resolution, ~25%)**: Arrive at some form of answer, transformation, or acknowledgment that the question remains open. Provide emotional closure even if the subject is ongoing.

### Scene Construction
Every scene in a documentary should accomplish at least one of:
- Advance the narrative (move the story forward in time or understanding)
- Develop a character/subject (reveal something new about who they are)
- Establish mood or world (immerse the audience in the environment)
- Present evidence (support the documentary's throughline with facts)

### The Paper Edit
Before touching editing software, create a paper edit:
1. Transcribe all interviews (use automated tools like Otter.ai, Descript, or Whisper)
2. Highlight the strongest quotes and moments
3. Arrange highlighted sections into a narrative order on paper or in a document
4. Identify gaps where you need additional footage, interviews, or archival material
5. Write transition notes between sections
6. Read it aloud to test flow and pacing

## Interview Techniques

### Pre-Interview Preparation
- Research your subject thoroughly. Know their background better than they expect.
- Prepare 15-25 questions organized by theme, but be ready to abandon your list and follow the conversation.
- Share general topics with the subject beforehand so they can reflect, but do not share specific questions.
- Avoid yes/no questions. Start questions with "Tell me about...", "Describe...", "What was it like when...", "Walk me through..."

### On-Camera Interview Setup
- **Framing**: Medium close-up to close-up. Give looking room in the direction they are speaking. Rule of thirds for eye placement.
- **Eye Line**: Subject looks at the interviewer, not the camera (unless you specifically want direct address). Position yourself as close to the lens as possible to keep the eye line near center.
- **Background**: Contextual backgrounds are stronger than blank walls. A scientist in their lab, a musician in their studio. Avoid clutter that distracts.
- **Lighting**: Soft key light at 30-45 degrees. Fill to reduce harsh shadows. A subtle backlight separates the subject from the background. Consistent throughout the interview.
- **Audio**: Lavalier mic as primary. Boom or shotgun as backup. Always record both if possible. Monitor with headphones.
- **Two cameras**: If available, use a second angle (over-the-shoulder or different focal length) to give yourself editing flexibility.

### During the Interview
1. Build rapport before the camera rolls. Chat casually for 5-10 minutes.
2. Start with easy, comfortable questions to warm up.
3. Listen actively. The best follow-up questions come from listening, not from your list.
4. Embrace silence. Do not rush to fill pauses. Subjects often add their most honest thoughts after a moment of quiet.
5. Ask them to incorporate the question in their answer ("So the reason I started was..." instead of just "Because...").
6. If an answer is good but wordy, ask them to say it again more concisely.
7. Save the hardest or most emotional questions for later in the interview when trust is established.
8. At the end, always ask: "Is there anything I haven't asked that you think is important?"

### B-Roll Strategy for Interviews
For every interview, plan B-roll that:
- Shows the subject in their environment doing what they do
- Illustrates specific stories they tell (if they mention a place, go film it)
- Provides visual metaphors for abstract concepts
- Captures hands, details, textures, establishing shots of the location
- Includes sequences (a series of shots showing a complete action: arriving, working, finishing)

Shoot at least 3x more B-roll than you think you need. It is the most common shortage in documentary editing.

## Ethical Considerations

### Informed Consent
- Explain clearly what the documentary is about, where it will be shown, and how the subject will appear.
- Disclose if the documentary takes a particular point of view.
- Subjects have the right to understand the context before agreeing to participate.
- Be honest about your intentions. Deception undermines trust and can destroy a documentary's credibility.

### Release Forms
Every person who appears on camera should sign a release form. At minimum, the form covers:
- Name and contact information
- Grant of permission to use their likeness, voice, and statements
- The scope of use (all media, worldwide, in perpetuity -- or more limited)
- Whether they are receiving compensation
- Acknowledgment that they understand the nature of the project
- Signature and date

For minors, a parent or guardian must sign. For subjects in vulnerable situations, consider having an independent advocate review the release with them.

### Vulnerable Subjects
When documenting people in crisis, poverty, illness, or other vulnerable situations:
- Prioritize their dignity over your footage
- Ask yourself: would you be comfortable if this person saw the final edit?
- Offer to show relevant sections before release
- Consider the long-term impact on their lives
- Avoid exploitation disguised as awareness-raising
- Be especially cautious with children

### Fairness and Balance
- Represent subjects' views accurately. Do not edit quotes out of context to change meaning.
- If your documentary criticizes individuals or organizations, give them the opportunity to respond on camera.
- Distinguish clearly between fact and opinion in narration.
- Disclose conflicts of interest.

## Archival Footage and Materials

### Sources for Archival Content
- **Public domain**: Internet Archive, Library of Congress, National Archives, Prelinger Archives
- **Creative Commons**: Wikimedia Commons, Flickr (filter by license)
- **Licensed stock**: Shutterstock Editorial, Getty, AP Archive, British Pathe
- **News archives**: Contact networks directly for licensing
- **Personal archives**: Home videos, photographs, letters from subjects and their families
- **Government footage**: NASA, NOAA, military archives (often public domain)

### Legal Considerations
- Public domain material is free to use but verify the status
- Creative Commons licenses have specific requirements (attribution, non-commercial, share-alike)
- Licensed footage requires payment per use, often per second, with rates varying by distribution
- Fair use is a legal defense, not a right. Consult a media lawyer for significant uses.
- Photographs and artwork may have separate copyright from the physical object

### Working with Archival Material
- Scan at the highest resolution possible
- Maintain original aspect ratios (do not stretch 4:3 to 16:9)
- Use the Ken Burns effect (slow pan and zoom) sparingly and purposefully
- Color-correct archival footage to match your documentary's palette, or leave it distinctly aged for contrast
- Always credit sources in end credits

## Narration and Voice-Over

### When to Use Narration
- To provide context the audience cannot get from interviews or visuals alone
- To bridge time gaps or transitions between sections
- To present data, statistics, or factual background
- To offer the filmmaker's perspective in first-person essay docs

### When to Avoid Narration
- When interviews and visuals tell the story effectively on their own
- When it creates a lecture feel that distances the audience from the subjects
- When observational style is your goal

### Writing Documentary Narration
- Write for the ear, not the eye. Short sentences. Simple language.
- Read every line aloud before recording. If it sounds written, rewrite it.
- Show, don't tell. If the visuals already communicate something, the narration should add, not repeat.
- Use present tense for immediacy when describing events on screen.
- Avoid cliches ("little did they know", "in a world where", "but everything was about to change").

### Recording Narration
- Record in a quiet, treated room (blankets on walls if no studio available)
- Use a large-diaphragm condenser mic (Rode NT1, Audio-Technica AT2020) on a boom stand with a pop filter
- Record at 24-bit, 48kHz
- Have the narrator do a full read-through first, then punch in corrections
- Direct the narrator toward conversational delivery, not broadcast announcer voice

## Post-Production for Documentaries

### Assembly Process
1. Transcribe everything. Tag quotes by theme.
2. Build the paper edit from transcripts.
3. Lay down interview audio first, building the narrative spine.
4. Add B-roll over interview audio to illustrate and pace.
5. Place archival material where it supports the story.
6. Add narration to fill gaps.
7. Layer music and ambient sound.

### Pacing
- Alternate between intensity and breathing room
- Use observational sequences (no dialogue, just ambient sound and visuals) to give the audience time to process
- Vary the rhythm: quick montage sections vs. long-held interview moments
- The opening 3 minutes determine whether the audience stays

### Music in Documentaries
- Original score gives you the most control and avoids licensing issues
- Licensed music must be cleared for all distribution platforms
- Royalty-free libraries: Artlist, Epidemic Sound, Musicbed
- Music should support emotion, not manipulate. If you remove the music and the scene loses all its power, the music is doing too much.

## Festival Submission Strategy

### Preparing Your Submission
- **Screener**: High-quality H.264 file or secure streaming link (Vimeo with password)
- **Synopsis**: 50-word, 100-word, and 250-word versions ready
- **Director's statement**: 200-400 words on why you made this film and your approach
- **Stills**: 3-5 high-resolution production stills (not screenshots from footage)
- **Poster/Key art**: Professional one-sheet
- **Trailer**: 90-120 second trailer

### Festival Tiers
- **Tier 1**: Sundance, TIFF, Berlin, IDFA, Tribeca, SXSW. Submit early, long shot but worth trying.
- **Tier 2**: Hot Docs, Full Frame, True/False, DOC NYC, Sheffield. Strong documentary-specific festivals.
- **Tier 3**: Regional and niche festivals aligned with your subject matter. Higher acceptance rates and valuable audiences.
- **Online**: YouTube Originals, Short of the Week, Vimeo Staff Picks for shorter works.

### Submission Tips
- Use FilmFreeway and Withoutabox to manage submissions
- Budget $500-$2000 for submission fees for a serious festival run
- Submit to festivals that program your style and subject matter
- Apply for fee waivers (many festivals offer them to first-time filmmakers)
- Early deadlines are cheaper -- plan your submission calendar in advance
- A world premiere at a strong festival significantly increases distribution interest
- If rejected from top festivals, do not self-distribute immediately. Work down the tiers first.

## Distribution After Festivals

### Self-Distribution
- YouTube (free, massive audience, harder to monetize)
- Vimeo On Demand (better margins, smaller audience)
- Your own website with embedded player and payment

### Broadcast and Streaming
- PBS (POV, Independent Lens, Frontline for investigative)
- Netflix, HBO, Hulu (competitive, usually requires sales agent or producer's rep)
- Regional broadcasters (BBC, Channel 4, Arte for European)

### Educational Distribution
- Kanopy, Swank, Alexander Street
- Significant revenue stream for issue-driven documentaries
- Often requires closed captioning and educational guides

### Impact Campaigns
For advocacy documentaries, pair distribution with:
- Community screenings with panel discussions
- Educational curriculum guides
- Partnerships with nonprofits and advocacy organizations
- Social media campaigns tied to the documentary's release
