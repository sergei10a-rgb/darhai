## Camera Settings for Landscapes

### Standard Landscape Settings

| Parameter | Setting | Reasoning |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| Mode | Manual or Aperture Priority | Full control over depth of field |
| Aperture | f/8 - f/11 | Lens sweet spot, maximum sharpness |
| ISO | 100 (base) | Minimum noise, maximum dynamic range |
| Shutter | Varies | Determined by aperture and light |
| Focus | Manual, hyperfocal distance | Maximum depth of field |
| Metering | Evaluative/Matrix | Good starting point, adjust with exposure comp |
| White Balance | Daylight or Kelvin | Consistency across a series |
| File Format | RAW | Maximum editing latitude |
| Image Stabilization | OFF on tripod | Prevents feedback vibration |

### Hyperfocal Distance Quick Reference

Focusing at the hyperfocal distance maximizes depth of field from half that distance to infinity.

| Focal Length | f/8 | f/11 | f/16 |
|-------------|------|------|------|
| 16mm | 1.1m | 0.8m | 0.5m |
| 24mm | 2.4m | 1.7m | 1.2m |
| 35mm | 5.1m | 3.7m | 2.5m |
| 50mm | 10.4m | 7.6m | 5.2m |

*Values approximate for full-frame sensors. Multiply focal length by 1.5 for APS-C.*

### Focus Stacking for Maximum Sharpness

When hyperfocal distance is not enough:

1. Set camera on tripod, compose shot
2. Focus on nearest foreground element, take exposure
3. Shift focus to middle distance, take exposure
4. Shift focus to infinity, take exposure
5. Blend in Photoshop using Auto-Blend Layers
6. Use 3-5 frames for most scenes, more for extreme depth

## Composition Principles

### Rule of Thirds

- Place horizon on upper or lower third line, not center
- Position key elements at intersection points
- Works as a starting point; break it intentionally

### Leading Lines

- Roads, rivers, fences, ridgelines guide the eye into the frame
- Diagonal lines create dynamic energy
- Converging lines add depth
- S-curves are the most pleasing leading line shape

### Foreground Interest

- The most important compositional tool in landscape photography
- Rocks, flowers, patterns, water give the viewer an entry point
- Get low: position camera close to foreground elements
- Use wide-angle lenses to exaggerate foreground-to-background relationship
- Foreground should complement, not compete with, the main subject

### Layering and Depth

- Create visual layers: foreground, midground, background
- Atmospheric haze between layers enhances sense of distance
- Overlapping elements reinforce three-dimensionality
- Side light separates layers with highlight and shadow

### Framing

- Use natural frames: tree branches, rock arches, doorways
- Frames direct attention and add context
- Partial frames work; the element does not need to surround the subject
- Dark frames around bright subjects are most effective

### Simplification

- Remove distracting elements by changing position or focal length
- Negative space gives the eye a place to rest
- Minimalist compositions can be the most powerful
- If an element does not serve the image, eliminate it

### Panoramic Composition

- Plan the full sweep before shooting
- Overlap frames by 30-40%
- Use manual exposure and focus across all frames
- Level the tripod head precisely
- Shoot in portrait orientation for higher resolution panoramas
- Stitch in Lightroom, Photoshop, or PTGui

## Timing and Light

### Golden Hour

- 30-60 minutes after sunrise and before sunset
- Warm color temperature (3000-4000K)
- Low angle creates long shadows and texture
- Best for warm, inviting landscape images
- Scout locations during midday, return for golden hour

### Blue Hour

- 20-40 minutes before sunrise and after sunset
- Cool color temperature (7000-10000K)
- Soft, even illumination with no harsh shadows
- City lights begin to glow, mixing warm and cool
- Requires longer exposures; tripod essential

### Magic Light Conditions

**Alpenglow**: Mountains glow pink/orange after sunset as atmosphere scatters light
**Fog and mist**: Simplifies scenes, creates layers, diffuses light
**Storm light**: Dramatic skies with shafts of sunlight breaking through
**Rainbows**: Appear opposite the sun after rain; shoot with sun behind you
**First snow**: Clean, high-contrast scenes with fresh texture

### Midday Solutions

- Shoot waterfalls and forests (canopy diffuses overhead light)
- Photograph desert slot canyons (reflected light creates color)
- Use polarizing filter to manage reflections and deepen skies
- Seek out intimate landscapes and macro details
- Convert to black and white where high contrast works

### Season Planning

| Season | Opportunities | Challenges |
|--------|--------------|------------|
| Spring | Wildflowers, waterfalls, fresh green | Unpredictable weather, mud |
| Summer | Long golden hours, Milky Way at altitude | Harsh midday, crowds |
| Autumn | Color foliage, mist, low sun | Short peak color window |
| Winter | Snow, ice, stark compositions, low sun all day | Cold gear management, short days |

## Long Exposure Techniques

### Smooth Water

- Shutter speed: 1-30 seconds for silk effect
- Use ND filter (6-10 stops) in daylight
- Tripod absolutely essential
- Cable release or 2-second timer to avoid camera shake
- Check composition without filter first, then add filter

### Cloud Streaks

- Shutter speed: 30 seconds to 4 minutes
- Requires 10-stop ND filter (ND1000) or stronger
- Clouds must be moving; check wind at cloud altitude
- Bulb mode for exposures beyond 30 seconds
- Calculate exposure: if correct exposure is 1/30 without filter, with 10-stop ND it becomes 30 seconds

### ND Filter Exposure Calculation

| Without Filter | 6-stop ND | 10-stop ND |
|---------------|-----------|------------|
| 1/500 | 1/8 | 2 sec |
| 1/250 | 1/4 | 4 sec |
| 1/125 | 1/2 | 8 sec |
| 1/60 | 1 sec | 16 sec |
| 1/30 | 2 sec | 30 sec |
| 1/15 | 4 sec | 60 sec |

### Star Trails

- Shutter speed: stacking method preferred (multiple 30-second exposures)
- Use intervalometer for continuous shooting
- ISO 400-800 for individual frames
- Stack 100-300 frames for full circular trails
- Point camera at Polaris (north) for concentric circles
- Process with StarStaX or Photoshop

### Light Painting

- Combine long exposure with handheld light source
- Illuminate foreground rocks, trees, or structures
- Warm-toned flashlight adds inviting glow
- Multiple exposures with different light positions, blend in post
- Practice timing: 2-5 seconds of illumination per area

## Filters for Landscape Photography

### Circular Polarizer (CPL)

- Reduces reflections on water and wet surfaces
- Deepens blue skies (maximum effect at 90 degrees from sun)
- Increases color saturation in foliage
- Removes 1-2 stops of light
- Essential filter; always carry one

### Graduated Neutral Density (GND)

- Balances bright sky with darker foreground
- Soft-edge: gradual transition, versatile for uneven horizons
- Hard-edge: sharp transition, best for flat horizons (ocean)
- Reverse-grad: darkest at horizon, lighter above, ideal for sunset
- Available in 2-stop and 3-stop strengths
- Square/rectangular system (e.g., 100mm) with holder preferred

### Solid Neutral Density (ND)

- Reduces light evenly across the frame
- 3-stop: subtle motion blur
- 6-stop: moderate long exposure (1-10 seconds)
- 10-stop: extreme long exposure (30+ seconds)
- 15-stop: ultra-long exposure (minutes)
- Check for color cast; quality varies by brand

## Post-Processing Workflow

### Step 1: Import and Organize

- Import RAW files to Lightroom or Capture One
- Add keywords, location data, and ratings
- Create collections by location or project
- Reject obvious failures immediately

### Step 2: Global Adjustments

1. Set white balance (use eyedropper on neutral area or set Kelvin)
2. Adjust exposure to protect highlights
3. Recover highlights (pull back to -50 to -100 if needed)
4. Open shadows (+30 to +60 for typical landscape)
5. Set white and black points using Alt/Option clipping preview
6. Add clarity (+15 to +30 for landscape texture)
7. Add vibrance (+10 to +25) over saturation for natural look

### Step 3: Local Adjustments

- Graduated filter to darken sky or brighten foreground
- Radial filter to draw attention to focal point
- Adjustment brush for specific areas (brighten water, darken edges)
- Luminance masking for targeted adjustments by brightness

### Step 4: Color Work

- HSL panel: fine-tune individual color channels
- Shift greens toward yellow for natural foliage
- Desaturate distracting colors
- Split toning: warm highlights, cool shadows for depth
- Calibration panel: subtle shifts in primary colors

### Step 5: Detail and Output

- Sharpening: Amount 60-80, Radius 1.0, Detail 25-40
- Masking: hold Alt/Option while dragging to sharpen only edges
- Noise reduction: Luminance 20-40 if needed (high ISO shots)
- Lens corrections: enable profile, remove chromatic aberration
- Crop to final composition
- Export: TIFF for print, JPEG for web (sRGB, 2048px long edge)

### Advanced: Exposure Blending

When dynamic range exceeds a single exposure:

1. Bracket exposures: -2, 0, +2 EV
2. In Lightroom: Photo > Photo Merge > HDR
3. Or manually blend in Photoshop using luminosity masks
4. Luminosity masks target specific brightness ranges
5. Paint in bright exposure for shadows, dark exposure for highlights
6. Result: natural-looking image with full tonal range

## Field Workflow Checklist

### Night Before

- Check weather forecast and cloud cover predictions
- Plan shooting location using maps and scouting apps
- Charge all batteries (camera, headlamp, phone)
- Format memory cards (after backing up)
- Pack gear: camera, lenses, tripod, filters, remote, headlamp
- Set alarm with buffer time for travel and setup

### On Location

1. Arrive 45 minutes before target light
2. Scout compositions without camera first
3. Set up tripod, level it, compose
4. Take test exposure, check histogram
5. Fine-tune composition and focus
6. Wait for the light, adjust settings as conditions change
7. Shoot variations: horizontal, vertical, different focal lengths
8. Review histogram after key shots (do not trust LCD brightness)
9. Do not pack up early; light changes until well past golden hour

### Back Home

1. Back up cards to two separate drives immediately
2. Import to catalog software
3. Quick review and flag best frames
4. Detailed edit of selects within 48 hours while memory is fresh
5. Archive RAW files with metadata

## Quick Reference Cheat Sheet

**Sunrise landscape in 5 steps:**
1. Arrive 45 minutes early, find foreground interest
2. Set tripod low, wide-angle lens, f/11, ISO 100
3. Focus at hyperfocal distance using manual focus
4. Expose for highlights, recover shadows in post
5. Shoot continuously as light changes; the best moment is fleeting

**Waterfall long exposure in 5 steps:**
1. Mount camera on tripod, compose with foreground rocks
2. Attach 6-stop ND filter
3. Set f/11, ISO 100, calculate shutter speed (typically 1-4 seconds)
4. Use cable release or timer to trigger
5. Check result, adjust exposure time for desired smoothness
