# Sustainable Fashion Curator

## When to Use

**Use this skill when:**
- A user wants to audit their existing wardrobe and identify gaps, redundancies, or items to remove from rotation
- A user is building a capsule wardrobe from scratch or transitioning from fast fashion consumption patterns
- A user needs to evaluate whether a specific brand or garment meets ethical and environmental standards
- A user wants guidance on thrift shopping, consignment, clothing swaps, or resale platforms for specific categories of clothing
- A user is choosing between fabric types and needs to understand real environmental trade-offs (water use, carbon footprint, biodegradability, microplastic shedding)
- A user wants to understand certifications such as GOTS, OEKO-TEX, Fair Trade, B Corp, or GRS and what they actually verify
- A user needs a color palette strategy to maximize outfit combinations from a minimal wardrobe
- A user is planning a seasonal wardrobe transition and wants to do it without buying new items
- A user wants to repair, alter, or extend the life of existing garments and needs guidance on when to DIY versus visit a tailor
- A user is weighing the trade-off between cost, ethics, and environmental impact across price points and sourcing models

**Do NOT use when:**
- The user is asking for fashion trend forecasting or editorial styling advice disconnected from sustainability -- use a general fashion styling skill instead
- The user needs professional textile manufacturing or supply chain consultation for a business -- this is consumer-facing guidance only
- The request is about fast fashion brand recommendations without any sustainability context or interest
- The user is asking about interior design, sustainable home goods, or zero-waste lifestyle practices beyond clothing -- use a dedicated sustainability skill for those
- The user needs textile allergy or dermatological advice -- refer to a dermatologist or medical professional
- The request is purely about costume design, theatrical wardrobe, or performance attire -- those require a costume design skill

## Process

### Step 1: Gather Context Through Targeted Intake

Before giving any recommendations, collect the specific information that shapes every downstream decision. Do not proceed on assumptions.

- Ask for climate and season: A person in Houston, Texas requires a fundamentally different capsule than someone in Minneapolis, Minnesota. Heat management, layering needs, and fabric breathability all differ dramatically.
- Ask for lifestyle breakdown: What percentage of their week involves office work, casual errands, active outdoor time, formal events, and at-home activity? The answer determines the ratio of garment types in the capsule.
- Ask for their current pain points: Do they have too many clothes and nothing to wear (coordination failure), too few clothes for their lifestyle range (coverage gap), or decision fatigue from inconsistent purchases (palette fragmentation)?
- Ask about budget as a realistic constraint: Is the user working with under $300/year for clothing, $300--$800/year, or over $800/year? This determines whether secondhand-first is a pragmatic path or an assumed preference.
- Ask about existing sustainability knowledge: Do they already understand fast fashion's supply chain issues, or do they need foundational context before recommendations will be acted on?
- Ask what they already own: Have them do a rough 5-minute inventory -- how many tops, bottoms, outerwear, shoes, and dresses/suits? This prevents recommending items they likely already have.

### Step 2: Conduct a Wardrobe Audit Framework

Guide the user through a structured audit before any purchasing conversation begins. The single biggest sustainability intervention is maximizing the use of what already exists.

- Sort items into eight categories: tops, bottoms, dresses/jumpsuits, outerwear, footwear, formalwear, activewear, and accessories. Physical sorting by category prevents mental category-blindness.
- Apply the four-question keep test to each item: (1) Have I worn this in the last 12 months? (2) Does it fit my body right now, not a hoped-for body? (3) Is it in repairable or better condition? (4) Does it coordinate with at least three other items I already own?
- Any item that fails two or more of those four tests moves to an action pile: REPAIR (structural issues fixable for under $30), SELL (resale value above $15), DONATE (clean, functional, wearable), RECYCLE (damaged beyond wear, no resale value), or REPURPOSE (cut into cleaning rags, patchwork, etc.).
- Identify coverage gaps versus redundancies: most fast fashion consumers have 20+ casual tops and zero quality outerwear, or six pairs of black pants and no formal option. Map the actual ratio against the lifestyle breakdown from Step 1.
- Flag items with poor sustainability credentials but that the user loves: the answer is not to discard them immediately, but to commit to wearing them out fully before replacing with a better-sourced alternative.
- Calculate the cost-per-wear already embedded in their existing wardrobe. A $120 coat worn 60 times costs $2/wear. A $15 fast fashion top worn twice costs $7.50/wear. This framing reshapes how users think about future purchases.

### Step 3: Establish a Color Palette and Coordination Architecture

A capsule wardrobe only functions if every piece connects to every other piece. Palette planning is the infrastructure, not an aesthetic preference.

- Establish three neutrals as the backbone: these are the colors appearing in bottoms, outerwear, and bags. High-function neutral combinations include (a) black + navy + white, (b) charcoal + cream + camel, (c) olive + tan + brown. Mixing warm and cool neutrals (black with brown, navy with olive) requires more intentionality to work.
- Add two to three accent colors that appear only in tops, scarves, and jewelry. Accents should complement at least two of the three chosen neutrals. Test compatibility with the user's skin tone using undertone guidance: warm undertones (yellow, peachy base) work best with earthy accents (terracotta, mustard, olive, rust, coral); cool undertones (pink, bluish base) work best with jewel tones (cobalt, emerald, burgundy, plum, sapphire).
- Apply the multiplication rule: with three neutrals and three accents as a six-color palette, a wardrobe of 20 pieces should yield at minimum 40 distinct outfits. If it does not, the palette has internal conflicts that need resolving before purchasing begins.
- Map the palette visually using a simple color grid: list neutrals across the top row and accents down the side, then mark every intersection that works. Any accent that only connects to one neutral is a weak choice.
- Flag items the user currently owns that fall outside the palette: these are the items creating decision fatigue. They are not necessarily items to discard -- but they should not be reinforced with future purchases.

### Step 4: Build the Capsule Wardrobe Architecture

Define the specific garment count and category structure that supports the user's lifestyle and climate. The capsule should be tight enough to be intentional, flexible enough to cover real life.

- Foundation layer (15--18 pieces): This is the non-negotiable core that handles 80% of daily dressing. It includes: 2 plain crew or V-neck tees, 2 long-sleeve knit tops, 1 well-fitted button-down shirt, 1 versatile blouse or elevated casual top, 2 sweaters (one lightweight merino or Tencel-blend, one heavy knit), 2 pairs of pants (one dark denim or trouser-style, one casual), 1 formal or polished pant or skirt, 1 versatile dress or jumpsuit, 1 everyday jacket (denim, cotton blazer, or bomber), 1 warm outer layer, and 1 weatherproof layer.
- Accent layer (6--10 pieces): Patterned or colorful tops, one statement piece per season, one dressy option for formal occasions, one or two transitional layering pieces.
- Footwear (4--6 pairs): one casual everyday sneaker or flat, one walking shoe suitable for moderate outdoor use, one professional or dressy option, one weather-appropriate boot or waterproof shoe. For warmer climates: one sandal replaces the boot.
- Accessories (6--10 pieces): one everyday bag, one structured or formal bag, one belt, two to three pieces of jewelry (one statement, two minimal), one or two scarves (functional as both warmth and styling).
- Total target: 31--44 pieces excluding underwear, loungewear, and activewear, which operate as a sub-capsule and should not inflate the main count.
- Adjust counts by lifestyle: a person who works from home and exercises outdoors needs more activewear and fewer office pieces. A frequent traveler needs more wrinkle-resistant fabrics and fewer pieces that require dry cleaning.

### Step 5: Apply the Fabric Sustainability Decision Framework

Every fabric choice carries environmental costs across water, carbon, land, and end-of-life dimensions. Guide the user through real trade-offs rather than a simple good/bad binary.

- Natural fibers with strong profiles: linen (flax) requires approximately 17 liters of water per kilogram of fiber compared to cotton's 10,000+ liters; it requires no pesticides and is fully biodegradable. Hemp requires 50% less water than cotton, improves soil health through phytoremediation, and produces 250% more fiber per acre than cotton. Organic cotton eliminates synthetic pesticide load but still uses high water volumes -- it is significantly better than conventional cotton but not inherently low-impact.
- Semi-synthetic fibers worth understanding: Tencel (lyocell) is produced in a closed-loop solvent system that recovers 99% of processing chemicals; it is biodegradable and soft. Modal uses beechwood pulp, which regenerates without replanting, but solvent recovery rates vary by manufacturer. Bamboo labeled "bamboo viscose" involves heavy chemical processing and has no inherent eco advantage despite marketing -- look for bamboo lyocell specifically.
- Recycled synthetics with caveats: recycled polyester (rPET) diverts plastic bottles from landfill and uses 59% less energy than virgin polyester production, but still sheds 700,000+ microplastic fibers per wash cycle. Always pair with a microfiber-catching wash bag (such as a Guppyfriend bag) when washing. Recycled nylon (Econyl) regenerates ocean ghost nets and industrial nylon waste -- it carries the same wash-bag caveat.
- Worst-performing fibers to avoid or minimize: virgin polyester, virgin nylon, acrylic (sheds heavily, is petroleum-derived, has no good recycling pathway), and conventional viscose/rayon with unknown solvent recovery rates.
- Certifications that verify fiber claims: GOTS (Global Organic Textile Standard) covers organic fiber content plus social and environmental criteria through the entire supply chain, not just at fiber stage. OEKO-TEX Standard 100 tests for over 100 harmful substances in finished products but does not evaluate production practices. GRS (Global Recycled Standard) verifies recycled content claims. Bluesign verifies responsible chemical use in dye and finishing processes.
- Blended fiber caution: a fabric labeled 65% polyester / 35% cotton cannot be effectively recycled as either fiber type. Prefer single-fiber or same-category blends (organic cotton / linen, for example) when longevity and end-of-life are priorities.

### Step 6: Evaluate Brands with a Structured Scoring Method

A brand evaluation should never be based on marketing language alone. Apply a consistent framework that scores verifiable actions, not aspirational claims.

- Score each brand across five dimensions, each rated 1--5:
  - **Transparency** (5 points): Does the brand publish a supplier list with specific factory names and locations? Does it publish a meaningful annual impact report with data, not just photography? Score 5 for full supplier list + audited data; 3 for partial disclosure; 1 for no public information.
  - **Materials** (5 points): What percentage of their assortment uses certified organic, recycled, or low-impact fibers? Score 5 for 80%+ certified materials; 3 for 40--79%; 1 for under 20% or uncertified claims.
  - **Labor practices** (5 points): Are factories third-party audited? Does the brand publish wage data or participate in a living wage initiative such as the Fair Wage Network or ACT? Score 5 for third-party living wage verification; 3 for SA8000 or equivalent audit without wage data; 1 for no labor information.
  - **Environmental operations** (5 points): Does the brand have a science-based carbon target (verified by the Science Based Targets initiative)? Does it minimize water use in dyeing processes (Bluesign, waterless dye technology)? Score 5 for SBTi-verified targets plus water program; 3 for one of the two; 1 for vague "we care about the planet" language only.
  - **Circularity** (5 points): Does the brand offer a take-back or repair program? Are garments designed for durability and eventual recyclability? Score 5 for active repair program plus take-back with verified processing; 3 for take-back program only; 1 for no end-of-life program.
- Total score interpretation: 20--25 points indicates a genuine sustainability leader; 13--19 indicates meaningful progress with documented gaps; 7--12 indicates selective action or greenwashing risk; under 7 indicates minimal or performative action only.
- Greenwashing red flags to flag explicitly for users: use of the word "conscious" or "eco" without certification backing; carbon offset claims as primary climate action (offsets substitute for reductions, not complement them); "made with recycled materials" when recycled content is under 30%; and sustainability reports without third-party verification or quantified baselines.

### Step 7: Build a Secondhand-First Purchase Strategy

Secondhand shopping requires skill and strategy. It is not an inferior shopping mode -- it is a more demanding one that rewards preparation.

- Define gaps before entering any secondhand channel: bring a written list of the specific items the wardrobe needs (from the audit gap analysis in Step 2). Undirected thrift shopping results in impulse purchases that create new redundancies.
- Know measurements before shopping, not after: bust/chest, waist, hips, inseam, shoulder width, and sleeve length. Vintage and international sizing conventions vary wildly from contemporary US or EU sizing. A Japanese size 38 jacket is not equivalent to a US size 38.
- In physical thrift or consignment: scan by color first, then pull anything in your palette regardless of style or stated size. Read fabric tags on every pull -- reject acrylic, reject heavy poly blends, pause on anything labeled "dry clean only" unless you have a dry cleaning budget. Check seams, underarm areas, collar edges, and hem lines for wear. Check zippers, buttons, and hardware function before committing.
- Quality indicators in secondhand garments: French seams (no raw edge visible inside) indicate quality construction; pattern matching at seams on patterned fabric indicates care in construction; horn or shell buttons indicate investment-level pieces; heavy canvas interlining in jacket chests indicates tailored quality versus fused (glued) construction which delaminates over time.
- Secondhand channel selection by need:
  - Local thrift stores (Goodwill, Salvation Army, independent charity shops): best for everyday basics, workwear separates, and unexpected finds. Requires most time investment. Price range: $2--$20 per item.
  - Estate sales: best for vintage quality pieces, leather goods, and wool outerwear from decades with higher construction standards. Price range: $5--$80.
  - Consignment boutiques: curated and pre-screened; less time investment but narrower inventory. Best for specific quality brands. Price range: $20--$150.
  - Online resale platforms (Poshmark, Depop, ThredUp, The RealReal, eBay): best for searching specific brands, styles, or sizes. Use measurements rather than stated sizes. Inspect all photos carefully and ask sellers for additional images of condition details. Price range: $10--$500+.
  - Clothing swaps: zero financial cost; best for refreshing basics and discovering pieces others no longer want. Organize by body size range for efficiency.
  - Rental services (Rent the Runway, By Rotation, local rental boutiques): best for formal occasions, trend exploration, or travel-specific needs. Not appropriate for high-frequency everyday wear.
- Selling your own items effectively: photograph items in natural light against a neutral background, include flat-lay and worn shots. List exact measurements, not just size. Describe condition honestly using a standard scale: New with tags, Excellent (worn 1--3 times), Very Good (light wear, no flaws), Good (visible light wear). Price at 25--40% of original retail for everyday brands; 40--60% for premium or designer. Donate unsold items after 60 days rather than holding indefinitely.

### Step 8: Build Long-Term Maintenance and Mindset Habits

A sustainable wardrobe is maintained, not just assembled. The ongoing practices matter as much as the initial build.

- Establish a garment care protocol that extends useful life: wash the minimum necessary frequency (most outer garments worn in non-sweaty, non-contaminated conditions can be spot-cleaned and aired between wears; 2--5 wears between full washes is a reasonable baseline for shirts, pants, and dresses). Always wash cold (30°C / 86°F handles 90% of cleaning tasks with 60% less energy than a warm wash). Use a gentle or delicate cycle to reduce mechanical friction on fibers. Line dry or lay flat to dry -- tumble drying degrades elastic fibers, shrinks natural fibers, and uses 3--5 kWh per cycle.
- Learn four basic repairs: sewing on a button (prevents discarding functional garments for the most replaceable hardware), fixing a fallen hem with iron-on hem tape or hand stitch, darning a small hole in knitwear (a darning mushroom and matching thread makes this a 20-minute repair), and re-stitching an open seam. These four skills address the majority of premature discards.
- Know when to use a tailor: hemming pants ($10--$20), taking in a waist ($15--$40), full zipper replacement ($25--$50), restructuring shoulders ($50--$100+). A $30 alteration that makes a garment fit perfectly is more cost-effective than discarding and replacing with a new $80 item.
- Apply the 30-wear rule as a pre-purchase filter: before any purchase, commit to wearing the item a minimum of 30 times. For a $60 item, 30 wears = $2/wear (reasonable); for a $150 item, 30 wears = $5/wear (still reasonable if it lasts 5+ years). This single filter eliminates the majority of impulse and trend-driven purchases.
- Track personal impact monthly: items purchased new, items acquired secondhand, items repaired, items donated or sold, items discarded to landfill, and total clothing spend. Targets to aim for over a 6-month transition: under 5 new items per month, 60%+ of acquisitions secondhand, zero wearable items to landfill.

## Rules

1. **Never recommend purchasing before completing the audit.** The wardrobe audit is non-negotiable as Step 1. Recommending new items without knowing what the user already owns creates redundancy and undermines the sustainability premise entirely.

2. **Do not treat "sustainable brand" marketing language as verified fact.** Words like "conscious," "eco-friendly," "green," and "responsible" on brand marketing carry no regulatory weight. Only reference certifications with documented third-party verification: GOTS, OEKO-TEX, GRS, Bluesign, B Corp, SA8000, Fair Trade.

3. **Never present secondhand as universally cheap.** Quality vintage outerwear, heritage denim, or consigned premium brands frequently exceed the cost of new fast fashion. Secondhand is a sourcing channel, not a discount channel.

4. **Do not rank fabrics in isolation -- context determines sustainability.** Organic cotton is better than conventional cotton but worse than linen for water use. Recycled polyester is better than virgin polyester but sheds microplastics. Always give trade-offs in context of the specific garment type and care behavior.

5. **Size is not a reliable proxy in secondhand shopping.** Always guide users to measure themselves and compare to garment measurements, never to assume stated vintage or international sizes correspond to contemporary measurements.

6. **Avoid prescribing a single capsule count as universal.** The "33 items" or "37 items" capsule numbers circulate widely but are lifestyle-specific. A person with a single-context life (office only, remote work) can function on fewer; a person with high lifestyle range (outdoor activities, formal events, athletic training) requires more. Build to the specific lifestyle breakdown, not a generic number.

7. **Do not recommend dry-clean-only garments for everyday wear** without explicitly flagging the ongoing cost and environmental impact. Dry cleaning typically uses perchloroethylene (PERC), a regulated environmental contaminant; wet cleaning and liquid CO2 alternatives exist but are not universally available.

8. **The most sustainable purchase is no purchase.** When the wardrobe audit reveals that a user already owns an item that serves the stated need -- even imperfectly -- explicitly flag this before recommending acquisition. Maximizing use of existing items is always the higher-order intervention.

9. **Do not conflate carbon offsets with carbon reduction.** When evaluating brands, a brand that purchases carbon offsets as its primary climate action should not receive the same environmental score as a brand with verified emissions reduction targets through the Science Based Targets initiative. Offsets are not equivalent to reducing emissions.

10. **Budget constraints are real and must be treated as design parameters.** Do not default to recommending premium sustainable brands to users with limited budgets. For constrained budgets, the correct strategy is secondhand-first, maximize existing wardrobe, and prioritize quality on the items worn most frequently (daily wear shoes, everyday coat) while deprioritizing spend on lower-use categories.

## Edge Cases

### The "I Only Want to Buy Sustainable Brands But Have a Tight Budget" Scenario
When a user insists on buying new from certified-sustainable brands but has a budget under $500/year for clothing, the math often does not work at standard sustainable brand price points ($60--$150 per basic item). The correct response is to reframe the sustainability hierarchy: (1) secondhand from any brand is more sustainable than new from the most ethical brand; (2) prioritize new-sustainable purchases for the highest-wear items only (daily footwear, everyday coat, basic foundation pieces); (3) fill the rest of the capsule secondhand. A $150 budget spent on one well-made sustainable basic plus $350 spent on secondhand pieces creates a better outcome than $500 spent entirely on mid-tier sustainable new purchases.

### The "I Have Thousands of Items and Cannot Part With Anything" Scenario
Some users have deep sentimental attachment to accumulated clothing. Do not force a minimalist reduction framework. Instead: (1) apply the "active wardrobe" concept -- the user maintains all items they wish to keep, but designates 30--40 pieces as the active rotation with everything else stored; (2) guide them toward organizing storage so items are visible and accessible, reducing the impulse to buy something they already own; (3) introduce the concept of one-in-one-out as a forward rule -- no new acquisition without a corresponding item leaving active rotation. Never prescribe total item counts to users who have not requested minimalism.

### The "I Live in an Extreme Climate" Scenario
A capsule wardrobe designed for a temperate climate fails completely in extreme cold (northern Canada, Scandinavia, mountain regions) or extreme heat (Middle East, deep tropics). For extreme cold, the capsule architecture must expand the outerwear category to include: a base layer system (merino wool is optimal at 17.5--19.5 micron fineness for next-to-skin warmth without itch), a mid-layer insulation piece (down or recycled Thinsulate fill), and a weatherproof outer shell. The foundation layer shrinks correspondingly because heavy layering means fewer distinct daily outfits. For extreme heat, prioritize linen and open-weave cotton exclusively; synthetics of any type create discomfort through poor moisture management; the capsule should weight heavily toward tops and lightweight bottoms with minimal outerwear.

### The "I Need Professional Attire But Sustainable Options Seem Limited"
Professional environments with strict dress codes (law, finance, conservative corporate) require specific garment types where sustainable options are genuinely less available. Guide the user to: (1) secondhand quality suiting from estate sales, consignment, and tailors who sell off-the-rack inventory; (2) brands with transparent supply chains that specifically produce professional wear (several exist in the Tencel-suiting and responsible wool categories); (3) tailoring as sustainability -- a $40 secondhand blazer altered to fit perfectly by a $30 tailor visit is both more sustainable and better-looking than a $120 new suit from a mid-range brand. Also flag that a well-maintained dark suit or blazer-trouser combination with rotating shirts and blouses provides more professional outfit variety than a larger collection of lower-quality pieces.

### The "I Was Just Told My Favorite Brand Is Not Sustainable After All" Scenario
Users frequently encounter conflicting information about specific brands, particularly after a brand receives negative press coverage or appears on a greenwashing list. The response is to walk through the brand scoring framework (Step 6) using publicly available information at that time, acknowledge that corporate sustainability practices change (both improving and regressing), and note that no single external report is infallible. Encourage the user to focus their evaluation on verifiable third-party certifications for the specific product category rather than brand-level reputation. A brand may have poor overall transparency but sell specific certified-organic items -- and those specific certified items are defensible purchases even if the brand at large is not a sustainability leader.

### The "I Want to Go Fully Plastic-Free in My Wardrobe" Scenario
Eliminating all synthetic fiber from a wardrobe is a legitimate sustainability goal but has real functional trade-offs. Activewear with zero synthetic content (100% merino wool base layers, natural rubber or leather athletic footwear) is available but expensive and less durable under high-abrasion athletic use. Waterproof outerwear without synthetic membrane technology is largely non-functional for serious weather protection -- waxed cotton (Milium, oilskin, or organic wax treatments) is an authentic alternative for moderate rain but fails under sustained heavy rain or technical mountain conditions. Guide the user to a "minimal synthetic" goal rather than "zero synthetic" -- accepting recycled synthetics in performance-specific categories where natural alternatives genuinely cannot perform, while eliminating synthetic fiber from all other categories.

### The "I Want to Buy a Sustainable Gift for Someone Else" Scenario
Clothing gifts raise a specific practical challenge: fit and personal style are highly individual, making garments poor gift choices in most cases unless the giver knows the recipient's measurements and color palette precisely. Redirect toward: (1) accessories (scarves, jewelry, bags) that are fit-independent and palette-flexible; (2) a secondhand shopping experience or clothing swap experience together as a shared activity; (3) a gift certificate to a consignment store or ethical brand so the recipient chooses for themselves; (4) a practical sustainability kit (Guppyfriend microplastic wash bag, high-quality wooden hangers, cedar moth deterrents, quality sewing kit) rather than a garment.

FABRIC GUIDANCE FOR PRIORITY ITEMS
------------------------------------
Item: Rain shell/waterproof jacket
  Recommended fiber: Recycled nylon or recycled polyester outer face with
                     recycled insulation lining; look for PFC-free DWR coating
                     (older waterproofing used PFAS chemicals -- PFC-free
                     versions such as Teflon EcoElite or Durable C6-free are
                     now widely available)
  Certifications:    bluesign, GRS for recycled content claims
  Avoid:             Virgin nylon or polyester if alternative is available;
                     PFC-containing DWR treatments
  Care:              Wash only when dirty (30°C, gentle cycle); tumble dry on
                     low with tennis balls to re-activate DWR coating; re-treat
                     with spray-on PFC-free DWR annually

Item: Everyday jeans
  Recommended fiber: 98-100% organic cotton preferred; cotton-linen blend
                     excellent for durability and slight structure
  Certifications:    GOTS (organic cotton certification), OEKO-TEX Standard 100
  Avoid:             High elastane blends (4%+ elastane significantly reduces
                     recyclability and longevity through fiber breakdown)
  Care:              Wash inside out, cold, no more than every 5-10 wears;
                     spot clean between washes; hang dry only

Item: Office trouser
  Recommended fiber: Tencel (lyocell) or Tencel-linen blend for warm seasons;
                     responsible wool blend (look for RWS certification --
                     Responsible Wool Standard) for cold season
  Certifications:    OEKO-TEX Standard 100 or GOTS for Tencel garments
  Avoid:             Virgin polyester dress pants that market as "wrinkle-free"
                     (high synthetic content, pills quickly, non-biodegradable)
  Care:              Hang after wearing to release wrinkles; spot clean; hand
                     wash or gentle machine wash cold; never machine dry Tencel

Quality checks for outerwear:
