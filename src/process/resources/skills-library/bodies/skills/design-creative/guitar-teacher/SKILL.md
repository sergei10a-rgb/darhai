## Questions to Ask First

Before providing guitar instruction:

1. Are you a complete beginner, or do you have some experience? If experienced, what can you currently play?
2. Do you have a guitar? Is it acoustic or electric? What make and model?
3. What kind of music do you want to play? (rock, blues, folk, country, jazz, classical, pop, metal)
4. Do you read music, tablature, or chord charts? Or are you starting from zero on music reading?
5. What is your primary goal? (play songs around a campfire, perform in a band, write songs, become a professional player)
6. How much time can you commit to practice daily or weekly?
7. Have you taken lessons before? What worked and what did not?
8. Do you have any physical limitations in your hands or arms?
9. Do you prefer learning by ear, by reading notation, or by visual demonstration?
10. Are there specific songs you want to learn?

## Beginner Stage (Months 1-6)

### Getting Started
Before playing a single note:
- **Guitar setup**: Ensure the guitar is properly set up. Action (string height) should be comfortable. New strings make a huge difference. If the guitar hurts to play, it may need professional setup ($40-$80).
- **Holding the guitar**: Sit with the guitar resting on your right thigh (right-handed player). Keep the neck at a slight upward angle. Relax your shoulders.
- **Left hand position**: Thumb behind the neck, roughly behind your middle finger. Fingers curved, pressing strings with the fingertips just behind the fret wire.
- **Right hand position**: For strumming, hang the hand loosely from the wrist and strum from the elbow with a relaxed wrist. For fingerpicking, rest the thumb on a bass string and let the fingers hang naturally.

### First Chords (Open Chords)
Learn in this order for maximum song access:
1. **Em** (easiest chord -- two fingers)
2. **Am** (one finger moves from Em)
3. **C** (builds on Am shape)
4. **G** (stretches the hand, introduces the pinky)
5. **D** (compact shape, introduces the ring finger reach)
6. **A** (three fingers in a row)
7. **E** (similar shape to Am, different strings)
8. **Dm** (smaller shape, develops finger independence)

### Chord Transition Practice
The hardest part of early guitar is switching between chords smoothly:
- Practice switching between just two chords. Set a metronome at 60 BPM. Switch on every beat.
- Focus on the "anchor finger" -- a finger that stays in the same position or moves minimally between chords.
- Lift all fingers simultaneously rather than one at a time.
- Practice "air changes" -- hover your hand above the strings in the shape of the next chord before pressing down.
- Gradually increase the metronome tempo as the switches become automatic.

### Basic Strumming Patterns
Start with downstrokes only, then add upstrokes:
1. **All downstrokes**: D D D D (one per beat, quarter notes)
2. **Down-up eighth notes**: D U D U D U D U (constant motion)
3. **Folk strum**: D D U U D U (common in countless songs)
4. **Pop/rock strum**: D D U - U D U (accent on beats 2 and 4)

The secret to strumming: your hand moves in a constant down-up motion like a pendulum. You simply choose when to make contact with the strings and when to miss them.

### Reading Tablature (TAB)
Tablature is the most common notation system for guitar:
```
e|---0---1---3---|  (thinnest string, highest pitch)
B|---1---1---0---|
G|---0---2---0---|
D|---2---3---0---|
A|---3---3---2---|
E|---x---1---3---|  (thickest string, lowest pitch)
   C   F   G
```
- Six lines represent six strings (bottom line = low E string)
- Numbers indicate which fret to press (0 = open string, x = do not play)
- Read left to right. Numbers stacked vertically are played simultaneously (chords).

### First Songs
Choose songs with 2-4 of the chords you know. Classics that use Em, G, C, D:
- Chord progressions to practice: G - Em - C - D, Am - C - G - Em, C - G - Am - F (requires F, which comes later)
- Look up songs you love and check if they use chords you know. Many popular songs use only 4 chords.

## Intermediate Stage (Months 6-18)

### Barre Chords
Barre chords use one finger to press all strings at a fret. They are physically demanding at first but unlock the entire fretboard.

**F major barre chord** (the first barre chord most students learn):
- Index finger bars all strings at the 1st fret
- Middle finger on the 3rd string, 2nd fret
- Ring finger on the 5th string, 3rd fret
- Pinky on the 4th string, 3rd fret
- This is an E-shape barre chord. Move this shape up the neck to play any major chord.

**Building barre chord strength**:
- Practice holding the barre for 30 seconds, release, repeat
- Press only hard enough for clean notes -- excessive force causes fatigue
- Position the index finger slightly on its side (the bonier edge) for better string contact
- Start with partial barres (barring only 2-3 strings) and gradually add strings

**Essential barre chord shapes**:
- E-shape major and minor (root on 6th string)
- A-shape major and minor (root on 5th string)
- These four shapes give you every major and minor chord on the fretboard.

### Scale Systems

#### The Minor Pentatonic Scale
The most important scale for rock, blues, and popular music:
- Five notes: 1 b3 4 5 b7
- Pattern 1 (Box 1) in A minor:
```
e|---5---8---|
B|---5---8---|
G|---5---7---|
D|---5---7---|
A|---5---7---|
E|---5---8---|
```
- Learn all five positions across the fretboard
- Connect positions by sliding between them
- Practice ascending, descending, and in sequences (groups of 3, groups of 4)

#### The Major Scale
The foundation of Western music theory:
- Seven notes: 1 2 3 4 5 6 7
- Learn the CAGED system: five patterns derived from C, A, G, E, D open chord shapes
- Understand how the major scale connects to chords (1-3-5 = major triad, 1-b3-5 = minor triad)
- Practice with a metronome, gradually increasing speed
- Play the scale in thirds, sixths, and other intervals for melodic vocabulary

#### The Blues Scale
Minor pentatonic plus the "blue note" (b5):
- Six notes: 1 b3 4 b5 5 b7
- The b5 adds tension and character. Bend it or slide through it rather than landing on it.
- Essential for blues, rock, and any music that wants grit and emotion.

### Fingerpicking Fundamentals
Right hand finger assignments:
- **p** (thumb/pulgar): Bass strings (6th, 5th, 4th)
- **i** (index/indice): 3rd string
- **m** (middle/medio): 2nd string
- **a** (ring/anular): 1st string

Basic patterns:
1. **Travis picking**: Alternating bass with thumb (p) while fingers play melody on treble strings. Foundation of folk and country fingerpicking.
2. **Arpeggios**: p-i-m-a ascending, then a-m-i-p descending, across chord shapes.
3. **Classical position**: Thumb plays bass notes independently while i-m-a play melody and harmony.

### Music Theory for Guitarists
- **The Nashville Number System**: Chords described by their scale degree (I IV V = G C D in the key of G). Allows instant transposition.
- **Chord families by key**: In any major key, the chords are: I(major) ii(minor) iii(minor) IV(major) V(major) vi(minor) vii(diminished).
- **The Circle of Fifths**: Visual tool showing key relationships. Adjacent keys share most of their chords.
- **Intervals**: Learn to hear and identify intervals. This is the foundation of playing by ear.

## Advanced Stage (18 Months and Beyond)

### Modal Playing
Modes are scales derived from each degree of the major scale:
- **Ionian** (1st mode): The major scale itself. Bright, resolved.
- **Dorian** (2nd mode): Minor with a natural 6th. Smooth, jazzy minor. (Santana, Pink Floyd)
- **Phrygian** (3rd mode): Minor with a flat 2nd. Dark, Spanish, metal.
- **Lydian** (4th mode): Major with a sharp 4th. Dreamy, floating. (Steve Vai, film scores)
- **Mixolydian** (5th mode): Major with a flat 7th. Bluesy major. (Classic rock, Grateful Dead)
- **Aeolian** (6th mode): Natural minor scale. Sad, serious.
- **Locrian** (7th mode): Diminished. Tense, unstable. Rarely used as a home base.

Practice each mode over a drone or backing track in the appropriate chord to hear its unique character.

### Advanced Techniques
- **Bending**: Push (or pull) the string to raise pitch. Bend to specific target notes (half step, whole step, minor third). Intonation matters -- the bend should arrive at a precise pitch.
- **Vibrato**: Oscillate the pitch of a sustained note. Controlled vibrato is a hallmark of advanced playing. Practice both wrist vibrato and finger vibrato.
- **Hammer-ons and pull-offs (legato)**: Play notes without picking. Develops speed and smoothness.
- **Slides**: Slide from one fret to another without releasing pressure. Connects phrases fluidly.
- **Tapping**: Use the picking hand to tap notes on the fretboard. Expands range and speed.
- **Hybrid picking**: Use the pick and fingers of the right hand simultaneously. Common in country and rock.
- **String skipping**: Skip strings for wide intervals. Creates angular, unexpected melodies.

### Improvisation Development
1. Learn the melody of a song or a head (jazz/blues melody)
2. Play the melody with variations (different rhythm, added notes, different octave)
3. Use the scale associated with the chord to create new phrases around the melody
4. Target chord tones (1, 3, 5, 7 of each chord) on strong beats
5. Develop a vocabulary of licks and phrases. Practice them in every key.
6. Play along with backing tracks daily. Quantity of practice builds fluency.
7. Record yourself and listen back critically. What sounds intentional? What sounds random?

### Song Learning Methodology
1. **Listen first**: Hear the entire song several times without your guitar. Internalize the form, feel, and energy.
2. **Identify the key and chords**: Use your ear and verify with a chord chart if needed.
3. **Map the form**: Write out the structure (verse, chorus, bridge, how many bars each).
4. **Learn the rhythm**: Get the strumming or picking pattern right before worrying about every chord voicing.
5. **Learn specific parts**: Riffs, solos, intros, fills. Break them into 2-4 bar phrases.
6. **Slow practice**: Learn difficult sections at half speed. Speed up gradually with a metronome.
7. **Play along with the recording**: Match the feel, timing, and dynamics of the original.
8. **Make it yours**: Once you can play it accurately, adapt it to your style.

## Practice Routines by Level

### Beginner (20-30 Minutes Daily)
- 5 min: Finger exercises and chord stretches
- 10 min: Chord transitions (two chords at a time, metronome)
- 10 min: Strumming patterns with chord changes
- 5 min: Play through a song you are learning

### Intermediate (30-45 Minutes Daily)
- 5 min: Warm-up scales and picking exercises
- 10 min: New chord forms or barre chord practice
- 10 min: Scale practice (pentatonic, major, or modes) with metronome
- 10 min: Song work (learning new material or refining current repertoire)
- 5 min: Improvisation over a backing track

### Advanced (45-90 Minutes Daily)
- 10 min: Technical exercises (speed building, string skipping, legato)
- 15 min: Scale and arpeggio work in multiple positions and keys
- 15 min: Learning new repertoire or transcribing solos by ear
- 15 min: Improvisation, composition, or creative exploration
- 10 min: Ear training (interval recognition, chord quality identification)
- 5 min: Review and reinforcement of previously learned material

### Practice Principles
- **Consistency over duration**: 20 focused minutes daily beats 3 hours once a week.
- **Use a metronome**: It builds internal timing that no amount of noodling will develop.
- **Practice what is hard**: If you always play what you already know, you will not grow. Spend time on weaknesses.
- **Slow practice is fast practice**: Learning something slowly and correctly is faster than learning it fast and sloppy.
- **Record yourself**: You will hear things you do not notice while playing.
- **Set specific goals**: "Learn the verse chords of Song X at 100 BPM by Friday" is better than "practice guitar."

## Equipment Essentials

### Acoustic Guitar Recommendations by Budget
- **Budget ($100-$300)**: Yamaha FG800, Fender CD-60S, Epiphone DR-100
- **Mid-range ($300-$800)**: Taylor 114e, Martin D-X2E, Yamaha FS800
- **Professional ($800+)**: Taylor 214ce, Martin D-28, Gibson J-45

### Electric Guitar Recommendations by Budget
- **Budget ($150-$400)**: Squier Classic Vibe Telecaster/Stratocaster, Epiphone Les Paul Standard, Yamaha Pacifica 112V
- **Mid-range ($400-$1000)**: Fender Player Stratocaster/Telecaster, Epiphone Les Paul Standard '50s, PRS SE Custom 24
- **Professional ($1000+)**: Fender American Professional, Gibson Les Paul Standard, PRS S2/CE series

### Essential Accessories
- Guitar tuner (clip-on or app: Snark, Peterson, GuitarTuna app)
- Metronome (app or device)
- Capo (for acoustic players especially)
- Picks in various thicknesses (start medium, experiment from there)
- Guitar stand (guitars left in cases do not get played)
- Extra strings (learn to change them yourself)
- Strap (even for seated playing, it helps stabilize)

### Amplifiers for Electric Guitar
- **Beginner**: Fender Frontman, Boss Katana Mini, Positive Grid Spark Mini
- **Intermediate**: Boss Katana 50, Fender Blues Junior, Orange Crush
- **Home recording**: Audio interface (Focusrite Scarlett, Audient iD) with amp simulation software (Neural DSP, AmpliTube, Guitar Rig)
