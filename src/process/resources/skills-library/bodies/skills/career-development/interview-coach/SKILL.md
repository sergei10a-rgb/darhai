# Interview Coach
## When to Use

**Use this skill when:**
- User asks about interview coach
- User needs guidance on interview coach topics
- User wants a structured approach to interview coach

**Do NOT use when:**
- Request requires professional consultation beyond educational guidance
- User needs emergency assistance

## Questions to Ask the User First

1. **What role are you interviewing for?** (title and company)
2. **What type of interview is it?** (phone screen, video, in-person, panel, technical, case study)
3. **What round is this?** (initial screen, second round, final round, etc.)
4. **Who are you interviewing with?** (HR, hiring manager, team members, executives)
5. **When is the interview?** (how much prep time do you have)
6. **What are you most nervous about?**
7. **Do you have the job description?**
8. **Have you interviewed for this type of role before?**
9. **Are there specific questions or topics you want to prepare for?**
10. **Have you researched the company yet?**

## Step 1: Company Research Checklist

### Before Every Interview
```
COMPANY RESEARCH CHECKLIST:

BASIC FACTS:
[ ] What does the company do? (products, services, mission)
[ ] How big is the company? (employees, revenue, locations)
[ ] Who are the founders / CEO / key leaders?
[ ] When was it founded and what is its history?
[ ] What industry and market segment?

RECENT NEWS:
[ ] Last 3-6 months of news coverage
[ ] Recent product launches or announcements
[ ] Earnings reports or funding rounds
[ ] Any controversies or challenges

CULTURE AND VALUES:
[ ] Company mission and values (from their website)
[ ] Glassdoor reviews (look for patterns, not individual complaints)
[ ] Social media presence and tone
[ ] Diversity and inclusion initiatives
[ ] Remote/hybrid work policies

COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE:
[ ] Who are the main competitors?
[ ] What differentiates this company?
[ ] What challenges does the industry face?
[ ] Where is the company positioned for growth?

THE ROLE:
[ ] Re-read the job description 3 times before the interview
[ ] Identify the top 3 requirements and prepare examples for each
[ ] Understand where this role sits in the organization
[ ] Research the team you would join (LinkedIn)
[ ] Understand the career path from this role

YOUR INTERVIEWERS:
[ ] Look up each interviewer on LinkedIn
[ ] Note their background, tenure at the company, and role
[ ] Find common ground (shared connections, schools, interests)
[ ] Understand what THEY care about (engineering cares about different
    things than marketing or finance)
```

## Step 2: The STAR Method for Behavioral Questions

### Framework
```
STAR METHOD:

S - SITUATION: Set the scene. Where were you? What was the context?
    (Keep brief: 2-3 sentences)

T - TASK: What was your specific responsibility or challenge?
    (1-2 sentences)

A - ACTION: What did YOU do? (Most important part -- be specific)

R - RESULT: What happened? Quantify if possible.
    (2-3 sentences with metrics)

BONUS: REFLECTION - What did you learn?
    (1 sentence showing growth)

TOTAL TIME: 1.5-2.5 minutes per answer
```

### STAR Answer Template
```
STAR PREPARATION WORKSHEET:

QUESTION: "Tell me about a time when {{BEHAVIORAL_PROMPT}}"

SITUATION:
"In my role as {{TITLE}} at {{COMPANY}}, we faced {{CHALLENGE/CONTEXT}}.
{{BRIEF_ADDITIONAL_CONTEXT}}."

TASK:
"I was responsible for {{YOUR_SPECIFIC_ROLE_IN_THE_SITUATION}}."

ACTION:
"First, I {{STEP_1}}.
Then, I {{STEP_2}}.
I also {{STEP_3}}.
When {{OBSTACLE_AROSE}}, I {{HOW_YOU_HANDLED_IT}}."

RESULT:
"As a result, {{QUANTIFIED_OUTCOME}}.
This led to {{BROADER_IMPACT}}.
{{OPTIONAL: My manager noted / the client said / this resulted in}}."

REFLECTION (optional but impressive):
"This experience taught me {{LESSON}} which I have applied to {{EXAMPLE}}."
```

### Prepare 8-10 STAR Stories

Map your stories to common competencies:
```
STORY BANK:

Prepare a STAR story for each of these:

1. LEADERSHIP: When you led a team or initiative
   Story: {{BRIEF_REFERENCE}}

2. CONFLICT: When you resolved a disagreement
   Story: {{BRIEF_REFERENCE}}

3. FAILURE: When something went wrong and what you learned
   Story: {{BRIEF_REFERENCE}}

4. ACHIEVEMENT: Your proudest professional accomplishment
   Story: {{BRIEF_REFERENCE}}

5. TEAMWORK: When you collaborated effectively
   Story: {{BRIEF_REFERENCE}}

6. PRESSURE: When you worked under a tight deadline or stress
   Story: {{BRIEF_REFERENCE}}

7. INITIATIVE: When you went above and beyond
   Story: {{BRIEF_REFERENCE}}

8. CHANGE: When you adapted to or drove significant change
   Story: {{BRIEF_REFERENCE}}

9. INFLUENCE: When you persuaded someone or got buy-in
   Story: {{BRIEF_REFERENCE}}

10. PROBLEM-SOLVING: When you solved a complex problem
    Story: {{BRIEF_REFERENCE}}

TIP: Most stories can be adapted to answer multiple questions.
A leadership story can also demonstrate conflict resolution,
pressure, or initiative.
```

## Step 3: Common Question Bank with Example Approaches

### Opening Questions

**"Tell me about yourself."** (Not a STAR question -- use a present-past-future framework)
```
TEMPLATE:
"Currently, I am {{PRESENT_ROLE_AND_KEY_RESPONSIBILITY}}.
Previously, I {{RELEVANT_PAST_EXPERIENCE_AND_ACHIEVEMENT}}.
I am excited about this role because {{CONNECTION_TO_TARGET_ROLE}}."

RULES:
- 60-90 seconds maximum
- Professional only (not personal life story)
- End with WHY THIS ROLE
- Practice until it sounds natural, not rehearsed
```

**"Why do you want to work here?"**
```
TEMPLATE:
"Three things stood out to me. First, {{SPECIFIC_COMPANY_ATTRIBUTE}},
which aligns with my {{VALUE_OR_INTEREST}}. Second, {{PRODUCT_OR_MISSION}}
excites me because {{REASON}}. And third, this role specifically appeals
to me because {{CONNECTION_TO_YOUR_SKILLS_AND_CAREER_GOALS}}."
```

**"Why are you leaving your current role?"**
```
RULES:
- Never badmouth your current employer
- Focus on what you are moving TOWARD, not running FROM
- Keep it brief and positive

SAFE ANSWERS:
- "I am looking for new challenges and growth opportunities in {{AREA}}"
- "I want to be in an environment where I can {{SPECIFIC_GOAL}}"
- "This role aligns with where I want to take my career in {{AREA}}"
- "I am drawn to {{COMPANY}}'s mission/scale/product/culture"
```

### Behavioral Questions

| Question | What They Are Assessing |
|---|---|
| "Tell me about a time you failed" | Self-awareness, learning from mistakes, resilience |
| "Describe a conflict with a coworker" | Conflict resolution, emotional intelligence, communication |
| "Tell me about your greatest achievement" | What you value, your impact, your motivation |
| "How do you handle multiple priorities?" | Organization, prioritization, time management |
| "Tell me about a time you disagreed with your manager" | Judgment, communication, professionalism |
| "Describe a time you had to learn something quickly" | Adaptability, learning agility, self-motivation |
| "When have you gone above and beyond?" | Initiative, work ethic, commitment |
| "Tell me about leading a difficult project" | Leadership, problem-solving, resilience |

### Weakness Question

**"What is your greatest weakness?"**
```
THE GOOD ANSWER FORMULA:
1. Name a REAL weakness (not a fake one)
2. Show you are AWARE of it
3. Describe what you are DOING to improve
4. Give EVIDENCE of progress

DO NOT SAY:
- "I am a perfectionist" (cliche)
- "I work too hard" (not credible)
- Anything that is a core requirement of the role
- Anything without a clear improvement plan
```

## Step 4: Questions to Ask the Interviewer

### High-Impact Questions
```
QUESTIONS BY INTERVIEW STAGE:

PHONE SCREEN (HR/Recruiter):
- "What does the interview process look like from here?"
- "What are the top priorities for this role in the first 90 days?"
- "What is the salary range for this position?" (if not already stated)
- "What is the team structure?"

HIRING MANAGER:
- "What does success look like in this role after 6 months? After a year?"
- "What is the biggest challenge the team is currently facing?"
- "How would you describe your management style?"
- "What are the growth opportunities from this role?"
- "Why is this position open?"

TEAM MEMBERS:
- "What do you enjoy most about working here?"
- "How does the team collaborate on projects?"
- "What is the onboarding process like?"
- "What tools and processes does the team use?"

SENIOR LEADERSHIP / FINAL ROUND:
- "Where do you see the company in 3 years?"
- "What are the biggest opportunities and threats facing the company?"
- "How does this team contribute to the company's strategic goals?"
- "What would make someone wildly successful in this role?"

QUESTIONS TO AVOID:
- Anything easily found on the company website
- "What does the company do?" (shows zero research)
- Salary/benefits in the first interview (unless they bring it up)
- How quickly you can get promoted
- "How soon can I take vacation?"
```

## Step 5: Salary Discussion Timing
```
SALARY CONVERSATION GUIDE:

WHEN THEY ASK YOUR SALARY EXPECTATIONS EARLY:
"I would like to learn more about the role and responsibilities
before discussing compensation. Could you share the budgeted
range for this position?"

IF PRESSED:
"Based on my research for this role in this market, I am targeting
a total compensation range of ${{LOW}} to ${{HIGH}}. However, I am
flexible depending on the full benefits package and growth
opportunities."

PREPARATION:
[ ] Research salary ranges on Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, Payscale, LinkedIn
[ ] Know your minimum acceptable number (do not share this)
[ ] Know the market range for the role + location + experience level
[ ] Factor in total compensation (base + bonus + equity + benefits)
[ ] Account for cost of living differences if relocating

TIMING RULES:
- Let the employer bring up compensation first when possible
- Do not negotiate until you have a written offer
- It is OK to ask the recruiter about the range in the first call
- Detailed negotiation happens after the offer, not during interviews
- See the Salary Negotiator skill for detailed negotiation strategies
```

## Step 6: Virtual Interview Setup
```
VIRTUAL INTERVIEW CHECKLIST:

TECHNOLOGY (test 24 hours before AND 30 minutes before):
[ ] Install and test the video platform (Zoom, Teams, Google Meet, etc.)
[ ] Test camera -- face is well-lit, centered, and at eye level
[ ] Test microphone -- clear audio, minimal background noise
[ ] Test internet connection (use ethernet if possible)
[ ] Close unnecessary apps and browser tabs
[ ] Silence phone notifications and computer notifications
[ ] Have the call link ready and accessible
[ ] Charge laptop or plug in
[ ] Have a phone number ready as backup if technology fails

ENVIRONMENT:
[ ] Clean, professional background (or use a tasteful virtual background)
[ ] Quiet space with door closed
[ ] Good lighting (face the light source, not your back to it)
[ ] Declutter visible areas
[ ] Alert household members that you are interviewing
[ ] Remove pets from the room

CAMERA TECHNIQUE:
[ ] Look at the camera when speaking (not the screen)
[ ] Position camera at eye level (use books or stand to elevate laptop)
[ ] Sit an arm's length from the camera
[ ] Dress professionally from head to toe (you might stand up)
[ ] Keep gestures within the frame
[ ] Nod and react visibly (slight delay in video means more expression helps)

MATERIALS:
[ ] Resume printed and in front of you (but do not read from it)
[ ] Notes with key points and questions (but do not read from them)
[ ] Pen and paper for notes
[ ] Glass of water nearby
[ ] Copy of the job description

BACKUP PLAN:
[ ] Interviewer's phone number or email
[ ] Your phone ready to call in if video fails
[ ] Prepared message: "I am having technical difficulties. May I call in?"
```

## Step 7: Panel Interview Strategies
```
PANEL INTERVIEW PREPARATION:

BEFORE:
[ ] Ask who will be on the panel (names and roles)
[ ] Research each panelist on LinkedIn
[ ] Prepare for different perspectives:
    - HR: Culture fit, communication, conflict resolution
    - Hiring manager: Skills, experience, growth potential
    - Team members: Collaboration, technical knowledge, personality
    - Senior leadership: Strategic thinking, big-picture vision

DURING:
[ ] Greet everyone individually when entering (in-person) or note names (virtual)
[ ] Direct your answer toward the person who asked the question
[ ] Include eye contact with other panelists during your answer
[ ] Note who asks what -- it reveals their priorities
[ ] Address panelists by name when possible
[ ] When answering, consider what each panelist cares about

TIPS:
- Do not ignore quieter panelists (they may be the decision-maker)
- Take brief notes if it helps you remember names and questions
- It is OK to pause briefly before answering
- Ask clarifying questions if needed
- Maintain consistent energy throughout (panels can be long)

COMMON PANEL FORMAT:
1. Introduction round (each panelist introduces themselves)
2. Structured questions (each panelist asks 2-3 prepared questions)
3. Scenario or case question (collaborative)
4. Your questions for them
5. Closing and next steps
```

## Step 8: Thank-You Follow-Up Templates

### Within 24 Hours of the Interview
```
THANK-YOU EMAIL TEMPLATE (INDIVIDUAL):

Subject: Thank you -- {{ROLE_TITLE}} Interview

Dear {{INTERVIEWER_NAME}},

Thank you for taking the time to speak with me today about the
{{ROLE_TITLE}} position at {{COMPANY_NAME}}.

I especially enjoyed our conversation about {{SPECIFIC_TOPIC_DISCUSSED}}.
{{BRIEF SENTENCE CONNECTING THAT TOPIC TO YOUR RELEVANT EXPERIENCE
OR ENTHUSIASM}}.

After learning more about {{SPECIFIC_ASPECT_OF_ROLE_OR_COMPANY}},
I am even more excited about the opportunity to {{VALUE_YOU_WOULD_BRING}}.

Please do not hesitate to reach out if you need any additional
information. I look forward to hearing about next steps.

Best regards,
{{YOUR_NAME}}
{{YOUR_PHONE}}
{{YOUR_LINKEDIN}}
```

### Panel Interview (Send Unique Emails to Each)
```
PANEL THANK-YOU STRATEGY:

[ ] Send a unique thank-you email to EACH panelist within 24 hours
[ ] Reference something specific you discussed with EACH person
[ ] Vary the wording so they do not compare identical emails
[ ] If you do not have individual emails, send through the recruiter
    or ask for contact information at the end of the interview
```

### Follow-Up After No Response
```
FOLLOW-UP TEMPLATE (1 week after expected timeline):

Subject: Following Up -- {{ROLE_TITLE}} Application

Dear {{RECRUITER/HIRING_MANAGER_NAME}},

I hope you are doing well. I wanted to follow up on our conversation
on {{DATE}} regarding the {{ROLE_TITLE}} position.

I remain very interested in the opportunity and would welcome the
chance to discuss next steps. If the timeline has shifted or if
there is any additional information I can provide, please let me know.

Thank you again for your time and consideration.

Best regards,
{{YOUR_NAME}}
{{YOUR_PHONE}}
```

## Interview Day Checklist
```
INTERVIEW DAY REMINDERS:

THE NIGHT BEFORE:
[ ] Review your STAR stories
[ ] Review the job description one more time
[ ] Plan your outfit and lay it out
[ ] Plan your route and transportation (arrive 10-15 min early)
[ ] Get a full night of sleep

THE MORNING OF:
[ ] Eat a proper meal (you need energy and focus)
[ ] Review your notes briefly (do not cram)
[ ] Bring copies of your resume (5 copies for in-person)
[ ] Bring a portfolio/notebook and pen
[ ] Verify the interview address/video link
[ ] Dress one level above the company's dress code

DURING:
[ ] Smile and make eye contact
[ ] Listen to the full question before answering
[ ] It is OK to pause 3-5 seconds to think
[ ] Use the interviewer's name naturally
[ ] Show enthusiasm (energy is contagious)
[ ] Watch your body language (open posture, no fidgeting)
[ ] Ask your prepared questions
[ ] Close strong: "I am very interested in this role. What are the next steps?"

AFTER:
[ ] Send thank-you emails within 24 hours
[ ] Write down questions they asked (for future prep)
[ ] Note any areas where you felt weak (practice those)
[ ] Follow up with recruiter if no response within stated timeline
```

## Example

**Input:** "Help me get started with interview coach"

## Edge Cases

- **Incomplete information:** Ask clarifying questions before proceeding. Do not assume details the user has not provided.
- **Out of scope requests:** Redirect to appropriate professional resources when the request exceeds educational guidance.
- **Conflicting requirements:** Present trade-offs clearly and let the user decide priorities.
