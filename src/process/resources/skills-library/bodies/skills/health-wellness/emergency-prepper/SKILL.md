# Emergency Preparedness Guide

> **Disclaimer:** This guide provides general emergency preparedness information. Specific risks vary by geographic location, climate, and individual circumstances. Always follow official guidance from local emergency management agencies, FEMA, and the Red Cross during actual emergencies. Medical preparedness should be discussed with your healthcare provider.


## When to Use

**Use this skill when:**
- User asks about emergency prepper techniques or best practices
- User needs guidance on emergency prepper concepts
- User wants to implement or improve their approach to emergency prepper

**Do NOT use when:**
- The request falls outside the scope of emergency prepper
- User needs a different specialized skill for their specific situation
- The topic requires professional consultation beyond general guidance

## Questions to Ask First

1. How many people are in your household (including pets)?
2. Are there any special needs (medical conditions, medications, mobility issues, infants, elderly)?
3. What natural disasters are most likely in your area (earthquake, hurricane, tornado, flood, wildfire, winter storm)?
4. Do you live in an area prone to power outages, and how long do they typically last?
5. Do you currently have any emergency supplies or plans in place?
6. Do you rent or own (affects what you can store and where)?
7. Do all household members know basic emergency procedures?
8. What is your budget for emergency preparedness?
9. Do you have pets that need to be included in your plan?
10. How far are you from major hospitals, fire stations, and evacuation routes?

## Phase 1: 72-Hour Emergency Kit

### The Foundation: Supplies for 3 Days

Every household should have enough supplies to be self-sufficient for at least 72 hours (3 days). Many experts now recommend 2 weeks of supplies for major disasters.

### Water Storage

**Rule: 1 gallon per person per day (minimum)**

```
WATER CALCULATION
=================
Number of people: _______
Number of pets: _______ (calculate pet water needs separately)
Days of supply desired: _______

People: _______ x 1 gallon x _______ days = _______ gallons
Pets: _______ x estimated need x _______ days = _______ gallons
Sanitation allowance (50% of drinking): _______ gallons
TOTAL WATER NEEDED: _______ gallons

Example: Family of 4, 14-day supply
  4 x 1 gallon x 14 days = 56 gallons (drinking/cooking)
  + 28 gallons (sanitation)
  = 84 gallons total
```

**Water storage options:**
| Method | Capacity | Cost | Shelf Life |
|--------|---------|------|-----------|
| Commercial bottled water | 16oz-1 gal | $1-$5/gal | 1-2 years (sealed) |
| Water storage containers (BPA-free) | 5-7 gallons | $10-$20 each | Rotate every 6 months |
| WaterBOB (bathtub bladder) | 100 gallons | $25-$35 | Fill when warning issued |
| Water barrel (food-grade) | 55 gallons | $50-$100 | Treat and rotate every 6-12 months |
| Water bricks (stackable) | 3.5 gallons each | $15-$25 each | Rotate every 6-12 months |

**Water purification backup:**
- Water purification tablets (Aquatabs, Potable Aqua)
- Portable water filter (Sawyer, LifeStraw, Katadyn)
- Unscented liquid chlorine bleach (8 drops per gallon, wait 30 minutes)
- Boiling (rolling boil for 1 minute; 3 minutes above 6,500 feet elevation)

### Food Reserves

**Food storage priorities:**
1. Foods your family actually eats (do not stockpile foods nobody likes)
2. No cooking required options (may not have power or fuel)
3. Long shelf life (canned goods, freeze-dried, dehydrated)
4. Calorie-dense (need approximately 2,000 calories per person per day)
5. Nutritionally balanced (include protein, fats, and carbohydrates)

**72-Hour Food Kit per Person:**

| Category | Items | Quantity |
|----------|-------|---------|
| Protein | Canned tuna/chicken, peanut butter, protein bars, jerky | 3-6 servings |
| Carbohydrates | Crackers, granola bars, dried fruit, instant oatmeal | 6-9 servings |
| Canned meals | Soup, chili, stew, pasta | 3-6 cans |
| Snacks | Trail mix, nuts, dried fruit, hard candy | Assorted |
| Comfort foods | Chocolate, coffee, tea (morale matters) | As desired |
| Special needs | Baby formula, dietary restriction items | As needed |

**Extended Supply (2 Weeks):**
- Canned vegetables and fruits
- Rice and dried pasta
- Dried beans and lentils
- Powdered milk
- Cooking oil
- Salt, pepper, basic spices
- Honey (indefinite shelf life)
- Freeze-dried meals (25-year shelf life)
- Multivitamins

**Also include:** Manual can opener, disposable plates/utensils (to save water), cooking fuel if applicable.

### Communication Tips During Emergencies
- Text messages often get through when voice calls cannot (uses less bandwidth)
- Designate your out-of-area contact as a central relay point
- Keep phones charged (portable battery packs are essential)
- Have a hand-crank or battery-powered radio for official information
- Know how to use social media to mark yourself as safe
- Program all emergency contacts into every family member's phone
- Write key numbers on a card in each family member's wallet (phones can die)

## Phase 3: Document Protection

### Critical Documents to Protect

- [ ] Identification (driver's licenses, passports, Social Security cards)
- [ ] Insurance policies (home, auto, health, life) with agent contact information
- [ ] Property documents (deed, mortgage, vehicle titles)
- [ ] Financial records (bank account numbers, credit cards, investment accounts)
- [ ] Medical records (prescriptions, conditions, immunization records)
- [ ] Legal documents (wills, power of attorney, custody agreements)
- [ ] Birth and marriage certificates
- [ ] Tax returns (most recent 2 years)
- [ ] Home inventory (photos/video of all rooms and contents for insurance)

### Protection Methods

| Method | Pros | Cons |
|--------|------|------|
| Fireproof safe (at home) | Immediate access, protects from fire | Can be lost in flood, theft, total destruction |
| Bank safe deposit box | Very secure | Limited access hours, may be inaccessible in disaster |
| Cloud storage (encrypted) | Accessible anywhere, protected from physical damage | Requires internet access and password |
| Trusted family member | Geographic redundancy | Privacy concerns |
| USB drive in go-bag | Portable, quick access | Can be lost or damaged |

**Best practice:** Use multiple methods. Store digital copies in encrypted cloud storage AND keep physical copies in a fireproof safe AND with a trusted out-of-area contact.

## Phase 4: Evacuation Plan

### Evacuation Planning Checklist

- [ ] Identify at least 2 evacuation routes from your neighborhood
- [ ] Know local shelter locations (Red Cross app shows open shelters)
- [ ] Plan where you would go (family, friends, hotel, shelter)
- [ ] Keep vehicles fueled (at least half tank at all times)
- [ ] Know how to shut off utilities (gas, water, electricity)
- [ ] Plan for pets (many shelters do not accept pets; know which do)
- [ ] Identify special needs (wheelchair accessible routes, medical transport)
- [ ] Practice evacuation with your family at least once per year

### Evacuation Go-Bag Checklist

**Each family member should have a bag that can be grabbed in minutes:**

**Essentials:**
- [ ] Water (at least 1 liter)
- [ ] Food (energy bars, nuts, dried fruit for 24 hours)
- [ ] Medications (3-7 day supply of all prescriptions)
- [ ] First aid kit (basic)
- [ ] Flashlight and extra batteries
- [ ] Phone charger and portable battery pack
- [ ] Cash (small bills, at least $200; ATMs may be down)
- [ ] Important documents (copies or USB drive)
- [ ] Change of clothes and sturdy shoes
- [ ] Rain poncho or jacket

**Additional Items:**
- [ ] Multi-tool or knife
- [ ] Whistle (for signaling)
- [ ] Dust mask or N95 respirator
- [ ] Duct tape
- [ ] Work gloves
- [ ] Plastic bags (zip-lock and trash bags)
- [ ] Local maps (do not rely solely on GPS)
- [ ] Notepad and pen
- [ ] Sunscreen and insect repellent
- [ ] Emergency blanket (Mylar)

**For Children:**
- [ ] Comfort item (stuffed animal, blanket)
- [ ] Activities (coloring book, small toy, book)
- [ ] Diapers and wipes (if applicable)
- [ ] Formula (if applicable)
- [ ] Copy of child's identification and medical information
- [ ] Photo of child with parent (for identification if separated)

**For Pets:**
- [ ] 3-day supply of food and water
- [ ] Leash, collar with ID, carrier
- [ ] Medications
- [ ] Vaccination records
- [ ] Recent photo of pet
- [ ] Plastic bags for waste

## Phase 5: Shelter-in-Place

### When to Shelter in Place
- Chemical or industrial accident with toxic release
- Severe weather (tornado warning, severe thunderstorm)
- Active threat in the area
- Authorities specifically instruct shelter-in-place

### Shelter-in-Place Procedure
1. Get inside the nearest sturdy building immediately
2. Close and lock all doors and windows
3. Turn off HVAC system, fans, and ventilation
4. If chemical or radiation threat: seal doors and windows with plastic sheeting and duct tape
5. Move to an interior room on the highest floor (for chemical threats -- chemicals tend to settle)
6. For tornado: move to the lowest floor, interior room, away from windows
7. Monitor radio or TV for official instructions
8. Do not leave until authorities give the all-clear

### Shelter-in-Place Supply Checklist
- [ ] Plastic sheeting (pre-cut for each window and door)
- [ ] Duct tape
- [ ] Battery-powered or hand-crank radio
- [ ] Flashlights and batteries
- [ ] Water and food (72-hour supply accessible inside)
- [ ] First aid kit
- [ ] Medications
- [ ] Phone chargers
- [ ] Sanitation supplies (garbage bags, toilet bags if water is off)

## Phase 6: Go-Bag (Bug-Out Bag)

### The Difference Between a Go-Bag and a 72-Hour Kit

**Go-Bag:** Portable backpack designed to sustain you for 72 hours away from home. Grab-and-go in minutes. One per person.

**72-Hour Kit:** Stationary supplies at home for sheltering in place for 3+ days. Larger quantity, more comfort items.

### Go-Bag Contents by Category

**Water (heaviest item, plan accordingly):**
- 1-2 liters of water in bottles
- Water purification method (filter, tablets)
- Collapsible water container

**Food (calorie-dense, lightweight):**
- Energy bars (1,200+ calories per day for 3 days)
- Trail mix and nuts
- Jerky
- Freeze-dried meals (if you have a way to heat water)

**Shelter and Warmth:**
- Emergency bivvy or space blanket
- Rain poncho
- Compact sleeping bag or liner (if space allows)
- Hand warmers
- Extra socks (dry socks are critical)

**Fire and Light:**
- Waterproof matches or lighter
- Flashlight (headlamp preferred for hands-free)
- Extra batteries or hand-crank light
- Glow sticks

**First Aid:**
- Adhesive bandages (assorted sizes)
- Gauze pads and roll
- Medical tape
- Antibiotic ointment
- Pain relievers (ibuprofen, acetaminophen)
- Anti-diarrheal medication
- Prescription medications (3-7 day supply)
- Tweezers, scissors
- Moleskin (for blisters)

**Tools and Navigation:**
- Multi-tool
- Fixed-blade knife
- Paracord (50 feet)
- Duct tape (wrap around a pencil to save space)
- Local maps
- Compass
- Whistle

## Phase 7: Seasonal Preparation

### Spring Preparation
- [ ] Test smoke detectors and CO detectors (replace batteries)
- [ ] Review and update emergency plans and contacts
- [ ] Check and rotate water storage
- [ ] Inspect and restock first aid kits
- [ ] Review insurance coverage
- [ ] Clean gutters and check drainage
- [ ] Prepare for severe weather season (tornado, flooding)
- [ ] Check sump pump operation

### Summer Preparation
- [ ] Prepare for heat emergencies (fans, hydration supplies, cooling center locations)
- [ ] Wildfire preparation (defensible space, evacuation routes)
- [ ] Hurricane preparation (if in hurricane zone: shutters, supplies, plan)
- [ ] Check vehicle emergency kits
- [ ] Update go-bags with seasonal clothing

### Fall Preparation
- [ ] Winterize home (insulation, weather stripping, pipe insulation)
- [ ] Service heating system
- [ ] Stock winter storm supplies
- [ ] Check generator (if applicable -- fuel, oil, test run)
- [ ] Prepare vehicles for winter (tires, emergency kit, blankets)
- [ ] Rotate food and water storage

### Winter Preparation
- [ ] Ensure adequate heating backup (wood stove, space heater with safety features)
- [ ] Stock extra blankets and warm clothing
- [ ] Maintain rock salt or sand for ice
- [ ] Keep vehicles fueled (at least half tank)
- [ ] Prepare for power outages (batteries, flashlights, radio)
- [ ] Know signs of frostbite and hypothermia

## Budget-Friendly Preparedness

### Building Your Kit Over Time

| Month | Focus Area | Budget |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| Month 1 | Water storage (containers, purification) | $20-$50 |
| Month 2 | Food supply (start with canned goods) | $30-$50 |
| Month 3 | First aid kit and medications | $25-$50 |
| Month 4 | Lighting and communication (flashlights, radio, batteries) | $25-$50 |
| Month 5 | Documents and communication plan | $10-$20 |
| Month 6 | Go-bag basics (backpack, essentials) | $30-$60 |
| Month 7-12 | Expand and refine all categories | $20-$50/month |

You do not need to spend hundreds of dollars at once. Build your preparedness gradually, focusing on the most critical items first: water, food, first aid, and a communication plan. Even basic preparation puts you far ahead of most households.

The time to prepare is before you need it. Review and practice your plans regularly, maintain your supplies, and keep your family informed and engaged.
