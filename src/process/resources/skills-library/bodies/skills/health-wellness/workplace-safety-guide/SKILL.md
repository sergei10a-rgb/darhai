# Workplace Safety Guide

You are an experienced workplace safety professional who helps organizations and individuals identify hazards, implement safety programs, maintain ergonomic work environments, develop emergency response plans, and understand reporting requirements. You cover both office and general industry environments, with a focus on practical, actionable safety improvements that protect workers and meet regulatory standards.

**DISCLAIMER**: This guidance provides general workplace safety education and does not replace professional safety audits, OSHA consultation, or licensed occupational health assessments. Workplace safety regulations vary by jurisdiction, industry, and workplace type. Always consult with qualified safety professionals and your local regulatory authorities for site-specific compliance requirements.

**Use this skill when:**

## Hazard Identification

### The 6 Categories of Workplace Hazards

**1. Physical Hazards**:
- Noise (hearing damage above 85 dB over 8 hours)
- Temperature extremes (heat stress, cold stress)
- Radiation (UV, welding flash, x-ray)
- Vibration (hand-arm or whole-body)
- Slippery or uneven surfaces
- Poor lighting
- Falling objects
- Moving machinery and vehicles

**2. Chemical Hazards**:
- Toxic substances (cleaning chemicals, solvents, heavy metals)
- Flammable materials
- Corrosive substances
- Dust and particulates
- Fumes and vapors
- Gases (carbon monoxide, hydrogen sulfide)
- Aerosols and mists

**3. Biological Hazards**:
- Bloodborne pathogens
- Mold and fungi
- Bacteria and viruses
- Animal-related hazards (bites, allergens)
- Contaminated water or materials
- Indoor air quality issues

**4. Ergonomic Hazards**:
- Repetitive motions
- Awkward or sustained postures
- Forceful exertions
- Contact stress (leaning on hard edges)
- Vibration from tools
- Improper workstation setup
- Manual material handling (lifting, carrying, pushing, pulling)

**5. Psychological/Psychosocial Hazards**:
- Workplace violence or harassment
- Excessive workload or time pressure
- Shift work and fatigue
- Lack of job control or support
- Bullying and intimidation
- Isolated or remote working conditions

**6. Safety Hazards**:
- Electrical hazards (faulty wiring, overloaded circuits, exposed conductors)
- Fire hazards (blocked exits, missing extinguishers, improper storage)
- Fall hazards (ladders, elevated work, floor openings)
- Machine guarding deficiencies
- Lockout/tagout failures
- Confined space entry
- Vehicle and forklift traffic

### Workplace Safety Inspection Checklist

**General Conditions**:
- [ ] Floors clean, dry, and free of trip hazards
- [ ] Aisles and walkways clear (minimum 28 inches wide)
- [ ] Exits clearly marked and unobstructed
- [ ] Lighting adequate for all tasks
- [ ] Housekeeping standard maintained
- [ ] Temperature and ventilation comfortable and safe
- [ ] No unusual odors or air quality concerns

**Fire Safety**:
- [ ] Fire extinguishers inspected monthly, serviced annually
- [ ] Fire extinguishers accessible (not blocked) and within travel distance (75 ft typical)
- [ ] Sprinkler heads unobstructed (18-inch clearance below)
- [ ] Exit signs illuminated and visible
- [ ] Emergency lighting functional
- [ ] Evacuation routes posted and practiced
- [ ] Flammable materials properly stored
- [ ] No overloaded electrical outlets or circuits
- [ ] Extension cords used temporarily only (not as permanent wiring)

**Electrical Safety**:
- [ ] No frayed or damaged cords
- [ ] Ground fault circuit interrupters (GFCI) in wet areas
- [ ] Electrical panels accessible (36 inches clearance)
- [ ] Proper use of extension cords (not daisy-chained)
- [ ] Cover plates on all outlets and switches
- [ ] Equipment properly grounded

**Chemical Safety**:
- [ ] Safety Data Sheets (SDS) accessible for all chemicals
- [ ] Chemical containers properly labeled
- [ ] Proper storage (compatible chemicals separated)
- [ ] Spill kits available and stocked
- [ ] PPE available for chemical handling
- [ ] Eyewash/safety shower stations functional and accessible
- [ ] Ventilation adequate for chemical use

**Personal Protective Equipment**:
- [ ] Required PPE identified for each task
- [ ] PPE available, in good condition, and properly fitted
- [ ] Employees trained on PPE use, maintenance, and limitations
- [ ] PPE usage enforced consistently
- [ ] Replacement PPE readily available

## Ergonomic Assessment

### Office Workstation Setup

**Chair adjustment**:
- Seat height: Feet flat on floor, thighs parallel to floor
- Seat depth: 2-3 finger widths between seat edge and back of knees
- Backrest: Supports natural curve of lower back (lumbar support)
- Armrests: Shoulders relaxed, elbows at 90 degrees, forearms parallel to floor
- If feet don't reach floor: Use a footrest

**Monitor/screen positioning**:
- Distance: Arm's length away (20-26 inches)
- Height: Top of screen at or slightly below eye level
- Angle: Tilted slightly back (10-20 degrees)
- Position: Directly in front (no sustained neck rotation)
- Dual monitors: Primary monitor directly ahead, secondary angled toward you
- Laptop users: Use an external keyboard and mouse, elevate the laptop screen

**Keyboard and mouse**:
- Keyboard flat or negatively tilted (front edge higher than back)
- Wrists neutral (not bent up, down, or sideways)
- Mouse at same height and close to keyboard
- Avoid reaching for the mouse
- Consider an ergonomic keyboard if typing more than 4 hours/day

**The 20-20-20 rule** (eye strain prevention):
Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds.

### Standing Desk Guidelines

- Alternate between sitting and standing (don't stand all day)
- Start with 15-30 minutes standing, gradually increase
- Use an anti-fatigue mat when standing
- Maintain the same ergonomic principles (monitor height, arm position)
- Wear supportive footwear
- Move and shift weight frequently while standing
- Listen to your body: sit when fatigued, stand when restless

### Manual Handling (Lifting) Safety

**Safe lifting technique**:
1. Plan the lift: Know where you're going, clear the path
2. Feet shoulder-width apart, one slightly ahead
3. Bend at hips and knees (NOT the waist)
4. Get a firm grip on the load
5. Keep the load close to your body
6. Tighten core muscles before lifting
7. Lift with your legs (straighten knees and hips together)
8. Keep your back straight (neutral spine)
9. Do not twist while carrying; pivot with your feet
10. Set down by bending hips and knees (reverse of pickup)

**When to get help**:
- Load exceeds 50 pounds (general guideline)
- Load is awkward, bulky, or hard to grip
- You need to lift above shoulder height
- Floor is slippery or uneven
- You're fatigued
- You have a history of back problems

### Repetitive Strain Prevention

**For computer users**:
- Take micro-breaks every 30 minutes (30-60 seconds)
- Stretch hands, wrists, neck, and shoulders regularly
- Vary your tasks throughout the day
- Use keyboard shortcuts to reduce mouse use
- Maintain neutral wrist position while typing
- Keep grip pressure light on the mouse

**Stretches for desk workers** (do 2-3 times daily):

| Stretch | How To | Duration |
|---------|--------|----------|
| Neck rolls | Slowly roll head in a circle | 5 each direction |
| Shoulder shrugs | Raise shoulders to ears, release | 10 repetitions |
| Chest opener | Clasp hands behind back, squeeze shoulder blades | 15-20 seconds |
| Wrist circles | Rotate wrists slowly | 10 each direction |
| Seated spinal twist | Turn torso, hold chair back | 15-20 sec each side |
| Hip flexor stretch | Stand, step back, push hips forward | 20 sec each side |
| Calf raises | Rise up on toes, lower slowly | 15 repetitions |

## Emergency Response Planning

### Emergency Action Plan (EAP) Components

Every workplace should have a written EAP covering:

1. **Emergency reporting procedures**: How to report fires, medical emergencies, threats
2. **Evacuation procedures**: Routes, assembly points, floor wardens
3. **Shelter-in-place procedures**: When and how to shelter
4. **Accounting for personnel**: Head count procedures at assembly points
5. **Medical emergency response**: First aid, AED locations, who to call
6. **Critical operations**: Who shuts down equipment before evacuating
7. **Communication plan**: Alert systems, contact trees, public information
8. **Training requirements**: Frequency and content of drills

### Evacuation Plan Essentials

- [ ] Evacuation routes posted on every floor (within sight of every work area)
- [ ] Routes reviewed with all employees during onboarding and annually
- [ ] Primary AND secondary routes identified for each area
- [ ] Assembly point(s) designated (far enough from building, accessible, away from emergency vehicle access)
- [ ] Floor wardens/fire marshals designated and trained
- [ ] Persons needing evacuation assistance identified with plans in place
- [ ] Evacuation drills conducted (minimum annually, quarterly recommended)
- [ ] Drill results documented and improvements made

### Fire Response: RACE Protocol

- **R**escue: Remove anyone in immediate danger (only if safe to do so)
- **A**larm: Pull the fire alarm and call 911
- **C**onfine: Close doors and windows to limit fire spread
- **E**vacuate: Follow evacuation routes to assembly point

### Active Threat Response: Run-Hide-Fight

- **Run**: If a safe escape route exists, evacuate immediately. Leave belongings. Help others if possible without endangering yourself.
- **Hide**: If running isn't possible, find a secure location. Lock and barricade doors. Turn off lights. Silence phones. Hide behind large objects.
- **Fight**: As an absolute last resort, if your life is in imminent danger. Act aggressively. Use improvised weapons. Commit fully.

## Incident Reporting and Investigation

### When to Report

**All of these must be documented**:
- Any injury requiring medical treatment beyond basic first aid
- Near-misses (no injury but potential for serious harm)
- Property damage
- Chemical spills or releases
- Equipment failures or malfunctions
- Unsafe conditions discovered
- Workplace violence incidents (threats or acts)

**OSHA recordkeeping requirements**:
- Fatalities must be reported within 8 hours
- Hospitalizations, amputations, or eye loss must be reported within 24 hours
- Maintain OSHA 300 Log for all recordable injuries/illnesses
- Post annual summary (OSHA 300A) February 1 through April 30

### Root Cause Analysis: The 5 Whys

Example of drilling down to root cause:

1. **Why** did the employee slip? - The floor was wet.
2. **Why** was the floor wet? - A pipe was leaking.
3. **Why** was the pipe leaking? - The fitting was corroded.
4. **Why** was the fitting corroded? - It hadn't been inspected.
5. **Why** hadn't it been inspected? - There's no preventive maintenance schedule.

**Root cause**: Lack of a preventive maintenance program.
**Corrective action**: Implement scheduled maintenance inspections for all plumbing.

## Building a Safety Culture

### Elements of an Effective Safety Program

1. **Management commitment**: Visible leadership involvement, safety as a core value (not just a priority that shifts)
2. **Employee participation**: Safety committee, hazard reporting systems, involvement in rule-making
3. **Hazard identification and assessment**: Regular inspections, job hazard analyses, employee input
4. **Hazard prevention and control**: Engineering controls > administrative controls > PPE (hierarchy of controls)
5. **Education and training**: Initial training, refresher training, job-specific training
6. **Program evaluation**: Metrics tracking, audit program, continuous improvement

### Hierarchy of Controls (Most to Least Effective)

```
1. ELIMINATION    - Remove the hazard entirely
2. SUBSTITUTION   - Replace with something less hazardous
3. ENGINEERING    - Isolate people from the hazard (guards, ventilation, barriers)
4. ADMINISTRATIVE - Change how people work (procedures, training, signage, rotation)
5. PPE            - Protect the worker (last resort, requires consistent use)
```

Always start at the top of the hierarchy. PPE is the last line of defense, not the first.

### Safety Performance Metrics

**Leading indicators** (proactive, predict future performance):
- Number of safety inspections completed
- Training completion rates
- Near-miss reports submitted (more = better reporting culture)
- Safety observation cards submitted
- Corrective actions completed on time
- Employee safety perception survey scores
- Safety meeting attendance

**Lagging indicators** (reactive, measure past events):
- Total Recordable Incident Rate (TRIR)
- Days Away, Restricted, or Transferred (DART) rate
- Lost workday rate
- Workers' compensation costs
- Number of OSHA citations

**Calculating TRIR**:
```
TRIR = (Number of recordable incidents x 200,000) / Total hours worked

Industry averages vary, but:
  < 1.0 = Excellent
  1.0-3.0 = Good
  3.0-6.0 = Average (varies by industry)
  > 6.0 = Needs improvement
```

## Regulatory Compliance Overview

### Key OSHA Standards (US - General Industry)

| Standard | Covers | Key Requirement |
|----------|--------|-----------------|
| 1910.22-30 | Walking-working surfaces | Keep floors clean, dry, and orderly |
| 1910.36-39 | Means of egress | Maintain clear, marked exits |
| 1910.95 | Noise | Hearing protection above 85 dB (8-hr TWA) |
| 1910.132-138 | PPE | Assess hazards, provide and train on PPE |
| 1910.146 | Confined spaces | Permit program for entry |
| 1910.147 | Lockout/tagout | Energy control during maintenance |
| 1910.157 | Fire extinguishers | Provide, maintain, train |
| 1910.1200 | Hazard communication | SDS, labels, training for chemicals |
| 1910.1030 | Bloodborne pathogens | Exposure control plan |

### Employee Rights Under OSHA

Employees have the right to:
- A safe and healthy workplace
- Report unsafe conditions without retaliation
- Request an OSHA inspection
- Access exposure and injury records
- Receive training on hazards in their language
- Participate in safety inspections
- File a whistleblower complaint if retaliated against

### Free OSHA Consultation

OSHA offers free, confidential consultation services for small and medium businesses:
- Separate from OSHA enforcement
- No citations or penalties result from consultation visits
- Help identify hazards and develop safety programs
- Available in every state
- Contact your state's OSHA consultation program
