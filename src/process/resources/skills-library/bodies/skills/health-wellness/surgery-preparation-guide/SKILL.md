## Questions to Ask First

- What surgery has been recommended, and when is it scheduled?
- Is this elective or urgent?
- Have you had surgery before? How was that experience?
- Do you have any chronic health conditions or take regular medications?
- Who is your support system (spouse, family, friends nearby)?
- Do you live alone?
- What is your work situation (how much time can you take off)?
- What concerns you most about the surgery?

## Important Disclaimer

This guide helps you prepare and ask informed questions. It does NOT replace your surgical team's specific instructions. Always follow your surgeon's and anesthesiologist's pre-operative orders, as they are tailored to your specific situation and procedure.

## Questions for Your Surgeon

### Before Agreeing to Surgery

Ask these at the consultation when surgery is first recommended:

**About the procedure:**
- What exactly will happen during the surgery?
- Why is this surgery recommended over other treatment options?
- What are the alternatives to surgery, and what happens if I choose not to have it?
- How many times have you performed this specific procedure?
- What is the success rate for this procedure?
- What are the risks and potential complications?
- How long will the surgery take?

**About the outcome:**
- What results can I realistically expect?
- How long until I see the full benefit of the surgery?
- What percentage of patients need a revision or repeat procedure?
- Are there lifestyle changes I should make before surgery to improve outcomes?

**About logistics:**
- Will this be inpatient or outpatient (same-day)?
- What type of anesthesia will be used?
- How long will I be in the hospital?
- What is the estimated total cost, and what does my insurance cover?
- Should I get a second opinion? (A good surgeon will not be offended by this question.)

### At the Pre-Op Appointment

- What medications should I stop or continue before surgery?
- When should I stop eating and drinking before surgery (NPO instructions)?
- Are there any supplements, vitamins, or over-the-counter medications I need to stop?
- Should I take my regular medications the morning of surgery?
- What should I bring to the hospital?
- What time should I arrive?
- Where should my family wait during the procedure?
- How will my family be updated during surgery?

### About Recovery

- What is the expected recovery timeline (return to work, driving, exercise)?
- What pain management plan do you recommend?
- Will I need physical therapy or rehabilitation?
- What are the warning signs that something is wrong after surgery?
- When is my first follow-up appointment?
- Who do I call if I have problems after hours?
- Will I have any restrictions (lifting, bending, driving, bathing)?
- What should my diet be after surgery?

## Pre-Surgery Checklist

### 4 Weeks Before Surgery

- [ ] Complete all pre-operative testing as ordered (blood work, EKG, imaging)
- [ ] Get medical clearance from your primary care doctor if required
- [ ] Review and understand your insurance coverage and expected out-of-pocket costs
- [ ] Begin stopping blood-thinning supplements as advised (fish oil, vitamin E, turmeric, ginkgo)
- [ ] Stop smoking if applicable (surgery complications increase significantly with smoking)
- [ ] Arrange time off from work
- [ ] Begin planning home recovery setup
- [ ] Stock up on easy-to-prepare meals or plan meal deliveries

### 2 Weeks Before Surgery

- [ ] Stop medications as directed by your surgeon (aspirin, blood thinners, anti-inflammatories)
- [ ] Fill post-surgery prescriptions in advance (pain medication, antibiotics, stool softeners)
- [ ] Confirm your ride home from the hospital (you CANNOT drive yourself after anesthesia)
- [ ] Confirm your support person for the first 24-48 hours after surgery
- [ ] Prepare your home recovery area (see Home Preparation section)
- [ ] Arrange help with children, pets, and household tasks
- [ ] Purchase recovery supplies (see supply list)
- [ ] Practice any pre-operative exercises your surgeon recommended

### 1 Week Before Surgery

- [ ] Confirm surgery date, time, and arrival time with the surgical center
- [ ] Confirm nothing-by-mouth (NPO) instructions
- [ ] Prepare a bag for the hospital (see packing list)
- [ ] Do a trial run of the drive to the hospital (parking, entrance location)
- [ ] Meal prep or fill the freezer with easy meals
- [ ] Clean your home (you will not want to do this during recovery)
- [ ] Wash bedding with fresh sheets
- [ ] Charge all devices, download entertainment for recovery
- [ ] Set up bill autopay if you do not already have it

### Night Before Surgery

- [ ] Stop eating at the time specified by your surgeon (typically midnight)
- [ ] Shower with antibacterial soap if instructed (commonly Hibiclens)
- [ ] Do not apply lotions, deodorant, or makeup (morning of)
- [ ] Lay out comfortable, loose clothing for the morning
- [ ] Pack your hospital bag
- [ ] Set multiple alarms (you will be anxious and may not sleep well; that is normal)
- [ ] Practice relaxation: deep breathing, meditation, or calming music

### Morning of Surgery

- [ ] Shower with antibacterial soap if instructed
- [ ] Take only the medications your surgeon approved with a small sip of water
- [ ] Do NOT eat or drink anything else (unless specifically told otherwise)
- [ ] Wear comfortable, loose clothing (easy to remove and put on)
- [ ] Leave jewelry, valuables, and contact lenses at home
- [ ] Bring your ID, insurance card, and advance directive if you have one
- [ ] Arrive at the time specified (usually 1-2 hours before surgery)

## Home Preparation

### Recovery Station Setup

Create a comfortable recovery base:

- Bed or recliner at the right height (getting up from low surfaces is hard after many surgeries)
- Side table within arm's reach for water, medications, phone, remote, tissues
- Phone charger accessible from the recovery spot
- Good lighting and a reading lamp
- Entertainment: books, tablet, remote control within reach
- Pillow for support (extra pillows for elevation if needed)
- Blanket within reach
- Small trash can nearby
- Bell or noisemaker to call for help if your support person is in another room

### Home Safety Modifications

| If Your Surgery Involves... | Prepare This |
|---------------------------|--------------|
| Lower body (hip, knee, foot) | Raised toilet seat, shower chair, grab bars, remove trip hazards (rugs, cords) |
| Upper body (shoulder, arm) | Pre-open jars, move daily items to counter height, button-free clothing |
| Abdominal | Pillow for splinting (hold against incision when coughing), stool softener ready |
| Any general anesthesia | Non-slip mats, clear pathways, nightlights for bathroom trips |

### Meal Preparation

Stock your kitchen before surgery:
- Freezer meals in single-serving portions
- Easy proteins: yogurt, cheese, rotisserie chicken, protein shakes
- Fresh fruit that requires no preparation (bananas, grapes, berries)
- Crackers, toast, and bland foods (useful if medications cause nausea)
- Electrolyte drinks (Pedialyte or sports drinks)
- Plenty of water
- Fiber-rich foods (surgery medications often cause constipation)
- Arrange meal delivery from friends, family, or meal delivery services

## Support System Coordination

### Roles to Assign

| Role | Responsibilities |
|------|-----------------|
| Hospital buddy | Drives you, waits during surgery, drives you home |
| First 24 hours | Stays with you the first night, helps with medications, monitors for complications |
| Meal coordinator | Organizes meal deliveries from friends/family or sets up meal train |
| Childcare/pet care | Handles kids and/or pets for the first 3-7 days |
| Errand runner | Pharmacy pickups, grocery runs, mail |
| Work liaison | Communicates with your workplace as needed |
| Communication hub | Updates family and friends so you do not have to field dozens of calls |

### Setting Up a Meal Train

Websites like MealTrain.com, TakeThemAMeal.com, or a simple shared spreadsheet:
1. Set dates and times for meal delivery
2. Note dietary restrictions and preferences
3. Share the link with friends, family, colleagues, and faith community
4. Include your address and delivery instructions
5. People sign up for specific dates

## Managing Pre-Surgery Anxiety

### Normal Feelings Before Surgery

It is entirely normal to feel:
- Anxious about the procedure and anesthesia
- Worried about complications
- Frustrated about the disruption to your life
- Vulnerable about losing control
- Grateful that treatment is available

All of these can coexist. None of them mean something is wrong with you.

### Anxiety Management Techniques

**Breathing exercise (use the night before and morning of):**
Inhale for 4 counts through your nose, hold for 4 counts, exhale for 6 counts through your mouth. The longer exhale activates calming pathways. Repeat 5-10 cycles.

**Information as anxiety reduction:**
For many people, understanding the procedure reduces fear. Ask your surgeon to explain what will happen step by step. Watch approved educational videos about the procedure (avoid random internet horror stories).

**Visualization:**
Close your eyes and mentally walk through the entire experience going well: arrival, check-in, the surgical team greeting you with confidence, waking up with the procedure complete, recovering at home feeling better each day.

**What to tell yourself:**
- "My surgical team does this every day. This is routine for them."
- "I have prepared thoroughly. I have done everything I can."
- "Anxiety is a normal response. It does not mean something will go wrong."
- "I will be asleep during the procedure. My body is in expert hands."

## Recovery Phase

### First 24-48 Hours

- Anesthesia effects: grogginess, nausea, confusion are normal and temporary
- Pain management: take medications on schedule, not just when pain is severe (staying ahead of pain is easier than catching up)
- Hydration: sip water frequently, even if not thirsty
- Movement: follow your surgeon's instructions (many surgeries require early gentle movement)
- Rest: sleep is healing; nap as much as your body wants

### Warning Signs to Call Your Surgeon

Call your surgeon's office immediately if you experience:
- Fever above 101.5°F / 38.6°C
- Increasing pain that medication does not control
- Redness, swelling, or drainage at the incision site
- Signs of blood clot: calf pain, leg swelling, shortness of breath
- Difficulty breathing or chest pain
- Inability to keep fluids down for more than 24 hours
- No bowel movement for 3+ days after surgery
- Any symptom that feels seriously wrong to you (trust your instincts)

### Recovery Timeline Management

Recovery takes longer than most people expect. Set realistic expectations:

| Phase | Duration (varies by surgery) | What to Expect |
|-------|------------------------------|----------------|
| Acute recovery | Days 1-7 | Most pain and limited mobility; rest is primary |
| Early recovery | Weeks 1-3 | Gradual improvement, still limited; increase activity slowly |
| Mid recovery | Weeks 3-6 | Significantly improved but not back to normal |
| Late recovery | Weeks 6-12 | Returning to most normal activities |
| Full recovery | 3-12 months | Complete healing (some surgeries take a full year) |

**The recovery dip:** Many patients feel great at week 2, overdo it, and feel worse at week 3. Follow your surgeon's activity restrictions even when you feel good.
