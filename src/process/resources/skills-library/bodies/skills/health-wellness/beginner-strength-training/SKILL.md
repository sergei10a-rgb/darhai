# Beginner Strength Training

> **Disclaimer:** This skill provides general wellness and health information for educational purposes only. It does NOT constitute medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment recommendations. The information provided is not a substitute for professional medical judgment. Always consult a qualified healthcare professional before starting a new fitness program, especially if you have pre-existing conditions, joint pain, cardiovascular concerns, or have been sedentary for an extended period. If you experience chest pain, dizziness, or acute joint pain during training, stop immediately and seek medical attention.

---

## When to Use

**Use this skill when:**
- The user explicitly states they are new to strength training, have never followed a structured lifting program, or are starting from zero
- The user asks how to "get started with weights," "build a beginner gym routine," or "learn to lift properly"
- The user has fewer than 3-4 months of consistent, structured barbell or dumbbell training history -- inconsistent gym visits over a year still qualify as beginner
- The user wants to understand progressive overload, how to choose starting weights, or how often to lift as a beginner
- The user asks for a 3-day-per-week full-body program, a first powerlifting-style routine, or a simple strength program they can follow for the next several months
- The user is returning to the gym after a break of 12+ months and needs to rebuild from scratch
- The user is a parent, student, or busy professional looking for a time-efficient entry point into structured resistance training

**Do NOT use when:**
- The user has 6+ months of consistent structured training -- use `intermediate-strength-training` instead, which covers undulating periodization and more complex split structures
- The user is recovering from a rotator cuff tear, spinal surgery, ACL repair, or any acute orthopedic injury -- refer them to a licensed physical therapist before programming
- The user needs a sport-specific strength program (e.g., in-season athlete, rugby player building power) -- use `sports-specific-conditioning`
- The user explicitly wants advanced programming concepts like conjugate method, block periodization, or max-effort/dynamic-effort training -- use `training-program-periodization`
- The user asks exclusively about bodyweight training with no equipment -- use `home-workout-no-equipment`
- The user's primary goal is weight loss with no interest in strength -- use `nutrition-and-fat-loss` as the primary skill and this as a supplementary reference
- The user is under 15 years old -- youth resistance training has different programming considerations regarding load, growth plates, and supervision that require specialized guidance

## Process

### Step 1: Gather Essential User Information

Ask the following before building any program. Do not skip this step or make assumptions.

- **Primary goal:** Distinguish between general strength (moving heavier loads over time), muscle hypertrophy (increasing muscle size), or general fitness (improving body composition and conditioning). These goals use identical beginner programming -- the distinction affects accessory selection and rep range emphasis (strength skews 5-6 reps; hypertrophy skews 10-12 reps; general fitness uses the middle 8-10).
- **Available equipment:** Full commercial gym (barbells, squat rack, cables, machines), home barbell setup (power rack or squat stands with plates), dumbbells only (adjustable or fixed), or kettlebells only. Equipment determines which movement variations are possible.
- **Days per week available:** Accept 2-4 days. Three days is optimal for beginners because it provides 48 hours of recovery between sessions while training each muscle group three times every two weeks. Four days is acceptable but not superior at this stage -- more is not better when the nervous system is still adapting to novel loading.
- **Session duration available:** 45 minutes versus 60-75 minutes affects how many accessory exercises are realistic per session.
- **Movement limitations:** Ask specifically about the following because they are the most common beginner restrictions: hip discomfort during squatting (depth limitation), shoulder pain during overhead pressing (impingement concerns), lower back sensitivity during hinging (prior injury or disc history, though this does not disqualify deadlifting -- it requires regression to safer loading positions).
- **Current activity level:** Completely sedentary (desk job, no regular physical activity), casually active (walking, recreational sports, but no structured training), or physically active in another domain (endurance athlete, martial artist, manual labor job). Sedentary beginners need a 2-week adaptation period at reduced intensity before full progressive overload begins.

### Step 2: Select the Correct Program Structure

Use this decision tree based on the user's input:

- **3 days/week + full gym or barbell:** Full-body A/B alternating split. Workout A anchors around the squat as the primary lower body movement. Workout B anchors around the deadlift or Romanian deadlift. Upper body push and pull movements vary between sessions to prevent repetitive stress and provide slightly different stimuli.
- **3 days/week + dumbbells only:** Full-body A/B alternating split using dumbbell-specific movement progressions. The goblet squat is the primary squat variation until form is grooved. The dumbbell Romanian deadlift serves as the primary hinge. Single-arm dumbbell rows replace barbell rows because they allow heavier loading per side and correct asymmetries.
- **2 days/week (minimum viable frequency):** Two identical full-body sessions per week. Add one extra set per compound movement (4 sets instead of 3) to compensate for lower frequency. Deload every 3rd week, not every 4th, because per-session volume is higher. Warn the user that 2 days is acceptable but progress will be measurably slower than 3 days -- studies on frequency consistently show 2x/week training produces roughly 60-70% of the strength gains seen with 3x/week in beginners.
- **4 days/week:** Upper/Lower split. Upper A (horizontal push/pull emphasis), Lower A (squat emphasis), Upper B (vertical push/pull emphasis), Lower B (hinge emphasis). This is appropriate only if the user has a strong preference for 4 days -- it is not more effective than 3 days for a beginner and adds scheduling complexity.
- **Kettlebell-only:** Full-body sessions built around the kettlebell goblet squat, Romanian deadlift, single-arm press, and single-arm row. The kettlebell swing serves as the hinge-based power exercise. Progression is achieved by increasing reps before moving up bell sizes (since kettlebell increments are typically 8-16 lbs/4-8 kg, double progression is mandatory).

### Step 3: Build Workout A and Workout B

Assign exercises in this exact loading order -- do not deviate, because this sequence places the most neurologically demanding, fatigue-sensitive movements first when the nervous system is freshest.

**Workout A -- Squat-Dominant Full Body:**
1. Primary compound lower (squat pattern): Barbell back squat, barbell front squat, goblet squat, dumbbell squat, leg press as last resort
2. Primary compound upper push (horizontal): Barbell bench press, dumbbell bench press, push-up with added load (weighted vest or band)
3. Primary compound upper pull (horizontal): Barbell bent-over row, dumbbell single-arm row, cable seated row
4. Accessory upper push (vertical): Dumbbell overhead press, seated barbell overhead press, landmine press (shoulder-friendly variation)
5. Accessory upper pull (vertical): Lat pulldown, cable pull-down, assisted or negative pull-ups, dumbbell pullover
6. Core anti-extension: Plank variations, ab wheel rollout (regression: plank; progression: ab wheel), cable Pallof press

**Workout B -- Hinge-Dominant Full Body:**
1. Primary compound lower (hinge pattern): Conventional barbell deadlift (if comfortable), Romanian deadlift (safer for beginners due to reduced lumbar load at initiation), dumbbell RDL, trap bar deadlift (ideal for beginners -- more upright torso, reduced shear on spine)
2. Primary compound upper push (incline or vertical): Dumbbell incline bench press (30-45 degree angle), barbell overhead press, landmine press
3. Primary compound upper pull (horizontal variation): Cable seated row (close grip or wide grip, alternating from Workout A), chest-supported dumbbell row (eliminates lower back fatigue from standing rows)
4. Accessory lower: Leg curl (machine), dumbbell Romanian deadlift (if conventional deadlift was used in #1), walking lunge with dumbbells
5. Accessory upper pull (vertical): Same lat pulldown or pull-up work as Workout A, or rotate to face-pull for rear delt and upper back health
6. Core anti-rotation or anti-lateral-flexion: Dead bug, Pallof press, suitcase carry (farmer's walk, single-arm)

**Selection hierarchy when equipment is limited:** If a squat rack is unavailable, the goblet squat is the first choice over the Smith machine squat -- the goblet squat teaches proper anterior core bracing and hip-hinge mechanics. The Smith machine is acceptable only as a last resort because it removes frontal-plane stabilizer demands.

### Step 4: Assign Sets, Reps, and Rest Intervals

Apply the following scheme based on the user's stated goal. These are not arbitrary -- they align with the minimum effective dose of volume for neuromuscular adaptation in untrained individuals:

**Strength emphasis (primary goal: getting stronger):**
- Compound movements (exercises 1-3 in each workout): 3 sets x 5-6 reps, 3 minutes rest
- Accessory movements: 3 sets x 8-10 reps, 90 seconds rest
- Core: 3 sets x 8-12 reps or 20-30 seconds, 60 seconds rest

**Hypertrophy/general fitness emphasis (primary goal: muscle size or body composition):**
- Compound movements: 3 sets x 8-10 reps, 2-3 minutes rest
- Accessory movements: 3 sets x 10-12 reps, 60-90 seconds rest
- Core: 2-3 sets x 30-60 seconds or 10-15 reps, 60 seconds rest

**Why these numbers matter:** Sets of 5 on compound movements allow heavier absolute loads, which drives neural adaptation faster in early training. Sets of 8-10 are the sweet spot for simultaneous strength and muscle development. Going below 5 reps as a beginner is counterproductive -- technique is not yet automated, and heavy singles or triples create high injury risk before motor patterns are grooved. Going above 15 reps on compound movements for strength goals wastes recovery capacity on cardiovascular conditioning when the nervous system needs mechanical tension stimulus.

**Rest intervals:** The 2-3 minute rest on compounds is evidence-based -- phosphocreatine resynthesis (the primary energy system for short, heavy sets) reaches approximately 94% recovery at 3 minutes. Cutting rest to 60 seconds on compound movements results in 15-20% performance decrements on subsequent sets, leading to technical breakdown under fatigue. For accessories and isolation work, 60-90 seconds is sufficient because local muscular fatigue, not phosphocreatine depletion, is the limiting factor.

### Step 5: Define the Starting Weight Protocol

The most common beginner mistake is starting too heavy. Provide this explicit guidance:

- **The empty bar test:** For any barbell movement, start with just the bar (45 lbs/20 kg) for the first session regardless of perceived strength. Perform 2 sets of 10 with perfect form. If it feels easy and the form is solid, add 20 lbs (10 kg) and try another set. Continue adding small increments until the movement feels like an 8 out of 10 effort for the last 2 reps of a set. That is the starting working weight.
- **The 10-rep comfort test for dumbbells:** Select a weight at which the 8th rep requires concentration and the 10th rep is genuinely difficult but achievable with good form. If the 10th rep is easy, go heavier. If the 8th rep is a grind, go lighter.
- **Goblet squat starting weight:** 15-25 lbs (7-11 kg) for most beginners. The goblet squat's positioning (weight in front) limits how heavy one can go while maintaining an upright torso -- this is a feature, not a bug.
- **Deadlift starting weight:** Begin with the bar elevated on plates or use a trap bar with the high handles if available. Never start a beginner's first conventional deadlift from the floor if lumbar rounding occurs at the starting position. An elevated bar (plates under the 45 lb plates, reducing the range of motion by 2-4 inches) allows proper bracing and neutral spine until hip mobility improves.

### Step 6: Define the Progressive Overload Protocol

This is the most critical long-term element of beginner programming. Beginners can add weight to the bar more frequently than any other population -- taking advantage of this window is the primary programming objective.

**Linear progression standard (works for the first 2-4 months):**
- Lower body compound lifts (squat, deadlift, leg press): Add 5 lbs (2.5 kg) when the user completes all prescribed sets at the top of the rep range for TWO consecutive sessions. Example: 3 sets x 10 reps of goblet squat with 45 lbs on Monday and Wednesday both → add 5 lbs, start next session at 50 lbs x 8-10.
- Upper body compound lifts (bench press, overhead press, rows): Add 2.5 lbs (1.25 kg) using microplates. If microplates are unavailable, use double progression -- add reps within the range before adding weight (e.g., 3x8 → 3x9 → 3x10 → add weight, drop back to 3x8).
- Accessory lifts (lateral raises, curls, tricep work): Add 2.5-5 lbs when completing all sets at the top rep range for two consecutive sessions. Dumbbell accessory progression is often limited by equipment increments (fixed dumbbells jump 5 lbs at a time) -- use double progression in this case.

**Failure handling:** If the user cannot complete the minimum rep range (e.g., fails to get 8 reps on the third set after a weight increase), they should stay at that weight for the next session and try again. If they fail at the same weight for THREE consecutive sessions, reduce the weight by 10% and rebuild from there. This is not a regression -- it is a sign that the previous weight increase was too large or recovery was insufficient.

**Rep range reset (the most useful beginner tool):** When a weight increase causes a drop from 3x10 to 3x8, that is normal and expected. The user is now working at the bottom of the rep range with the new weight. They build back to 3x10 at the new weight before increasing again. This creates a natural undulating stimulus within the rep range without complex periodization.

### Step 7: Define the Deload Protocol and 12-Week Cycle Structure

**Standard deload (every 4th week):**
- Reduce all working weights by 40% (a 100-lb squat becomes a 60-lb squat during deload week)
- Reduce sets by 1 per exercise (3 sets becomes 2 sets for compounds; 2 sets becomes 1-2 sets for accessories)
- Maintain the same rep ranges and exercises -- deload is not a vacation, it is planned under-stimulation that allows connective tissue, the nervous system, and systemic fatigue to recover
- Train all scheduled days during the deload week -- skipping sessions disrupts the habit and delays return to full training
- After deload, resume at PRE-DELOAD weights and continue progression from there. Do not resume at deload weights.

**12-week cycle structure:**
- Weeks 1-3: Training weeks with progressive overload applied session by session
- Week 4: Deload
- Weeks 5-7: Training weeks (resume at Week 3 weights, continue progression)
- Week 8: Deload
- Weeks 9-11: Training weeks
- Week 12: Deload + assessment week (test a 5-rep max on squat, bench, and deadlift to quantify progress)

**Why 3 weeks loading, 1 week deload?** In beginners, systemic fatigue accumulates quickly because the movements are neurologically novel. Three weeks of loading followed by one week of recovery strikes the balance between stimulus frequency and recovery adequacy. Some advanced programs use 4-6 weeks before deloading, but beginners benefit from more frequent recovery windows while motor patterns are being established.

### Step 8: Specify Warm-Up and Cooldown Protocol

**Warm-up (not optional -- skip this only if session must be compressed under 45 minutes, in which case general cardio can be omitted but bar warm-up sets cannot):**

1. 5 minutes general cardio at moderate intensity: brisk walk on treadmill (3.5 mph, 1% incline), stationary bike (level 5-7), or rowing machine (moderate pace). Purpose: elevate core temperature, increase synovial fluid viscosity in joints, prime cardiovascular system.
2. Dynamic mobility circuit specific to that day's movements (takes 2-3 minutes): For squat days -- leg swings (front to back, 10 each leg), hip circles (10 each direction), bodyweight squat holds (2 x 10 reps pausing 2 seconds at the bottom). For deadlift days -- cat-cow (10 reps), glute bridges (2 x 10), Romanian deadlift with bodyweight or PVC pipe (2 x 10).
3. Bar warm-up sets for the first compound exercise: Set 1 -- empty bar (or lightest available weight) x 10 reps at working pace. Set 2 -- 50% of working weight x 6 reps. Set 3 -- 75% of working weight x 3 reps. Then proceed to working weight.
4. For subsequent compound exercises in the session: One warm-up set at 60-70% of working weight for that exercise. No additional warm-up needed for accessory movements.

**Cooldown (recommended but not mandatory if time is limited):**
- 5 minutes of walking at a slow pace to bring heart rate back toward resting
- Static stretching held 30-40 seconds per position (not 20 seconds -- research shows 30+ seconds is needed to produce meaningful acute flexibility changes): Hip flexor stretch (kneeling lunge position), hamstring stretch (supine or standing), pectoral stretch (doorway or foam roller), thoracic extension over foam roller (especially valuable for desk workers who will be squatting and pressing)

## Rules

1. **Always include the medical disclaimer before delivering any program.** Do not abbreviate or omit it. Users may have undiagnosed cardiovascular conditions, herniated discs, or joint pathology that would make unsupervised heavy lifting dangerous.

2. **Never prescribe specific starting weights in pounds or kilograms.** Prescribe the protocol for FINDING the right starting weight (empty bar test, 10-rep comfort test). The correct starting weight is individual and cannot be known without observing the user. Providing a number risks under-loading (no adaptation stimulus) or over-loading (injury or form breakdown).

3. **Never remove a fundamental movement pattern -- substitute it.** If the user cannot squat, substitute with a goblet squat, split squat, or leg press. If they cannot deadlift conventionally, use a trap bar deadlift or Romanian deadlift. The four patterns -- squat, hinge, horizontal push, horizontal pull -- must appear in the weekly program. Removing a pattern creates imbalances.

4. **Always prescribe rest intervals as a range, not a fixed number, for compounds.** "2-3 minutes" is correct. "2 minutes" is too rigid. Heart rate variability, prior fitness, and session fatigue all affect recovery time. The user should rest until they feel ready to give full effort on the next set, with 3 minutes as the upper limit before sessions run too long.

5. **The deload must be active training, not complete rest.** The 40% weight reduction is non-negotiable. Users who feel "too good to deload" are the ones who most need it -- subjective freshness does not indicate readiness for continued loading. Connective tissue adaptation lags behind muscular adaptation by weeks, and the deload gives tendons and ligaments time to catch up.

6. **The A/B workout alternation must not be disrupted to create consecutive same-muscle-group sessions.** If Monday is Workout A (squat-dominant), Wednesday must be Workout B. Never schedule two squat-dominant days back-to-back. The 48-hour minimum between sessions training the same primary movers is a hard rule, not a guideline.

7. **If the user mentions any pain during a specific movement -- not general soreness, but sharp or joint pain -- that movement must be immediately flagged and excluded from the program until they consult a healthcare professional.** Provide a substitution, but explicitly state the movement should be evaluated before returning to it. Do not attempt to diagnose the cause of pain.

8. **Do not recommend training more than 4 days per week for a beginner.** Five or six days per week for a beginner creates systemic fatigue that outpaces recovery. More frequency does not accelerate beginner gains -- it increases dropout rates and injury risk. If the user strongly prefers more days, redirect to active recovery activities (walking, swimming, mobility work) on non-training days.

9. **Never suggest supplements, protein powders, pre-workouts, or specific nutritional products.** These fall outside this skill's scope and introduce product recommendations that are not medically supervised. If the user asks about nutrition, acknowledge its importance and refer them to a registered dietitian or nutritionist.

10. **Do not program failure (going until reps cannot be completed) for any compound lift.** Beginners training to failure on barbell squats, deadlifts, or bench presses dramatically increases injury risk because they cannot safely bail out of a failed lift until spotting and safety bar techniques are mastered. Leave 1-2 reps in reserve (RIR) on every compound set. Failure is appropriate for machines and isolation work only after technique is established.

11. **Always include a tracking mechanism.** A beginner who does not track their sets, reps, and weights cannot apply progressive overload correctly. The training log template is not optional -- it is the accountability system that makes progressive overload possible. Recommend a simple notebook, a notes app, or a training journal app.

12. **When the user asks "how much weight should I start with?" -- do not give a number, but do not just say "start light."** Provide the specific protocol: empty bar test for barbell movements, 10-rep comfort test for dumbbells. These are actionable methods, not vague advice.

## Edge Cases

### Dumbbells Only (No Barbell or Cable Machines)
Replace all barbell movements with dumbbell equivalents: goblet squat (primary lower body), dumbbell bench press (horizontal push), single-arm dumbbell row (horizontal pull), dumbbell Romanian deadlift (hinge), dumbbell overhead press (vertical push), dumbbell pullover (vertical pull substitute). The goblet squat has a natural load ceiling of approximately 80-100 lbs for most people due to the grip and position -- when the user approaches this ceiling and it feels light, transition to dumbbell front squat (holding two dumbbells at shoulder height) or recommend a barbell as the next equipment investment. Progression increments become 2.5-5 lbs for all movements since the smallest dumbbell jump is typically 5 lbs (2.5 kg) -- double progression (adding reps before adding weight) is mandatory for every movement in this setup.

### Only 2 Days Per Week Available
Combine A and B elements into a single full-body session. Include one squat pattern, one hinge pattern, one horizontal push, one horizontal pull, one vertical pull, and one core movement per session. Increase compound sets from 3 to 4 per exercise to compensate for reduced weekly frequency. Deload every 3rd week (not every 4th) because per-session volume is higher and fatigue accumulates faster. Warn the user that twice-weekly training is sufficient for measurable progress -- research indicates ~70% of the strength gains achievable with 3 days/week can be obtained in 2 days/week for beginners -- but that 3 days is meaningfully more effective if scheduling can be adjusted.

### User Is 50+ Years Old or Returning After 12+ Months Inactive
Prescribe a 2-week "practice phase" before formal progressive overload begins. During weeks 1-2, the user trains with 50-60% of perceived maximum effort -- focusing entirely on movement quality, not load. Extend rest intervals to 3-4 minutes between compound sets. Begin deloading every 3rd week instead of every 4th. Reduce initial rep range to 8-10 instead of 8-12 to limit time under tension while connective tissue readapts. Note that the neuromuscular system retains muscle memory ("muscle memory" via myonuclei retention) from prior training -- if the user trained seriously in their 20s or 30s, they will regain strength faster than a true beginner, which may mean the linear progression phase is shorter.

### User Cannot Perform Conventional Deadlift (Lower Back Sensitivity)
Do not remove the hinge pattern -- instead, regress aggressively. The safety hierarchy from lowest lumbar shear load to highest is: dumbbell RDL with dumbbells held at sides → barbell RDL starting from hip height (rack pulls) → trap bar deadlift with high handles → conventional deadlift from the floor. Start the user at the appropriate position in this hierarchy. Educate them that the deadlift family is not inherently dangerous -- technique, starting position, and load selection determine spinal load, not the movement pattern itself. Recommend consulting a physical therapist specifically about the deadlift if they have had a prior disc injury before progressing to conventional loading.

### User Has No Microplates (Cannot Add 2.5 lbs to Upper Body Barbell Lifts)
Implement mandatory double progression for all upper body barbell work. Use the following explicit progression table:
- Session 1: 3 sets x 8 reps at weight X (bottom of range)
- Session 2: 3 sets x 9 reps at X
- Session 3: 3 sets x 10 reps at X (top of range, achieved for all sets)
- Session 4: 3 sets x 10 reps at X again (second consecutive session confirming readiness)
- Session 5: Add 5 lbs (the smallest available increment), drop back to 3 x 8
Repeat. This prevents the stalling that occurs when a 5-lb jump is applied before the nervous system has fully adapted to the current weight.

### User Has a Demanding Physical Job (Construction, Nursing, Manual Labor)
The user is not truly sedentary, but their fatigue profile is different -- their work creates high cumulative lower body and grip fatigue from walking, lifting patients, carrying materials. Reduce the initial starting intensity to 50-60% perceived effort for the first 2 weeks. Prioritize upper body training on days following especially demanding work shifts. Consider programming training days on lighter work days if schedule allows. Warn the user that progress may stall faster than average because total systemic stress (training + occupational) must be managed. Deload protocols become more important, not less.

### User Asks About Adding Cardio to This Program
Beginner strength training and cardio are compatible but require intelligent scheduling. For fat loss goals, 2-3 sessions of 20-30 minute low-intensity steady-state cardio (LISS) per week on non-training days is additive and does not meaningfully interfere with strength adaptations. High-intensity interval training (HIIT) done on the same day as lower body strength training significantly impairs strength adaptation through the "interference effect" -- specifically, AMPK-mediated inhibition of mTOR signaling. Recommend LISS on off days if cardio is desired. Never program HIIT on lower body strength training days.

### User Has Access to a Smith Machine But No Squat Rack
The Smith machine is a viable tool for beginners with caveats. For squats, the fixed bar path means the feet need to be placed approximately 6-12 inches further forward than a free barbell squat to accommodate the vertical movement pattern without excessive lumbar flexion. Smith machine rows are acceptable. Overhead pressing on the Smith machine is useful for beginners learning to press without balance concerns. Clearly explain to the user that the Smith machine reduces stabilizer muscle activation compared to free weights -- it is a training tool with a ceiling, and transitioning to free weights should occur within 4-8 weeks as motor patterns develop.

## Example

**User Input:** "I just joined a gym for the first time ever. I'm 32, work a desk job, and I want to get stronger and put on some muscle. The gym has barbells, dumbbells, cables, and machines. I can go Monday, Wednesday, and Friday. I have about 60 minutes per session. I have no injuries but my hips are a little tight from sitting all day."

---

## Beginner Strength Program: Strength + Hypertrophy

**Schedule:** 3 days/week -- Monday (Workout A), Wednesday (Workout B), Friday (Workout A), alternating weekly (Week 2: B/A/B, Week 3: A/B/A)
**Equipment:** Full commercial gym (barbells, dumbbells, cables, machines)
**Program Duration:** 12-week cycle (Weeks 1-3: training, Week 4: deload -- repeated 3 times)
**Starting Approach:** Use the empty bar test for all barbell movements on your first session. Use the 10-rep comfort test for all dumbbell movements.
**Hip Tightness Note:** Hip flexor mobility work is included in the Workout A warm-up. If you experience hip or groin discomfort during squatting, reduce depth to a pain-free range and consult a healthcare professional before progressing depth.

---

### Workout A -- Squat-Dominant Full Body

| Order | Exercise                 | Sets x Reps   | Rest    | Key Form Cue                                                                 |
|-------|--------------------------|--------------|---------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| A1    | Barbell Back Squat       | 3 x 8-10     | 2-3 min | Brace your core before unracking. Push knees out over toes. Descend to parallel -- do not force depth if hips are tight. |
| A2    | Barbell Bench Press      | 3 x 8-10     | 2-3 min | Retract and depress shoulder blades before lowering the bar. Touch lower chest. Feet flat on the floor. |
| A3    | Barbell Bent-Over Row    | 3 x 8-10     | 2-3 min | Hinge to roughly 45 degrees. Pull the bar to your lower ribcage. Do not use momentum -- lower under control. |
| A4    | Dumbbell Overhead Press  | 3 x 10-12    | 90s     | Press in a slight forward arc (about 15 degrees in front of the head), not perfectly vertical. Elbows at 30-45 degrees to the torso at the bottom. |
| A5    | Lat Pulldown             | 3 x 10-12    | 90s     | Lean back 10-15 degrees. Initiate by depressing the shoulder blades, then drive elbows toward your hips. Pull to upper chest. |
| A6    | Dead Bug                 | 2 x 10/side  | 60s     | Press your lower back firmly into the floor throughout. Extend opposite arm and leg simultaneously. Do not let your spine arch off the mat. |

**Workout A Total Volume:** 17 working sets -- estimated 50-60 minutes including warm-up

---

### Workout B -- Hinge-Dominant Full Body

| Order | Exercise                     | Sets x Reps   | Rest    | Key Form Cue                                                                 |
|-------|------------------------------|--------------|---------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| B1    | Romanian Deadlift (Barbell)  | 3 x 8-10     | 2-3 min | Start from the rack at hip height. Hinge back by pushing hips behind you -- the bar stays in contact with your legs throughout. Lower until you feel a hamstring stretch (usually just below the knee), then drive hips forward to stand. |
| B2    | Dumbbell Incline Press       | 3 x 8-10     | 2-3 min | Set bench at 30-45 degrees. Press dumbbells in a slight arc, touching them lightly at the top. Do not let elbows flare past 45 degrees from torso. |
| B3    | Cable Seated Row (Close Grip)| 3 x 8-10     | 2-3 min | Sit upright -- do not lean back. Pull the handle to your lower sternum, squeeze shoulder blades together at the end of the pull. Return under control. |
| B4    | Dumbbell Walking Lunge       | 3 x 10/side  | 90s     | Step forward far enough that your front shin stays vertical when your back knee nearly touches the floor. Take your time -- do not rush the rhythm at the expense of knee alignment. |
| B5    | Face Pull (Cable, rope)      | 3 x 12-15    | 60s     | Set the cable at roughly eye level. Pull the rope to your ears, not your face. External rotation at the end of the movement is the goal. Critical for long-term shoulder health if you are pressing frequently. |
| B6    | Pallof Press (Cable)         | 2 x 10/side  | 60s     | Stand perpendicular to the cable stack. Hold the handle at chest height with both hands. Press directly forward and hold 2 seconds, then return. Resist the cable pulling you to rotate -- that resistance IS the exercise. |

**Workout B Total Volume:** 17 working sets -- estimated 50-60 minutes including warm-up

### Progressive Overload Protocol

| Movement Type               | Weight Increment   | Trigger                                                      |
|-----------------------------|--------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------|
| Barbell Back Squat          | +5 lbs (2.5 kg)    | 3 x 10 achieved for 2 consecutive sessions                   |
| Romanian Deadlift           | +5 lbs (2.5 kg)    | 3 x 10 achieved for 2 consecutive sessions                   |
| Barbell Bench Press         | +2.5 lbs (1.25 kg) | 3 x 10 achieved for 2 consecutive sessions (use microplates) |
| Barbell Bent-Over Row       | +2.5 lbs (1.25 kg) | 3 x 10 achieved for 2 consecutive sessions                   |
| Dumbbell Incline Press      | Double progression  | Add reps (8→9→10) before adding weight (then drop back to 8) |
| Cable Seated Row            | +5 lbs on stack    | 3 x 10 achieved for 2 consecutive sessions                   |
| Dumbbell Overhead Press     | Double progression  | Add reps (10→11→12) before adding weight                     |
| Lat Pulldown                | +5 lbs on stack    | 3 x 12 achieved for 2 consecutive sessions                   |
| All other accessories       | +2.5-5 lbs         | Top rep range achieved for all sets x 2 sessions             |

**If you miss reps:** Stay at the same weight. After 3 failed sessions in a row, reduce by 10% and rebuild.

---

### Deload Protocol (Every 4th Week)

| Variable          | Normal Training Week       | Deload Week                              |
|-------------------|---------------------------|------------------------------------------|
| Working weight    | Prescribed load            | Reduce by 40% (e.g., 135 lb squat → 81 lbs -- round to 80 lbs) |
| Compound sets     | 3 sets                     | 2 sets                                   |
| Accessory sets    | 3 sets                     | 1-2 sets                                 |
| Rep ranges        | Prescribed                 | Same -- do not change                    |
| Exercise selection| Prescribed                 | Same -- do not swap movements            |
| Training days     | Mon/Wed/Fri                | Mon/Wed/Fri -- do not skip               |
| After deload      | --                         | Return to pre-deload weights             |

### Warm-Up Protocol

**Workout A (Squat Day) Warm-Up:**
1. 5 minutes brisk walk on treadmill at 3.5 mph, 1% incline -- or stationary bike at moderate resistance
2. Dynamic mobility (3 minutes): Leg swings front-to-back x 10 each leg → lateral leg swings x 10 each leg → kneeling hip flexor stretch 30 seconds each side (directly addresses your hip tightness) → bodyweight squat x 10 with 2-second pause at bottom
3. Barbell Back Squat warm-up: Empty bar x 10 → 50% working weight x 6 → 75% working weight x 3 → working weight
4. Barbell Bench Press warm-up: 1 set at 60% of working weight x 5 reps
5. Barbell Bent-Over Row warm-up: 1 set at 60% of working weight x 5 reps

**Workout B (Hinge Day) Warm-Up:**
1. 5 minutes stationary bike or walking
2. Dynamic mobility (3 minutes): Cat-cow x 10 → glute bridge x 15 → single-leg glute bridge x 8 each side → bodyweight Romanian deadlift x 10 (use a PVC pipe or empty barbell to feel the movement)
3. Romanian Deadlift warm-up: Empty bar x 10 → 50% working weight x 6 → 75% working weight x 3 → working weight
4. All subsequent compounds: 1 warm-up set at 60% of working weight

### What to Expect: Realistic Progress Timeline

- **Weeks 1-3:** Significant strength gains from neural adaptation -- your muscles are not yet meaningfully larger, but your nervous system is learning to recruit muscle fibers more efficiently. You may add weight to the bar every session or every other session during this phase. This is normal and expected.
- **Weeks 4-6 (after first deload):** Neural adaptation continues. True muscular hypertrophy begins appearing, particularly in the legs, upper back, and shoulders. Weight increases on the bar may slow from every session to every 1-2 weeks for upper body lifts.
- **Weeks 9-12:** Strength gains require more effort. Lower body lifts (squat, RDL) will have progressed faster than upper body lifts. By Week 12, expect your squat to have increased by 30-60 lbs from your starting weight and your bench press by 15-30 lbs, depending on consistency, sleep, and nutrition.
- **After 12 weeks:** Reassess. If linear progression continues, repeat the 12-week cycle. If progress has stalled significantly despite consistent deloads and sleep, consider transitioning to an intermediate program.
