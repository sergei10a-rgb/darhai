## Process

## The Neutral Posture Standard

The foundation of all ergonomic setup is achieving a neutral body posture -- the position where joints are naturally aligned and muscles are at their resting length, minimizing internal forces and stress on structures.

```
NEUTRAL SEATED POSTURE CHECKLIST
===================================

HEAD & NECK:
  [ ] Head balanced directly over shoulders (not jutting forward)
  [ ] Ears aligned over shoulders when viewed from the side
  [ ] Chin slightly tucked (not tilted up or down excessively)

EYES:
  [ ] Top of monitor at or slightly below eye level
  [ ] Screen distance: arm's length (20-26 inches / 50-66 cm)
  [ ] Gaze angle: slightly downward (15-20 degrees)

SHOULDERS:
  [ ] Relaxed and dropped (not hunched up toward ears)
  [ ] Pulled slightly back (not rounded forward)

ARMS & ELBOWS:
  [ ] Upper arms hang close to body
  [ ] Elbows at 90-110 degree angle
  [ ] Forearms roughly parallel to floor or angled slightly downward

WRISTS & HANDS:
  [ ] Wrists in neutral position (straight, not bent up/down/sideways)
  [ ] Fingers gently curved over keyboard
  [ ] Mouse within easy reach without extending arm

BACK:
  [ ] Lower back supported by chair lumbar support
  [ ] Natural S-curve of spine maintained
  [ ] Back resting against chair backrest

HIPS:
  [ ] Hips at 90-110 degree angle
  [ ] Thighs roughly parallel to floor
  [ ] Slight gap between seat edge and back of knees (2-3 fingers)

FEET:
  [ ] Feet flat on floor or on footrest
  [ ] Knees at approximately 90 degrees
  [ ] Weight distributed evenly
```

## Desk Setup Guide

### Monitor Placement

**Single Monitor:**
- Center the monitor directly in front of you
- Top of screen at or just below eye level
- Distance: approximately one arm's length (20-26 inches)
- Tilt screen back 10-20 degrees if the top is at eye level
- Reduce glare by positioning perpendicular to windows

**Dual Monitors:**

```
DUAL MONITOR CONFIGURATIONS
==============================

OPTION A: Equal use (50/50)
  Place monitors side by side, angled inward
  Center the seam between them with your nose
  Angle each monitor ~15 degrees inward

  [Monitor A]  |  [Monitor B]
       \       |       /
        \      |      /
         [  YOU  ]

OPTION B: Primary + secondary (70/30)
  Center primary monitor directly in front
  Place secondary monitor adjacent, angled inward

  [Secondary]  [  Primary  ]
        \            |
         \           |
          [   YOU   ]

Common mistake: Placing both monitors to one side,
causing sustained neck rotation. Avoid this.
```

**Laptop Users:**
- A laptop alone forces a compromise: screen too low OR keyboard too high
- Best solution: Laptop stand + external keyboard and mouse
- Budget solution: Stack of books under laptop + external keyboard ($15-30)
- Minimum: Tilt laptop screen to reduce neck flexion

### Chair Adjustment Sequence

Adjust your chair in this specific order:

```
CHAIR SETUP PROCEDURE (5 steps)
==================================

STEP 1: SEAT HEIGHT
  Adjust so feet are flat on the floor
  Thighs parallel to floor (or slightly downward slope)
  If desk is too high, raise chair and add footrest

STEP 2: SEAT DEPTH
  Slide seat pan so there is a 2-3 finger gap
  between the front edge and the back of your knees
  This prevents pressure on the back of your thighs

STEP 3: LUMBAR SUPPORT
  Adjust lumbar support (height and depth) to fit
  the natural inward curve of your lower back
  Should feel supportive, not pushy
  If chair lacks lumbar support: use a small rolled towel
  or lumbar cushion ($15-25)

STEP 4: ARMRESTS
  Adjust so your elbows rest at 90 degrees
  Shoulders should remain relaxed (not pushed up)
  Armrests should not prevent you from pulling
  close to the desk
  If armrests hit the desk: lower them or remove them

STEP 5: BACKREST ANGLE
  Slight recline (100-110 degrees) reduces disc pressure
  compared to sitting bolt upright at 90 degrees
  Lock the recline or set tension to support you
```

### Keyboard and Mouse Positioning

| Element | Correct Position | Common Mistake |
|---------|-----------------|----------------|
| Keyboard height | Elbows at 90-110 degrees, wrists straight | Keyboard too high, wrists bent upward |
| Keyboard tilt | Flat or negative tilt (front edge higher) | Positive tilt with feet extended (increases wrist extension) |
| Keyboard placement | Centered with B key aligned to your navel | Off-center due to number pad pushing it left |
| Mouse location | Immediately beside keyboard, same height | Mouse far to the right, reaching required |
| Mouse grip | Light grip, move from shoulder/elbow | Death grip, wrist-only movement |
| Wrist rest | For resting between typing bursts, not while typing | Resting wrists on pad while actively typing (increases pressure) |

**Keyboard tilt tip:** Most keyboards have flip-out feet on the back. Counter-intuitively, raising the back of the keyboard increases wrist extension strain. Either keep feet retracted (flat) or use a negative-tilt keyboard tray.

## Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI) Prevention

### Understanding RSI Risk Factors

```
RSI RISK ASSESSMENT
=====================
Rate each factor (1=low risk, 5=high risk):

DURATION:
  Hours of continuous keyboard/mouse use per day:    ___
  (1=<2hrs, 2=2-4hrs, 3=4-6hrs, 4=6-8hrs, 5=8+hrs)

FORCE:
  How hard do you press keys / grip mouse:           ___
  (1=light touch, 5=heavy pounding/tight grip)

REPETITION:
  Frequency of same motions (clicking, typing):      ___
  (1=varied tasks, 5=continuous same motion)

POSTURE:
  How often are wrists/arms in awkward positions:     ___
  (1=neutral, 5=consistently bent/twisted)

BREAKS:
  How often do you take movement breaks:              ___
  (1=every 30 min, 5=rarely/never)

STRESS:
  Current stress level (increases muscle tension):    ___
  (1=low, 5=high)

TOTAL: ___/30
  6-12:  Low risk (maintain good habits)
  13-18: Moderate risk (address weak areas now)
  19-24: High risk (make changes immediately)
  25-30: Very high risk (consider professional evaluation)
```

### The 20-20-20 Rule for Eyes

Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds. This relaxes the ciliary muscles that focus on close objects.

### Microbreak Protocol

```
MICROBREAK SCHEDULE
=====================
Every 20 minutes: Eye break (20-20-20 rule)
Every 30 minutes: Posture reset (10 seconds)
  - Roll shoulders back
  - Tuck chin
  - Reset hand position

Every 60 minutes: Movement break (2-5 minutes)
  - Stand up
  - Walk around
  - Gentle stretches (see below)

Every 2 hours: Extended break (5-10 minutes)
  - Walk to a different area
  - Full stretch routine
  - Hydrate

TOOLS TO REMIND YOU:
  - Stretchly (free, open source)
  - Time Out (Mac)
  - Built-in OS focus/break timers
  - Simple phone timer set to repeat
```

### Desk Stretch Routine (5 minutes)

```
OFFICE STRETCH SEQUENCE
=========================
Hold each stretch 15-30 seconds. Never force or bounce.

1. NECK RELEASE
   Gently tilt head to right (ear toward shoulder)
   Hold 15 sec, then left side
   Slowly look left, hold 15 sec, then right

2. SHOULDER ROLLS
   Roll shoulders forward 5 times
   Roll shoulders backward 5 times
   Squeeze shoulder blades together, hold 5 sec, release x3

3. CHEST OPENER
   Clasp hands behind back
   Squeeze shoulder blades together
   Lift hands slightly, hold 15-30 sec

4. WRIST STRETCHES
   Extend right arm, palm up
   Use left hand to gently pull fingers down, hold 15 sec
   Flip: palm down, pull fingers toward you, hold 15 sec
   Repeat with left arm

5. FINGER SPREADS
   Spread all fingers wide, hold 5 sec, make fist, repeat 5x
   Individual finger touches to thumb (each finger) x3

6. SEATED SPINAL TWIST
   Sit upright, cross right leg over left
   Twist torso gently to the right, hold 15 sec
   Repeat on other side

7. HIP FLEXOR STRETCH
   Stand up, step one foot forward into a slight lunge
   Tuck pelvis under, feel stretch in front of back hip
   Hold 15-30 sec each side

8. STANDING BACK EXTENSION
   Stand, place hands on lower back
   Gently lean backward, hold 5-10 sec
   Repeat 3 times
```

## Standing Desk Transition Guide

Transitioning to a standing desk requires a gradual approach. Jumping to full-day standing causes its own set of problems.

### Transition Schedule

```
STANDING DESK TRANSITION PLAN
================================
WEEK 1-2: Introduction
  Stand: 15-20 minutes, 2-3 times per day
  Sit: remainder of the day
  Total standing: ~1 hour/day

WEEK 3-4: Building
  Stand: 30 minutes, 3-4 times per day
  Sit: alternate every 30-45 min
  Total standing: ~2 hours/day

WEEK 5-6: Developing
  Stand: 30-45 minutes at a time
  Aim for roughly 50/50 sit-stand ratio
  Total standing: ~3-4 hours/day

WEEK 7+: Maintenance
  Alternate every 30-60 minutes
  Listen to your body
  Target: 2-4 hours of standing in an 8-hour day
  More is NOT necessarily better

IMPORTANT: The best posture is the NEXT posture.
Alternating between sitting and standing is the goal,
not standing all day.
```

### Standing Desk Setup

| Element | Sitting Position | Standing Position |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Desk height | Elbows at 90 degrees seated | Elbows at 90 degrees standing |
| Monitor | Eye level seated | Eye level standing (may need monitor arm) |
| Keyboard | Same neutral wrist rules | Same neutral wrist rules |
| Floor | Feet flat, chair supporting | Anti-fatigue mat recommended |
| Footwear | Any | Supportive shoes (no heels, no bare feet on hard floors) |

### Standing Comfort Tips
- Use an anti-fatigue mat (3/4 inch thick, beveled edges)
- Shift weight between feet regularly
- Place one foot on a low footrest or step (alternating)
- Wear supportive footwear
- Keep knees slightly soft (not locked)
- If feet, knees, or lower back hurt, sit down -- pain is a signal

## Special Situations

### Laptop-Only Workers
1. Get an external keyboard and mouse (non-negotiable for daily use)
2. Elevate laptop to eye level (stand, stack, or arm)
3. If traveling: portable laptop stand + compact keyboard fit in a bag
4. In a pinch: raise laptop, use external mouse, accept keyboard compromise temporarily

### Dual-Screen Plus Laptop (Triple Display)
- Laptop screen as tertiary reference only
- Center between the two external monitors
- Position laptop to the side, slightly angled
- Use a laptop stand to reduce the height difference

### Couch and Bed Working (Emergency Only)
- This is inherently non-ergonomic -- minimize time
- Lap desk to create a flatter surface
- Pillow behind lower back
- Limit sessions to 30 minutes
- Return to proper workspace as soon as possible

## Posture Correction Habits

### The Posture Reset Technique (10 seconds)

Perform this whenever you notice yourself slouching:

1. Plant feet flat on floor
2. Roll shoulders up, back, and down
3. Imagine a string pulling the crown of your head toward the ceiling
4. Tuck chin slightly (make a "double chin")
5. Engage core lightly (10-20% effort)
6. Relax and breathe -- this is your new baseline

### Strengthening Exercises for Desk Workers

Key exercises (10 min daily): chin tucks (10 reps), wall angels (10 reps), band pull-aparts (15 reps), dead bugs (10 per side), glute bridges (15 reps). Do before work, at lunch, or after work. Consistency matters more than intensity.

## Output Format

When providing ergonomic advice, deliver:

1. **Current setup assessment** -- Identify specific issues based on the user's description
2. **Priority fixes** -- Rank improvements by impact (address pain sources first)
3. **Step-by-step adjustments** -- Exact instructions for repositioning equipment
4. **Equipment recommendations** -- Specific suggestions within their budget
5. **Movement plan** -- Break and stretch schedule tailored to their work pattern
6. **Progress markers** -- What improvements to expect and when
7. **Warning signs** -- When to seek professional evaluation

## Example

**Input:** "I work from home at a desk for 8+ hours a day and my neck and lower back hurt by the end of each day. My setup is a laptop on a regular desk with a dining chair."

**Output:**

## Ergonomic Assessment: Home Office

**Primary complaints:** Neck pain, lower back pain
**Root cause:** Laptop-only setup forces head-down posture; dining chair provides zero lumbar support

### Immediate Fixes (Cost: $0)
| Fix | How | Impact |
|-----|-----|--------|
| Raise laptop screen | Stack on books/boxes until top of screen is at eye level | Eliminates neck flexion |
| External keyboard + mouse | Required when laptop is elevated (borrow or buy basic set) | Proper arm position |
| Rolled towel for lumbar | Roll a bath towel, place at the curve of your lower back | Temporary lumbar support |
| Feet flat on floor | If chair is too high, use a footrest (stack of books works) | Reduces lower back strain |

### Ideal Measurements
- Monitor: Top of screen at eye level, 20-26 inches from eyes (arm's length)
- Chair seat height: Thighs parallel to floor, feet flat
- Desk height: Elbows at 90 degrees when typing, forearms parallel to floor
- Keyboard: Shoulders relaxed (not shrugged), wrists neutral (not bent up or down)

### Equipment Priority (If Buying)
| Priority | Item | Budget Option | Impact |
|----------|------|---------------|--------|
| 1 | External keyboard + mouse | $25-40 | Essential with raised laptop |
| 2 | Laptop stand | $20-30 | Better than book stack |
| 3 | Office chair with lumbar | $150-300 | Biggest comfort improvement |
| 4 | External monitor | $150-250 | Larger screen reduces eye strain |

### Break Schedule (Non-Negotiable)
- Every 30 minutes: Look away from screen for 20 seconds (20-20-20 rule)
- Every 60 minutes: Stand and stretch for 2 minutes (neck rolls, shoulder shrugs, hip flexor stretch)
- Every 2 hours: Walk for 5 minutes (refill water, bathroom, quick lap)
