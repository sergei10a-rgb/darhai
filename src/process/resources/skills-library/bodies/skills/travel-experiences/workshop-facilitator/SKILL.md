# Workshop Facilitator

## When to Use


## Process

1. **Gather requirements.** Ask the user clarifying questions about their specific context, goals, constraints, and experience level.

2. **Analyze the situation.** Review the information provided and identify key factors, challenges, and opportunities relevant to workshop facilitator.

3. **Develop the framework.** Create a structured approach tailored to the user's needs, incorporating best practices and domain-specific considerations.

4. **Deliver actionable output.** Present specific, implementable recommendations with clear rationale, timelines, and success criteria.

5. **Address edge cases.** Proactively identify potential issues, alternative approaches, and contingency plans.

**Use this skill when:**
- User needs guidance on workshop facilitator
- User asks about workshop facilitator best practices or techniques
- User wants a structured approach to workshop facilitator

**Do NOT use this skill when:**
- A more specialized skill exists for the specific subtopic
- The request is outside the scope of workshop facilitator

## Questions to Ask First

Before providing guidance, establish the facilitator's situation:

1. What is the workshop topic and desired outcome?
2. Who is the audience? (Team, public, students, executives)
3. How many participants are expected?
4. How long is the workshop? (2 hours, half day, full day, multi-day)
5. Is it in-person, virtual, or hybrid?
6. What is the experience level of participants with the topic?
7. Is this a standalone workshop or part of a larger program?
8. Who is the stakeholder/client requesting this workshop?
9. What should participants be able to DO after the workshop?
10. What is your experience level as a facilitator?

## Workshop Design

### The Design Framework

```
STEP 1: DEFINE THE OUTCOME
  Before anything else, answer: "After this workshop, participants will be
  able to ________________________."

  Be specific. Not "understand project management" but "create a project
  timeline using the critical path method and identify resource constraints."

STEP 2: KNOW YOUR AUDIENCE
  - Current knowledge level (pre-assessment or intake survey)
  - Motivation for attending (voluntary vs. mandatory)
  - Job roles and daily responsibilities
  - Learning style preferences
  - Potential resistance or challenges
  - Group dynamics (do they know each other? Hierarchy present?)

STEP 3: STRUCTURE THE CONTENT
  Opening (10-15% of time): Hook, objectives, agreements
  Body (70-80% of time): Core content in 3-5 modules
  Closing (10-15% of time): Summary, action planning, evaluation

STEP 4: DESIGN ACTIVITIES
  For every concept taught, include at least one practice activity
  Rule: Never lecture for more than 15-20 minutes without an activity
  Aim for 60% participant activity, 40% facilitator delivery

STEP 5: CREATE MATERIALS
  Slide deck (if using), handouts, worksheets, templates
  Participant workbook (for full-day workshops)

STEP 6: REHEARSE
  Run through the entire workshop at least once
  Time each section, identify where you run long
  Practice transitions between sections
  Test all technology and materials
```

### Learning Objectives (SMART)

```
STRONG LEARNING OBJECTIVES:
  Use action verbs from Bloom's Taxonomy:

  REMEMBER:    Define, list, identify, recall, name
  UNDERSTAND:  Explain, summarize, describe, interpret, compare
  APPLY:       Use, demonstrate, implement, solve, calculate
  ANALYZE:     Differentiate, examine, categorize, compare, contrast
  EVALUATE:    Judge, assess, critique, justify, recommend
  CREATE:      Design, construct, develop, produce, formulate

EXAMPLES:
  Weak: "Participants will learn about communication skills"
  Strong: "Participants will practice delivering feedback using the
          SBI (Situation-Behavior-Impact) framework and receive peer
          feedback on their delivery"

  Weak: "Attendees will understand strategic planning"
  Strong: "Attendees will create a 90-day strategic action plan
          with measurable objectives and resource requirements"

TEMPLATE:
  "By the end of this workshop, participants will be able to
   [action verb] + [specific skill/knowledge] + [context/condition]."

MAXIMUM OBJECTIVES:
  2-hour workshop: 2-3 objectives
  Half-day workshop: 3-4 objectives
  Full-day workshop: 4-6 objectives
  Multi-day: 6-10 objectives
```

## Activities and Engagement

### Activity Types

```
INDIVIDUAL ACTIVITIES:
  - Reflection writing (journaling, prompts)
  - Self-assessment questionnaires
  - Worksheet completion
  - Action planning
  - Skill practice with observation
  Best for: Personal insight, introverts, sensitive topics

PAIR ACTIVITIES:
  - Think-pair-share
  - Peer coaching/feedback
  - Role play with partner
  - Teach-back (explain concept to partner)
  - Interview exercise
  Best for: Low-risk practice, building rapport, diverse perspectives

SMALL GROUP (3-6 people):
  - Case study analysis
  - Problem-solving challenge
  - Design exercise
  - Discussion with report-out
  - Simulation
  Best for: Complex problems, diverse solutions, team building

WHOLE GROUP:
  - Facilitated discussion
  - Polling/voting
  - Gallery walk
  - Fishbowl debate
  - Group brainstorm
  Best for: Shared understanding, energy, community building
```

### Engagement Techniques

```
OPENERS (First 10 Minutes Are Critical):
  1. Provocative question: "What's the biggest mistake you've made in [topic]?"
  2. Surprising statistic: "Research shows that 70% of [relevant fact]..."
  3. Quick poll: "How many of you have experienced [relevant situation]?"
  4. Short story: A personal anecdote that illustrates the need
  5. Challenge: "By the end of today, you'll be able to [impressive outcome]"
  6. Physical movement: "Stand up if you've ever [relevant experience]"
  7. Pair introduction: "Tell your neighbor one thing you hope to learn today"

ENERGIZERS (Use After Lunch or During Slumps):
  - Stand and stretch with a prompt
  - Quick partner activity with movement
  - Speed networking (1-minute conversations)
  - Group challenge (build something, solve a puzzle)
  - Music and movement
  - Change the physical environment (move seats, go outside)

CLOSERS:
  - One word: "Describe today in one word" (go around the room)
  - Postcard to self: Write yourself a commitment, mailed in 30 days
  - Action planning: "I will _____ by _____ to _____ "
  - Gallery walk: Post commitments, everyone reads
  - Thank-you circle: Each person thanks one other person or shares a takeaway
  - 3-2-1: 3 things you learned, 2 things you'll apply, 1 question remaining
```

### Slide Design for Workshops

```
WORKSHOP SLIDES ARE DIFFERENT FROM PRESENTATIONS:

DO:
  - Use slides as visual anchors, not lecture notes
  - One idea per slide
  - Activity instructions on screen (so participants can reference)
  - Timer countdowns for activities
  - Key frameworks and models as visual reference
  - Include discussion prompts on screen

DON'T:
  - Pack slides with text (no one reads them)
  - Have more slides than minutes of workshop
  - Read from your slides
  - Use slides during activities (screen should show instructions or be blank)

SLIDE FLOW:
  Title slide → Agenda → Learning objectives → Content block 1 →
  Activity instructions → Debrief prompt → Content block 2 →
  Activity instructions → Debrief prompt → Summary → Action planning →
  Evaluation → Thank you
```

## Timing and Pacing

### Workshop Agenda Template

```
HALF-DAY WORKSHOP (3.5 hours):

  0:00  Welcome and introductions              15 min
  0:15  Opening activity / icebreaker           10 min
  0:25  Workshop objectives and agenda          5 min
  0:30  Content Module 1 (presentation)         15 min
  0:45  Activity 1 (practice / application)     20 min
  1:05  Debrief Activity 1                      10 min
  1:15  BREAK                                   15 min
  1:30  Content Module 2 (presentation)         15 min
  1:45  Activity 2 (small group exercise)       25 min
  2:10  Report-out and debrief                  15 min
  2:25  Content Module 3 (presentation)         15 min
  2:40  Activity 3 (application exercise)       20 min
  3:00  Action planning                         15 min
  3:15  Wrap-up, evaluation, closing            15 min
  3:30  END

TIMING RULES:
  - Plan to 90% of available time (buffer for running over)
  - Know which sections to cut if running long
  - Know which sections to expand if running short
  - Never cut breaks (participants need them)
  - Start on time even if people are late (rewards punctuality)
  - End on time or early (never late)
```

### Energy Management Throughout the Day

```
FULL-DAY WORKSHOP ENERGY MAP:

9:00 AM   [HIGH]     Morning energy peak → hardest content here
10:30 AM  [MODERATE] First dip → energizing activity needed
11:00 AM  [GOOD]     Pre-lunch productive window
12:00 PM  [LOW]      Lunch → allow networking
1:00 PM   [LOWEST]   Post-lunch slump → physical/interactive activity
2:00 PM   [RISING]   Getting back on track → partner/group work
3:00 PM   [MODERATE] Afternoon productive window → application exercises
4:00 PM   [DECLINING]Action planning and wrap-up → keep it participatory
4:30 PM   END

PACING TIPS:
  - Front-load the hardest content (morning)
  - After lunch: NEVER lecture. Use interactive activities
  - Alternate between seated and standing activities
  - Change the activity type every 15-20 minutes
  - Watch the room: glazed eyes = time to change something
  - If energy drops: call an impromptu 5-minute movement break
```

## Group Dynamics

### Managing Different Participant Types

```
THE DOMINATOR (Talks too much):
  - "Thank you [Name]. Let's hear from someone who hasn't shared yet."
  - Use structured turn-taking activities
  - Talk to them privately during break: "I appreciate your engagement.
    Could you help me bring in the quieter voices?"

THE SILENT ONE (Doesn't participate):
  - Use pair work (lower stakes than whole group)
  - Ask direct but non-threatening questions: "What are your thoughts, [Name]?"
  - Written activities before verbal sharing (think-write-share)
  - Check in privately: "I'd value your perspective. Any thoughts on...?"

THE SKEPTIC (Challenges everything):
  - Acknowledge their perspective: "That's a valid concern."
  - Redirect to the group: "Who else has experience with this?"
  - Park it: "Great point. Let's capture that and revisit."
  - Channel their energy: Give them a leadership role in group activity

THE DISTRACTED (Phone/laptop/side conversations):
  - Establish ground rules upfront (phones away, laptops closed)
  - Move closer to them physically
  - Make activities engaging enough that distraction isn't attractive
  - If persistent, address privately during break

THE EXPERT (Knows more than you about subtopics):
  - Leverage their expertise: "You have experience in this. What would you add?"
  - Don't compete or get defensive
  - Acknowledge: "Great point. [Name] brings real-world experience to this."
  - Privately: Ask them to share a relevant example during the session

CONFLICT BETWEEN PARTICIPANTS:
  - Acknowledge both perspectives
  - Reframe as learning opportunity: "Interesting to see two valid approaches."
  - If escalating, park it: "Both perspectives have merit. Let's discuss offline."
  - Private conversation with both parties during break if needed
```

### Establishing Group Agreements

```
AT THE START OF EVERY WORKSHOP, ESTABLISH:

  Suggested agreements (customize per group):
  1. What's said here stays here (confidentiality)
  2. Step up / step back (balance participation)
  3. One conversation at a time (respect for speaker)
  4. Phones on silent and away
  5. Be present (mentally and physically)
  6. Assume positive intent
  7. It's okay to disagree respectfully
  8. Questions are welcome at any time

  Process:
  - Present proposed agreements
  - Ask: "Anything to add or modify?"
  - Get explicit agreement (thumbs up, head nods)
  - Post visibly in the room
  - Reference during the session if needed
```

## Remote vs. In-Person Facilitation

### Virtual Workshop Adjustments

```
TECHNOLOGY:
  Platform: Zoom, Teams, Google Meet
  Interactive tools: Miro, MURAL, Mentimeter, Slido, Jamboard
  Breakout rooms: Essential for small group activities
  Chat: Use for parallel participation and question capture
  Recording: Ask permission, share after

VIRTUAL DESIGN DIFFERENCES:
  Maximum virtual session: 3-4 hours with generous breaks
  Chunk content into 45-60 minute blocks with 10-minute breaks
  Every 5-7 minutes: Require participant interaction
  (Poll, chat response, annotation, breakout room, unmute)
  Use chat for "temperature checks": "1-5, how clear is this concept?"

  Activity Adaptations:
  In-person flip chart → Virtual whiteboard (Miro/MURAL)
  In-person pair work → Breakout rooms (2 people)
  In-person gallery walk → Virtual board tour
  In-person sticky notes → Digital sticky notes
  In-person polling → Mentimeter or Zoom poll

ENGAGEMENT RULES FOR VIRTUAL:
  1. Cameras on (request, don't demand)
  2. Use participant names frequently
  3. Cold-call (gently): "[Name], what do you think about this?"
  4. Use the chat proactively: "Type in the chat: one word for how you're feeling"
  5. Breakout rooms every 20-30 minutes
  6. Music during breaks and activities
  7. Shorter attention spans online: cut content by 30-40% vs. in-person
  8. Have a co-facilitator manage chat and tech
```

## Feedback Collection

### Evaluation Methods

```
IMMEDIATE EVALUATION (End of Workshop):

LEVEL 1 - REACTION (Did they like it?):
  Overall rating: 1-5
  Best part of the workshop:
  What would you change?
  Would you recommend to a colleague? Yes/No
  Net Promoter Score: 0-10

LEVEL 2 - LEARNING (Did they learn?):
  Pre/post knowledge assessment
  Confidence rating before and after
  Skill demonstration during activities

DELAYED EVALUATION (2-4 Weeks After):

LEVEL 3 - BEHAVIOR (Are they applying it?):
  Survey 30 days post-workshop
  "Have you used [specific skill] since the workshop?"
  "What barriers have you encountered?"
  Manager observation (if corporate training)

LEVEL 4 - RESULTS (Business impact):
  Measurable outcomes tied to workshop objectives
  Tracked over 60-90 days
  "Did the workshop contribute to [specific metric]?"

EVALUATION FORM TEMPLATE:
  1. The workshop met its stated objectives:     1-5
  2. The content was relevant to my role:         1-5
  3. The facilitator was effective:               1-5
  4. The activities were valuable:                1-5
  5. The pacing was appropriate:                  1-5
  6. I feel confident applying what I learned:    1-5
  7. What was most valuable? [open text]
  8. What would you improve? [open text]
  9. NPS: How likely to recommend? 0-10
```

```
  [ ] Follow-up survey on application of learning

  [ ] Share relevant new resources or articles
  [ ] Invite to community or ongoing learning group

TO STAKEHOLDER/CLIENT:
  [ ] Summarize participant feedback
  [ ] Report on learning objectives met
  [ ] Recommendations for next steps
  [ ] Proposal for follow-up sessions (if applicable)
```

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Too much content, not enough practice (participants retain through doing, not listening)
2. Lecturing for more than 20 minutes without interaction
3. Not having clear, measurable learning objectives
4. Ignoring the post-lunch energy dip (always plan active sessions after lunch)
5. Not managing dominant voices (silence doesn't mean agreement)
6. Skipping the opening activity (critical for safety and engagement)
7. Not rehearsing timing (almost every new facilitator runs over)
8. Reading from slides instead of facilitating conversation
9. Not having a plan for when activities run short or long
10. Failing to follow up after the workshop (the learning happens in application)


## Output Format

Deliver the response as a structured document with clear headings and actionable content. Use tables for comparisons, numbered lists for sequential steps, and bullet points for options. Include specific examples where applicable.

```
[Workshop Facilitator deliverable]
1. Context and objectives
2. Analysis or framework
3. Specific recommendations with rationale
4. Action items with timeline
```


## Example

**Input:** "Help me with workshop facilitator for a mid-size project."

**Output:** A complete workshop facilitator framework tailored to the specific context, with actionable steps, relevant considerations, and measurable outcomes.


## Edge Cases

- **Incomplete information:** Ask clarifying questions before proceeding rather than making assumptions
- **Conflicting requirements:** Identify trade-offs explicitly and present options with pros and cons
- **Scale mismatch:** Adapt recommendations to match the user's context (individual vs. team vs. organization)
- **Domain crossover:** When the request overlaps with other skill domains, address what falls within scope and reference specialized skills for the rest
