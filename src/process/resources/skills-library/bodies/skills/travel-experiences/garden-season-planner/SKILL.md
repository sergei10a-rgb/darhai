## Understanding Your Growing Season

### Finding Your Zone and Frost Dates

Your growing season is defined by your last spring frost and first fall frost. Everything else builds from these two dates.

**How to find your dates:**
1. Look up your USDA hardiness zone at planthardiness.ars.usda.gov
2. Search "[your city] last frost date" for your specific microclimate
3. Ask local garden centers or your county extension office

**Average frost dates by zone (approximate):**

| Zone | Last Spring Frost | First Fall Frost | Growing Season |
|------|-------------------|------------------|----------------|
| 3 | May 15 - June 1 | Sep 1 - Sep 15 | 90-120 days |
| 4 | May 1 - May 15 | Sep 15 - Oct 1 | 120-150 days |
| 5 | Apr 15 - May 1 | Oct 1 - Oct 15 | 150-170 days |
| 6 | Apr 1 - Apr 15 | Oct 15 - Nov 1 | 170-190 days |
| 7 | Mar 15 - Apr 1 | Nov 1 - Nov 15 | 190-210 days |
| 8 | Mar 1 - Mar 15 | Nov 15 - Dec 1 | 210-240 days |
| 9 | Feb 1 - Feb 15 | Dec 1 - Dec 15 | 250-300 days |

## Seed Starting Schedule

### Indoor Seed Starting Timeline

Count backward from your last frost date:

| Weeks Before Last Frost | Start These Seeds Indoors |
|------------------------|---------------------------|
| 12-14 weeks | Onions, leeks, celery |
| 10-12 weeks | Peppers, eggplant |
| 8-10 weeks | Tomatoes |
| 6-8 weeks | Broccoli, cabbage, cauliflower, kale |
| 4-6 weeks | Lettuce, chard, herbs (basil, parsley) |
| 3-4 weeks | Cucumbers, squash, melons (if transplanting) |

### Seed Starting Setup

**Minimum viable setup:**
- Seed starting trays with drainage
- Seed starting mix (NOT garden soil - too heavy, may carry disease)
- Light source (south-facing window is okay; grow lights are better)
- Warmth for germination (heat mat helps peppers and tomatoes)
- Labels (you may overlook what you planted where)

**Seed starting basics:**
1. Fill trays with moistened seed starting mix
2. Plant seeds at the depth listed on the packet (general rule: 2x the seed diameter)
3. Cover with plastic wrap or humidity dome until germination
4. Remove cover once seeds sprout
5. Provide 14-16 hours of light daily (grow lights 2-4 inches above seedlings)
6. Water from the bottom (set trays in water, let soil absorb upward)
7. Thin to one seedling per cell once true leaves appear
8. Harden off before transplanting (see below)

### Hardening Off

Transplanting seedlings directly from indoors to the garden causes transplant shock. Harden off over 7-10 days:

| Day | Outdoor Time | Conditions |
|-----|-------------|------------|
| 1-2 | 1-2 hours | Sheltered shade, no wind |
| 3-4 | 3-4 hours | Partial sun, some wind |
| 5-6 | 5-6 hours | Full conditions |
| 7-8 | Full day | Leave out overnight if no frost risk |
| 9-10 | Plant in garden | Water well after transplanting |

## Direct Sowing Calendar

Some crops do best planted directly in the garden (they dislike transplanting):

### Before Last Frost (2-4 Weeks Before)

Direct sow as soon as soil can be worked:
- Peas
- Spinach
- Radishes
- Arugula
- Lettuce (can also transplant)
- Carrots
- Beets
- Onion sets

### After Last Frost (1-2 Weeks After)

Wait until soil is warm and frost risk has passed:
- Beans (bush and pole)
- Corn
- Cucumbers
- Squash (summer and winter)
- Zucchini
- Melons
- Pumpkins
- Sunflowers

## Succession Planting

### Why Succession Plant

A single planting of lettuce gives you two weeks of harvest, then it bolts. Succession planting means staggering plantings so you harvest continuously.

### Succession Schedule

| Crop | Plant Every | Method |
|------|-------------|--------|
| Lettuce | 2-3 weeks | New planting from seed or transplant |
| Spinach | 3 weeks (spring and fall) | Direct sow; stops in summer heat |
| Radishes | 2 weeks | Direct sow; fast 25-day crop |
| Bush beans | 3 weeks | Direct sow after last frost through midsummer |
| Cilantro | 2-3 weeks | Bolts quickly; keep new plantings coming |
| Arugula | 2-3 weeks | Direct sow; pause in midsummer |
| Beets | 3-4 weeks | Direct sow spring through midsummer |
| Carrots | 3-4 weeks | Direct sow spring through midsummer |

**The relay planting method:**
When one crop finishes in a bed, immediately plant the next. Example:
- Spring: peas (finish by early summer)
- Summer: beans or cucumbers in the same bed
- Fall: kale or garlic after beans finish

### Companion Planting Quick Reference

| Plant Together | Benefit |
|---------------|---------|
| Tomatoes + basil | Basil may repel some pests; harvest together |
| Beans + corn + squash | "Three Sisters" - beans fix nitrogen, corn provides support, squash shades soil |
| Carrots + onions | Each repels the other's pests |
| Lettuce + tall plants | Tall plants provide shade for heat-sensitive lettuce |
| Marigolds + everything | Marigold roots repel nematodes; flowers attract pollinators |

| Keep Apart | Reason |
|-----------|--------|
| Tomatoes + fennel | Fennel inhibits tomato growth |
| Beans + onion family | Onions may stunt bean growth |
| Dill + carrots (mature dill) | Mature dill can inhibit carrot growth |

## Harvest Timing

### When to Pick for Best Quality

| Crop | Harvest When... | Check By... |
|------|----------------|-------------|
| Tomatoes | Fully colored, slightly soft to gentle squeeze | Color, feel |
| Peppers | Full size, firm, desired color reached | Size, color |
| Cucumbers | 6-8 inches for slicing, 2-4 inches for pickling | Size (do not let them yellow) |
| Zucchini | 6-8 inches long (do NOT let them get huge) | Size |
| Beans | Pods are firm, seeds inside are small | Feel, snap test |
| Lettuce | Before the center stalk starts to elongate (bolting) | Visual |
| Carrots | Shoulders visible at soil line, 60-80 days from planting | Pull one test carrot |
| Beets | 1.5-3 inches diameter | Gently brush soil to check top |
| Kale | Leaves are large but not yellowing, harvest outer leaves | Visual |
| Potatoes | Foliage dies back (wait 2 weeks after dieback to harvest) | Foliage condition |
| Garlic | Lower 1/3 of leaves are brown, upper 2/3 still green | Leaf color |
| Corn | Silk turns brown and dry, kernels release milky juice when pierced | Silk color, kernel test |

### Harvest Tips

- Harvest in the morning when plants are hydrated and cool
- Use scissors or pruners for clean cuts (tearing damages plants)
- Harvest zucchini and cucumbers frequently - missed large fruit signals the plant to stop producing
- Tomatoes can be picked slightly underripe and ripened indoors if frost threatens
- Continuous harvest encourages continuous production for most crops

## Season Extension

### Extending Your Season by 4-8 Weeks

**In spring (start earlier):**
- Cold frames: clear-topped boxes that trap heat; plant 2-4 weeks before last frost
- Row cover (floating fabric): drape over beds for 4-8°F of frost protection
- Wall-o-Water: water-filled cloches around individual tomato plants
- Black plastic mulch: warms soil 5-10°F for earlier warm-season planting

**In fall (harvest later):**
- Row cover over fall crops protects down to 28°F
- Cold frames keep greens growing into December+ in zones 5-7
- Mulch root crops (carrots, beets, parsnips) heavily and harvest all winter from unfrozen soil
- Plant cold-hardy varieties: winter lettuce, claytonia, mache, winter spinach

### Fall Garden Planting Calendar

Count BACKWARD from your first fall frost date:

| Weeks Before First Frost | Plant These |
|--------------------------|-------------|
| 14-16 weeks | Broccoli, cauliflower, Brussels sprouts (transplant) |
| 10-12 weeks | Cabbage, kale, collards (transplant) |
| 8-10 weeks | Beets, carrots, turnips (direct sow) |
| 6-8 weeks | Lettuce, spinach, arugula, radishes (direct sow) |
| 4-6 weeks | Garlic cloves for next year's harvest |

## Troubleshooting Common Problems

| Problem | Likely Cause | Solution |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| Leggy seedlings | Not enough light | Move closer to light or add grow lights |
| Tomato blossom drop | Too hot (>90°F) or too cold (<55°F) | Wait for moderate temps; shade cloth |
| Yellow leaves on tomatoes | Nitrogen deficiency, overwatering, or disease | Check watering, feed with balanced fertilizer |
| Blossom end rot (tomatoes, peppers) | Calcium uptake issue from inconsistent watering | Mulch, water consistently, do not over-fertilize nitrogen |
| No fruit on squash | Poor pollination | Hand-pollinate with paintbrush, attract bees with flowers |
| Bolting lettuce/spinach | Too hot or too long days | Succession plant, use shade cloth, grow in spring/fall |
| Carrot forking | Rocky soil, fresh manure | Sift soil deeply, use aged compost only |
| Seeds not germinating | Too cold, too deep, dried out, or old seed | Check soil temp, planting depth, keep moist, test germination rate |
