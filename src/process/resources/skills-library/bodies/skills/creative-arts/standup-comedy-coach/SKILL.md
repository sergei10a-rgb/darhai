## Joke Writing Fundamentals

### The Setup-Punchline Structure

Every joke has two essential components:

**Setup:** Establishes an assumption or expectation in the audience's mind
**Punchline:** Violates that expectation in a surprising, satisfying way

The comedy comes from the gap between what the audience expects and what you deliver.

**Example structure:**
- Setup: "I told my doctor I broke my arm in two places."
- Punchline: "He told me to stop going to those places."
- The setup leads the audience to expect a medical response. The punchline pivots to take "places" literally.

### The Rule of Three

- Present two items that establish a pattern
- The third item breaks the pattern
- The audience's brain predicts the third will continue the pattern; the surprise creates laughter
- The first two items are the setup; the third is the punchline
- Works for lists, descriptions, and escalation

### Tags (Toppers)

- Additional punchlines that follow the original joke
- Build on the same setup; no new setup needed
- The best jokes have 2-4 tags after the initial punchline
- Tags extend the laugh and increase the joke's value
- Each tag should be a genuine new angle, not a repetition
- Tags often get bigger laughs than the original punchline because the audience is already engaged

### Joke Tightening

**Remove every unnecessary word:**
- Shorter setups hit harder
- Get to the punchline as fast as possible
- Every word in the setup should either establish the assumption or be necessary context
- Read the joke aloud; if you can remove a word without losing meaning, remove it

**Punchline at the end:**
- The funny word should be the very last word of the punchline
- Anything after the funny word steps on the laugh
- Restructure sentences so the surprise lands at the end

**Specificity beats vagueness:**
- "I went to a restaurant" is weaker than "I went to an Applebee's"
- Specific details create vivid images and funnier surprises
- Real brand names, numbers, and details ground the joke in reality

### Writing Exercises

**Exercise 1: List Ten**
1. Pick a topic (dating, work, family, technology, food)
2. Write ten things that are true, annoying, weird, or surprising about that topic
3. For each observation, ask "Why is that funny?" or "What is the unexpected angle?"
4. Turn the strongest observations into setups
5. Write 3-5 possible punchlines for each setup
6. Keep the one that surprises you most

**Exercise 2: What Else?**
1. Start with a single joke that works
2. Ask "What else is true about this situation?"
3. Write 5-10 additional angles or observations
4. These become tags or entirely new jokes on the same topic
5. This is how 30 seconds of material becomes 3 minutes

**Exercise 3: Act-Out Exploration**
1. Take a joke and identify a character or scenario within it
2. Physically act out what that character would do or say
3. Exaggerate the physicality, voice, and attitude
4. Often the act-out becomes funnier than the written punchline
5. Act-outs add variety and visual humor to your set

**Exercise 4: Reverse Engineering**
1. Watch a set by a comedian you admire
2. Transcribe three jokes
3. Identify: setup, punchline, tags, act-outs, misdirection
4. Map the structure: how was the assumption created? How was it broken?
5. Apply the same structures to your own topics

### Writing Routine

- Write daily, even if only 15-30 minutes
- Carry a notebook or use a notes app for capturing ideas throughout the day
- Do not judge ideas during capture; collect everything
- Dedicated writing sessions: expand captured ideas into full jokes
- Weekly goal: 5-10 new joke attempts
- Most jokes will fail; that is normal. Keep the ones that survive stage testing

## Timing and Delivery

### Pacing

- Slow down; new comics almost always rush
- Give the audience time to process the setup
- Pause before the punchline; the anticipation builds
- Pause after the punchline; let the laugh breathe
- Do not step on your laughs by starting the next joke too soon

### The Pause

Types of pauses and when to use them:

**The Setup Pause:** Brief pause between setup and punchline. Creates anticipation.
**The Punchline Pause:** After the punchline, let the laugh happen. Do not speak over it.
**The Dramatic Pause:** Long, deliberate pause for emphasis. Use sparingly for maximum effect.
**The Callback Pause:** Brief pause before a callback to signal "here it comes."
**The Reset Pause:** After a big laugh, pause to let the room settle before changing topics.

### Attitude and Persona

- Your stage persona is a heightened version of yourself
- Consistent attitude gives the audience a framework to laugh within
- Common attitudes: frustrated, confused, too-confident, self-deprecating, blissfully unaware
- The attitude colors how you deliver every line
- Find your natural comedic voice; do not imitate another comedian's persona

### Physicality

- Use the microphone stand deliberately: remove mic or leave it, do not fidget
- Hand gestures for emphasis and act-outs
- Movement on stage: move with purpose, not from nervous energy
- Act-outs: physically embody characters in your stories
- Facial expressions sell punchlines; let the audience see your face
- Camera-friendly: for recorded sets, stay in the light and face the audience

### Energy Management

- Match your energy to the room; then raise it slightly
- High energy works in large rooms and late shows
- Lower, conversational energy works in intimate rooms
- Build energy throughout your set; do not peak in the first minute
- Your closer should have the most energy and confidence

## Crowd Work

### Why Crowd Work Matters

- Creates spontaneous, unrepeatable moments
- Builds connection between you and the audience
- Proves you are present and in control, not just reciting memorized material
- Fills time naturally if something goes wrong with prepared material
- The best crowd work feels like a conversation, not an interrogation

### Crowd Work Techniques

**Opening Questions:**
- "Where are you from?" (always gets a response)
- "What do you do for a living?" (rich material in any answer)
- "Are you two together? How long?" (relationship dynamics are universal)
- Keep questions open-ended but not too broad
- Have follow-up angles ready for common answers

**Responding to Answers:**
- Listen genuinely; do not just wait for your turn to talk
- Find the funny angle in what they actually said
- Heighten: take their answer to an absurd but logical extreme
- Relate: connect their answer to your own experience or prepared material
- Callback: reference their answer later in your set for a delayed payoff

**Dealing with Hecklers:**
- Most "hecklers" are just drunk audience members trying to participate
- First response: acknowledge briefly and move on ("Thank you, I have this handled")
- Second interruption: address directly with humor, not hostility
- Third interruption: be firm; the audience wants you to win
- Never get angry; the audience turns on whoever loses composure first
- Having 2-3 prepared lines for common interruptions is valuable
- The rest of the audience is on your side; play to them, not the heckler

### Crowd Work Practice

- Start small: one question before your first joke
- Build the muscle gradually; add more as you get comfortable
- Practice listening and responding quickly in everyday conversations
- Record crowd work sets; review what generated the best responses
- Have prepared callbacks ready for common audience occupations and situations

## Open Mic Preparation

### Before Your First Open Mic

**Preparation:**
1. Have 3-5 minutes of material memorized thoroughly
2. Practice delivering it aloud (not just reading it) at least 20 times
3. Time yourself; open mic slots are typically 3-5 minutes
4. Visit the open mic as an audience member first to understand the vibe
5. Record every performance from the beginning of your career

**Managing Nerves:**
- Nervousness is normal and never fully goes away
- Physical warm-up: shake out limbs, loosen jaw, breathe deeply
- Arrive early enough to settle but not so early that you overthink
- Remind yourself: the audience wants you to succeed
- Commit to just getting on stage; lower the bar for "success" to simply doing it

**Open Mic Etiquette:**
- Sign up early; slots fill fast
- Stay for other performers; support builds community
- Do not record other comics without permission
- Bring friends but not too many (small open mic audiences affect the room)
- Thank the host, even if your set was rough
- Do not apologize on stage for being new or nervous

### After Each Performance

1. Review your recording within 24 hours
2. Note what got laughs and what got silence
3. Transcribe the set from memory while it is fresh
4. Mark laugh points, dead spots, and unexpected reactions
5. Identify one thing to keep and one thing to change for next time
6. Write down any improvised lines that worked; they belong in the set now

### Set Development Cycle

1. **Write** new material in dedicated writing sessions
2. **Test** new jokes at open mics (place one new joke between two proven ones)
3. **Record** every performance
4. **Review** the recording and note results
5. **Edit** based on audience response (tighten, tag, rewrite, or cut)
6. **Retest** edited versions at the next mic
7. **Repeat** until the joke either works consistently or gets permanently cut

## Building a Set

### Set Structure

**Strong Opening:**
- Establish your persona and energy in the first 30 seconds
- A reliable, proven joke that gets an early laugh
- Earns the audience's attention and trust
- Can be self-referential, observational, or crowd work

**Building the Body:**
- Group jokes by topic or theme for flow
- Transition between topics naturally; avoid abrupt shifts
- Alternate between types: one-liners, stories, act-outs, crowd moments
- Place new or untested material in the middle, cushioned by proven jokes
- Build momentum: each section should be slightly stronger than the last

**The Closer:**
- Your absolute strongest joke or bit
- Leave the audience on a high note
- Often works as a callback to an earlier joke, tying the set together
- The last impression is the lasting impression
- When your closer hits, say "thank you, goodnight" and leave; do not add more

### Callbacks

- Reference an earlier joke later in the set
- The audience feels smart for remembering; creates a shared experience
- Set up the callback early; pay it off later
- The callback does not need a new setup; a brief reference is enough
- Callbacks in the closer create a satisfying sense of completeness

### Transitions

- Thematic transitions: connect topics through shared themes
- "Speaking of..." is a simple, direct transition
- Attitude shift: change your energy to signal a new topic
- Crowd work between bits: natural transition while maintaining engagement
- Smooth transitions make a set feel like a conversation, not a list

### Set Timing

| Set Length | Context | Material Needed |
|------------|---------|----------------|
| 3-5 minutes | Open mic | 5-7 jokes with tags |
| 5-7 minutes | Showcase, guest spot | 8-12 jokes, tight flow |
| 10-15 minutes | Feature spot | Full themed sections |
| 20-30 minutes | Headliner opening | Multiple topics, crowd work |
| 45-60 minutes | Headliner | Deep exploration, callbacks, arc |

## Growth and Development

### Tracking Progress

- Log every performance: date, venue, set length, notes
- Track which jokes work and which do not (hit rate by joke)
- Video review monthly: watch yourself with fresh eyes
- Seek honest feedback from other comics, not just friends and family
- Set measurable goals: number of mics per week, minutes of material per month

### Common Beginner Mistakes

- Writing too much material before testing any of it
- Not performing frequently enough (stage time is everything)
- Trying to be someone else instead of finding your own voice
- Apologizing or signaling insecurity on stage
- Not recording performances
- Giving up after a bad set (every comedian has bad sets regularly)
- Performing only at one venue (different rooms develop different skills)
- Not editing material based on audience response

### Stage Time Goals

| Phase | Frequency | Purpose |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| First 6 months | 3-5 mics per week | Build comfort on stage |
| 6-12 months | 3-5 mics per week | Develop 15+ minutes of working material |
| Year 2 | 4+ mics per week, seek booked spots | Refine voice, build 30+ minutes |
| Year 3+ | Regular booked shows, produce own shows | Develop full sets, headline opportunities |

## Quick Reference Cheat Sheet

**Write a joke in 5 steps:**
1. Pick a topic and list 10 things that are true, weird, or frustrating about it
2. Choose the strongest observation and form a setup that creates an assumption
3. Write 5 possible punchlines that violate that assumption in a surprising way
4. Keep the funniest one, trim every unnecessary word, put the funny word last
5. Add 2-3 tags that explore different angles of the same premise

**Prepare for an open mic in 5 steps:**
1. Memorize 3-5 minutes of material thoroughly (20+ run-throughs aloud)
2. Time yourself; respect the slot length exactly
3. Visit the venue as an audience member first to know the room
4. Before going up: breathe, shake out tension, commit to being present
5. Record your set, review within 24 hours, note what got laughs and what did not
