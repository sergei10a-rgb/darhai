## Song Structure

### Common Structures

| Structure | Pattern | Used In |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| Verse-Chorus | V-C-V-C-C | Pop, rock, country (most common) |
| Verse-Chorus-Bridge | V-C-V-C-B-C | Pop, rock (adds contrast) |
| Verse-Pre-Chorus-Chorus | V-PC-C-V-PC-C-B-C | Modern pop (builds anticipation) |
| AABA | A-A-B-A | Jazz standards, classic pop |
| Through-composed | No repeated sections | Art songs, some indie, storytelling |
| Verse-Refrain | V(refrain)-V(refrain)-V(refrain) | Folk, blues, some country |

### What Each Section Does

**Verse:** Sets the scene, tells the story, provides new information each time. Melody is typically more conversational and lower in energy.

**Pre-Chorus:** Builds tension and anticipation for the chorus. Harmonically moves toward the chorus key center. Melodically rises. Creates the feeling of "here it comes."

**Chorus:** The emotional and melodic peak. Contains the song's central message or hook. Should be singable after one listen. Repeats with the same (or very similar) lyrics each time.

**Bridge:** Provides contrast in every dimension - new melody, new chords, new perspective. Often the emotional climax or revelation. Appears only once, typically after the second chorus.

**Hook:** The most memorable element of the song. Can be a lyric phrase, a melodic motif, or a production element. It is what the listener hums after the song ends.

### Structure Decision Framework

| If Your Song... | Consider This Structure |
|----------------|----------------------|
| Has a strong, singable central phrase | Verse-Chorus (let the chorus do the work) |
| Tells a story with a twist | Verse-Chorus-Bridge (bridge delivers the twist) |
| Needs momentum building | Verse-Pre-Chorus-Chorus (the pre-chorus drives anticipation) |
| Is intimate and personal | Verse-Refrain (keeps the focus close) |
| Has a lot of lyrical content | AABA (maximizes verse space) |

## Melody Writing

### Melodic Principles

**Range:** Most singable melodies stay within an octave and a half. The wider the range, the fewer people can sing along.

**Contour:** Melodies that rise toward important words and fall toward resolution are intuitively satisfying. Map the emotional arc of your lyric to the melodic contour.

**Repetition with variation:** Repeat your melodic phrases, but alter them slightly each time. This creates familiarity without boredom.

**Rhythm variety:** A melody that follows the same rhythm for every line is monotonous. Alternate between shorter, punchy phrases and longer, flowing ones.

### Melody Writing Techniques

**Sing before you play:**
Put down your instrument. Hum or sing melodic ideas without harmonic constraint. Your voice will find natural, singable melodies that your instrument's patterns might not suggest.

**The constraint method:**
Limit yourself to 5 notes and write a melody. Constraints breed creativity. Many iconic melodies use very few notes ("Mary Had a Little Lamb" uses three).

**Record everything:**
Keep a voice memo recorder accessible. Melodic ideas arrive at inconvenient times. Capture them in 10 seconds now rather than trying to remember later.

**Prosody check:**
Sing your lyric with the melody. Do the important words land on strong beats and high notes? Does the rhythm of the melody match the natural speech rhythm of the words? Prosody is the alignment of lyric and melody, and misalignment feels wrong even to listeners who cannot articulate why.

### Melody Diagnostic

| Problem | Solution |
|---------|----------|
| Melody feels flat/boring | Add larger intervals (4ths, 5ths) at key moments |
| Too many notes | Strip it back; find the essential melody within the clutter |
| Verse and chorus sound the same | Change the register, rhythm, or note density |
| Not memorable after one listen | Simplify the hook phrase until a child could sing it |
| Feels predictable | Delay the resolution; go where the listener does not expect |

## Lyric Writing

### The Lyric Hierarchy

1. **Title/hook line:** The most important element. If this is weak, nothing else can save the song.
2. **First line:** Must earn the listener's attention in seconds
3. **Chorus lyrics:** Must be universal enough for anyone to sing, specific enough to feel true
4. **Verse lyrics:** Earn the right for the chorus to hit hard
5. **Bridge lyrics:** The shift in perspective, the deeper truth

### Show, Don't Tell

The single most transformative lyric principle:

| Telling (Weak) | Showing (Strong) |
|----------------|-----------------|
| "I'm lonely" | "The other side of the bed is cold" |
| "Time passes" | "The coffee's still warm but you're already gone" |
| "I'm angry" | "I threw your records in the yard" |
| "She's beautiful" | "She wrecks the room just walking through the door" |
| "We're in love" | "Your name is the first word I say every morning" |

### Rhyme Strategies

**Perfect rhyme** (love/above, fire/desire): Strong, satisfying, can sound cliche if obvious

**Near rhyme/slant rhyme** (love/enough, time/mine): More conversational, modern, less predictable

**Internal rhyme** (rhyme within a line, not just at the end): Adds musicality without the sing-song feeling

**Rhyme scheme options:**
- AABB: Coupled rhymes, strong forward momentum
- ABAB: Alternating rhyme, classic and balanced
- ABCB: Only the 2nd and 4th lines rhyme, more conversational
- XAXA: First and third lines do not rhyme, less formal

**The rhyme test:** If a rhyme forces an unnatural word choice, cut the rhyme. Meaning always beats rhyme.

### Common Lyric Problems

| Problem | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Too vague/abstract | Ground every idea in a concrete image |
| Too many ideas | One song = one emotional idea explored deeply |
| Cliche lyrics | Write the cliche, then ask "what would I actually say?" |
| Forcing rhyme | Use near rhymes, change the rhyme scheme, or restructure the line |
| Sermon/lecture tone | Write from inside the experience, not above it |
| No emotional arc | The song should go somewhere emotionally from start to finish |

### Lyric Writing Exercises

**Object writing (10 minutes daily):**
Pick a random object. Write about it using all five senses for exactly 10 minutes. Do not edit, just write. This trains your brain to generate sensory, specific language on demand.

**Title collection:**
Keep a running list of potential song titles. Overheard phrases, interesting word combinations, emotional truths. When you sit down to write, pick one from the list.

**Rewrite a favorite:**
Take a song you love and rewrite the lyrics from a different perspective or about a different situation, keeping the same structure and syllable count. This teaches structure through imitation.

## Co-Writing

### Why Co-Write

- Two perspectives catch each other's blind spots
- Accountability: harder to procrastinate with a partner in the room
- Skills complement: lyricist + melodicist, or two different emotional viewpoints
- Industry standard: most hit songs are co-written

### Co-Writing Session Structure

| Phase | Time | Activity |
|-------|------|----------|
| Connect | 15 min | Catch up, share what you have been listening to, build rapport |
| Seed | 15 min | Share ideas: titles, concepts, melodic fragments, chord progressions |
| Choose | 10 min | Agree on the strongest idea and the approach |
| Write | 90-120 min | Build the song section by section |
| Review | 15 min | Play the song through, note what works and what needs more |
| Next steps | 5 min | Assign homework: who finishes what before the next session |

### Co-Writing Etiquette

- Come prepared with 3-5 ideas (even if they are rough)
- Say "yes, and" more than "no"
- If you do not like an idea, suggest an alternative rather than just rejecting
- Be willing to kill your darlings if the song is better for it
- Agree on splits upfront (default is equal, regardless of who wrote what)
- Record the full session for reference
- Follow up: send a rough recording within 24 hours while it is fresh

## Demo Recording

### What a Demo Needs

A demo must communicate the song's potential. It does not need to be a finished production.

**Minimum viable demo:**
- Clear vocal (in tune, confident delivery)
- Simple accompaniment (guitar or piano)
- Correct tempo and feel
- Good enough audio quality that the listener focuses on the song, not the recording

### Home Demo Setup

| Need | Budget Option | Better Option |
|------|--------------|---------------|
| Recording software | GarageBand (free, Mac), Audacity (free) | Logic Pro, Ableton Live, Reaper |
| Microphone | Phone voice memo | USB condenser mic (AT2020, Rode NT-USB) |
| Headphones | Any earbuds | Closed-back studio headphones (ATH-M50x) |
| Recording space | Closet or small carpeted room | Treated room or portable vocal booth |

### Demo Recording Workflow

1. Practice the song until you can play and sing it without stopping
2. Record the instrument part first (guitar or piano) to a click track
3. Record the vocal over the instrument track
4. Add a simple bass line if it helps communicate the feel
5. Keep it to 2-3 tracks total for a writing demo
6. Mix: vocal on top, instrument supporting, nothing else competing
7. Export as MP3 and WAV

## Music Publishing

### Understanding Publishing

When you write a song, you own the copyright automatically. Publishing is the business of exploiting that copyright - getting the song recorded, placed in media, and generating income.

### Income Streams from Songs

| Stream | What It Is | Who Pays |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Mechanical royalties | Income from reproductions (streams, downloads, CDs) | Streaming services, labels |
| Performance royalties | Income from public performance (radio, live, TV, streaming) | PROs (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC) |
| Sync licensing | Song placed in film, TV, ads, games | Production companies, ad agencies |
| Print royalties | Sheet music sales | Music print publishers |

### Getting Started with Publishing

1. **Register with a PRO** (Performing Rights Organization): ASCAP, BMI, or SESAC in the US. This is free or low-cost and collects your performance royalties.
2. **Register songs** with your PRO as they are written
3. **Consider a publishing administrator** (Songtrust, CD Baby Pro, TuneCore Publishing) to collect mechanical royalties worldwide
4. **Build a catalog** - publishers are interested in writers with a body of work, not a single song

### Pitching Songs

If writing for other artists:
- Research who the artist's producers and A&R contacts are
- Listen to the artist's recent work to understand their current direction
- Pitch songs that fit what they would record, not what you wish they would record
- Professional demo quality is essential for pitches
- Build relationships over time; cold pitches rarely land

## Overcoming Writer's Block

### Prevention Strategies

- Write on a schedule, not only when inspired (inspiration follows routine)
- Keep an idea notebook or voice memo folder active at all times
- Listen widely outside your genre
- Collaborate when solo writing stalls
- Exercise before writing (physical movement stimulates creativity)

### When You Are Stuck

| Situation | Technique |
|-----------|-----------|
| Cannot start | Set a timer for 10 minutes and write the worst possible version. Then improve it. |
| Stuck on a verse | Skip it. Write the chorus or bridge. Come back to the verse later. |
| Melody feels forced | Walk away from your instrument. Hum in the shower. Let your voice lead. |
| Lyrics are cliche | Write 10 different ways to say the same thing. Number 7 or 8 is usually the breakthrough. |
| Second verse problem | Change the time, place, or perspective from verse one. |
| Nothing feels honest | Write in your journal for 20 minutes first. Get to the real feeling, then write the song about that. |
