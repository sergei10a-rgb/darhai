## Floor Plan Creation

### Floor Plan Types
- **Concept sketch**: Hand-drawn or rough digital sketch showing general room layout and flow. Used in early design conversations.
- **Schematic floor plan**: Accurate wall placement with room labels, door swings, and window locations. Not dimensioned in detail.
- **Dimensioned floor plan**: Full dimensions, wall thicknesses, structural elements, and technical annotations. Used for construction.
- **Furniture layout plan**: Shows furniture placement within the space. Used for interior design presentations and space planning.
- **Rendered floor plan**: Color-coded, visually polished plan with material indications, shadows, and furniture icons. Used for marketing and client presentations.

### Floor Plan Fundamentals
- **Scale**: Choose a consistent scale. Common: 1/4" = 1'-0" (residential), 1/8" = 1'-0" (commercial/larger buildings)
- **Wall representation**: Thicker lines for exterior and structural walls, thinner for interior partition walls
- **Doors**: Show the swing arc (90-degree quarter circle for hinged doors). Indicate door width.
- **Windows**: Show as a break in the wall with a thin line representing the glass
- **North arrow**: Always include orientation
- **Room labels**: Name each room with area in square feet or square meters

### Space Planning Principles
- **Circulation**: Ensure clear pathways of 36" (91cm) minimum for residential hallways and between furniture groups
- **Zones**: Group related activities (cooking/dining, sleeping/bathing, working/meeting). Separate public zones from private zones.
- **Furniture clearances**:
  - Dining: 36" from table edge to wall for seated access, 48" to allow passage behind seated people
  - Living: 14-18" between sofa and coffee table, 36" passage around furniture groups
  - Bedroom: 24" minimum beside the bed, 36" preferred. 36" in front of closet and dresser.
  - Kitchen: 42-48" between opposing counters. 36" minimum for single-cook kitchens.
- **Focal points**: Each room should have a primary focal point (fireplace, view window, feature wall). Arrange furniture to orient toward it.
- **Natural light**: Position living spaces to maximize natural light. Bedrooms can tolerate less. Kitchens and living rooms benefit most from south or west exposure (in the Northern Hemisphere).

### Floor Plan Tools
- **Quick concept plans**: Magicplan (phone app), RoomSketcher, Floorplanner (web-based)
- **Professional 2D drafting**: AutoCAD, DraftSight, SketchUp (with plan view)
- **Integrated BIM**: Revit, ArchiCAD (generates floor plans from the 3D model)
- **Rendered plans**: Export from any tool above, render in Photoshop or Illustrator for polished presentation

## Mood Board Development

### Purpose
Mood boards communicate the design intent visually before any detailed design work begins. They align the designer and client on aesthetic direction, preventing costly misunderstandings later.

### Creating an Effective Mood Board

**Content to include**:
- Color palette swatches (4-7 colors) with names or codes
- Material textures (wood grain, stone, fabric, metal)
- Furniture style references (key pieces that define the look)
- Lighting fixtures that represent the intended ambiance
- Architectural details (molding profiles, tile patterns, window styles)
- Accessory and decor references
- Overall atmosphere photography (not necessarily of interiors -- landscapes, nature, fashion, and art can convey mood effectively)

**Layout principles**:
- One mood board per room or design zone (do not combine the kitchen and bedroom mood board unless they are identical in style)
- Arrange images in a balanced, intentional composition. Not a random collage.
- Include a brief text description: "The living room combines warm oak, soft bouclé textiles, and blackened steel accents for a refined yet inviting atmosphere."
- Show the mood board on a clean, neutral background.
- Typical size: 16x20" for print presentation, 1920x1080 or 1920x1200 for screen.

**Tools**:
- Canva: Quick assembly, template-based
- Figma: Precise control, collaboration features
- InDesign: Professional layout, print-ready output
- Milanote: Visual organization tool specifically designed for mood boards
- Pinterest: Research and collection (but not for final presentation)

### Material and Finish Selection

**Hard surfaces**:
- **Flooring**: Hardwood (species, stain, plank width), tile (porcelain, ceramic, natural stone, size, pattern), carpet (pile type, fiber), luxury vinyl plank/tile
- **Countertops**: Natural stone (marble, granite, quartzite), engineered stone (Quartz/Silestone/Caesarstone), butcher block, concrete, laminate
- **Walls**: Paint (flat, eggshell, satin, semi-gloss finishes), wallpaper, tile, paneling, plaster, exposed brick
- **Cabinetry**: Wood species and stain, painted MDF, thermofoil, metal, door style (shaker, slab, raised panel)

**Soft furnishings**:
- **Upholstery**: Performance fabrics (Crypton, Sunbrella for durability), velvet, leather, linen, bouclé, chenille
- **Window treatments**: Drapery (lining weight, fullness), roller shades, Roman shades, blinds, shutters
- **Rugs**: Wool, cotton, jute, synthetic, hand-knotted, machine-made. Size relative to furniture grouping.
- **Bedding and cushions**: Texture, pattern, and color to complement the palette

**Metal finishes**:
- Brass/gold (warm, luxurious)
- Matte black (modern, dramatic)
- Chrome (cool, contemporary)
- Brushed nickel (neutral, versatile)
- Copper (warm, industrial)
- Oil-rubbed bronze (traditional, rustic)
- Maintain consistency: all hardware, lighting, and plumbing fixtures in a room should share the same metal finish or a deliberate combination (typically limit to two metal finishes per space)

### Creating a Material Board
A physical or digital board showing actual material samples:
- Include a swatch or image of every material in the room
- Arrange proportionally to how much of each material will be used (dominant materials get larger swatches)
- Label each material with product name, manufacturer, finish, and code/SKU
- Place materials adjacent to ones they will neighbor in the actual space

## 3D Rendering

### Choosing a Rendering Approach

**Real-time rendering** (fast, interactive):
- Tools: Enscape, Twinmotion, Lumion, Unreal Engine
- Renders in seconds. Allows walkthroughs and VR.
- Quality is good and improving rapidly. Suitable for most client presentations.
- Best for: Quick iterations, design exploration, client walkthroughs, VR presentations

**Offline/Path-traced rendering** (photorealistic, slower):
- Tools: V-Ray, Corona Renderer, Cycles (Blender), KeyShot
- Renders in minutes to hours per image.
- Highest visual quality. Accurate light simulation.
- Best for: Marketing images, competition entries, publications, high-end client presentations

### 3D Modeling Tools
- **SketchUp**: Easiest to learn. Great for conceptual modeling and quick 3D visualization. Pairs with Enscape, Lumion, or V-Ray for rendering.
- **Blender**: Free, powerful, steep learning curve. Excellent renderer (Cycles). Good for detailed scenes and animations.
- **Revit**: BIM tool that generates floor plans, sections, and 3D models simultaneously. Essential for architecture firms. Renders via Enscape or exported to 3ds Max.
- **3ds Max**: Industry standard for architectural visualization. Powerful modeling and V-Ray integration. Complex.
- **Rhino + Grasshopper**: Parametric modeling for complex geometry. Used for unique architectural forms.

### Rendering Quality Factors
1. **Lighting**: The single most important factor. Use realistic light sources (sun, sky, artificial fixtures with accurate IES profiles). Avoid flat, uniform lighting.
2. **Materials**: Accurate material properties (roughness, reflection, translucency). Use PBR (Physically Based Rendering) textures from Poliigon, Quixel Megascans, or Texture Haven.
3. **Composition**: Apply photography principles -- rule of thirds, leading lines, focal point, depth. A well-composed rendering with moderate quality outperforms a poorly composed photorealistic one.
4. **Scale reference**: Include human figures, furniture, or everyday objects so the viewer understands the space's scale.
5. **Details**: Life makes spaces feel real. Books, plants, cups, blankets, personal objects. Empty rooms feel sterile and unreal.
6. **Post-processing**: Adjust exposure, contrast, color temperature, and add subtle vignette in Photoshop or Lightroom after rendering. This is standard practice.

### Camera Settings for Architectural Rendering
- **Focal length**: 18-24mm for interiors (wide angle, shows more of the room). 35-50mm for exteriors and detail shots.
- **Camera height**: 3.5-5 feet (approximately eye level when standing or seated)
- **Vertical lines**: Keep vertical lines straight (no lens distortion tilting the camera up). Use a two-point perspective or correct in post.
- **Depth of field**: Slight depth of field blur on foreground/background elements adds realism and focus.
- **Exposure**: Match to natural conditions. Interiors with window views often need careful exposure balancing (interior exposure vs. exterior view brightness).

## Presentation Board Design

### Types of Presentation Boards
- **Concept board**: Mood, inspiration, initial direction. Images, textures, colors, keywords.
- **Scheme board**: Detailed design scheme for one room or area. Floor plan, material selections, key furniture, elevations, 3D views, fabric swatches.
- **Material/Finish board**: Focused on materials. Samples or high-quality images with specifications and supplier information.
- **Comparison board**: Two or more design options presented side by side for client decision-making.

### Board Layout Principles
- **Hierarchy**: The most important element (rendering, floor plan, or hero image) should be the largest and most prominent
- **Alignment**: Use a grid. Align elements to each other. Consistent margins.
- **Whitespace**: Do not fill every inch. Breathing room makes the presentation feel professional and allows each element to be appreciated.
- **Flow**: Guide the eye from the title to the main visual to supporting details. Left to right, top to bottom.
- **Consistency**: Matching fonts, image treatment, and layout structure across all boards in a presentation.

### Board Specifications
- **Print**: A3 (297x420mm) or A2 (420x594mm) boards printed on foam core, mounted, or in a portfolio binder
- **Digital**: 1920x1080 (landscape for screen presentations), 1920x1200, or custom dimensions
- **PDF**: Multi-page PDF compiling all boards for email delivery or digital review

## Client Communication

### Presentation Flow
Structure your client meeting for maximum clarity:

1. **Context recap** (2 min): "Based on our discovery session, here is what we are solving for..."
2. **Mood and direction** (5 min): Present the mood board. Confirm the aesthetic direction.
3. **Space plan** (10 min): Walk through the floor plan. Explain zoning, circulation, and furniture placement. Answer spatial questions.
4. **Materials and finishes** (10 min): Present the material board. Pass physical samples if available.
5. **3D visualization** (10 min): Show renderings or a walkthrough. Explain key design features in context.
6. **Details and specifications** (5 min): Highlight specific product selections, fixtures, and finishes.
7. **Discussion and feedback** (15 min): Open floor for questions and reactions.
8. **Next steps** (5 min): Summarize decisions, outline the next phase, confirm timelines.

### Gathering Useful Feedback
- Ask specific questions: "Does the flooring direction feel right?" not "What do you think?"
- Show physical material samples whenever possible. Screens cannot accurately represent texture or true color.
- Present options when decisions are needed. "Option A uses marble countertops for a refined look. Option B uses butcher block for warmth. Which direction feels right for your family's daily use?"
- Document all decisions and feedback in writing after each meeting.
- Distinguish between aesthetic preferences (subjective, client's choice) and design principles (professional guidance). Be willing to advocate for good design.

### Managing Expectations
- Renderings are representations, not photographs. Actual materials, lighting, and execution will have natural variations.
- Include disclaimers: "Colors and materials may vary from screen representation. Final selections should be confirmed with physical samples."
- Communicate timeline and budget implications of design changes promptly.
- Provide a revision limit and additional revision fees in your contract.

## Workflow for Common Project Types

### Residential Interior Design Visualization
1. Site visit and measurements (or review existing drawings)
2. Discovery meeting with client (lifestyle, preferences, budget)
3. Mood boards for each major room (2-3 directions)
4. Space planning and furniture layout
5. Material and finish selections
6. 3D renderings of key views (2-3 per room)
7. Presentation boards compiling all elements
8. Client review and revisions
9. Specification documents for purchasing/procurement

### Commercial Space Visualization
1. Review program requirements (number of workstations, meeting rooms, amenities)
2. Zoning and adjacency planning
3. Space plan with furniture layout
4. Material palette development
5. Brand integration (signage, colors, environmental graphics)
6. 3D renderings for key spaces (reception, open office, meeting rooms, break room)
7. Presentation to stakeholders
8. Revisions and final documentation

### Marketing Visualization (for developers/real estate)
1. Review architectural drawings and specifications
2. Develop a visual style that matches the marketing positioning
3. Create photorealistic exterior renderings (golden hour lighting, landscaping, context)
4. Create photorealistic interior renderings (staged with furniture, lifestyle accessories)
5. Create aerial/drone perspective views
6. Post-process all images to marketing quality
7. Deliver high-resolution files for print, web, and signage

## Building Your Visualization Portfolio

### What to Include
- A range of project types (residential, commercial, hospitality if applicable)
- Before-and-after for renovation projects (existing photo vs. rendered proposal)
- Multiple visualization types (floor plans, mood boards, 3D renderings, walkthroughs)
- Process documentation (concept sketch to final rendering)
- Both interior and exterior work if you offer both

### Creating Speculative Work
If you lack client projects for your portfolio:
- Redesign a space you have access to (your apartment, a friend's living room, a local cafe)
- Create concept designs for fictional projects (a boutique hotel lobby, a creative agency office)
- Participate in architectural visualization competitions (CGArchitect, Evermotion)
- Recreate real architectural projects from published plans as practice

### Presentation
- Show work in context: renderings alongside floor plans and mood boards
- Include brief project descriptions (type, location, scope, your role)
- Host on a clean portfolio website (Squarespace, Cargo, Behance, ArtStation)
- Keep the portfolio current: remove older work as your skills improve
