## Acting Techniques Overview

### Stanislavski System

The foundation of modern acting technique. Key principles:

**Given Circumstances**
- Analyze everything the script tells you about the character
- Who am I? Where am I? When is it? What surrounds me?
- What are the social, political, and historical circumstances?
- Build a complete world around the character's reality

**Objective and Super-Objective**
- Objective: what the character wants in each scene (use active verbs)
- Super-objective: what the character wants throughout the entire play
- Every line should serve the objective
- Phrase objectives as "I want to..." followed by an active verb
- Examples: "I want to convince," "I want to escape," "I want to seduce"

**Magic If**
- "What would I do IF I were in this character's circumstances?"
- Bridge between self and character
- Not pretending to be someone else; exploring what you would do
- Grounds performance in personal truth

**Emotional Memory (Affective Memory)**
- Recall personal sensory experiences to access emotions
- Use sense memory: recall the sights, sounds, smells, textures
- Not about reliving trauma; about accessing emotional availability
- Use with caution; should not cause psychological distress
- This technique is debated; some modern coaches advise against it

### Meisner Technique

Based on truthful behavior under imaginary circumstances.

**The Repetition Exercise**
1. Two partners face each other
2. One makes an observation: "You're wearing a blue shirt"
3. The other repeats it: "I'm wearing a blue shirt"
4. Continue repeating, allowing the reading to change based on genuine response
5. The words stay the same; the meaning shifts with each genuine reaction
6. Develops instinct to respond truthfully rather than performing
7. Practice for 10-15 minutes daily with a partner

**Emotional Preparation**
- Before entering a scene, prepare an emotional state using personal daydream
- Not specific to the scene; creates emotional availability
- "Come to the door" ready to be affected by the other actor
- The preparation opens you; the other actor determines where it goes

**Working Off the Other Actor**
- Focus entirely on your scene partner, not on your own performance
- Listen with full attention; respond to what you actually receive
- If your partner gives you something unexpected, follow that truth
- The best acting happens between actors, not inside one performer
- Stop monitoring yourself; let the moment carry you

**Independent Activity**
- Practice doing a difficult physical task while a partner disrupts you
- Builds the ability to pursue an objective while being affected
- The activity must matter to you and be genuinely difficult
- Develops multidimensional behavior: doing and being affected simultaneously

### Practical Aesthetics (Atlantic Theater Company / David Mamet)

**Script Analysis Steps:**
1. **Literal**: What is the character literally doing in the scene?
2. **Want**: What does the character want from the other person (one essential action)?
3. **Essential Action**: Translate the want into a universal human experience
4. **As If**: Find a personal parallel: "It's as if I'm trying to get my best friend to forgive me"

**Performance:**
- Pursue the action moment to moment
- Respond truthfully to what you receive from the other actor
- No emotional preparation; the action and the partner provide everything
- Simple, direct, and grounded

### Physical Approaches

**Viewpoints (Mary Overlie / Anne Bogart)**
- Nine viewpoints of movement: tempo, duration, kinesthetic response, repetition, shape, gesture, architecture, spatial relationship, topography
- Develop physical awareness and ensemble connection
- Practice in group settings; build scenes from movement vocabulary
- Useful for devised and physical theater

**Laban Movement Analysis**
- Effort qualities: Weight (strong/light), Time (sudden/sustained), Space (direct/indirect), Flow (bound/free)
- Every character has a unique combination of effort qualities
- Use Laban to physicalize character from the outside in
- Experiment: how does your character move through space?

**Chekhov Technique (Michael Chekhov)**
- Psychological Gesture: a single physical gesture that captures the character's inner life
- Imaginary Body: adopt a different physical form than your own
- Atmospheres: the emotional quality of a space that affects behavior
- Radiating and receiving: directing or absorbing energy

## Character Development

### Script Analysis Process

1. **Read the full script three times minimum**
   - First read: audience experience, emotional response, story
   - Second read: character focus, relationships, arc
   - Third read: technical analysis, structure, given circumstances

2. **Build the Character Biography**
   - Facts from the script (only what is explicitly stated)
   - Inferences (reasonable conclusions from facts)
   - Choices (creative decisions to fill gaps the script leaves open)
   - Physical life: age, health, habits, mannerisms
   - Social life: status, relationships, education, work
   - Psychological life: temperament, beliefs, fears, desires

3. **Map the Arc**
   - Where does the character start emotionally and psychologically?
   - What events change the character during the story?
   - Where does the character end up?
   - What is the transformation or realization?
   - Identify the turning points; these are the performance peaks

4. **Relationship Mapping**
   - How does the character feel about every other character?
   - How does each relationship serve or obstruct the objective?
   - What is the history between these characters?
   - How does the relationship change during the story?

### Making Strong Choices

- Specificity is power: vague choices produce vague performances
- Choose the most extreme interpretation that the text supports
- Play against expectation when possible (anger played quietly is more threatening)
- Find the humor in serious scenes and the gravity in comic ones
- Commit fully; a bold wrong choice is more useful in rehearsal than a safe one
- You can always adjust; you cannot adjust from nothing

### Finding the Opposites

- Characters are rarely one thing consistently
- A villain who loves their dog is more human and more frightening
- Find the vulnerability in strong characters, the strength in weak ones
- Contradictions create dimension
- Ask: what is the character hiding? What do they not want others to see?

## Audition Preparation

### Before the Audition

**Research**
- Know the project: full script if possible, synopsis and tone at minimum
- Research the director, the production company, the creative team
- Understand the role: age, type, arc, requirements
- Watch previous work by the director if available

**Material Preparation**
- Memorize fully; holding sides is acceptable but know the text cold
- Make specific choices about objective, relationship, circumstances
- Prepare the moment before: what just happened before the scene begins
- Have one clear, strong take on the material
- Prepare a subtle alternative if redirected

**Physical Preparation**
- Dress to suggest the character without wearing a costume
- Arrive 15 minutes early to settle in and warm up
- Physical warm-up: stretch, shake out tension, align posture
- Vocal warm-up: resonance exercises, articulation, breath support
- Emotional warm-up: Meisner preparation or visualization

### In the Room

- Walk in with confidence and positive energy
- Make genuine human connection before performing
- Slate clearly: name, representation if applicable
- Take a beat to prepare before beginning; do not rush into it
- Perform to or with the reader, not to the camera (unless directed)
- If you make a mistake, continue; do not break and ask to restart unless major
- Take direction gracefully; show you can adjust
- Thank them and leave cleanly; do not linger or apologize

### Self-Tape Guidelines

- Frame: chest and above for close-up; wider for physical scenes
- Background: plain, neutral color (medium gray or blue, not white)
- Lighting: well-lit face, no harsh shadows, natural or soft artificial
- Sound: quiet room, clear audio, no echo
- Reader: off-camera reader is preferred; record reader's lines if alone
- Eye line: just off-camera, not directly into the lens (unless instructed)
- Slate at the beginning: name, role, brief look at camera
- One clean take is better than many choices; submit your strongest
- Follow all submission instructions exactly

### Callback Strategy

- Recreate what you did in the first audition (they called you back for that)
- Be prepared to take direction and adjust
- If given new material, ask for preparation time if needed
- Demonstrate versatility while maintaining your core interpretation
- Energy and professionalism matter as much as performance

## Monologue Selection

### Choosing the Right Monologue

**Match the Material:**
- Age-appropriate: within 5 years of your actual age
- Type-appropriate: cast yourself honestly
- Tone-appropriate: match the project or audition requirements
- Contemporary for contemporary calls; classical for classical calls
- Avoid overdone monologues (ask acting teachers what they see constantly)

**Technical Criteria:**
- 60-90 seconds for most auditions (know the time limit)
- Has a clear arc: beginning, shift, end
- Contains an active objective directed at a specific person
- Offers emotional range within a short span
- Showcases your strengths while challenging you appropriately
- From a published play (not film, TV, or self-written, unless specified)

**Selection Process:**
1. Read widely: visit the library, read published play anthologies
2. Shortlist 10-15 potential pieces
3. Read the full play for each shortlisted monologue (context matters)
4. Narrow to 4-6 monologues that genuinely excite you
5. Prepare 2 contrasting pieces minimum (one comedic, one dramatic)
6. Work the monologues with a coach before performing them in auditions

### Preparing a Monologue

1. Identify the objective: what do you want from the person you are speaking to?
2. Identify the person: who specifically are you talking to? Visualize them
3. Establish the relationship: what is your history with this person?
4. Find the moment before: what just happened that compels you to speak now?
5. Map the beats: where does the intention or tactic shift?
6. Find the build: where is the emotional climax of the piece?
7. Determine the ending: how do you leave the moment? What comes next?

## Warm-Up Routine

### Physical Warm-Up (10 minutes)

1. **Alignment check**: Stand feet hip-width apart, stack joints, release tension
2. **Shake-out**: Shake hands, arms, legs, whole body vigorously for 30 seconds
3. **Roll-down**: Slowly roll spine down to hanging position, roll back up
4. **Stretch**: Neck circles, shoulder rolls, torso twists, hip circles
5. **Tension release**: Squeeze and release each body part from toes to face
6. **Grounding**: Plant feet, feel the floor, breathe deeply into the body

### Vocal Warm-Up (10 minutes)

1. **Breath**: Deep diaphragmatic breaths, exhale on a hiss (sss), extend duration
2. **Hum**: Start low, sweep through range, feel vibration in chest, face, head
3. **Lip trills**: Sustained lip trill through range, relaxes the voice
4. **Resonance**: "Mmm-ah" from hum to open vowel, place sound forward
5. **Articulation**: Tongue twisters at increasing speed (red leather yellow leather)
6. **Range**: Siren from lowest comfortable note to highest, smooth transitions
7. **Text**: Run through opening lines at half speed, then full speed, then with emotion

### Emotional Warm-Up (5 minutes)

1. **Stillness**: Close eyes, breathe, notice physical sensations
2. **Observation**: Open eyes, notice the room; let details affect you
3. **Imagination**: Visualize the world of the scene, the relationship, the stakes
4. **Preparation**: Meisner emotional preparation or sense memory exercise
5. **Readiness**: Stand at the entrance, breathe, and enter when genuinely ready

## Practice Plan

### Daily Practice (30 minutes)

- 10 minutes: physical and vocal warm-up
- 10 minutes: observation exercise (watch people, notice behavior, replicate)
- 10 minutes: text work (cold read a scene, analyze a character, run monologue)

### Weekly Practice (2-3 hours)

- Run monologues for maintenance (perform, do not just recite)
- Work a new scene with a partner
- Watch one performance (live theater, film, recorded stage performance) analytically
- Journal: What did you discover this week? What challenged you?

### Monthly Goals

- Add one new monologue to your repertoire
- Attend a class, workshop, or open mic night
- Record yourself performing and review objectively
- Seek feedback from trusted peers or coaches
- Set a specific skill to develop each month

## Quick Reference Cheat Sheet

**Prepare an audition in 5 steps:**
1. Research the project, role, and creative team thoroughly
2. Memorize the material and make specific character choices (objective, relationship, given circumstances)
3. Prepare the moment before, map the beat changes, find the emotional arc
4. Do a full physical, vocal, and emotional warm-up before entering the room
5. Perform with full commitment, take direction gracefully, leave clean

**Develop a character in 5 steps:**
1. Read the full script three times (story, character, technical)
2. List every fact, inference, and creative choice about the character
3. Define the super-objective and scene-by-scene objectives
4. Map the arc: where they start, what changes them, where they end
5. Find the contradictions and specificity that make the character human
