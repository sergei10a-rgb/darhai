# Family Meeting Facilitator

## When to Use

**Use this skill when:**
- User asks about family meeting facilitator
- User needs guidance on family meeting facilitator topics
- User wants a structured approach to family meeting facilitator

**Do NOT use when:**
- Request requires professional consultation beyond educational guidance
- User needs emergency assistance

## Overview

This skill helps families establish and run effective family meetings -- regular, structured gatherings where every family member has a voice. Grounded in the Adlerian psychology framework (Rudolf Dreikurs), positive discipline family meeting methodology (Jane Nelsen), and family systems therapy principles, this tool provides everything needed to make family meetings a cornerstone of household communication, problem-solving, and connection.

## Questions to Ask the User First

1. **Who is in the family?** (Names, ages, roles -- include all household members)
2. **Have you held family meetings before?** (What worked? What didn't?)
3. **What's prompting this?** (Recurring conflict, better organization, more connection, all of the above)
4. **What's the biggest challenge in your family right now?**
5. **How do family members currently communicate about issues?** (Individually? During arguments? Never?)
6. **What's the family's general schedule?** (To find a consistent meeting time)
7. **How old are the children?** (Determines participation level and format)
8. **Is everyone willing to participate?** (Resistant members need special handling)
9. **Any specific topics you want to address first?**
10. **What tone do you want for these meetings?** (Formal, casual, fun, structured)

### Meeting Format Overview

```
STANDARD FAMILY MEETING STRUCTURE (30-45 minutes):

1. OPENING (3-5 min)
   - Compliments and appreciations round
   - Each person shares something they appreciated about
     another family member this week

   - Any follow-up needed?

3. OLD BUSINESS (5-10 min)

## Age-Appropriate Participation

### Toddlers and Preschoolers (Ages 2-5)

```
PARTICIPATION LEVEL: Observer becoming participant

THEIR ROLE:
  - Sit at the table with the family (with activities if needed)
  - Give/receive appreciations (with prompting)
  - Vote on simple choices (hold up a card, point to option)
  - Participate in closing fun activity

ACCOMMODATIONS:
  - Keep meeting SHORT (15-20 minutes maximum)
  - Use visual aids (pictures for agenda items)
  - Let them draw during discussion parts
  - Ask yes/no questions: "Should we go to the park this weekend?"
  - Celebrate their participation enthusiastically
  - Don't force participation -- model it, they'll join when ready

EXAMPLE PROMPT:
  "We're going to talk about our family. First, let's say nice
   things. [Child's name], can you tell [sibling] something nice?
   Like 'I like playing with you' or 'Thank you for sharing'?"
```

### Early School Age (Ages 6-8)

```
PARTICIPATION LEVEL: Active participant with support

THEIR ROLE:
  - Share appreciations independently
  - Add items to the agenda during the week (with help writing)
  - Participate in brainstorming solutions
  - Help with calendar review
  - Vote on decisions
  - Take on roles: timer, snack preparer

ACCOMMODATIONS:
  - Keep meetings to 20-25 minutes
  - Use visual agenda posted on wall
  - Write ideas on a whiteboard they can see
  - Give them specific prompts if stuck: "What's one idea for solving this?"
  - Respect their ideas even if impractical (discuss, don't dismiss)
```

### Tweens (Ages 9-12)

```
PARTICIPATION LEVEL: Full participant, emerging leader

THEIR ROLE:
  - All basic roles
  - Serve as facilitator (with coaching)
  - Serve as scribe
  - Propose agenda items independently
  - Research solutions before the meeting
  - Help plan family activities and meals

ACCOMMODATIONS:
  - Standard meeting length (30-45 minutes)
  - Include topics that matter to them (screen time, room rules)
  - Give them real decision-making power (not just input)
  - Let them lead portions of the meeting
  - Respect growing need for privacy (not everything is a meeting topic)
```

### Handling Heated Moments

```
WHEN EMOTIONS ESCALATE:

DE-ESCALATION TOOLS:
  1. PAUSE: "Let's take a 2-minute break. Everyone take 3 deep breaths."
  2. REFRAME: "It sounds like everyone cares about this a lot. That's good."
  3. REFLECT: "I'm hearing that [Name] feels ___ and [Name] feels ___."
  4. REDIRECT: "Let's focus on solutions instead of what happened."
  5. TABLE IT: "This topic is too hot right now. Let's come back to it
     next week when we've all had time to think."

GROUND RULES FOR HARD CONVERSATIONS:
  [ ] Use "I feel..." not "You always..."
  [ ] One person speaks at a time (use a talking object)
  [ ] No name-calling, eye-rolling, or mocking
  [ ] It's okay to disagree. It's not okay to be disrespectful.
  [ ] Anyone can call a break if they need one
  [ ] What's shared in the meeting stays in the meeting (trust)
```

### Common Family Conflicts to Address

```
TOPIC: Screen time disagreements
  Approach: Use data (current usage) + feelings + family values
  Template: "Let's look at what's happening, how everyone feels,
            and find a plan we all agree to try for one week."

TOPIC: Chore fairness
  Approach: List all household tasks, see total workload, redistribute
  Template: "Let's make sure the work is shared fairly. Here's everything
            that needs to happen in our house each week..."

TOPIC: Sibling conflict
  Approach: Both sides heard, joint problem-solving, ground rules
  Template: "You're both important to this family. Let's figure out
            how you can [share the space/respect belongings/etc.]"
# ... (condensed) ...
TOPIC: Changing rules as kids grow
  Approach: Acknowledge growing maturity, negotiate collaboratively
  Template: "You're getting older and we want to give you more freedom.
            Let's talk about what that looks like and what responsibilities
            come with it."
```

## Getting Started Guide

### Week 1: The First Family Meeting

```

   "We're starting something new in our family! We're going to have

### Building the Habit

```
FAMILY MEETING ESTABLISHMENT TIMELINE:

Weeks 1-2: NOVELTY PHASE
  - Keep it short, fun, and positive
  - Only discuss easy, pleasant topics
  - Focus on appreciations and fun planning
  - Don't bring up big problems yet

Weeks 3-4: BUILDING TRUST
  - Introduce the agenda board (post in common area)
  - Start addressing small problems
  - Practice the problem-solving process with easy issues
  - Begin rotating facilitator role

ONGOING:
  - Review and refresh format every few months

  - It's okay to skip occasionally, but return to routine quickly
```

## Handling Resistance

```
IF A FAMILY MEMBER REFUSES TO PARTICIPATE:

CHILD RESISTANCE:
  - Don't force it. They can observe quietly.
  - Make meetings appealing (snacks, fun closing activity)
  - Give them a special role (timer, snack manager)
  - Ask privately what would make it better
  - Demonstrate that their input leads to real changes
  - Keep it short -- long meetings kill buy-in

TEEN RESISTANCE:
  - Acknowledge: "I know this might feel weird or forced"
  - Give them ownership: "How would you structure this?"
  - Address real topics that matter to them (curfew, privileges)
  # ... (condensed) ...
  - Share the research on family meetings and outcomes
  - Start small: "Can we try it for 4 weeks?"
  - Let them shape the format
  - Don't make it feel like therapy
  - Focus on practical benefits (coordination, fewer arguments)
```
