# Divorce Logistics Checklist

Divorce is one of the most logistically complex transitions a person can go through, affecting finances, housing, legal status, and family dynamics simultaneously. This skill provides the organizational frameworks and checklists for managing the practical side of that transition. It does not assess relationships, advise on whether to divorce, or provide legal counsel. For emotional support during this process, a licensed therapist specializing in divorce transition can provide individualized guidance. For legal strategy, an attorney in your jurisdiction is essential.

## When to Use

**Use this skill when:**
- User asks about the practical steps of going through a divorce
- User needs help organizing the financial aspects of a separation
- User wants a checklist for managing divorce logistics
- User asks about co-parenting logistics after separation
- User needs to know what documents and information to gather for a divorce

**Do NOT use this skill when:**
- User asks whether they should get divorced (relationship decision, not logistics)
- User needs legal strategy or custody advice (attorney required)
- User wants help processing the emotional impact of divorce (therapist needed)
- User asks about specific divorce laws in their jurisdiction (attorney required)
- User wants help mediating disputes with their spouse (mediator or attorney needed)

## Process

1. **Gather the financial inventory.** Before any legal steps, document the complete financial picture:

   **Income and Employment:**
   - [ ] Both spouses' current income (gross and net)
   - [ ] Employment details: employer, position, tenure, benefits
   - [ ] Other income sources: rental property, investments, side businesses, freelance
   - [ ] Recent tax returns (past 3 years minimum)
   - [ ] Pay stubs from the past 3 months

   **Assets:**
   - [ ] Bank accounts (checking, savings) -- all institutions, current balances
   - [ ] Investment accounts (brokerage, retirement: 401k, IRA, pension)
   - [ ] Real estate: market value, mortgage balance, equity
   - [ ] Vehicles: make, model, year, current value, loan balance
   - [ ] Other significant assets: jewelry, art, collectibles, business ownership
   - [ ] Life insurance policies (cash value, death benefit, beneficiaries)

   **Debts:**
   - [ ] Mortgage(s): balance, monthly payment, whose name
   - [ ] Credit cards: all accounts, balances, whose name (individual vs. joint)
   - [ ] Student loans: balance, payment, whose name
   - [ ] Auto loans: balance, payment
   - [ ] Personal loans or lines of credit
   - [ ] Any debts owed to family members

   **Monthly Expenses:**
   - [ ] Housing (mortgage or rent, utilities, insurance, taxes, maintenance)
   - [ ] Transportation (car payment, insurance, fuel, maintenance)
   - [ ] Insurance (health, life, disability)
   - [ ] Children's expenses (childcare, education, activities, medical)
   - [ ] Food and household
   - [ ] Debt payments
   - [ ] Subscriptions and memberships
   - [ ] Personal spending for each spouse

2. **Secure important documents.** Gather and make copies of:

   **Legal Documents:**
   - [ ] Marriage certificate
   - [ ] Prenuptial or postnuptial agreement (if exists)
   - [ ] Property deeds and titles
   - [ ] Vehicle titles
   - [ ] Birth certificates (both spouses and all children)
   - [ ] Social Security cards
   - [ ] Passports
   - [ ] Immigration documents if applicable

   **Financial Documents:**
   - [ ] Tax returns (past 3-5 years with all schedules)
   - [ ] Bank statements (past 12 months, all accounts)
   - [ ] Investment account statements
   - [ ] Retirement account statements
   - [ ] Credit card statements (past 12 months)
   - [ ] Mortgage documents
   - [ ] Insurance policies (health, life, auto, home)
   - [ ] Pay stubs and employment contracts
   - [ ] Business financial statements if either spouse owns a business

   **Store copies securely:** Keep copies in a location the other spouse cannot access (trusted family member's home, safe deposit box in your name only, secure digital storage). This is not about secrecy -- it is about ensuring you have access to documents you may need.

3. **Plan the immediate financial separation:**

   **Immediate Steps (First 2 Weeks):**
   - [ ] Open an individual bank account in your name only (if you do not already have one)
   - [ ] Establish credit in your own name if you do not have individual credit history
   - [ ] Do NOT close joint accounts without legal advice (this can have legal consequences)
   - [ ] Do NOT hide assets, transfer large sums, or make major purchases -- courts view this negatively
   - [ ] Review all automatic payments and subscriptions to understand cash flow
   - [ ] Check credit reports for both spouses to identify all debts and accounts

   **Budget for Two Households:**

   | Expense Category | Current (Joint) | Estimate (Your Household) |
   |-----------------|-----------------|--------------------------|
   | Housing | $X,XXX | $X,XXX |
   | Utilities | $XXX | $XXX |
   | Food | $XXX | $XXX |
   | Transportation | $XXX | $XXX |
   | Insurance | $XXX | $XXX |
   | Children's expenses | $XXX | $XXX (your share) |
   | Personal | $XXX | $XXX |
   | Debt payments | $XXX | $XXX (your share) |
   | **Total** | $X,XXX | $X,XXX |

   Note: Living expenses typically increase 30-50% total when one household becomes two. This budget helps identify where adjustments are needed.

4. **Address housing decisions:**

   **Housing Options Assessment:**

   | Option | Considerations | Timeline |
   |--------|---------------|----------|
   | One spouse stays in the home | Can they afford it alone? Refinance needed? | Negotiate during settlement |
   | Sell the home and split equity | Market timing, selling costs (6-8% of sale price), both need new housing | 2-6 months for sale process |
   | Temporary arrangement (one stays, sell later) | Sets a specific future date; may reduce disruption for children | Agreement on timeline required |
   | Both leave and rent the property | Creates income but requires landlord responsibilities | Legal agreement on management |

   **If You Need to Move Out:**
   - [ ] Determine budget for new housing (use the two-household budget above)
   - [ ] Research housing options in the area (proximity to children's school if applicable)
   - [ ] Understand your rights regarding the marital home -- do NOT move out without legal advice, as this can affect your legal position
   - [ ] If renting: you may need to show income verification; prepare documentation

5. **Build the co-parenting logistics framework (if children are involved):**

   **Custody Schedule Logistics:**
   - [ ] Draft a proposed schedule: weekdays, weekends, holidays, school breaks, summer
   - [ ] Consider children's school schedule, activities, and social commitments
   - [ ] Determine pick-up and drop-off logistics (locations, times, who drives)
   - [ ] Plan for schedule changes: how to request changes, minimum notice period, backup plans

   **Co-Parenting Communication System:**
   - [ ] Choose a communication method: dedicated co-parenting app, email, text
   - [ ] Set ground rules: business-like communication about children's needs only
   - [ ] Establish response time expectations (within 24 hours for non-urgent, immediately for emergencies)
   - [ ] Document decisions in writing (verbal agreements get skipped or disputed)

   **Shared Parenting Decisions Checklist:**
   - [ ] Medical decisions: who takes to appointments, how to handle emergencies, insurance coverage
   - [ ] Education decisions: school enrollment, parent-teacher conferences, IEP meetings
   - [ ] Extracurricular activities: who transports, who pays, how to decide on new activities
   - [ ] Technology and screen time: consistent rules across households or household-specific
   - [ ] Discipline approach: alignment on major boundaries even if styles differ
   - [ ] Introduction of new partners: timeline and approach

   **Telling the Children:**
   - Tell them together if possible (united message, reduced confusion)
   - Use age-appropriate language
   - Emphasize: this is an adult decision, it is not the child's fault, both parents still love them
   - Keep the explanation simple: "We have decided to live in separate homes" is sufficient
   - Do not assign blame or share adult details
   - Answer questions honestly but briefly
   - For guidance on age-specific conversations, child development specialists and family therapists can help

6. **Create the legal process checklist:**

   **Finding an Attorney:**
   - [ ] Research family law attorneys in your jurisdiction
   - [ ] Get referrals from trusted sources (not from your spouse's connections)
   - [ ] Schedule consultations with 2-3 attorneys (many offer free or low-cost initial consultations)
   - [ ] Bring the financial inventory and document list to the first consultation
   - [ ] Ask about: fees, timeline, approach (litigation vs. collaborative vs. mediation), experience with cases similar to yours

   **Legal Process Options:**

   | Approach | Best For | Cost Range | Timeline |
   |----------|----------|-----------|----------|
   | Mediation | Couples who can communicate and negotiate | Lower | 3-6 months |
   | Collaborative divorce | Complex situations where both parties want to avoid court | Moderate | 4-9 months |
   | Litigation | High-conflict situations or significant asset disputes | Higher | 6-18+ months |
   | Uncontested (agreement-based) | Couples who agree on all terms | Lowest | 1-3 months |

   Note: These are general categories. Your attorney can advise on the best approach for your specific situation. Costs and timelines vary significantly by jurisdiction.

   **Items to Discuss with Your Attorney:**
   - [ ] Division of assets and debts
   - [ ] Alimony or spousal support (eligibility, amount, duration)
   - [ ] Child custody and visitation schedule
   - [ ] Child support calculation
   - [ ] Division of retirement accounts (may require a Qualified Domestic Relations Order)
   - [ ] Health insurance continuation or transition
   - [ ] Tax implications of the divorce (filing status, exemptions, deductions)

## Rules

1. NEVER advise on whether to divorce -- this is a deeply personal decision outside the scope of logistics planning
2. NEVER provide legal strategy or interpret jurisdiction-specific divorce laws -- all legal guidance requires an attorney
3. NEVER assess or comment on the quality of the relationship or assign blame
4. ALWAYS emphasize: do not hide assets, close joint accounts, or make major financial moves without legal advice -- courts take a dim view of unilateral financial actions
5. ALWAYS include the financial inventory as the first step -- it is the foundation for everything else
6. Include the co-parenting logistics framework whenever children are involved
7. ALWAYS recommend attorney consultation before taking significant legal or financial steps
8. Present all financial information in table format for clarity
9. Include the "telling the children" framework whenever children are involved, but note that family therapists can provide more nuanced age-specific guidance
10. NEVER recommend specific attorneys, mediators, or therapists by name

## Edge Cases

- **High-conflict situation:** When communication between spouses is hostile or abusive, adjust the logistics plan: all communication through attorneys, use a co-parenting app that creates documented records, include safety planning if there is any history of violence or threats, and do not recommend mediation (it requires a baseline of good-faith communication that may not exist). If there is domestic violence, safety comes first -- contact a domestic violence resource before proceeding with logistics.

- **One spouse controls all finances:** When one spouse has no access to financial information or accounts, the financial inventory step becomes more urgent and more difficult. Priority actions: consult an attorney immediately (they can compel financial disclosure), obtain copies of tax returns from the tax authority (they are filed jointly and both spouses are entitled to copies), check credit reports for a list of all accounts and debts, and establish an individual bank account with any available funds for immediate living expenses.

- **Business ownership involved:** When one or both spouses own a business, the divorce logistics are significantly more complex. A business valuation may be required. Business assets, income, and debts must be separated from personal finances. An attorney experienced with business ownership in divorce and a forensic accountant may both be needed. Do not attempt to divide business assets without professional guidance.

- **Same-sex divorce:** Same-sex divorce follows the same legal process as any divorce in jurisdictions where same-sex marriage is recognized. However, there may be additional complexities around: children conceived through assisted reproduction (parentage determination), property acquired before marriage was legally recognized, and jurisdictions that may have different precedents for same-sex family law. Seek an attorney with specific experience in same-sex family law.

- **Gray divorce (later in life):** Divorcing after 50 adds retirement division complexity: Social Security benefits may be affected by marriage duration (10+ years married may entitle a spouse to benefits based on the other's record), retirement account division requires careful planning to avoid tax penalties, health insurance transition is critical if one spouse was on the other's employer plan, and estate planning documents must be completely updated. Financial advisor consultation alongside attorney representation is especially important.

  - What are your fees and how are they structured?
  - What approach do you recommend: mediation, collaborative, or litigation?
  - How is child custody typically handled in this jurisdiction?
  - What should I do and NOT do before filing?

- [ ] Open an individual bank account in your name only (do not transfer marital funds without legal advice)
- [ ] Check your individual credit score and credit report
- [ ] Do NOT close joint accounts, hide money, or make large purchases
- [ ] Create a two-household budget estimate:

### Phase 4: Housing Decision

| Option | For Your Situation | Considerations |
|--------|--------------------|----------------|
| You keep the house | Can you afford the mortgage, taxes, and maintenance on a single income? | May need to refinance to remove spouse from mortgage |
| Spouse keeps the house | Would you need to find new housing near the kids' school? | Budget for rent + deposit |
| Sell the house | How is the current market? How much equity after selling costs? | Takes 2-6 months; both need new housing |
| Stay together temporarily | Reduces disruption for kids while logistics are resolved | Clear timeline and boundaries needed |

### Phase 5: Co-Parenting Framework

**Proposed Schedule Draft:**
- Weekdays: [Primary parent for school nights]
- Weekends: [Alternating or split]
- Holidays: [Alternating by year or splitting the day]
- Summer: [Extended time arrangement]
- Pick-up/drop-off: [Location, times]

**Communication Rules:**
- Use a co-parenting app for all scheduling and logistics
- Respond to non-urgent messages within 24 hours
- Emergency contact: always reachable by phone
- Discuss children's needs only -- personal matters handled through attorneys

**Telling Your 8 and 12 Year Old:**
- Tell them together, both parents present
- Keep it simple: "We have decided to live in separate homes. We both love you. This is not about anything you did."
- The 8-year-old needs reassurance about daily life: "You will still go to the same school, see both of us, keep your room/toys"
- The 12-year-old needs honesty and space: "We know this is hard. You can ask us questions anytime."
- Do not share adult details or assign blame
- If either child has a strong reaction, consider a few sessions with a family therapist who specializes in children and divorce

### Professional Support
- **Attorney:** Essential for all legal and financial decisions
- **Therapist:** Recommended for you and strongly recommended for the children
- **Financial advisor:** Recommended if complex assets (retirement accounts, property, business) require division planning
