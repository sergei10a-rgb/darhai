# Family Reunion Planning

## When to Use

**Use this skill when:**
- A user wants to plan a family reunion of any size -- from 20 people for a single afternoon to 150+ people across a multi-day destination event
- A user needs a structured planning framework for a multi-household, multi-generational family gathering including venue selection, budget modeling, food coordination, and activity programming
- A user is managing RSVP complexity across different family branches with different travel distances, budget capacities, and accessibility needs
- A user wants to create communication templates for save-the-dates, potluck assignments, budget contribution requests, or day-of schedules
- A user is planning a milestone reunion -- a family's first gathering after a long gap, a reunion honoring a recently deceased elder, or a centennial anniversary of immigration or family founding
- A user is trying to revive or formalize a reunion tradition that has lapsed and needs help setting structure around recurring events
- A user is inheriting the organizer role from a previous reunion planner and needs help learning what that role requires

**Do NOT use when:**
- The event is a wedding or wedding-adjacent gathering (rehearsal dinner, engagement party) -- use wedding planning skills, which involve vendors, contracts, formal timelines, and different budget norms
- The event is a corporate retreat, team-building event, or any gathering without a family relationship at its core -- event planning skills apply instead
- The user is planning a small family dinner or holiday meal for fewer than 15 people from 1-3 households -- general host/entertaining guidance is more appropriate
- The user is planning a funeral reception, memorial service, or wake -- grief and logistics intersect in ways this skill does not address
- The event is purely virtual (video call family gatherings) -- the in-person logistics this skill focuses on do not apply
- The user is seeking professional event planning certification or business startup guidance -- this skill serves family organizers, not professionals building a service business

## Process

### Step 1: Gather the Foundational Parameters

Before producing any plan, ask the user for the information that changes every decision downstream. Do not skip this step or make assumptions.

- **Headcount and household count:** Get a range ("about 40-50 people" is workable). Households matter as much as individuals -- a gathering of 40 people from 6 households is far easier to coordinate than 40 people from 22 households. Each additional household adds communication overhead, budget complexity, and scheduling friction.
- **Age distribution:** Ask specifically: How many children under 5? School-age children (6-12)? Teenagers (13-17)? Adults? Seniors over 70? This drives activity design, facility requirements (playground proximity, accessible restrooms, ramp access), and food considerations more than any other factor.
- **Geographic spread:** Local (everyone within 60 miles), regional (within a 4-hour drive), national (cross-country flights), or international (passport required). This determines whether housing logistics are part of the plan, how far in advance save-the-dates must go out (6 weeks for local, 6 months for national/international), and whether a virtual attendance option is needed.
- **Duration:** Single afternoon (4-6 hours), full day (8-10 hours), weekend (Fri-Sun), or extended (4-7 days). Duration is the single biggest cost driver and the biggest logistics multiplier.
- **Budget structure:** Who pays? A single family branch hosting everyone, shared contributions from all households, per-person fees, or tiered options (those who can afford more contribute more)? This is a sensitive question -- offer framing that normalizes all options.
- **Hard constraints:** Any mobility limitations requiring wheelchair or walker access? Any severe food allergies (anaphylactic-level nut or shellfish allergies change catering decisions significantly)? Any known family dynamics (estrangement, recent loss, conflict) the planner needs to design around rather than ignore?
- **Reunion history:** Is this the first reunion ever? A recurring event with existing traditions? A revival after a long gap? Existing traditions should be preserved unless the user specifically wants to change them.

### Step 2: Select the Venue Framework

Venue type is the foundational decision that locks in cost, capacity, and logistics. Use this decision framework:

**Park pavilion / picnic shelter (free or $25-150 reservation fee):**
- Best for: 20-75 people, single-day events, local families, tight budgets
- Requires: On-site power access (check before booking), covered area for rain/sun, accessible restrooms within 100 feet, parking for estimated vehicle count (assume 1 vehicle per 2.5 adults)
- Critical: Reserve the specific pavilion number, not just "the park." Unreserved spaces disappear. Book 8-12 weeks out for weekend dates in spring and fall.
- Limitation: No overnight accommodation, no private kitchen, limited power access for large appliances

**Vacation rental (group house or cabin, $300-3,000/night):**
- Best for: 15-40 people, overnight or weekend events, families wanting a shared home experience
- Requires: Confirm maximum occupancy in writing (not just bed count -- legal occupancy limits matter), verify parking for all vehicles, confirm kitchen capacity (number of burners, oven size, refrigerator space), and check noise ordinances if evening events are planned
- Platforms like vacation rental sites often have group-specific inventory -- search for properties by occupancy rather than bedroom count
- Limitation: Sleeping arrangements create interpersonal complexity. Have a clear bed/room assignment plan before arrival.

**Camp or retreat center ($50-150/person/night, meals often included):**
- Best for: 40-200 people, multi-day events, families wanting structured accommodation with built-in meal service and activity space
- Advantage: All-inclusive pricing simplifies budgeting dramatically. Meal service removes the biggest logistics burden.
- Key differentiators when evaluating: ADA-accessible cabins (many older camps are not), ability to bring outside food (for dietary restrictions not handled by camp kitchen), minimum group size requirements, and alcohol policies
- Limitation: Camp aesthetics and bunk-style sleeping are not appealing to all family members. Assess family culture honestly.

**Hotel block (room block with meeting space):**
- Best for: National or international reunions, families with mixed comfort levels (some will want their own private room), large groups (50+) who cannot coordinate sleeping logistics
- Room block mechanics: Negotiate a room block contract with a minimum pickup commitment (typically 80% of blocked rooms) and a cutoff date for reservations. Get group rates in writing. Negotiate complimentary meeting room space as part of the block agreement -- most hotels will provide it at no cost if the room block is large enough.
- Suite or hospitality room: Book one suite or hospitality room as a common gathering space outside official programming hours. This is worth the cost.
- Limitation: Expensive, impersonal, and families scatter to their rooms -- harder to maintain the gathering energy

**Family property (farm, large home, family land):**
- Best for: When one branch of the family has land or property that accommodates the group and is willing to host
- Requires: Honest assessment of sanitation capacity (portable restroom rental: $75-150/unit per day, 1 unit per 25 people), generator access if power is needed, parking space, and -- critically -- liability. Check homeowner's or farm insurance before hosting a large gathering on private property.
- Limitation: Enormous burden on the hosting branch. Distribute tasks aggressively to avoid resentment.

### Step 3: Build the Planning Timeline

Work backward from the event date. These timeframes are not suggestions -- they reflect real lead times for bookings, printing, food ordering, and human RSVP behavior.

**12+ months before (national/international events only):**
- Lock the date and announce it immediately. Cross-country families need this much lead time for vacation planning, airfare, and PTO requests.
- Secure venue contract and pay deposit
- Establish the organizing committee (minimum 3 people, ideally from different branches of the family)

**6-8 months before (all events with travel; 8-12 weeks before for local day events):**
- Send save-the-dates with RSVP link and deadline
- Establish the budget model and communicate it clearly
- Begin venue contract review or pavilion reservation
- Identify any committee leads: food lead, activities lead, communications lead, treasurer

**3-4 months before:**
- RSVP deadline -- set it and enforce it. Families who RSVP late should be accommodated if possible but should not hold up planning.
- Collect dietary restrictions and accessibility needs in the RSVP form, not later
- Begin activity planning based on confirmed age distribution
- If potluck: assign food categories by household group at this stage

**6-8 weeks before:**
- Send detailed logistics communication: venue address (with pin, not just street address), parking instructions, schedule, what to bring, what is provided
- Finalize food orders for purchased items
- Purchase non-perishable supplies (paper goods, activity materials)
- Confirm all volunteer task assignments in writing

**2 weeks before:**
- Final headcount reconciliation -- account for expected late additions (roughly 10-15% of families will add or drop guests after the RSVP deadline)
- Send day-of schedule to all attendees
- Confirm setup crew and cleanup crew with specific arrival/departure times

**Day of:**
- Setup crew arrives 90 minutes before the announced start time -- not 30 minutes. Large-group setups always take longer than expected.
- Designate one person as the day-of point of contact whose only job is solving problems, not executing tasks

**3-7 days after:**
- Send thank-you message with shared photo album link
- Collect feedback if planning to hold another reunion
- If recurring event: announce next year's date while energy is high

### Step 4: Design the Food Strategy

Food is the highest-cost, highest-complexity, and highest-satisfaction element of a family reunion. Handle it deliberately.

**Potluck (best for budget-constrained events and families with cooking culture):**
- Assign categories by household cluster, not individual household -- it creates more interesting dishes and reduces the burden on each family. Assign by last name initial letter, by family branch, or by geographic cluster.
- Categories to assign: protein main dishes, side dishes/salads, desserts, drinks/beverages, breads/rolls, condiments and serving supplies
- Create a master list of who is bringing what and share it 2 weeks before so duplicates can be avoided and gaps identified
- Always purchase backup protein (a tray of rotisserie chicken, a large pan of deli sandwiches) -- potluck protein is the most unreliable category
- For serious allergies: designate an allergen-safe table with dishes that have been verified free of the allergen. Do not try to make all food allergen-free -- it creates an impossible burden.

**Catered or purchased (best for larger groups, venues with no kitchen access, or time-constrained planners):**
- Restaurant catering for 50+ people: Order from restaurants that regularly do catering (not just call-in orders) -- they have the packaging, labeling, and transport containers necessary. Order 10% above headcount.
- BBQ by family (designated grill lead): Assign one person as grill lead with a helper. Budget 1/3 pound of finished protein per adult, 1/4 pound per child. A standard 22-inch kettle grill handles 12-15 burgers at a time -- plan cook time accordingly.
- Food trucks: A genuine option for larger urban reunions. Contact food truck operators directly -- many have minimum revenue guarantees ($500-1,500) for private events rather than per-person pricing.

**Food quantity planning (real numbers):**
- Potato salad or pasta salad: 1/2 cup per person as a side
- Green salad: 1.5 cups per person
- Dessert: Assume 2/3 of attendees take dessert, 1.5 servings each
- Drinks: 3 drinks per person over a 4-hour event in mild weather; 5+ per person in hot weather
- Sheet cake sizing: A full sheet cake (18"x26") serves approximately 70-80 people for a modest slice

### Step 5: Design the Activity Program

Activities must serve three constituencies simultaneously: children who need structure and stimulation, adults who want to talk without constant child-wrangling, and seniors who want participation without physical strain. Design explicitly for each.

**Activity design principles:**
- The first 30-45 minutes of any reunion is always unstructured -- families are arriving, setting up, greeting each other. Do not schedule a structured activity during this window. It will be ignored.
- Plan one anchor activity that everyone participates in together. Group photo is the universal anchor. Family trivia, memory-sharing circles, and talent shows work for families with that culture.
- Plan parallel tracks for different age groups during non-anchor time -- this is the key to managing multi-generational events.
- Leave 25-30% of the schedule as unstructured free time. This is not wasted time -- it is the most valuable time in the event.

**Activity options by category:**

*Young children (under 10):*
- Scavenger hunt with printed cards, age-appropriate clues, and small prizes (bubbles, stickers, sidewalk chalk)
- Sidewalk chalk station, bubble station, water table (summer)
- Beanbag toss, ring toss -- simple motor skill games with adjustable difficulty
- Craft table: decorating picture frames, painting rocks, making a family flag pennant

*Teenagers:*
- Do not program teenagers -- they will resist structured activities. Create a designated space with access to charging stations, some autonomy, and an optional role (photographer, activity helper for younger kids, social media documentation). Give them a job; do not give them mandatory fun.
- Tournament games with a bracket (cornhole, horseshoes, bocce, ladder toss) work well for teens and adults together -- competition provides structure without forcing participation.

*Adults:*
- Lawn tournament games (cornhole, bocce, horseshoes, disc golf)
- Recipe exchange -- physical cards printed in advance or digital via a shared document
- Family history display or slideshow playing in the background, not as a sit-down event

*Seniors:*
- Shaded seating area near the action so they can observe and participate in conversation without physical exertion
- Memory sharing circle (structured or informal): "Tell us about the first reunion you remember" is a powerful prompt
- Do not schedule activities that require getting up from seated position repeatedly -- design the space so seniors can participate from where they are comfortable

*Whole-group anchors:*
- Group photo: Designate a photographer (ideally one person with a real camera, not just phones), choose a backdrop, and direct the group to gather at a specific time with 5 minutes of lead time for a 50-person group and 10 minutes for a 75+ group
- Family trivia: Write 15-20 questions about family history, geography, traditions. Use team format (groups of 5-8) rather than individual -- teams create cooperation, not competition.
- Family video tribute: A 5-7 minute slideshow of family photos set to music, played on a laptop or projected. Collect photos from multiple households in advance.

### Step 6: Build the Budget Framework

Budget accuracy requires category-by-category estimation, not a top-down total. Use this structure:

**Fixed costs (do not vary with headcount):**
- Venue reservation fee
- Permits (some parks require special event permits for groups over a certain size -- 50+ is a common threshold; check with the local parks department)
- Audio equipment rental if any (Bluetooth speaker: personal/owned, or rented PA system for large outdoor events with announced activities)
- Decorations, signage

**Variable costs (scale with headcount):**
- Paper goods: budget $2.50-3.50 per person for plates (2 per person), napkins, cups (2-3 per person), utensils. Always order for 110% of headcount.
- Food if catered or purchased: $8-25 per person depending on menu
- Drink supply: $3-5 per person for a non-alcoholic event; adjust for alcohol if applicable (and check venue alcohol policies)
- Activity prizes and supplies: $0.50-2.00 per child for scavenger hunt prizes; $5-15 for group game prizes

**Per-event costs (do not scale linearly):**
- Portable restroom rental: $75-150/unit/day (1 unit per 25 people for a full-day event)
- Generator rental (if venue has no power): $75-200/day depending on size needed
- Tables and chair rental: $8-15 per folding table, $0.75-2.00 per folding chair; confirm whether venue provides these

**Contingency line:** Always budget 10-15% above the sum of all other categories. Family events reliably generate unexpected expenses -- forgotten ice, last-minute attendees, broken equipment.

**Budget contribution models:**
- Flat per-household fee: Simple, but penalizes small households
- Per-person fee: Fairest for large families, creates complexity for partial attendees
- Voluntary tiered contribution: "Suggested contribution: $20/person; any amount welcomed" -- reduces barrier for lower-income branches while allowing others to contribute more
- Hosted by one branch: That branch absorbs all cost and should be publicly thanked, not just thanked privately

### Step 7: Create the Communication Infrastructure

Communication is where most family reunion planning fails. Information gets lost in text threads, email chains fragment across branches, and late additions cause confusion.

**Communication tools ranked by effectiveness:**
- A single shared document (living document with all logistics, visible to all attendees): most reliable
- A group text or messaging app channel: good for time-sensitive day-of communication, not for detailed logistics
- Email: reliable for the initial RSVP link and official communications; not for rapid back-and-forth
- A free event management page (a basic event page with RSVP form): helpful for collecting structured RSVP data including dietary restrictions and headcount per household
- Social media event page: useful for extended family discovery, but do not rely on it as the sole communication channel -- privacy settings and algorithm changes make it unreliable

**RSVP form fields (do not omit these):**
- Household name
- Number of adults attending
- Number of children attending (with age ranges: under 5, 6-12, 13-17)
- Dietary restrictions or allergies (with severity -- is it a preference or anaphylactic?)
- Accessibility needs (wheelchair, mobility aid, shaded seating, accessible restroom proximity)
- Distance traveling from (city/state -- helps plan for travel-related timing)
- Optional: "What are you most looking forward to?" -- generates warmth and helps planners identify expectations

**Communication cadence:**
- Save-the-date: As early as possible (6 months for travel, 8 weeks for local)
- RSVP reminder: 2 weeks before the RSVP deadline, not the event date
- Logistics packet: 6-8 weeks before the event
- Final reminder with schedule: 1 week before
- Day-before message: Quick confirmation with any last-minute changes, weather forecast, and the point-of-contact phone number
- Post-event: Thank-you with photo sharing within 3-7 days

### Step 8: Assign Tasks and Establish the Committee Structure

A family reunion planned by one person is a reunion where one person is exhausted and resentful. Distribute ownership early.

**Core committee roles (each role needs one lead and ideally one helper):**
- **Overall coordinator:** Sets deadlines, runs communications, makes final decisions when consensus fails
- **Treasurer:** Collects contributions, tracks expenses, provides a final accounting after the event
- **Food lead:** Manages potluck assignments, orders purchased food, coordinates allergen accommodations
- **Activities lead:** Plans and executes the activity program, handles setup of activity supplies
- **Logistics lead:** Manages venue reservation, equipment rental, setup/teardown crew, day-of point of contact
- **Communications lead:** Drafts and sends all messages, manages the RSVP form, maintains the attendance list

**For events under 40 people:** Two or three people can cover all of these roles. Identify at least one co-planner so the lead organizer has a sounding board.

**For events over 75 people:** Each role needs its own lead, and the food lead should have 2-3 helpers.

**Day-of crew assignments (non-negotiable roles):**
- Setup crew: 4-6 people for an event of 50, arriving 90 minutes early
- Parking/greeting: 1-2 people to direct arrivals and greet late-comers
- Food setup and replenishment: 2-3 people who manage the food table throughout the event
- Child supervision support: 2-3 adults designated to run children's activities so parents can socialize
- Cleanup crew: 4-6 people who stay 30-60 minutes after the announced end time

## Rules

1. **Never conflate "headcount" with "household count."** A gathering of 50 people from 8 households requires one set of communication channels; 50 people from 25 households requires an entirely different infrastructure. Always establish both numbers and plan accordingly.

2. **Do not allow one person to plan the entire event alone.** A solo organizer creates a single point of failure for execution, a recipe for burnout, and a power dynamic that breeds resentment. Require at minimum one co-planner, and for events over 50 people, require a named committee with distinct roles before producing a detailed plan.

3. **RSVP deadlines must be set and enforced -- with buffer built into planning.** Add 10-15% to the RSVP count to account for late additions who will appear regardless of the stated deadline. Never plan food and supplies to the exact RSVP number.

4. **Collect dietary restrictions and accessibility needs in the RSVP form -- not informally, not later.** A wheelchair user who arrives to find the only restroom requires a flight of stairs, or an anaphylactic guest who encounters an unlabeled dish, represents a planning failure. These needs must be collected structurally, not remembered.

5. **The activity schedule must include explicit unstructured free time.** At minimum 25-30% of the event duration should have no scheduled programming. Over-scheduled family reunions produce compliance, not connection. Do not fill every time slot.

6. **Budget must include a contingency line of 10-15% -- no exceptions.** Family events generate surprise expenses reliably: forgotten ice, broken equipment, a bag of supplies left at home, extra guests, a ruined dish. The contingency line is not optional padding; it is operational reality.

7. **For any outdoor event, a weather contingency must be addressed explicitly.** This means a named backup plan (covered section, indoor overflow, tent rental), a stated decision-making timeline (how far in advance the call is made), and a communication method for last-minute changes to attendees.

8. **Do not recommend specific venues, caterers, vendors, or named businesses.** Provide selection criteria, category guidance, and evaluation frameworks. The planner must choose based on their local market, relationships, and context.

9. **Never let the food quantity planning rely solely on potluck contribution.** Always include a purchased backup protein and at least one purchased beverage supply. Potluck contributions are variable and sometimes do not arrive. The backup ensures no one leaves hungry.

10. **Post-event tasks are part of the plan, not afterthoughts.** The thank-you message, shared photo album, and -- if recurring -- the next-reunion announcement must be included in the task assignment table with a named responsible person and a deadline. These tasks are where reunion memory and tradition are built; they should not be left to chance.

11. **For multi-day events, always plan explicit downtime blocks.** 72 hours of continuous group programming is psychologically exhausting. Evening downtime, morning optional activities, and time for families to break off in smaller groups are not scheduling failures -- they are features.

12. **When known family conflicts or estranged members are mentioned, design the space and schedule -- do not offer to mediate.** Provide enough physical space, activity variety, and unstructured time that individuals can manage their own comfort level. A family reunion planner's job ends at logistics.

## Edge Cases

### Large-scale events (75+ attendees)
A group of 75+ fundamentally changes the execution model. One food table becomes a bottleneck -- use two parallel buffet lines with identical food to cut food service time in half. One group activity becomes unmanageable -- switch to parallel activity stations operating simultaneously. One announcer cannot be heard -- invest in a small PA system (a Bluetooth speaker with a microphone input is sufficient for outdoor groups up to 100). Committee structure becomes non-optional. Communication must go through a central document rather than a single person because no individual can track 75+ RSVPs accurately. Budget for 1 portable restroom unit per 25 people if the venue's restrooms are limited. Plan the parking logistics explicitly: 75 people arrive in approximately 25-35 vehicles; confirm the venue's parking capacity before booking.

### Families with known estrangement or significant interpersonal conflict
The planning role is logistical, not therapeutic. When a user mentions that certain family members are estranged, have a history of conflict, or have not seen each other in years under difficult circumstances: design space into the venue layout so individuals can maintain comfortable distance, ensure the activity schedule has enough variety that no one is forced into close, sustained interaction with someone they are avoiding, avoid creating seating arrangements or assigned groups without the user's input, and do not program "reconciliation" activities or prompt memory sharing that could reopen wounds. If the user asks how to handle the interpersonal dynamics directly, gently note that the planning role addresses logistics and that interpersonal dynamics are outside its scope -- a family therapist or mediator is the right resource for that layer.

### First reunion after a significant family loss (death of a patriarch, matriarch, or central family figure)
This event carries emotional weight that a standard planning framework does not account for. Acknowledge this explicitly when producing the plan. Recommend including: a brief, structured tribute moment (a moment of silence, a memory-sharing prompt, a displayed photo of the deceased), while keeping the tribute time-bounded (10-15 minutes maximum) so it does not consume the event's emotional register entirely. The event should be forward-looking -- a celebration of continued family connection -- not a extended memorial. Communication language for the save-the-date and logistics packet should be warm and affirming, not somber. Leave the tribute design to the family; suggest only that it be planned intentionally rather than improvised.

### Geographically dispersed family requiring significant travel (national/international)
When family members are traveling cross-country or internationally, the calculus changes: the event duration must justify the travel investment (a one-day event for someone flying 8 hours is difficult to justify -- a 3-day weekend is the minimum). Save-the-dates should go out 6-9 months before the event. Provide detailed travel logistics in the communications packet: nearest airports, ground transportation options, hotel block information if applicable, and the address formatted for GPS (not just a city name). Consider extending an optional pre-reunion dinner the night before for early arrivals -- this informally warms up the group before the official event. For international travelers, build jet-lag accommodation into the schedule: do not plan intensive programming in the first 3-4 hours of an event if attendees may be arriving on the morning of the event after overnight travel. A virtual attendance option (live-streamed group photo and anchor activity) is worth offering for those who cannot travel despite wanting to be present.

### Severely limited budget (under $8 per person)
A meaningful family reunion does not require money; it requires coordination. At this budget level, the plan should center on: a free or very low-cost public venue (park pavilion reservations are often $0-50 for local residents; a state or county park day pass for a group is often $1-3 per car), a pure potluck model with clearly assigned categories to ensure variety and coverage, activities using items families already own (lawn games, frisbees, sidewalk chalk, card games, footballs), and a single purchased anchor item that signals intentionality without cost (a printed family photo book from a one-hour print shop: $15-30; a custom banner: $20-35; a sheet cake: $30-50). Frame the budget constraint as a design challenge, not a deficit. The gathering is the point.

### Recurring reunion with an established but flagging tradition
When a family has held reunions for years or decades but attendance is declining, enthusiasm is waning, or the same person has been organizing it alone for too long, the plan needs a revitalization component alongside the logistics. Recommend: passing organizational responsibility to a younger generation branch of the family, introducing one new activity or format element while preserving established traditions (the annual group photo, the traditional dish, the family trivia game), asking the family directly via a pre-reunion survey what they want more and less of, and considering a format change (day event to weekend event, backyard gathering to destination camp) to renew investment. Declining attendance at recurring reunions is usually a signal of format fatigue or organizer burnout, not family disinterest.

### Multi-venue or destination reunion (reunion spanning multiple locations or requiring block travel)
Some large or geographically dispersed families choose a destination model -- everyone travels to a resort town, a national park area, or a family ancestral location, with some activities at a central venue and others distributed across local attractions. This model requires: a central coordination point (one venue where all-group activities happen), a clear distinction between "required" programming (anchor activities everyone attends) and "optional" programming (smaller group excursions, restaurant dinners, sightseeing), a shared itinerary document rather than a day-of schedule, and explicit guidance on transportation between locations. Budget for the coordination overhead -- destination reunions require significantly more pre-event communication and logistics than single-venue events. Designate a transportation coordinator as a named committee role if the group is moving between locations.
