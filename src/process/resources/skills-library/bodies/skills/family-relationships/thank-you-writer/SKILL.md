# Thank You Writer

## When to Use

**Use this skill when:**
- A user needs to write any kind of personal or professional social note and is unsure how to start, structure, or phrase it -- including thank you notes for gifts, hospitality, acts of kindness, or professional favors
- A user has a deadline pressure (interview follow-up due within 24 hours, wedding gift acknowledgment overdue, RSVP date approaching) and needs an immediate, ready-to-send draft
- A user is navigating a socially sensitive situation -- a loss, an estrangement, a difficult apology, or a relationship they want to repair through written communication
- A user wants to write something more meaningful than a generic card message and needs help finding the specific, personal details that make a note feel genuine rather than formulaic
- A user is unsure of tone calibration -- how formal or informal to be with a boss, a distant relative, a new acquaintance, or someone from a different cultural background
- A user is responding to a social obligation they have been avoiding (overdue thank you, late sympathy card, unanswered invitation) and needs both the note and the nerve to send it
- A user wants templates for recurring social correspondence -- holiday cards to a client list, annual thank you notes to donors, or party invitations

**Do NOT use when:**
- The user needs a formal business letter, a professional recommendation letter, or a cover letter -- use a professional correspondence or career writing skill instead
- The user is writing a eulogy or funeral speech -- that requires a structured public speaking and eulogy skill with different arc and delivery considerations
- The user needs legal correspondence such as demand letters, dispute letters, or formal complaints to institutions -- those require precision and framing beyond social note conventions
- The user is writing marketing copy, customer appreciation emails to mass audiences, or donor newsletters -- those are marketing and copywriting tasks, not personal social correspondence
- The user needs help navigating a deeply complex interpersonal conflict that requires therapeutic guidance -- social notes can be part of repair, but you are not the tool for processing the underlying emotional or relational work
- The request is to write on behalf of a business to a large mailing list -- this crosses into email marketing
- The user needs to write a formal complaint or grievance to a company or institution

## Process

### Step 1: Identify the Note Type and Urgency Level

- Classify the note into one of eight categories: gift thank you, interview follow-up, hospitality thank you, sympathy/condolence, congratulations, apology, invitation, RSVP response, or holiday/seasonal message
- Immediately assess timing. The following deadlines are non-negotiable conventions in social correspondence: interview follow-up must go within 24 hours (same day if afternoon interview), gift thank you within 2 weeks for everyday gifts and within 3 months for wedding gifts, hospitality thank you within 5--7 days, RSVP by the date specified (never later without a personal call), sympathy notes within 2--4 weeks but late is always better than never
- If the user is past the deadline, do not shame them -- acknowledge it briefly in the note itself with a simple "I've been meaning to write sooner" rather than over-explaining
- Determine delivery format: handwritten notes on physical stationery carry the highest emotional weight for personal occasions; email is fully acceptable for interview follow-ups and professional contexts; text is appropriate only for casual, close relationships and very brief responses

### Step 2: Gather the Minimum Required Details

Before drafting anything, collect these five core inputs:

- **Recipient name and relationship** -- the exact name used in address (Dr. Chen vs. Margaret vs. Grandma Ruth) and how closely the person is known to the user
- **The specific occasion or trigger** -- not just "a gift" but what the gift was; not just "interview" but the position title, company name, and one topic from the conversation
- **One or two personal details** -- how the gift was or will be used, a specific dish at the dinner party, a specific quality of the person being remembered in a condolence, a moment from the occasion
- **Desired tone** -- formal, warm and personal, professional, brief and efficient, or deeply heartfelt
- **Any constraints** -- word count limit (for a card with limited space), cultural sensitivities, religion, language considerations, or relationship complications (estrangement, tension)

If the user cannot provide a personal detail, prompt gently: "Can you tell me one specific thing about the gift, the event, or the person that stood out to you?" A single concrete detail transforms a generic note into a memorable one.

### Step 3: Apply the Five-Part Note Architecture

Every effective social note, regardless of type, follows a five-part structure. Compress or expand each section based on note length and occasion:

- **Opening address** -- use the person's name; "Dear" for anything formal or semi-formal; first name alone for very close relationships; never start with "I" as the first word of the note body (it reads as self-centered)
- **The direct acknowledgment** -- state the purpose in the first sentence, specifically. "Thank you for the hand-embroidered quilt" is better than "Thank you so much for everything." The word "so" before "much" often signals vagueness -- replace it with a specific noun
- **The personal detail that shows you paid attention** -- this is the sentence that separates a real note from a form letter. It references something specific: the color chosen, the memory shared, the effort made, the quality of the person. One genuine sentence here is worth more than three generic ones
- **The forward-looking or relational bridge** -- a sentence that looks ahead: when you'll use it, that you look forward to seeing them, that you're thinking of them in the coming weeks, that you hope to return the hospitality. This closes the loop and maintains the relationship
- **The closing** -- match the closing phrase to the tone. "With gratitude" for professional or formal; "Warmly" or "With love" for personal; "Best regards" for professional; "Thinking of you" for sympathy; never use "Sincerely yours" in a personal note -- it reads as cold

### Step 4: Apply Category-Specific Techniques

**Gift Thank You:**
- Name the gift specifically in sentence one -- never refer to it only as "your gift" or "the present"
- For a cash or check gift, specify what it will be used for ("I'm putting it toward my textbooks this semester" or "We're adding it to our kitchen renovation fund") -- this is the only form of accountability the giver can receive
- For a gift you don't love: never lie by calling it "perfect" or "exactly what I wanted." Instead, pivot to the giver's thoughtfulness and the relationship: "It was so kind of you to think of me" and "It was wonderful to hear from you" are genuine without being dishonest

**Interview Follow-Up:**
- Send within 24 hours; same-day is ideal for afternoon interviews
- Write a unique note to every person who interviewed you -- even if there were five people in a panel
- Reference one specific moment, question, or topic from the conversation with that specific person
- Restate your interest in the role and add one sentence that connects your experience or values to something the interviewer mentioned
- Keep it to three short paragraphs; hiring managers read quickly
- Subject line format: "Thank you -- [Job Title] Interview, [Your Full Name]" -- the full name in the subject prevents it from being lost

**Sympathy and Condolence:**
- Acknowledge the loss by the deceased person's name in the first two sentences -- this is the most important rule in condolence writing
- Share a specific memory, quality, or impact that person had. Even one sentence like "I will always remember how she laughed at her own jokes before the punchline" is more comforting than five sentences of platitudes
- Offer specific, actionable help rather than the open-ended "let me know if you need anything" -- that phrase, despite good intentions, puts the burden on the grieving person to ask. Instead: "I'll drop off dinner on Thursday unless I hear otherwise" or "I'd love to take the kids for an afternoon this week"
- Avoid all platitudes: "Everything happens for a reason," "They're in a better place," "I know how you feel," "At least they lived a long life," "God needed another angel." These minimize the loss rather than honoring it

**Apology Letters:**
- A genuine apology has five components and must have all five: (1) specific acknowledgment of what was done -- not vague ("I'm sorry for what happened") but precise; (2) ownership without conditions -- "I was wrong" not "I was wrong, but you also..."; (3) acknowledgment of the impact on the other person -- this is where you show you understand how they were hurt, not how you feel about having hurt them; (4) statement of amends or a genuine question about what repair looks like; (5) a commitment to specific changed behavior
- Never write "I'm sorry you felt that way" -- this is not an apology and recipients recognize it immediately as deflection
- Never over-apologize with excessive "I'm so so sorry, I can't believe I did this, I'm terrible" -- this shifts the emotional labor to the recipient, who must now comfort you
- Do not expect or request forgiveness in the note itself -- forgiveness is the recipient's prerogative and asking for it in the apology note puts pressure on them

### Step 5: Calibrate Tone and Register

Tone calibration is the most frequently misjudged element of social correspondence:

- **Formal register** is appropriate for: notes to employers, professors, people you've met only once or twice, older relatives from more formal generations, professional contacts in conservative fields (law, medicine, finance), thank you notes for significant professional favors
- **Warm/personal register** is appropriate for: close friends, family members, people you see regularly, notes where you want emotional warmth to come through, sympathy notes to close friends
- **Professional but approachable** is appropriate for: interview follow-ups, thank you notes to vendors or colleagues, congratulations to professional contacts, holiday cards to clients
- Signs that tone is miscalibrated: using "Hey" in a note to someone over 60 who you don't know well; using "Dear Mr. Smith" to a close friend; excessive exclamation points in a sympathy note; clinical language in a personal apology; jokes in a condolence

Cultural considerations: in Japanese-American contexts, extreme directness can feel abrupt -- add softening phrases. In British-influenced contexts, understatement reads as sophisticated; overwrought emotion can feel uncomfortable. In Latin American and Mediterranean cultural contexts, warmth and relationship language is expected even in professional notes.

### Step 6: Draft, Then Apply the Quality Test

After producing a draft, apply these five quality checks before presenting it:

- **The specificity test** -- could this note have been written by anyone, to anyone? If yes, it needs at least one more personal detail
- **The "I" test** -- does the note start with the word "I"? If so, restructure the first sentence. Does the note have more sentences about the writer's feelings than the recipient's experience? Rebalance
- **The cliche test** -- scan for: "words cannot express," "thinking outside the box" (irrelevant but a marker of auto-pilot writing), "heartfelt thanks," "means the world to me," "at a loss for words." Replace with direct, plain language
- **The length test** -- a typical handwritten thank you note should be 3--6 sentences for a casual occasion, 4--8 sentences for a meaningful one, and no more than three short paragraphs for a formal letter. Interview follow-up emails: 3 paragraphs, 150--200 words total. Sympathy notes: brief is better -- 4--6 sentences of genuine feeling outperforms two paragraphs of filler
- **The delivery format alignment test** -- does the note's length, formality, and structure match how it will be delivered? A note meant to be handwritten on a 4x6 card cannot exceed 6--8 sentences. An email can be slightly longer. A text-delivered message needs to be under 50 words

### Step 7: Provide Actionable Send Guidance

After delivering the draft, always include:

- The recommended send date (calculated from the occasion date if known)
- The recommended delivery method with the reason
- For handwritten notes: recommend cream or white stationery for formal occasions, personal notecards for casual; blue or black ink only; avoid pencil
- A note about any additional gesture that would amplify the written note (a small token with a hospitality thank you, a food delivery after a sympathy note, a LinkedIn connection request after an interview thank you)
- Any follow-up actions the user should take (calling to confirm RSVP was received, checking in after a sympathy note 2--3 weeks later when the immediate support has faded)

## Output Format

When presenting a note draft, use the following structure consistently:

```
═══════════════════════════════════════════════
NOTE DRAFT
═══════════════════════════════════════════════
Type:         [gift thank you / interview follow-up / sympathy / congratulations /
               apology / hospitality thank you / invitation / RSVP / holiday]
Recipient:    [Full name as addressed + relationship to user]
Occasion:     [Specific triggering event]
Tone:         [formal / warm-personal / professional / heartfelt / brief-casual]
Delivery:     [handwritten card / email / typed letter / text]
Send by:      [Specific date or timeframe]
Word count:   [Approximate]
───────────────────────────────────────────────

[COMPLETE NOTE TEXT -- formatted exactly as it should appear when written or sent,
including salutation, body paragraphs, and closing/signature line]

───────────────────────────────────────────────
QUALITY NOTES:
• Specificity check: [what personal detail is doing the work in this note]
• Tone check: [confirmation that tone matches recipient/occasion]
• Timing: [send by date and whether any urgency applies]
• Delivery recommendation: [format + reasoning]
• Optional additional gesture: [if applicable]
• Alternate closing options: [2 alternates if the user wants a different register]
═══════════════════════════════════════════════
```

For multiple note variants (e.g., formal vs. casual version, or three interview follow-ups to different panelists), repeat the block for each variant with a clear heading.

For templates (e.g., holiday card messages for a client list), present as a numbered list of complete, ready-to-use options organized by tone and relationship type.

## Rules

1. **Never start the note body with the word "I."** The first word of a social note signals whose perspective dominates. Notes that begin with "I want to thank you" center the writer. Restructure to center the recipient or the occasion: "Your generosity at the shower..." or "Thank you for the evening at your home..." or "The news of your promotion..."

2. **Always name the specific gift, occasion, or person.** A thank you note that refers only to "your gift" or "the lovely evening" signals that the writer didn't pay close attention. Specificity is the primary mechanism by which a note communicates genuine care. If the user cannot remember the specific gift, ask them to look it up before drafting.

3. **Do not draft a sympathy note that contains any of these phrases:** "Everything happens for a reason," "They're in a better place," "I know exactly how you feel," "God needed another angel," "At least they had a long life," "Stay strong," or "Let me know if you need anything." These phrases, despite being well-intentioned, have been identified repeatedly in grief research as unhelpful or even harmful. Replace them with direct acknowledgment and specific offers.

4. **Interview follow-up emails must be sent within 24 hours.** If the user tells you they interviewed two or more days ago, note this but still draft the note -- a late thank you is meaningfully better than none. Do not suggest skipping it. The note should not apologize for or mention the delay.

5. **Cash and check gifts require specific use-of-funds language.** When someone gives money as a gift, they have no way of seeing how it was received or used. A sentence like "I'm putting it toward my first car" or "We're adding it to our vacation fund" provides the only form of feedback a monetary gift-giver receives.

6. **Apology notes must never contain the word "but" after the acknowledgment of wrongdoing.** The construction "I was wrong to say that, but I was frustrated" is a conditional apology and invalidates everything before the "but." If context matters, it can be addressed separately and later -- not in the same sentence as the apology.

7. **Match the closing phrase to the relationship and tone, and never use "Sincerely" in a personal note.** "Sincerely" belongs on business correspondence and formal letters to institutions. In a personal note, it reads as cold and distancing. The closing is the emotional handshake at the end of the note -- choose it with care.

8. **Handwritten notes for personal occasions always outperform typed or digital ones when the user has the option.** When a user asks whether to handwrite or email a personal thank you, recommend handwriting unless there is a genuine barrier (geographic urgency, disability, illegible handwriting). The physical effort of a handwritten note is itself a form of message.

9. **A note written late is always better than a note not written.** Never advise a user to skip writing a thank you, sympathy note, or apology because they've passed the conventional window. Acknowledge the lateness briefly and without excessive self-flagellation: "I've been meaning to write for weeks and I'm sorry I'm only doing so now" is sufficient. Then move into the substance of the note.

10. **When the user has a complicated relationship with the recipient, do not ignore the complexity.** If a user says "I need to thank my estranged mother for a gift she sent" or "I want to apologize to a friend I had a falling out with two years ago," the note must be carefully calibrated to acknowledge what's true without over-promising or re-opening wounds carelessly. Ask the user what outcome they hope the note will produce, and draft accordingly.

## Edge Cases

### The Gift You Don't Like or Can't Use
The user must not lie in the note -- calling something "perfect" or "exactly what I needed" when it isn't will feel hollow and sets up a false premise. Instead, focus entirely on the giver's relationship, thoughtfulness, and effort. Pivot immediately to a memory or feeling: "It was so like you to think of me" or "I was touched that you remembered my birthday from so far away." If the gift is something genuinely unusable (the wrong size, a duplicate), the user does not need to mention this at all. The note honors the intention, not the object.

### The Deeply Overdue Thank You
When a user is writing a thank you note six months after a wedding gift, or a year after a funeral, the impulse to apologize profusely can hijack the entire note. The correct approach is a single, brief acknowledgment of lateness -- one sentence -- followed by a genuine, full-length note. "I'm embarrassed that it's taken me this long to write, but I didn't want another day to pass without telling you..." The lateness sentence should never exceed one sentence. After that, the note proceeds as normal. Do not make the recipient manage the writer's guilt about being late.

### Loss of a Child or Pregnancy Loss
These notes require exceptional care. Avoid all phrases that minimize: "You're young, you can try again," "At least it was early," "This was God's plan," "You have other children." The loss of a child at any stage is a full and devastating loss. Keep the note brief and direct. Acknowledge the specific person or pregnancy loss by name if a name was given. Express sorrow without explanation. Offer a specific, practical form of help. A short, honest note is far better than a long note straining for comfort it cannot provide.

### Panel Interview with Multiple Interviewers
Write a unique note to every person who interviewed the user -- even if there were six people. The notes share a structure but must each contain a unique, specific reference to that person's questions, comments, or area of focus in the interview. Sending identical notes to multiple people in the same organization is a significant professional misstep; hiring managers sometimes share these notes. If the user doesn't remember individual details for each person, help them reconstruct what they can recall, and differentiate based on role or the general area of conversation.

### The Apology When the User Believes They Are Also Wronged
This is the most common apology writing challenge. The user wants to apologize for their part while also communicating that they were hurt. A combined apology-and-grievance letter almost always backfires -- the recipient experiences it as a non-apology or as an attack disguised as an apology. The correct approach: write the apology note first, separately, without conditions. Address the user's own hurt separately, in a different conversation or letter, after the apology has been received. If the user insists on combining them, at minimum ensure the apology comes first and occupies at least 75% of the note, with no "but" constructing a bridge between apology and grievance.

### Cultural and Religious Context in Sympathy Notes
When the user knows the recipient practices a specific faith, the note can reference that faith if done carefully and authentically -- but only if the user has genuine knowledge of those beliefs and relationship with the recipient's practice. If uncertain, write a secular note that honors the person and the loss without religious framing. It is far better to write a sincere, non-religious note than to reference a faith tradition incorrectly or insincerely. For Jewish mourners, acknowledging "sitting shiva" and the structured nature of Jewish mourning shows awareness and care. For Muslim recipients, "Inna lillahi wa inna ilayhi raji'un" (To God we belong and to God we return) is deeply meaningful when used appropriately. Research before including religious content.

### RSVP for an Event the User Cannot Attend
The RSVP decline note is frequently over-explained or under-explained. The correct approach: decline promptly, thank for the invitation genuinely, express regret briefly, wish the occasion well, and stop there. The user does not owe a detailed explanation for why they cannot attend -- in fact, lengthy explanations can read as insincere or as an attempt to avoid guilt. "I'm so sorry I won't be able to make it -- I'll be out of town that weekend" is complete. "I'm so sorry, I have this other thing but it's complicated and I really wish I could come but it's just not possible this time" reads as distancing. If the user wants to maintain the relationship, add one brief forward-looking line: "I'd love to get together soon and celebrate separately."

### Holiday Cards During a Year of Loss or Difficulty
When a user has experienced a significant loss, illness, or hardship during the year and must now write holiday cards that traditionally carry a year-in-review tone, the note must be recalibrated. Pretending the year was fine when it wasn't reads as hollow; over-sharing grief in a holiday card can burden recipients. The correct balance: acknowledge that the year has been hard without detailing it, express genuine gratitude for the relationship and its support, and look forward briefly. "This has been one of the harder years of my life, and your friendship has meant more than I can say" is honest without being heavy. For cards to recipients who were themselves part of the user's support, a more specific acknowledgment of their specific help is appropriate and deeply appreciated.

## Example

**User Input:** "I need to write a thank you note to my girlfriend's parents. We stayed at their house for Thanksgiving -- four days. Her mom cooked for what felt like 24 hours straight and her dad gave me a really thorough tour of his garden, which he clearly loves. I want them to like me. The note should feel warm but not try-hard. Handwritten card. Her name is Maya and her parents are Diane and Robert Chen."

---

```
═══════════════════════════════════════════════
NOTE DRAFT
═══════════════════════════════════════════════
Type:         Hospitality thank you
Recipient:    Diane and Robert Chen -- girlfriend's parents (first meeting or
              early-relationship context)
Occasion:     Four-day Thanksgiving stay at their home
Tone:         Warm, genuine, slightly formal -- not overly familiar, not stiff
Delivery:     Handwritten card
Send by:      Within 5 days of returning home (no later than one week
              after departure date)
Word count:   Approximately 80--100 words (fits a standard 4x6 notecard)
───────────────────────────────────────────────

Dear Diane and Robert,

Thank you for welcoming me into your home for Thanksgiving. Diane, the meal
you put together was extraordinary -- I'm still thinking about that stuffing --
and I want you to know I noticed how much went into it. Robert, the tour of
your garden was a real highlight for me. The way you talked about what you're
planning for spring made me want to come back and see it in bloom.

I'm grateful for your hospitality and your warmth. I hope I'll have the
chance to return it one day.

With gratitude,
[User's name]

───────────────────────────────────────────────
QUALITY NOTES:
• Specificity check: The stuffing reference and the spring garden detail show
  genuine attention -- these are the sentences that will matter most to Diane
  and Robert respectively. Each parent gets their own acknowledged moment,
  which is important when addressing a couple.

• Tone check: "Warm but not try-hard" is achieved by avoiding superlatives
  ("the BEST Thanksgiving ever!") and over-the-top declarations ("you're the
  most wonderful hosts I've ever had"). The note is direct, specific, and
  slightly formal in a way appropriate for a first or early-relationship visit.
  The closing "With gratitude" is warm without being presumptuous.

• Timing: Send within 5 days of returning home. If the user is back on Monday,
  the card should be in the mail by Thursday at the latest. A card that arrives
  the week after Thanksgiving makes a strong impression.

• Delivery recommendation: Handwritten is the correct choice here. This is
  exactly the kind of note that a girlfriend's parents will notice, comment on,
  and potentially display or keep. The effort is visible and appropriate.

• Optional additional gesture: If the user wants to add a small token, a jar
  of local honey, a small plant cutting (given Robert's gardening), or a bottle
  of wine left behind are all tasteful. The note alone is sufficient and strong.

• Alternate closing options:
  - "Warmly, [Name]" -- slightly more casual, appropriate if the visit felt
    genuinely warm and relaxed
  - "Looking forward to seeing you again, [Name]" -- forward-looking, expresses
    interest in the relationship, appropriate if the visit went very well and a
    return visit is likely
═══════════════════════════════════════════════
```

**Why this note works:**

The note addresses both parents in the salutation (never address only one member of a couple when both hosted), then pivots to name each parent individually within the body -- Diane for her cooking effort and Robert for his garden tour. This structure ensures neither parent feels like a footnote. The specific mention of "the stuffing" (not just "the food" or "the meal") and "what you're planning for spring" (not just "your garden") signals that the writer was genuinely present and attentive. The closing offer to "return it one day" is relationship-building without being presumptuous. The note avoids the common pitfall of trying too hard to impress with elaborate language -- it reads like a person, not a performance.
