## Questions to Ask First

1. **What country are you coming from?** (Processes vary by nationality)
2. **What is your current immigration status?** (Outside US, tourist visa, student visa, undocumented, etc.)
3. **What is your goal?** (Temporary work, permanent residency, citizenship, asylum, family reunification)
4. **Do you have family members who are US citizens or permanent residents?**
5. **Do you have a job offer or employer sponsor?**
6. **What is your educational background and profession?**
7. **What is your timeline?** (Urgent vs. planning ahead)
8. **Have you ever been denied a visa or had immigration issues?**
9. **Do you have an immigration attorney?**
10. **Do you have dependents who will be included in your case?**

## Visa Types Overview

### Non-Immigrant (Temporary) Visas

```
WORK VISAS:
  H-1B:  Specialty occupation (requires bachelor's degree or equivalent)
         - Employer-sponsored, cap-subject (annual lottery) or cap-exempt
         - 3-year initial period, extendable to 6 years
         - Dual intent (can pursue green card while on H-1B)

  L-1:   Intracompany transferee (manager/executive or specialized knowledge)
         - Must have worked abroad for the company 1+ year in last 3 years
         - L-1A (managers): up to 7 years
         - L-1B (specialized knowledge): up to 5 years

  O-1:   Extraordinary ability in sciences, arts, education, business, athletics
         - No annual cap, but high evidentiary standard
         - Requires sustained national/international acclaim

  TN:    USMCA (formerly NAFTA) professionals
         - Canadian and Mexican citizens only
         - Specific qualifying professions listed in treaty
         - Relatively straightforward for qualifying professions

  E-2:   Treaty investor visa
         - Must be citizen of treaty country
         - Substantial investment in US business
         - Renewable indefinitely but not a path to green card by itself

  H-2B:  Temporary non-agricultural workers
  J-1:   Exchange visitors (researchers, interns, au pairs)
  R-1:   Religious workers

STUDENT VISAS:
  F-1:   Academic students
         - Must maintain full-time enrollment
         - Limited work authorization (CPT during, OPT after graduation)
         - STEM OPT extension: 24 additional months for qualifying fields

  M-1:   Vocational students
  J-1:   Exchange visitor programs

FAMILY/VISITOR:
  B-1/B-2: Business/tourism visitor (6 months max, no work authorized)
  K-1:     Fiancé(e) of US citizen (must marry within 90 days of entry)

HUMANITARIAN:
  U:     Victims of certain crimes who cooperate with law enforcement
  T:     Victims of human trafficking
  TPS:   Temporary Protected Status (designated countries)
  Asylum: Persecution-based protection (apply within 1 year of arrival)
```

### Immigrant (Permanent) Visas — Green Card Pathways

```
FAMILY-BASED:
  Immediate relatives of US citizens (no quota):
    - Spouse
    - Unmarried children under 21
    - Parents (if petitioner is 21+)

  Family preference categories (annual quotas, often long waits):
    F1: Unmarried adult children of US citizens
    F2A: Spouse and minor children of permanent residents
    F2B: Unmarried adult children of permanent residents
    F3: Married adult children of US citizens
    F4: Siblings of US citizens

EMPLOYMENT-BASED:
  EB-1: Priority workers (extraordinary ability, outstanding professors, multinational managers)
  EB-2: Advanced degree professionals or exceptional ability
        - Includes NIW (National Interest Waiver) — no employer sponsor needed
  EB-3: Skilled workers, professionals, other workers
  EB-4: Special immigrants (religious workers, certain government employees)
  EB-5: Investor visa ($800,000-$1,050,000 investment creating 10 US jobs)

DIVERSITY VISA LOTTERY:
  - 55,000 visas annually to underrepresented countries
  - Random selection; free to enter
  - Registration period: usually October-November each year
  - Must meet education or work experience requirements

SPECIAL PROGRAMS:
  - VAWA (Violence Against Women Act) self-petition
  - DACA (Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals) — not a visa, deferred enforcement
  - Refugee resettlement (applied for from outside the US)
  - Asylum (applied for from within the US or at a port of entry)
```

## Application Process General Framework

### Steps for Most Immigration Processes
```
1. DETERMINE ELIGIBILITY
   - Which visa/status category fits your situation?
   - Do you meet all requirements?
   - Are there any bars to eligibility? (prior violations, criminal history)
   - Consult an immigration attorney for assessment

2. GATHER DOCUMENTATION
   - Personal documents (passport, birth certificate, etc.)
   - Supporting evidence specific to your category
   - Translations of non-English documents (certified)
   - Financial evidence (if required)
   - Medical examinations (if required)

3. FILE PETITION/APPLICATION
   - Correct forms with USCIS, Department of State, or DOL
   - Filing fees (vary widely; fee waivers available in some cases)
   - Supporting evidence packet
   - Keep copies of EVERYTHING you submit

4. WAIT AND TRACK
   - Processing times vary dramatically (weeks to years)
   - Check USCIS case status online
   - Respond promptly to any Requests for Evidence (RFE)
   - Do not miss deadlines

5. INTERVIEW (if required)
   - USCIS office (adjustment of status) or US Embassy/Consulate (consular processing)
   - Bring all original documents
   - Be honest and consistent with your application

6. DECISION
   - Approved: Receive visa stamp or status change
   - Denied: Understand reasons, appeal options, and next steps
   - Additional evidence requested: Respond within deadline
```

## Settlement Planning

### Before You Arrive
```
ESSENTIALS TO ARRANGE:
  [ ] Housing (temporary at minimum; research neighborhoods)
  [ ] Health insurance (employer-provided, marketplace, or private)
  [ ] Bank account (some banks allow remote opening; bring documents)
  [ ] Phone plan (unlocked phone + local SIM or US carrier plan)
  [ ] Transportation plan (public transit, car, ride-sharing)
  [ ] School enrollment for children (contact local school district)
  [ ] Important documents organized and accessible

FINANCIAL PREPARATION:
  [ ] Understand US currency and banking system
  [ ] Research cost of living in your destination city
  [ ] Bring sufficient funds for first 2-3 months
  [ ] Understand how to build US credit history
    - Secured credit card
    - Credit builder accounts
    - Some banks consider international credit history

WHAT TO BRING:
  [ ] All original immigration documents plus copies
  [ ] Academic transcripts and professional credentials (originals)
  [ ] Medical records and vaccination history
  [ ] Prescription medications (with doctor's letter)
  [ ] Driver's license (international driving permit may help initially)
  [ ] Insurance documents
  [ ] Emergency cash (USD)
```

## Cultural Adjustment

### Understanding Culture Shock
```
STAGES OF CULTURAL ADJUSTMENT:

  1. HONEYMOON (first weeks-months):
     Everything is exciting and new. You notice differences with curiosity.
     Energy is high.

  2. FRUSTRATION (months 2-6):
     Daily challenges accumulate. Language barriers feel exhausting.
     Homesickness intensifies. "Why can't things work like at home?"
     This is normal and temporary.

  3. ADJUSTMENT (months 6-12):
     You develop coping strategies. Things become more familiar.
     You start to feel competent navigating daily life.
     You find your community.

  4. ADAPTATION (year 1+):
     You develop a bicultural identity. You can navigate both
     cultures. You find comfort while maintaining your heritage.

IMPORTANT: Culture shock is not weakness. It is a natural
psychological response to a major life change. Be patient
with yourself and seek support when you need it.
```

### Common Cultural Differences (US-Specific)
```
WORKPLACE:
  - Direct communication is generally valued (not seen as rude)
  - Punctuality is important (arriving 5 minutes late is noticed)
  - "How are you?" is a greeting, not a genuine question (answer: "Good, thanks!")
  - Small talk before business is common but brief
  - Personal space is larger than in many cultures (~arm's length)
  - Tipping culture: 15-20% at restaurants, other services vary

DAILY LIFE:
  - Units of measurement: Fahrenheit, miles, pounds, gallons
  - Date format: Month/Day/Year (not Day/Month/Year)
  - Driving on the right side of the road
  - Tax is added at the register (not included in displayed prices)
  - Healthcare system requires proactive navigation (insurance, networks, referrals)

SOCIAL:
  - Making friends as an adult requires intentional effort everywhere
  - Neighbors may be friendly but not necessarily close
  - Joining groups, clubs, or religious communities helps build connections
  - Americans may say "we should get together sometime" without concrete plans
    (this is politeness, not a commitment — if you want to connect, suggest a specific time)
```

## Legal Requirements and Rights

### Maintaining Legal Status
```
CRITICAL OBLIGATIONS:
  - Always carry proof of immigration status
  - Report address changes to USCIS within 10 days (AR-11 form)
  - Maintain the conditions of your visa (employment, enrollment, etc.)
  - Do not overstay your authorized period
  - File taxes (most visa holders are required to file)
  - Register for Selective Service if male, age 18-25 (required for most non-citizens)
  - Renew your visa/status before it expires
  - Keep ALL immigration documents safe (make copies, store securely)

GREEN CARD HOLDERS ADDITIONALLY:
  - Carry your green card at all times
  - Do not abandon residency (extended trips abroad may be seen as abandonment)
  - Report address changes within 10 days
  - File US taxes on worldwide income annually
  - Apply to renew green card before expiration (Form I-90)
```

### Know Your Rights
```
ALL PEOPLE IN THE US, REGARDLESS OF STATUS, HAVE:
  - Right to remain silent (5th Amendment)
  - Right to refuse consent to a search
  - Right to an attorney in criminal proceedings
  - Right to emergency medical care
  - Right to K-12 public education for children
  - Protection from discrimination based on national origin
  - Right to due process before deportation
  - Right to report crimes without immigration consequences (in many jurisdictions)

IF ENCOUNTERED BY IMMIGRATION ENFORCEMENT:
  - You have the right to remain silent
  - You do not have to sign any documents
  - You can ask if you are free to leave
  - Memorize the phone number of an immigration attorney
  - Carry a know-your-rights card
  - Do not provide false documents
```

### Path to Citizenship
```
NATURALIZATION REQUIREMENTS (general):
  - Be at least 18 years old
  - Be a permanent resident for 5 years (3 if married to a US citizen)
  - Continuous residence and physical presence requirements
  - Good moral character
  - Pass English language test (reading, writing, speaking)
  - Pass US civics test (100 possible questions, must answer 6/10 correctly)
  - Take the Oath of Allegiance

PREPARATION:
  - Study materials available free at uscis.gov/citizenship
  - Practice tests and flashcards available
  - Citizenship classes often available through community organizations
  - Accommodations available for age and disability (language exemptions for 50/20 and 55/15 rules)
```

## Quick Reference

When providing guidance, apply these principles:
- **"Where do I start?"** → Determine your category, then consult an attorney. Start with USCIS.gov for general information.
- **"How long will this take?"** → Check current processing times at egov.uscis.gov/processing-times
- **"I can't afford a lawyer"** → Legal aid organizations, law school immigration clinics, DOJ-accredited representatives, CLINIC network
- **"My case was denied"** → Do NOT panic. Review denial reasons, consult attorney immediately, understand appeal options and deadlines
- **"I feel overwhelmed"** → This is one of the hardest things a person can do. Take it one step at a time. Find community. You are not alone.

Immigration is a marathon, not a sprint. Be patient with the process and with yourself. Millions of people have walked this path before you, and there are people and resources ready to help.
