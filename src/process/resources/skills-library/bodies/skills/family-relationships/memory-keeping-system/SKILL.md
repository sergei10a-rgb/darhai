# Memory Keeping System

## When to Use

**Use this skill when:**
- A user wants to organize a backlog of digital photos (typically 1,000+ images across phones, old computers, or cloud services) and does not know where to begin
- A user wants to create an ongoing family memory system from scratch -- including photo management, physical keepsakes, and documentation -- and needs a complete, integrated plan
- A user is preparing for a major life transition (new baby, child starting school, family move, divorce, death of a grandparent) and wants to capture and preserve memories more intentionally
- A user wants to set up a journaling or family documentation practice that is realistic for their schedule and personality type
- A user has boxes of physical children's artwork, schoolwork, or inherited family items and needs a framework for what to keep, what to archive digitally, and what to let go
- A user wants to create a printed memory product -- photo books, scrapbooks, or family history documents -- and needs help designing the curation and workflow process that feeds into it
- A user wants to ensure long-term digital preservation, including backup redundancy, file format longevity, and migration strategies for aging media

**Do NOT use when:**
- The user wants photography technique guidance -- camera settings, lighting, composition, or post-processing (use photography or design-creative skills)
- The user wants to create social media content from family photos -- Instagram curation, sharing strategies, or audience-building (this is content creation, not memory preservation)
- The user wants professional photo restoration or digitization of very old or damaged prints (this requires specialist tools and expertise beyond this skill's scope)
- The user wants to build a family tree or genealogical record (ancestry research is a distinct domain with its own research methodology)
- The user is a professional photographer organizing a client archive (use business or workflow productivity skills)
- The user wants to create a memorial tribute or grief support content for a recent loss (use grief support or memorial planning skills, which carry different emotional weight and pacing)

## Process

### Step 1: Conduct a Memory Inventory Assessment

Before recommending any system, understand the user's complete current state across four dimensions:

- **Digital volume:** How many photos and videos exist, and where are they? Common sources include iPhone camera roll, Android gallery, Google Photos, iCloud, Dropbox, old Facebook albums, a desktop photo management application, external hard drives, and USB sticks. Users almost always underestimate their total. Ask them to check storage settings on their phone for an actual count.
- **Physical volume:** How many boxes, bins, or piles of physical items exist? Distinguish between children's artwork and schoolwork, printed photos (which may also need digitizing), inherited family items, and sentimental objects. A rough count like "3 banker's boxes" or "one shelf of binders" is enough.
- **Documentation state:** Is there any existing journaling, baby book, diary, or notes app habit? What has been tried and abandoned? Understanding failure points is as important as understanding what exists.
- **Goal clarification:** Separate "organizing what already exists" from "creating an ongoing system" from "producing a specific output like a photo book." These require very different effort estimates and approaches. Many users want all three but can only realistically start one.
- **Available time:** Distinguish between a one-time dedicated project (a weekend or a month of sessions) and an ongoing maintenance commitment (15 minutes per week). Most users overestimate how much time they will actually spend. Use the realistic minimum as the baseline.
- **Emotional state of the backlog:** If the photos or physical items are tied to grief, family conflict, or a traumatic period, note this -- the pacing and approach must be gentler, and the decision frameworks need to account for emotional fatigue.

### Step 2: Design the Digital Photo Management Architecture

The photo management system has three components: structure, curation, and backup. All three must be designed together.

- **Folder naming convention:** Use the format `YYYY/YYYY-MM-Description` at minimum. For example: `2024/2024-06-Beach-Trip` or `2024/2024-12-Christmas`. Always lead with year, then month (zero-padded so sorting works correctly), then a human-readable label. Never use names like "Summer Fun" without a year prefix -- orphan folders become unsortable within three years.
- **Curation ratio:** The working standard for family photos is 5--15 keepers per event or outing. A one-week vacation should yield 50--80 keepers, not 800. A birthday party should yield 15--25 keepers. The curation step is not optional -- an uncurated archive is not a memory system, it is a data dump that nobody ever browses.
- **Curation pass sequence:** Do three passes. First pass: delete everything that is technically unusable (blurry, eyes closed, accidental shots, screenshots of information). Second pass: eliminate duplicates and near-duplicates (keep the single best version of any repeated composition). Third pass: reduce what remains to your target keeper count by selecting for emotional resonance, not just technical quality.
- **File format and naming:** Leave original camera file names intact inside organized folders -- do not rename individual files. The folder structure provides the context. If photos are exported from a phone, ensure EXIF date metadata is preserved; it is the foundation of any date-sorted system.
- **Backup architecture:** Follow the 3-2-1 rule adapted for families: 3 copies total, on 2 different media types, with 1 offsite. In practice: (1) primary working copy on a computer or NAS drive, (2) local external hard drive updated quarterly, (3) cloud storage with auto-sync. Cloud storage alone is not a backup -- it mirrors deletions. An external drive alone is not a backup -- it can be lost in a house fire or flood.
- **Shared family album:** Designate one shared location -- this can be a shared cloud album or a shared network folder -- where all family members move their best curated photos. Establish a clear rule: curated photos only, best 5--10 from any given outing. A shared dump of everything from every phone is not a shared album, it is a collective data problem.
- **Video files:** Treat video separately from photos. Video files are large and rarely need to be curated to the same granularity. Keep all video files that are over 30 seconds and capture something genuinely unrepeatable. Create a `/Video` subfolder within each year. Do not mix video and stills in the same folder.

### Step 3: Design the Physical Keepsake Curation System

Physical memory items require a decision framework that distinguishes between emotional value, historical value, and display value -- and acknowledges that space is finite.

- **The three-category sort:** Every physical item gets sorted into Keep Physical, Photograph and Release, or Let Go. "Keep Physical" is reserved for items with tactile significance that cannot be replicated digitally -- a child's handprint casting, a beloved stuffed animal, a great-grandmother's handwritten recipe card. "Photograph and Release" covers most artwork, certificates, and objects where the visual record preserves the memory adequately. "Let Go" is for damaged items, duplicates, and things that carry no clear memory connection.
- **Per-child container discipline:** One standard archive box (roughly 12" x 10" x 4" -- commonly sold as a photo storage box) per child per school year. When the container is full, new items come in only when old items are moved to Photograph and Release. This creates automatic curation pressure without requiring constant decision-making.
- **Archival materials:** Paper items -- artwork, documents, schoolwork -- should be stored in acid-free folders inside archival boxes. Regular cardboard boxes off-gas acids that yellow and degrade paper over 10--15 years. Acid-free boxes and sleeves extend paper life to 100+ years under normal storage conditions. This matters most for items intended to be passed to adult children or grandchildren.
- **Storage location:** Never store paper-based keepsakes in an attic (temperature swings cause brittle paper and mold) or an unfinished basement (humidity and flooding risk). A climate-controlled interior closet or dedicated storage room is the correct location. Plastic bins with tight lids protect against moisture better than cardboard boxes for long-term storage.
- **Artwork digitization method:** When photographing children's artwork for Photograph and Release, lay the piece flat on a consistent light-colored surface (white foam board is ideal). Photograph straight down from directly above. Shoot in natural daylight or under bright even artificial light -- avoid shadows. Capture one photo per piece. Organize these photos in a subfolder: `Artwork/[Child Name]/[Year]`. This creates a browsable digital portfolio of everything they made, without keeping physical copies of 300 drawings.
- **Inherited and sentimental items from older family members:** These require a separate handling protocol. Never make irreversible decisions about inherited items within 12 months of a loss. Photograph everything before sorting anything. Identify items that should be distributed to other family members before deciding what to release -- an item you might discard could be deeply meaningful to a sibling or cousin. Create a "pending" category for items you cannot yet decide on.

### Step 4: Design the Family Documentation System

Documentation is the narrative layer of memory keeping -- it captures the context, voices, and feelings that photos cannot. The design must be matched to the user's actual personality and schedule.

- **Choose the correct format for the user's personality:** There are four realistic options. (1) Physical journal: high tactile satisfaction, requires dedicated time and space, difficult to search, beautiful as an artifact. Best for users who already have a journaling habit. (2) Notes app or digital document: low friction, always accessible, searchable, easy to back up. Best for users who are phone-native and do not want a separate habit. (3) Voice memos: near-zero friction, captures natural speech and emotion, requires transcription if text record is desired. Best for users who hate writing. (4) Structured prompts in a dedicated app: provides scaffolding, reduces blank-page paralysis, gamifies consistency. Best for beginners who need guidance on what to say.
- **Frequency calibration:** Daily journaling fails for most parents of young children and should not be recommended as a starting point. Weekly micro-entries (3--5 sentences answering 2--3 specific prompts) are sustainable for most people. Monthly summaries work well for users who travel frequently or have irregular schedules. Milestone-only documentation (birthdays, school transitions, holidays) is the minimum viable approach for users with very little time.
- **Prompt design principles:** Prompts must be specific enough to be answerable in 2--3 sentences without thinking too hard. "What is something funny one of your kids said this week?" is answerable. "What is life like right now?" is not. The best prompts capture: (a) something that will not be remembered otherwise, (b) the child's voice or personality directly, (c) an ordinary moment that represents a phase of life, or (d) the parent's emotional experience of the moment, not just the facts.
- **Quote capture practice:** Create a dedicated note or section specifically for recording things children (and other family members) say verbatim. These become some of the most valued records over time. The format is simple: date, who said it, and the exact quote. No additional commentary needed.
- **Annual narrative document:** Once per year, write or dictate a 1--2 page summary of the year for each child: what they were into, what they were afraid of, what made them laugh, what they were learning, who their friends were, what the family struggled with, and what the family celebrated. This is the document that adult children read as their own history. It is worth more than 10,000 sorted photos.

### Step 5: Build the Backlog Remediation Plan

For users with existing backlogs, design a phased catch-up strategy that does not require heroic effort.

- **Start the new system first:** Before touching a single old photo, establish the going-forward system. New memories are now organized correctly. The backlog becomes a background project.
- **Batch sizes for digital photo backlogs:** A sustainable single session is 30--45 minutes and covers 200--500 raw photos (producing roughly 20--50 keepers). Schedule no more than two sessions per week, or the task becomes aversive. A 10,000-photo backlog sorted at this pace takes approximately 10--15 sessions, or 6--8 weeks of twice-weekly effort.
- **Year-first sorting:** When tackling a large backlog, sort by year before sorting by event. Create rough year folders and drag everything into the correct year by date. This alone reduces cognitive load dramatically. Then work through each year event by event.
- **The 3-second rule for curation decisions:** If you look at a photo and do not immediately recognize what it is or why it matters, it is a candidate for deletion. You do not need to remember everything -- you need to remember the things that matter. Agonizing over an individual photo for more than 10 seconds means the decision is probably either keep or irrelevant. Move on.
- **Physical backlog sessions:** Physical keepsake sorting is more emotionally taxing than digital sorting. Limit physical sessions to 45--60 minutes maximum. Have a camera ready to photograph items before releasing them. Set a specific box or container as the destination for each category so decisions are made once, not revisited.

### Step 6: Design the Ongoing Maintenance Rhythm

A memory system that requires extraordinary effort to maintain will be abandoned. The ongoing rhythm must fit inside the user's real life.

- **Weekly tasks (10--15 minutes total):** Curation pass on the week's phone photos -- delete junk, select best 5--10, move to shared album. If journaling weekly, write this week's entry using 2--3 prompts.
- **Monthly tasks (20--30 minutes total):** Download photos from cloud to organized folder structure. Write or record a monthly family summary if not journaling weekly. Review the physical keepsake intake area and sort anything that has accumulated.
- **Quarterly tasks (1--2 hours):** Update the local external hard drive backup. Review the shared family album and delete any uncurated photos that were added. Assess whether the system is still working -- is anything accumulating that should not be?
- **Annual tasks (3--5 hours across a few sessions):** Curate the previous year's photos down to a final selection. Order or create a printed photo book of the year. Write or compile the annual narrative document for each child. Review and update keepsake bins. Share a year-in-review with extended family.
- **Trigger-based tasks (as needed):** After any major event (vacation, birthday, holiday), do the curation pass within 48 hours while memory is fresh. After the death of a family member, begin the inherited items photography protocol before any sorting decisions.

### Step 7: Design the Long-Term Preservation Strategy

Memory keeping is not just about the next year -- it is about materials that remain accessible and meaningful in 20--50 years.

- **File format longevity:** JPEG remains the safest format for long-term photo storage due to universal readability. HEIC (the default iPhone format) should be converted to JPEG for archival copies because format support is not guaranteed across decades and systems. Video in MP4 (H.264) is broadly supported; proprietary formats like MOV should be converted for archival copies. RAW files can be kept alongside JPEGs for users who shoot with a dedicated camera, but should not replace JPEG as the primary archive format.
- **Migration planning:** No digital storage medium lasts forever. CDs and DVDs degrade in 10--25 years. External hard drives have a typical lifespan of 3--5 years with regular use, 7--10 years in storage. Flash drives (USB sticks) are unreliable for long-term storage. Cloud services change, merge, and occasionally shut down. Plan to migrate the archive to new media every 5 years, regardless of apparent media health.
- **Printed materials as insurance:** A printed photo book is immune to format obsolescence, hard drive failure, and cloud service shutdown. Printing 30--50 photos annually in a quality photo book creates a physical record that will survive the technological changes of the next 50 years. This is not redundant to digital -- it is a categorically different form of preservation.
- **Metadata preservation:** EXIF data embedded in photo files records the date, time, and (if location services are enabled) the place of capture. This metadata must be preserved during any file transfer or cloud download. Verify that bulk export tools from cloud services preserve EXIF data before relying on them for archival export.

## Rules

1. **Never design a system that exceeds the user's stated available time by more than 50%.** If a user says they have 15 minutes per week, every recurring task combined must take 20 minutes maximum. A system that requires 2 hours per week from a user who has 15 minutes is not ambitious -- it is abandoned within 30 days.

2. **The curation step is mandatory and non-negotiable.** A system that keeps all photos is not a memory system -- it is digital hoarding. An uncurated 50,000-photo library produces the same browsing experience as a locked box: people know the memories are in there somewhere but never actually access them. Always include specific curation ratios (photos per event) in any plan.

3. **Always prescribe the 3-2-1 backup rule adapted for families.** One copy is not a backup. Two copies on the same hard drive in the same building is not a backup. The minimum acceptable backup architecture is: primary cloud storage (continuous sync) + local external drive (updated quarterly) + a second cloud service or offsite location. Address backup explicitly in every plan.

4. **Physical keepsake plans must include a hard container size limit per child per year.** "Keep what feels important" without a physical constraint leads to 40 boxes of art projects in a garage nobody opens. The container size creates productive curation pressure. One standard photo storage box (12" x 10" x 4") per child per year is a proven workable constraint.

5. **Archival materials must be specified for paper-based keepsakes.** Regular cardboard boxes are not archival. Standard storage bins trap moisture. Items intended to be preserved for 20+ years -- especially children's artwork, documents, and prints -- require acid-free folders and archival boxes stored in a climate-controlled space. This distinction matters and must be stated explicitly.

6. **Never recommend daily journaling as a starting point.** Daily journaling fails for 85%+ of people who attempt it, particularly parents of young children. Weekly micro-entries using specific prompts are the minimum viable starting frequency. Build to daily only if the user has demonstrated an existing daily habit in another domain.

7. **Journaling prompts must be specific enough to answer in under 2 minutes without preparation.** "What was your week like?" requires thinking. "What made one of your kids laugh out loud this week?" is immediately answerable. Test every prompt you recommend by asking: can a tired parent answer this at 9:30pm in 3 sentences or less? If not, rewrite it.

8. **HEIC files must be flagged for conversion when designing archival systems.** HEIC is the default format for iPhone photos taken after 2017. While space-efficient, HEIC's long-term archival viability is uncertain across decades and non-Apple systems. Any plan that includes iPhone photos must address HEIC-to-JPEG conversion for the archival copy, even if HEIC files are kept for working use.

9. **Backlog remediation must start going forward, not backward.** The first step for any user with a backlog is always to set up the going-forward system first. Trying to sort a 10,000-photo backlog before establishing a new system means the new photos are accumulating into the same mess simultaneously. Stabilize the present before addressing the past.

10. **The inherited and deceased family member item protocol requires a no-decision window.** Never recommend immediate curation decisions for items received after a death or during grief. The standard guidance is: photograph everything first, create a "pending indefinitely" category, and do not make final release decisions within 12 months of the loss. Items released during acute grief are permanently lost; items kept for a year longer than needed cost only a little storage space.

11. **Printed photo books must be recommended in every plan as a distinct preservation strategy.** Printed books are not redundant with digital archiving -- they are categorically different preservation. Digital files require working hardware and software to access. Printed books require only light and eyes. Every annual plan should include at least one printed artifact (30--50 image photo book) as insurance against the inevitable failure of digital infrastructure.

12. **Shared family albums must have explicit contribution rules.** "Add your photos here" always results in everyone dumping full uncurated rolls into the shared location. The shared album rule must be stated explicitly: curated photos only, maximum 5--10 photos per person per outing, no duplicates of what others have already added. Without this rule, the shared album becomes a different kind of chaos within 3 months.

## Edge Cases

### Large Inherited Photo Collections (Prints from Previous Generations)

When a user inherits boxes of physical prints from parents or grandparents, the workflow requires a distinct approach from digital management. First, sort by decade rather than year -- exact dates on old prints are often unavailable, and decade grouping is achievable by visual inspection of clothing, cars, and print format. Second, identify any prints with handwriting on the back before scanning -- back-of-photo notes contain names, dates, and context that are irreplaceable and must be captured. Scan priority order: labeled photos with context first, photos of people second, event photos third, landscape or location photos last. For scanning, 600 DPI minimum for standard prints, 1200 DPI for small or damaged prints. The "photograph and release" category is appropriate for most prints after scanning -- keeping 500 physical prints is not realistic for most families. Identify 10--20 exceptional prints worth keeping as physical artifacts (size, condition, emotional significance) and archive the rest digitally.

### Multiple Devices and Family Members Contributing Photos

When both parents, older children, and potentially grandparents are all taking photos of the same family events, the system must address the collection problem before it becomes a deduplication nightmare. Establish a single shared album as the canonical family archive and train all contributors to add only their best curated shots -- never full camera rolls. Designate one person per month as the "photo steward" responsible for doing a final curation pass on the shared album and moving the month's keepers into the organized folder structure. Implement a file naming check during quarterly backups to catch duplicate filenames from different devices (two people's iPhone photos named IMG_4287 will conflict in the same folder -- use subfolders per contributor within each event folder if needed, or rename using a date-device convention before merging).

### Catastrophic Loss Events (House Fire, Flood, Theft)

If a user comes to this skill after a loss of physical or digital materials, the tone and process must shift fundamentally. Do not begin with a systems design conversation. Begin with a recovery assessment: what was lost, what was backed up, what may be recoverable. Sources of recoverable photos after a loss include: cloud accounts still accessible, photos shared with or tagged by extended family on social platforms, photos that exist on other family members' phones from shared events, and photos printed in prior photo books. For physical items, once the loss has occurred, the conversation shifts to preservation of what survived and preventing the same loss in future. This is also the most powerful motivation moment for implementing the 3-2-1 backup rule -- use it.

### Children Who Are Now Adults (Sorting Childhood Keepsakes for Handoff)

When a user is sorting childhood keepsakes for a child who is now 18+ and preparing to leave home, the process has a different endpoint: handoff, not ongoing curation. Sort the materials collaboratively with the adult child if possible -- their perspective on what matters to them will surprise their parents. Create a "handoff box" of physical items for the adult child to take to their new home. Create a digital folder of their childhood photos and artwork as a gift -- properly organized, curated, and named. The annual narrative documents, if they exist, become a gift of immeasurable personal value. The items the adult child does not want should be released without guilt -- the parent's attachment to a child's baby clothes is not the same as the now-adult child's relationship to those items.

### Divorce or Family Structure Changes

Memory keeping during or after a divorce presents specific challenges: photos and keepsakes that are emotionally loaded, shared photo archives that need to be divided, and children's memories that need to be preserved neutrally regardless of adult conflict. Practical guidance: make a complete duplicate copy of the digital archive before any division so both parties have full access to the children's memories. Do not discard or alter photos that include an ex-spouse -- children deserve access to their own history, which includes both parents. Shared custody families benefit from a "kid's photo" shared album that both co-parents contribute to and can access, kept strictly for the children's memories rather than documentation of time with each parent. Physical keepsakes follow the child -- they belong to the children, not to the parents' separate households.

### Users Who Are Professional-Level Photo Volume Creators

Some users -- particularly those who shoot with a dedicated camera in addition to a smartphone, or who document their children very intensively -- may have 20,000--50,000+ photos per year rather than 2,000--5,000. The curation ratios change: target 1--3% of total photos as final keepers rather than a fixed per-event count. The tools also change: at this volume, manual file-by-file curation becomes impractical, and a dedicated photo management application with star ratings, color labels, and batch processing is necessary. AI-assisted duplicate detection tools can reduce a 50,000-photo library by 20--40% in the first pass. The backup strategy must also scale -- storage costs and management complexity increase linearly, and a NAS (Network Attached Storage) device becomes more appropriate than a single external drive. The annual photo book may need to expand to a quarterly or themed format to remain manageable.

### Users Who Want to Preserve Digital-Only Memories That Predate Smartphones (Early Digital Era, 2000--2010)

Many users have a gap era: digital photos taken with early digital cameras and stored on CDs, DVDs, old Windows PCs, or defunct cloud services like Picasa Web Albums or the original Kodak Gallery. These files face unique risks: optical disc degradation, discontinued software formats, and orphaned cloud accounts. Immediate priorities: copy all CD/DVD photos to current hard drives now -- optical discs degrade unpredictably after 10--25 years, and some may already be unreadable. Check file formats -- early cameras used JPEG broadly, but some used proprietary RAW formats that require manufacturer software to open. Export from any still-accessible legacy cloud service immediately and do not assume the service will remain available. Google Photos and Apple iCloud allow export of all data through their respective download portals, and this export should be done and stored locally as part of the 3-2-1 backup architecture.

### Digital Photo System

**Folder Structure:**
```
Family Photos/
  2026/

      2025-2026-Second-Grade/
      2024-2025-First-Grade/
    James/
      2025-2026-Fourth-Grade/
      2024-2025-Third-Grade/
```

**Curation Rules:**
- Ordinary day or errand: keep 0--3 photos (most days do not need documentation)
- Weekend outing or activity: keep 5--10 photos
- Birthday party or holiday: keep 15--25 photos
- Multi-day vacation: keep 10--15 photos per day, 60--80 total for a week-long trip
- School milestone (first day, last day, performance): keep 5--8 photos
- Curation sequence: (1) delete blurry, eyes-closed, accidental, and screenshots -- be aggressive, (2) eliminate near-duplicates -- keep the single best version of any repeated pose or composition, (3) from what remains, choose by emotional resonance -- the photo that shows their personality, not just their face

**File Format Standard:** Both iPhone and Android produce JPEGs as the standard export. iPhone in iCloud may store HEIC files natively -- when downloading from iCloud for your organized archive, export as JPEG. Your archive copy should be JPEG only. Do not store HEIC in the organized folder structure.

**Backup Architecture:**
| Copy | Location | Medium | Update Frequency |
|------|----------|--------|-----------------|
| Primary | iCloud (your iPhone) + Google Photos (husband's Android) | Cloud, auto-sync | Continuous |
| Secondary | Organized folder structure on dedicated external hard drive | External drive (at least 2TB) | Monthly download, quarterly full backup |
| Tertiary | Second cloud service (used as archive export backup) | Cloud | Annually -- export full archive |

**Practical note on the old laptop:** Before the laptop dies completely, your first priority (this month) is to copy every photo off it to your external drive. Do not organize -- just copy. You can sort them into the folder structure later. A dead laptop with photos on it is a recovery problem; photos on a working external drive are just unsorted files.

**Shared Family Album:** Create one shared cloud album accessible to both you and your husband. Rule: curated photos only -- best 5--10 from any given outing. Each of you moves your curated keepers to the shared album within 48 hours of an event, while your memory of what happened is fresh. Full camera rolls do not go into the shared album.

### Physical Keepsake System

**Decision Framework:**
| Category | Keep Physical | Photograph and Release | Let Go |
|----------|--------------|----------------------|--------|
| Children's artwork | 2--3 truly standout pieces per school year per child (the ones that stop you when you look at them) | All other artwork -- every piece, photographed flat before releasing | Practice sheets, damaged pieces, 47 similar crayon drawings |
| Schoolwork | Report cards, one writing sample per grade that shows their voice, IEP documents if applicable | Class photos (scan), achievement certificates, special projects | Routine worksheets, spelling tests, math drills |
| 3D projects | 1 special piece per year if small enough to store (clay sculpture, wood project) -- photograph first regardless | All others -- photograph from 3 angles before releasing | Anything deteriorating, broken, or generic |
| Cards and letters | Handwritten cards with personal messages from grandparents, relatives, the kids themselves | Printed cards with only a signature | Store-bought cards with no personal note added |
| Clothing | One sentimental item per child (coming-home outfit, beloved lovey if not still in use) | | Everything else -- donate |
| School photos | Keep the 5"x7" for grandparents, keep one wallet-size in the box | Scan the rest | Duplicates |
| Inherited items from grandparents | Pending -- do not make decisions until you have photographed everything and taken at least 6 months | | |

**Container System:**
- Per child per school year: one standard photo storage box (12" x 10" x 4") -- available at any craft or office supply store
- Label each box: `[Child Name] -- [School Year] -- Grade [#]`
- Materials: Store paper items in acid-free folders inside the box. The box itself should be archival (acid-free) for paper items you intend to keep 20+ years, particularly report cards and writing samples
- Stack boxes in a climate-controlled interior closet -- not the attic, not the garage, not the basement. Temperature swings cause paper brittleness; humidity causes mold
- You have 3 banker's boxes covering 5 years for 2 children. That is approximately 10 school years of material. Your target after sorting: 10 photo storage boxes (one per child per year). You will consolidate from 3 large boxes to 10 smaller, organized boxes -- which may actually take up less total space and will be infinitely more usable

**Artwork Digitization Protocol:**
1. Lay the piece flat on white foam board or a white wall
2. Stand directly above it, phone camera pointing straight down
3. Use natural window light or a well-lit room -- no flash
4. Photograph the front. If the child wrote their name or added details on the back, photograph the back too
5. File immediately to: `Artwork/[Mia or James]/[School Year]/`
6. Release the physical original to recycling or donation after photographing -- you now have the memory without the paper

**Curation Schedule for Physical Items:** End of each school year (late May or early June), do a 30--45 minute sort of that year's accumulation before the box overflows. Do not wait until the boxes are all full -- sort annually so the decisions are recent and easy.

### Documentation System

**Format:** Notes app on your phone -- this is the lowest-friction option for someone without an existing journaling habit. Create a note titled `Family Notes -- [Month] [Year]` at the start of each month.

**Frequency:** Weekly, Sunday evening, 5--10 minutes. This is the only documentation task on your weekly list.

**Weekly Prompts -- answer any 2--3 that resonate this week:**
1. What made Mia or James laugh out loud this week?
2. What did one of them do or say that showed you who they are becoming?
3. What ordinary moment do I want to remember in 10 years? (Bedtime routines, the way they talk to each other, Tuesday afternoon after school)
4. What was hard this week -- for them or for us?
5. What is each of them really into right now?

**Quote Capture:** At the top of each monthly note, create a running section called "Things They Said." Format: date -- who said it -- exact quote. No analysis needed. These become the most-read entries later.
Example entry: `March 4 -- James (9) -- "Mom, do you think clouds feel lonely when it rains?"`

**Annual Narrative Document (write every January, one per child):**
This is the most valuable documentation you can create. Write 1--2 pages per child covering:
- What Mia / James is like at this age -- their personality, quirks, specific characteristics
- What they were into this year (shows, books, games, activities, foods, obsessions)
- What made them laugh / what scared them
- Important relationships -- friends, teachers, anyone who mattered to them
- Milestones and firsts this year
- Something they said or did that perfectly captures who they are right now
- What this year felt like as their parent -- the honest version

Write it in the second person addressed to the child: "You were seven this year. You were completely obsessed with..." This format is deeply personal and is the document they will want to read as adults.

### Backlog Plan

**Total estimated photos to sort:** ~14,000 across all sources
**First step -- consolidation:** Before sorting anything, copy all photos from all sources (iPhone, husband's Android, old laptop, any downloaded Google Photos or iCloud exports) into a single temporary folder called `[Unsorted Raw]` on your external drive. Now everything exists in one place and nothing is lost. This consolidation step may take a full afternoon.

**Then sort:**
- Recommended session length: 30--45 minutes maximum
- Sessions per week: 1--2 (any more becomes aversive and you will stop)
- Estimated total sessions at this pace: 12--16 sessions
- Estimated completion if you start this month: 2--4 months of background work alongside the new system

| Phase | Period Covered | Approach | Target Completion |
|-------|---------------|----------|-------------------|
| Consolidation | All sources | Copy everything into one raw folder | This month |
| Phase 1 | 2025 photos | Sort into year/month folders, curate | Month 2 |
| Phase 2 | 2024 photos | Same | Month 3 |
| Phase 3 | 2022--2023 photos | Same | Month 4--5 |
| Phase 4 | 2021 and earlier | Same | Month 5--6 |

**Session Protocol:**
1. Open one month's worth of raw photos (or one year for sparse years)
2. First 10 minutes: batch-delete obvious junk -- blurry shots, screenshots, food photos you do not care about, accidental captures. Be aggressive. You have 14,000 photos; you will not miss 2,000 obvious failures.
3. Next 15 minutes: eliminate near-duplicates. For any group of 5 similar photos of the same moment, keep 1--2 and delete the rest.
4. Final 10 minutes: move keepers to the correct organized folder (year/month/event). Apply the 3-second rule -- if you look at a photo and cannot immediately place it or feel anything, it is a candidate for deletion.
5. Do not agonize over individual borderline photos. If you hesitate more than 10 seconds, keep it and move on. Your time is better spent on the next 200 photos than on debating one.

**Physical backlog (3 banker's boxes):** Schedule one 45-minute physical sort session per month on a weekend morning. Bring: the boxes, 10 empty photo storage boxes (one per child per year), a camera for artwork digitization, acid-free folders, and a donation bag. Sort one year for one child per session. At this pace, you clear the physical backlog in 10--12 sessions -- about 3 months.

### Annual Memory Review (Every January)

- Download the previous year's curated photos from the shared album and do a final pass -- choose 40--60 favorites for the photo book
- Order one printed photo book of the year (30--50 photos, hardcover for durability) -- this is your physical insurance policy against digital failure
- Write the annual narrative document for each child while the year is fresh
- Re-read the year's monthly journal notes together with your husband on one evening
- Do a check: is anything in the physical system overflowing? Is the backup current? Is the shared album still working?
- Send a simple year-in-review to grandparents: 10--12 photos and 2--3 paragraphs. They want this more than you realize.

### Long-Term Preservation Notes

- **HEIC files:** When you download from iCloud, export as JPEG. Your working archive should be JPEG throughout.
- **The old laptop:** This is your most urgent task. Get everything off it before it fails. Copy everything to the external drive now -- organize later.
- **Media migration:** Plan to copy your entire archive to fresh drives every 5 years. External hard drives are not permanent. Set a calendar reminder for 2031.
- **Printed photo books:** These outlast every piece of technology in this plan. Every hard drive fails eventually. Every cloud service will change. A printed book requires only light and eyes to access. Print one every year without exception.
- **Do not rely on one cloud service:** You currently have both iCloud and Google Photos. That is actually good -- do not consolidate to one. Two cloud services plus a local drive is stronger than any single backup solution.
