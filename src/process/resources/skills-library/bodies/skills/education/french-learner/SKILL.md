## CEFR-Aligned Study Path

### A1 - Beginner (0-3 months)

```
A1 CORE OBJECTIVES
====================
Grammar:
  - Present tense of regular -ER, -IR, -RE verbs
  - Etre and avoir (to be and to have) - essential irregular verbs
  - Articles: le/la/les, un/une/des, du/de la/des (partitive)
  - Negation: ne...pas (with contractions: n'...pas)
  - Question formation: intonation, est-ce que, inversion
  - Adjective agreement (gender and number) and placement
  - Possessive adjectives (mon/ma/mes, ton/ta/tes, son/sa/ses)
  - Prepositions of place (a, en, dans, sur, sous, devant, derriere)

Vocabulary targets (500-800 words):
  - Greetings and introductions
  - Numbers 0-100, days, months
  - Family, professions, nationalities
  - Food and drink, clothing
  - Directions and places in a city

Speaking milestones:
  - Introduce yourself and ask personal questions
  - Order at a cafe or restaurant
  - Ask for and understand directions
  - Talk about your daily routine
```

### A2 - Elementary (3-6 months)

```
A2 CORE OBJECTIVES
====================
Grammar:
  - Passe compose (with avoir and etre auxiliaries)
  - Imparfait (habitual past, descriptions)
  - Futur proche (aller + infinitive)
  - Object pronouns (le, la, les, lui, leur, me, te, nous, vous)
  - Reflexive verbs (se lever, se coucher, s'habiller)
  - Comparatives and superlatives (plus...que, le plus...)
  - Y and en (pronoun replacements for places and quantities)

Vocabulary targets (1,500-2,000 words):
  - Travel and transportation
  - Health and body
  - Weather and seasons
  - Hobbies and leisure
  - Shopping and services

Speaking milestones:
  - Narrate events in the past
  - Make plans and appointments
  - Describe people, places, and experiences
  - Handle basic travel situations
```

### B1 - Intermediate (6-12 months)

```
B1 CORE OBJECTIVES
====================
Grammar:
  - Subjonctif present (after il faut que, je veux que, pour que)
  - Conditionnel present (polite requests, hypothetical)
  - Plus-que-parfait (had done)
  - Relative pronouns (qui, que, ou, dont)
  - Reported speech (discours indirect)
  - Passive voice (etre + past participle)
  - Advanced negation (ne...jamais, ne...rien, ne...personne)

Vocabulary targets (3,000-4,000 words):
  - Current events and media
  - Work and professional life
  - Abstract concepts (freedom, education, environment)
  - Connectors and transitions (cependant, neanmoins, par consequent)

Speaking milestones:
  - Express and defend opinions
  - Discuss news and current events
  - Handle unexpected situations
  - Write formal emails and short essays
```

### B2 - Upper Intermediate (12-18 months)

```
B2 CORE OBJECTIVES
====================
Grammar:
  - Subjonctif past (mastery of all subjunctive triggers)
  - Conditionnel passe (would have done)
  - Si clauses (all three types)
  - Gerondif (en + present participle)
  - Advanced pronoun combinations
  - Literary tenses awareness (passe simple for reading)
  - Nuanced connector usage

Vocabulary targets (5,000-6,000 words):
  - Specialized vocabulary (your field of interest)
  - Idiomatic expressions (50+)
  - Register variation (formal, standard, familiar, argot)
  - Francophone cultural vocabulary

Speaking milestones:
  - Debate abstract topics with nuance
  - Present complex arguments
  - Understand humor, irony, and cultural references
  - Read literature and journalism fluently
```

## French Pronunciation Guide

### Vowel System

```
FRENCH VOWELS
===============
ORAL VOWELS:
  /a/  - patte (paw) - open, like English "father" but shorter
  /e/  - ete (summer) - closed, like "ay" in "say" without the glide
  /ɛ/  - fete (party) - open, like "e" in "pet"
  /i/  - vie (life) - like "ee" in "see"
  /o/  - mot (word) - closed, rounded, like "oh" without glide
  /ɔ/  - mort (death) - open, like "o" in British "hot"
  /u/  - vous (you) - like "oo" in "food"
  /y/  - tu (you) - NO English equivalent!
         Say "ee" but round your lips like "oo"
  /ø/  - peu (little) - say "ay" but round your lips
  /œ/  - peur (fear) - say "eh" but round your lips
  /ə/  - le (the) - unstressed, like "uh"

NASAL VOWELS (air through nose AND mouth):
  /ɑ̃/  - an, en, am, em: dans (in), enfant (child)
         Open mouth wide, air through nose
  /ɛ̃/  - in, im, ain, ein: vin (wine), pain (bread)
         Lips spread, air through nose
  /ɔ̃/  - on, om: bon (good), nom (name)
         Lips rounded, air through nose
  /œ̃/ - un, um: un (one), parfum (perfume)
         (Merging with /ɛ̃/ in many dialects)

PRACTICE TIP: Hold your nose while saying the vowel.
If the sound changes, you are nasalizing correctly.
```

### Consonant Challenges

```
DIFFICULT FRENCH CONSONANTS
==============================
R - French uvular R /ʁ/:
  Produced in the back of the throat (NOT tongue tip)
  Like a gentle gargle or the "ch" in German "Bach" but voiced
  Practice: Say "ga" then try to make the "g" continuous
  Example: rouge (red), regarder (to look), Paris

LIAISON (connecting sounds between words):
  When a normally silent final consonant is pronounced
  before a word starting with a vowel:
    les amis = /le-za-mi/ (the friends)
    un homme = /œ̃-nɔm/ (a man)
    vous avez = /vu-za-ve/ (you have)

  REQUIRED liaisons: article + noun, pronoun + verb, adjective + noun
  FORBIDDEN liaisons: after "et" (and), after singular nouns
  OPTIONAL liaisons: formal speech uses more liaisons

ENCHAÎNEMENT (linking):
  Final pronounced consonant links to next vowel:
    elle a = /ɛ-la/ (she has)
    il est = /i-lɛ/ (he is)

SILENT LETTERS:
  Most final consonants are SILENT: petit, grand, chat, bras
  Exception: C, R, F, L are often pronounced (think "CaReFuL")
    avec (with), pour (for), neuf (nine), hotel
```

## Key Grammar Patterns

### Passe Compose vs. Imparfait

```
PAST TENSE DECISION GUIDE
============================
PASSE COMPOSE (completed action)    IMPARFAIT (ongoing/habitual)
  - One-time event                    - Repeated/habitual action
  - Specific start/end                - Description/background
  - Sequence of events                - Ongoing state
  - Interrupting action               - Action being interrupted

PASSE COMPOSE FORMATION:
  Subject + avoir/etre + past participle
  J'ai mange (I ate) -- regular -ER: manger --> mange
  Il a fini (He finished) -- regular -IR: finir --> fini
  Elle a vendu (She sold) -- regular -RE: vendre --> vendu

ETRE VERBS (use etre instead of avoir):
  DR MRS VANDERTRAMP (mnemonic):
  Devenir, Revenir, Monter, Retourner, Sortir,
  Venir, Aller, Naitre, Descendre, Entrer, Rentrer,
  Tomber, Rester, Arriver, Mourir, Partir
  + all reflexive verbs

  Past participle AGREES with subject when using etre:
  Elle est allee (she went) -- add -e for feminine
  Ils sont partis (they left) -- add -s for plural

IMPARFAIT FORMATION:
  Take nous form of present tense, drop -ons, add:
  -ais, -ais, -ait, -ions, -iez, -aient
  Example: nous parlons --> je parlais, tu parlais...

EXAMPLE:
  "Quand j'etais (IMPARFAIT) jeune, j'habitais (IMPARFAIT)
   a Lyon. Un jour, j'ai decide (PASSE COMPOSE) de demenager
   a Paris."
```

### The Subjunctive

```
SUBJONCTIF TRIGGERS AND FORMATION
====================================
The subjunctive expresses subjectivity: wishes, emotions, doubt, necessity.

COMMON TRIGGERS:
  Necessity: il faut que, il est necessaire que
  Desire: je veux que, je souhaite que, j'aimerais que
  Emotion: je suis content que, j'ai peur que, c'est dommage que
  Doubt: je doute que, il est possible que, je ne pense pas que
  Purpose: pour que, afin que, avant que

FORMATION (regular):
  Take the ils/elles form of present, drop -ent, add:
  -e, -es, -e, -ions, -iez, -ent

  ils parlent --> que je parle, que tu parles, qu'il parle,
                  que nous parlions, que vous parliez, qu'ils parlent

IRREGULAR SUBJUNCTIVE FORMS (memorize these):
  etre: sois, sois, soit, soyons, soyez, soient
  avoir: aie, aies, ait, ayons, ayez, aient
  aller: aille, ailles, aille, allions, alliez, aillent
  faire: fasse, fasses, fasse, fassions, fassiez, fassent
  pouvoir: puisse, puisses, puisse, puissions, puissiez, puissent

WHEN NOT TO USE SUBJUNCTIVE:
  Same subject: Use infinitive instead
    Wrong: Je veux que je parte (I want that I leave)
    Right: Je veux partir (I want to leave)
```

## DELF Exam Preparation

```
DELF CERTIFICATION OVERVIEW
==============================
DELF = Diplome d'Etudes en Langue Francaise
Issued by: France Education International
Recognized: Internationally, for immigration, work, and study

LEVELS: A1, A2, B1, B2 (DALF for C1, C2)

EXAM SECTIONS (all levels):
  1. Comprehension orale (Listening) -- 25 min
  2. Comprehension ecrite (Reading) -- 30-60 min
  3. Production ecrite (Writing) -- 45-60 min
  4. Production orale (Speaking) -- 10-15 min

SCORING: Each section worth 25 points. Pass = 50/100 minimum,
         with at least 5/25 in each section.

PREPARATION TIPS:
  - Use official DELF sample papers from CIEP
  - Practice timed writing (letter, essay, opinion piece)
  - For oral: practice monologue AND interactive dialogue
  - B1 oral: present a document and discuss it
  - B2 oral: present an argument on a topic, defend your position
  - Read French news daily for reading comprehension practice
```

## Cultural Communication

```
FRENCH COMMUNICATION NORMS
=============================
1. FORMALITY (Tu vs. Vous):
   Vous (formal): Strangers, elders, workplace superiors, clients
   Tu (informal): Friends, family, children, peers who invite it
   When in doubt, use VOUS. Wait for the other person to propose "tu."
   The act of switching is called "tutoiement" and is socially significant.

2. GREETINGS:
   Always greet before any transaction (shops, offices, taxis)
   "Bonjour, madame/monsieur" is expected, not optional
   La bise (cheek kisses): 1-4 kisses depending on region
   Handshake: Professional settings

3. MEAL CULTURE:
   Meals are social events, not just fuel
   Bon appetit before eating
   Do not start eating before the host
   Bread goes directly on the table (not on the plate)
   Wine is sipped, not gulped

4. CONVERSATION STYLE:
   French conversation values wit, debate, and intellectual exchange
   Disagreement is not rude -- it shows engagement
   "Je ne suis pas d'accord" (I disagree) is perfectly acceptable
   Avoid overly personal questions early in acquaintance
```

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

```
TOP 10 ENGLISH SPEAKER MISTAKES IN FRENCH
============================================
1. Pronouncing silent letters: "petit" = /pə.ti/ not /pe.tit/
2. skipping gender agreement: "elle est content" --> "elle est contente"
3. Using wrong auxiliary in passe compose: "je suis mange" --> "j'ai mange"
4. skipping partitive articles: "je veux eau" --> "je veux de l'eau"
5. Translating "I am + age": "je suis 25" --> "j'ai 25 ans" (I have 25 years)
6. Word-for-word translation of "it is": Context determines c'est vs. il est
7. Neglecting liaison: "les enfants" must link: /le.zɑ̃.fɑ̃/
8. Confusing "savoir" and "connaitre" (both mean "to know"):
   savoir = know a fact/how to do something
   connaitre = know/be familiar with a person/place
9. skipping ne...pas around verb: "je sais pas" is spoken but "je ne sais pas" is correct
10. Not using subjunctive after emotional/doubt triggers
```
