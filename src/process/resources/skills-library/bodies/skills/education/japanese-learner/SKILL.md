## Writing System Mastery

### Learning Order

```
JAPANESE WRITING SYSTEM ROADMAP
==================================
Phase 1: HIRAGANA (46 base characters)
  Timeline: 1-2 weeks of daily practice
  Used for: Native Japanese words, grammar particles, verb endings
  Priority: ESSENTIAL - learn this first

Phase 2: KATAKANA (46 base characters)
  Timeline: 1-2 weeks of daily practice
  Used for: Foreign loanwords, emphasis, onomatopoeia, technical terms
  Priority: ESSENTIAL - learn immediately after hiragana

Phase 3: KANJI (2,136 joyo kanji for literacy)
  Timeline: Ongoing (1-3 years for core set)
  Used for: Most content words (nouns, verb stems, adjectives)
  Priority: Begin after mastering kana; learn progressively

LEARNING APPROACH:
  - Learn kana by row (a-i-u-e-o, ka-ki-ku-ke-ko, etc.)
  - Practice writing by hand (even if you will mostly type)
  - Use mnemonics to associate shapes with sounds
  - Test with reading practice, not just recognition
```

### Kanji Learning Strategy

```
KANJI ACQUISITION METHOD
==========================
1. LEARN RADICALS FIRST (214 traditional radicals)
   Radicals are building blocks. Knowing them helps you:
   - Guess meaning of unknown kanji
   - Remember complex characters
   - Look up kanji in dictionaries

   Common radicals:
   water: three dots on left side
   person: two strokes on left side
   tree/wood: cross shape
   mouth: square shape
   sun: rectangle shape
   hand: three horizontal strokes on left

2. FREQUENCY-BASED ORDER
   Learn the most commonly used kanji first:
   JLPT N5: ~100 kanji (basic: numbers, directions, time)
   JLPT N4: ~300 kanji (elementary: actions, descriptions)
   JLPT N3: ~650 kanji (intermediate: abstract concepts)
   JLPT N2: ~1,000 kanji (advanced: news, business)
   JLPT N1: ~2,000 kanji (near-native: literature, specialized)

3. LEARN READINGS IN CONTEXT
   Each kanji has ON (Chinese-derived) and KUN (Japanese) readings.
   Do NOT memorize all readings in isolation.
   Learn the reading as part of a WORD:
     Mountain: yama (kun) / SAN (on)
     Learn: yama (the mountain), fuji-SAN (Mt. Fuji)

4. SPACED REPETITION
   Use tools like WaniKani, Anki, or Kanji Study
   Review schedule: 1 day, 3 days, 7 days, 14 days, 30 days
```

## Grammar Fundamentals

### Sentence Structure

```
JAPANESE SENTENCE ORDER
=========================
English: Subject - Verb - Object (SVO)
  "I eat sushi."

Japanese: Subject - Object - Verb (SOV)
  "Watashi wa sushi wo tabemasu."
  (I [topic] sushi [object] eat)

KEY PRINCIPLE: The verb ALWAYS goes at the end.

PARTICLES (the grammar glue):
  wa  - Topic marker ("as for X...")
  ga  - Subject marker (identifies who/what does the action)
  wo  - Direct object marker
  ni  - Direction/target/time marker ("to", "at", "on")
  de  - Location of action / means ("at", "by", "with")
  no  - Possession ("of", "'s")
  he  - Direction of movement ("toward")
  to  - "And" (exhaustive list) / quotation marker
  mo  - "Also", "too"
  ka  - Question marker (placed at sentence end)

EXAMPLE BUILDING:
  Watashi wa        = As for me (topic)
  gakkou de         = at school (location)
  nihongo wo        = Japanese (object)
  benkyou shimasu   = study (verb)
  "As for me, I study Japanese at school."
```

### Verb Conjugation Basics

```
VERB GROUPS AND CONJUGATION
==============================
GROUP 1 (U-verbs / Godan):
  Dictionary form ends in: -u, -ku, -gu, -su, -tsu, -nu, -bu, -mu, -ru
  Example: nomu (to drink), kaku (to write), hanasu (to speak)

GROUP 2 (RU-verbs / Ichidan):
  Dictionary form ends in: -iru or -eru
  Example: taberu (to eat), miru (to see), okiru (to wake up)

GROUP 3 (Irregular):
  Only two: suru (to do), kuru (to come)

BASIC CONJUGATIONS (polite form / -masu form):
  taberu  --> tabemasu (eat)      --> tabemasen (not eat)
  nomu    --> nomimasu (drink)    --> nomimasen (not drink)
  suru    --> shimasu (do)        --> shimasen (not do)
  kuru    --> kimasu (come)       --> kimasen (not come)

PAST TENSE (polite):
  tabemasu    --> tabemashita (ate)
  nomimasu    --> nomimashita (drank)

TE-FORM (connecting, requesting):
  taberu  --> tabete      nomu --> nonde
  kaku    --> kaite       hanasu --> hanashite
  Uses: requests (tabete kudasai = please eat),
        progressive (tabete iru = is eating),
        connecting actions (tabete, nonde... = eat, drink, and...)
```

### Politeness Levels

```
JAPANESE POLITENESS SYSTEM
=============================
CASUAL (used with close friends, family, younger people):
  Taberu.           = I eat. / I'll eat.
  Oishii!           = Delicious!
  Nani?             = What?

POLITE / DESU-MASU (default for most situations):
  Tabemasu.         = I eat. / I'll eat.
  Oishii desu.      = It is delicious.
  Nan desu ka?      = What is it?

HONORIFIC / KEIGO (business, strangers, service industry):
  Meshiagarimasu.   = [You] eat. (sonkeigo - elevating others)
  Itadakimasu.      = [I] eat. (kenjougo - humbling self)

WHEN TO USE WHICH:
  Always start with DESU-MASU form as a learner.
  Use casual only when:
    - Speaking with close friends who have invited casual speech
    - Writing in a diary or informal messages
    - Speaking with children
  Never use casual with:
    - Strangers, elders, superiors, customers, in-laws (initially)
```

## JLPT Preparation

```
JLPT EXAM STRUCTURE
=====================
Japanese Language Proficiency Test (held in July and December)

N5 (Beginner):
  Kanji: ~100 | Vocabulary: ~800 | Grammar: ~50 patterns
  Can understand basic Japanese in everyday situations
  Study time: 300-400 hours

N4 (Elementary):
  Kanji: ~300 | Vocabulary: ~1,500 | Grammar: ~130 patterns
  Can understand basic Japanese at slightly natural speed
  Study time: 550-700 hours

N3 (Intermediate):
  Kanji: ~650 | Vocabulary: ~3,700 | Grammar: ~200 patterns
  Can understand Japanese in everyday situations to a degree
  Study time: 950-1,100 hours

EXAM SECTIONS:
  1. Language Knowledge (Vocabulary + Grammar)
  2. Reading Comprehension
  3. Listening Comprehension

PREPARATION STRATEGY:
  - Use official JLPT practice tests (published by Japan Foundation)
  - Study grammar patterns using Shin Kanzen Master or Sou Matome series
  - Build vocabulary with JLPT-level word lists
  - Practice listening with Japanese podcasts and shadowing
  - Time yourself on reading sections (speed is crucial)
```

## Pronunciation and Pitch Accent

```
JAPANESE PRONUNCIATION BASICS
================================
GOOD NEWS: Japanese pronunciation is relatively simple for English speakers.
  - 5 pure vowels (same as Spanish: a, i, u, e, o)
  - Most consonants exist in English
  - Syllable timing (each mora gets equal length)

CHALLENGING SOUNDS:
  R/L:  Japanese "r" is between English "r", "l", and "d"
        Tongue taps the ridge behind upper teeth (like quick "d")
        Practice: ramen, roku (six), ringo (apple)

  TSU:  Not "too" - tongue touches behind upper teeth, releases air
        Practice: tsuki (moon), tsukau (to use)

  F/H:  Japanese "fu" is made with both lips (not teeth on lip)
        Softer than English "f"

PITCH ACCENT (often overlooked):
  Japanese is NOT monotone - it has a pitch accent system.
  Words distinguished by high/low pitch patterns:
    hashi (chopsticks) = HA-shi (high-low)
    hashi (bridge) = ha-SHI (low-high)
    hashi (edge) = ha-SHI (low-high, drops after)

  Recommendation: Do not stress about pitch accent at beginner level.
  Begin noticing patterns at intermediate level.
  Use the OJAD (Online Japanese Accent Dictionary) tool for reference.
```

## Cultural Context for Communication

```
ESSENTIAL CULTURAL KNOWLEDGE
===============================
1. GREETINGS are time-specific:
   ohayou gozaimasu (morning), konnichiwa (daytime),
   konbanwa (evening), oyasuminasai (goodnight)

2. BOWING: depth and duration indicate respect level
   Slight nod (15 degrees): casual greeting
   Medium bow (30 degrees): standard polite greeting
   Deep bow (45+ degrees): deep respect, apology

3. INDIRECT COMMUNICATION:
   Japanese often communicates indirectly.
   "Chotto..." (a little...) often means "no" politely.
   "Kangaete okimasu" (I'll think about it) often means "probably no."
   Read context and tone, not just words.

4. GIFT GIVING: Always give and receive with both hands.
   Say "tsumaranai mono desu ga" (it's a trivial thing, but...)

5. SELF-INTRODUCTION (jikoshoukai):
   Standard pattern: "[Name] to moushimasu. [Company/school] no [role] desu.
   Yoroshiku onegaishimasu."
   (My name is ___. I am a ___ at ___. Please be kind to me.)
```
