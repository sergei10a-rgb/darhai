## Hangul Mastery

### The Korean Alphabet

```
HANGUL OVERVIEW
=================
Hangul was scientifically designed in 1443 by King Sejong the Great.
It is considered one of the most logical writing systems in the world.

CONSONANTS (14 basic):
  g/k   n    d/t   r/l   m    b/p   s
  ㄱ    ㄴ    ㄷ    ㄹ    ㅁ    ㅂ    ㅅ

  (silent/ng)  j    ch    k     t     p     h
  ㅇ          ㅈ   ㅊ    ㅋ    ㅌ    ㅍ    ㅎ

DOUBLE CONSONANTS (5 tense):
  gg    dd    bb    ss    jj
  ㄲ    ㄸ    ㅃ    ㅆ    ㅉ
  (Tense: held with tension, no aspiration)

VOWELS (10 basic):
  a     ya    eo    yeo   o     yo
  ㅏ    ㅑ    ㅓ    ㅕ    ㅗ    ㅛ

  u     yu    eu    i
  ㅜ    ㅠ    ㅡ    ㅣ

COMPOUND VOWELS (11):
  ae    yae   e     ye    wa    wae   oe    wo    we    wi    ui
  ㅐ    ㅒ    ㅔ    ㅖ    ㅘ    ㅙ    ㅚ    ㅝ    ㅞ    ㅟ    ㅢ

SYLLABLE BLOCK STRUCTURE:
  Korean is written in syllable blocks, not individual letters.
  Each block has: Initial consonant + Vowel (+optional final consonant)

  Examples:
  가 = ㄱ + ㅏ = ga
  한 = ㅎ + ㅏ + ㄴ = han
  글 = ㄱ + ㅡ + ㄹ = geul
  한글 = han + geul = Hangul
```

### Hangul Learning Strategy

```
HANGUL MASTERY TIMELINE: 1-3 DAYS
====================================
Day 1: Learn 14 basic consonants + 10 basic vowels
  - Associate consonant shapes with mouth positions
  - ㄱ looks like the tongue touching the back of the mouth
  - ㄴ looks like the tongue touching the top of the mouth
  - ㅁ looks like a mouth (square shape)
  - Practice writing each character 10 times

Day 2: Learn compound vowels + syllable block assembly
  - Practice reading simple syllable blocks
  - Write your name in Hangul
  - Practice common words: 안녕 (hello), 감사 (thanks)

Day 3: Learn final consonants (batchim) + double consonants
  - Final consonants change pronunciation
  - ㄱ at end sounds like "k", ㄷ at end sounds like "t"
  - Practice reading simple sentences

Day 4-7: Speed drills
  - Time yourself reading Hangul
  - Target: read any syllable block within 2 seconds
  - Practice with K-pop lyrics or simple children's books

PRONUNCIATION NOTES:
  eo (ㅓ) = NOT "ee-oh" -- it's like "uh" in "sun"
  eu (ㅡ) = NOT "ee-you" -- it's like the "u" in "put" without rounding
  o (ㅗ) = like "oh" in "go"
  u (ㅜ) = like "oo" in "food"
```

## Grammar Fundamentals

### Sentence Structure and Particles

```
KOREAN SENTENCE ORDER
========================
English: Subject - Verb - Object (SVO)
  "I eat rice."

Korean: Subject - Object - Verb (SOV)
  "Na-neun bap-eul meogeoyo."
  (I [topic] rice [object] eat)

KEY PRINCIPLE: The verb ALWAYS goes at the end.

ESSENTIAL PARTICLES:
  은/는 (eun/neun) - Topic marker
    "As for X..." / marks what you are talking about
    저는 학생이에요. (As for me, I am a student.)

  이/가 (i/ga) - Subject marker
    Identifies who/what does the action (new info or emphasis)
    비가 와요. (Rain is coming.)

  을/를 (eul/reul) - Object marker
    Marks the direct object
    커피를 마셔요. (I drink coffee.)

  에 (e) - Location / time marker
    학교에 가요. (I go to school.)
    세 시에 만나요. (Let's meet at 3 o'clock.)

  에서 (eseo) - Location of action
    학교에서 공부해요. (I study at school.)

  의 (ui) - Possession (like 's)
    한국의 음식 (Korea's food / Korean food)

  와/과, 하고, (이)랑 - "and" (listing)
    커피와 차 (coffee and tea - formal)
    커피하고 차 (coffee and tea - casual)
    커피랑 차 (coffee and tea - informal)

PARTICLES AFTER CONSONANT vs. VOWEL:
  Consonant ending: 은, 이, 을
  Vowel ending: 는, 가, 를
```

### Verb Conjugation

```
KOREAN VERB CONJUGATION
=========================
All Korean verbs end in -다 (da) in dictionary form.

PRESENT TENSE (polite informal - 요 form):
  Remove 다, add appropriate ending:
  하다 (to do) --> 해요
  가다 (to go) --> 가요
  먹다 (to eat) --> 먹어요
  마시다 (to drink) --> 마셔요

  PATTERN: Look at the last vowel of the stem:
  ㅏ or ㅗ --> add 아요
  All others --> add 어요
  하다 verbs --> 해요

PAST TENSE (polite informal):
  Same vowel harmony, but add 았/었/했 + 어요:
  가다 --> 갔어요 (went)
  먹다 --> 먹었어요 (ate)
  하다 --> 했어요 (did)

FUTURE/INTENTION:
  Stem + (으)ㄹ 거예요
  가다 --> 갈 거예요 (will go)
  먹다 --> 먹을 거예요 (will eat)

NEGATIVE:
  안 + verb: 안 먹어요 (don't eat)
  Or: verb stem + 지 않다: 먹지 않아요 (don't eat, more formal)
```

### Honorific Speech Levels

```
KOREAN SPEECH LEVELS
=======================
Korean has multiple speech levels based on formality and politeness.
As a learner, master these three first:

1. FORMAL POLITE (합쇼체 / hapsyo-che):
   Used: Presentations, news, announcements, first meetings with elders
   Ending: -ㅂ니다/-습니다 (statements), -ㅂ니까/-습니까 (questions)
   감사합니다. (Thank you.)
   어디에 가십니까? (Where are you going?)

2. INFORMAL POLITE (해요체 / haeyo-che):
   Used: Most everyday situations, default for learners
   Ending: -아요/-어요/-해요
   감사해요. (Thank you.)
   어디에 가요? (Where are you going?)

3. CASUAL (해체 / hae-che):
   Used: Close friends of same age or younger, children
   Ending: -아/-어/-해 (drop the 요)
   고마워. (Thanks.)
   어디 가? (Where are you going?)

HONORIFIC VERB FORMS (elevating the subject):
  When the subject is someone you respect, use -(으)시-:
  가다 --> 가시다 (to go [honorific])
  먹다 --> 드시다 (to eat [special honorific word])
  있다 --> 계시다 (to be/exist [special honorific word])

RULE OF THUMB:
  - Start with INFORMAL POLITE (해요체) as your default
  - Use FORMAL POLITE in professional/public contexts
  - Only use CASUAL when explicitly invited by someone your age or younger
  - Add -(으)시- when talking about elders, superiors, or strangers
```

## TOPIK Preparation

```
TOPIK EXAM OVERVIEW
=====================
TOPIK = Test of Proficiency in Korean
Administered by: National Institute for International Education (NIIED)

TWO EXAM LEVELS:
  TOPIK I (Beginner): Tests for Level 1 and Level 2
    Sections: Listening (30 min) + Reading (40 min)
    No writing section

  TOPIK II (Intermediate-Advanced): Tests for Levels 3-6
    Sections: Listening (60 min) + Writing (50 min) + Reading (70 min)

LEVEL DESCRIPTIONS:
  Level 1: Basic survival Korean, simple sentences
  Level 2: Daily life conversations, basic functions
  Level 3: Social relationships, paragraph-level communication
  Level 4: General professional and academic topics
  Level 5: Professional field discussions, formal/informal register
  Level 6: Near-native fluency, nuanced expression

TOPIK II WRITING SECTION:
  Q51-52: Fill in the blanks (short sentences)
  Q53: Write a short paragraph explaining data (graph/chart)
  Q54: Write an essay (600-700 characters) on an abstract topic

PREPARATION STRATEGY:
  - Use official TOPIK practice tests from topik.go.kr
  - Study grammar patterns by TOPIK level
  - Build vocabulary with TOPIK-level word lists
  - Practice writing within time limits
  - Listen to Korean podcasts and news at appropriate speed
  - Study Sino-Korean vocabulary (Chinese-origin words) for levels 3+
```

## K-Media Immersion Strategy

```
USING KOREAN MEDIA FOR LEARNING
==================================
BEGINNER (TOPIK 1-2):
  K-Drama: Start with Korean subtitles + English subtitles
  Pick slice-of-life dramas (simpler vocabulary than historical)
  Recommended: Weightlifting Fairy Kim Bok Joo, My Love from the Star
  K-Pop: Study lyrics of favorite songs (learn vocabulary through music)
  YouTube: Korean Unnie, Talk To Me In Korean, Billy Go

INTERMEDIATE (TOPIK 3-4):
  K-Drama: Korean subtitles only (wean off English)
  Recommended: Reply 1988, Misaeng, Hospital Playlist
  Variety shows: Running Man, Knowing Bros (colloquial Korean)
  YouTube: Korean content creators on topics you enjoy
  Webtoons: Naver Webtoon (read popular series in Korean)

ACTIVE WATCHING METHOD:
  1. Watch a scene normally (enjoy the content)
  2. Re-watch with Korean subtitles, pause for unknown words
  3. Write down 5-10 new words or phrases
  4. Re-watch once more without pausing
  5. Try to use 3 new phrases in your own sentences

LISTENING PROGRESSION:
  Week 1-4: Korean with English subtitles (get used to sounds)
  Week 5-8: Korean with Korean subtitles (connect sound to text)
  Week 9-12: Korean subtitles only, pause to look up words
  Week 13+: Gradually reduce subtitle dependence
```

## Cultural Communication

```
KOREAN CULTURAL ESSENTIALS
=============================
1. AGE HIERARCHY:
   Korean society is strongly age-conscious.
   One of the first questions you may be asked: "How old are you?"
   This determines speech level and social dynamics.
   Even a one-year difference matters among peers.

2. BOWING:
   Standard greeting: slight bow (15 degrees)
   Respectful greeting: deeper bow (30-45 degrees)
   Always bow to elders and in professional settings

3. TWO-HANDED GIVING AND RECEIVING:
   Give and receive objects (especially to elders) with both hands
   Or right hand supported by left hand on forearm

4. DINING ETIQUETTE:
   Wait for the eldest person to start eating
   Do not pour your own drink (pour for others, they pour for yours)
   Do not stick chopsticks upright in rice (funeral association)

5. NAMES AND TITLES:
   Address people by title, not first name (unless very close)
   선생님 (seonsaengnim) = teacher/Mr./Ms. (respectful default)
   Family terms used broadly: 형/오빠 (older brother from male/female),
   누나/언니 (older sister from male/female)

6. INDIRECT COMMUNICATION:
   "Maybe" or "it might be difficult" often means "no"
   Reading between the lines (nunchi) is an important social skill
   Avoid giving direct negative responses to elders
```
