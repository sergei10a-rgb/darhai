## Reading Speed Reality Check

### Average Reading Speeds

| Reader Level | Words Per Minute (WPM) | Comprehension |
|-------------|----------------------|---------------|
| Average adult | 200-250 | 60-70% |
| Good reader | 300-400 | 70-80% |
| Speed reader | 400-700 | 70-85% |
| Elite speed reader | 700-1000+ | Varies (often 50-70%) |

**Key insight**: Speed without comprehension is not reading - it is scanning. The goal is to increase speed while maintaining or improving comprehension. Most people can realistically double their reading speed (to 400-500 WPM) with full comprehension through technique and practice.

### Measure Your Baseline

Before training, establish your starting point:
1. Choose a non-fiction passage of approximately 1,000 words
2. Set a timer and read at your normal pace
3. Calculate: (Word count / Minutes) = WPM
4. Without re-reading, answer 10 comprehension questions about the material
5. Score: (Correct / 10) x 100 = Comprehension %

```
BASELINE ASSESSMENT

Date: ___________
Passage: _________________________
Word count: _______
Time (minutes): _______
WPM: _______
Comprehension score: _______%
Effective reading rate: WPM x Comprehension% = _______
```

**Effective reading rate** is the metric that matters. 500 WPM at 40% comprehension (200 effective) is worse than 300 WPM at 80% comprehension (240 effective).

## The Five Speed Killers

### 1. Subvocalization
**What it is**: Silently pronouncing every word in your head as you read.
**Why it slows you down**: Limits reading speed to speaking speed (~150-200 WPM).
**Fix**: You do not need to eliminate subvocalization entirely (it aids comprehension for complex material). Instead, reduce it for easy material by:
- Counting "1-2-3-4" silently while reading (occupies the inner voice)
- Humming while reading (disrupts the vocalization pathway)
- Focusing on meaning rather than individual words
- Practice reading in word groups rather than individual words

### 2. Regression
**What it is**: Re-reading words or lines you have already read.
**Why it slows you down**: Average readers regress 15-20% of the time, adding ~50 WPM drag.
**Fix**:
- Use a pacer (finger, pen, or cursor) moving steadily forward
- Trust that meaning accumulates; minor missed words rarely matter
- If comprehension drops, the issue is focus, not reading speed
- For critical material, make one forward pass, then a second deliberate pass

### 3. Small Fixation Span
**What it is**: Eyes focusing on one word at a time instead of groups of 3-5 words.
**Why it slows you down**: Each fixation (eye stop) takes 200-250 milliseconds.
**Fix**:
- Practice reading in chunks: "The quick brown" / "fox jumps over" / "the lazy dog"
- Start by reading 2 words per fixation, progress to 3-4
- Use peripheral vision to capture words on either side of your focus point

### 4. Slow Saccades
**What it is**: Eye movements between fixation points are slow or imprecise.
**Fix**: Practice drills that train faster, more precise eye movements (see drills below).

### 5. Lack of Purpose
**What it is**: Reading without a clear goal or framework.
**Why it slows you down**: Without purpose, attention drifts, requiring re-reading.
**Fix**: Before reading anything, define:
- Why am I reading this?
- What do I need to get from it?
- How will I use this information?

## Core Techniques

### Technique 1: The Pacer Method

Use your finger, a pen, or a pointer to guide your eyes along the text. This:
- Reduces regression (eyes follow the pacer forward)
- Increases focus (gives eyes a target)
- Sets a pace (gradually move the pacer faster)

**Practice**:
1. Start at your current speed, pacer moving under each line
2. Each session, increase pacer speed slightly
3. Your eyes will work to keep up, building faster processing

### Technique 2: Chunking (Word Grouping)

Instead of reading word-by-word, read in meaningful phrases:

**Before** (word-by-word, 7 fixations):
> The | man | walked | slowly | across | the | bridge

**After** (chunked, 2-3 fixations):
> The man walked | slowly across | the bridge

**Practice**:
- Draw vertical lines in a passage, dividing text into 3-4 word groups
- Read group-by-group
- Gradually widen the groups
- Focus on the center of each group; let peripheral vision capture the edges

### Technique 3: Previewing and Skimming

Before deep reading, preview the structure:
1. Read the title, subtitle, and any abstract/summary
2. Read all headings and subheadings
3. Read the first sentence of each paragraph
4. Look at all images, charts, tables, and captions
5. Read the conclusion

This creates a mental framework that makes detailed reading 20-40% faster because your brain knows what is coming and where information fits.

### Technique 4: Meta-Guiding

A more advanced pacer method:
- Instead of underlining each line, move your finger in a smooth S-pattern or zigzag down the page
- This forces your eyes to take in more of each line per fixation
- Works best after you have mastered basic pacing and chunking

### Technique 5: RSVP (Rapid Serial Visual Presentation)

Words are displayed one at a time at a set speed, centered on screen:
- Eliminates regression entirely (words disappear)
- Eliminates saccades (eyes stay fixed)
- Good for training speed processing
- Poor for complex or technical material
- Tools: Spreeder, Spritz, ReadMe!, Reedy

## Practice Drills

### Drill 1: Timed Reading (Daily, 15 minutes)
1. Choose a book or article slightly below your difficulty level
2. Read for 3 minutes at normal pace, count words, calculate WPM
3. Read for 3 minutes at "push" pace (20% faster than comfortable), accept lower comprehension
4. Read for 3 minutes at normal pace again - notice it feels easier
5. Record all three WPM scores

### Drill 2: Fixation Training (5 minutes)
1. Take a newspaper column (narrow text)
2. Draw a single vertical line down the center
3. Try to read each line with only ONE fixation on that center line
4. Use peripheral vision to capture the whole line
5. Start with narrow columns, progress to wider text

### Drill 3: Comprehension Speed Sprints (10 minutes)
1. Read a passage at 2x your normal speed (will feel too fast)
2. Immediately write down everything you remember
3. Re-read at 1.5x normal speed
4. Add to your notes
5. Read a third time at normal speed, focusing only on what you missed
6. This trains your brain to extract more information at higher speeds

### Drill 4: Peripheral Vision Expansion
1. Focus on the center word of a line
2. Without moving your eyes, try to read the words to the left and right
3. Practice widening this span daily
4. Goal: 5-7 words in a single fixation

### Weekly Practice Schedule

| Day | Drill | Duration |
|-----|-------|----------|
| Monday | Timed Reading + Pacer | 15 min |
| Tuesday | Fixation Training | 10 min |
| Wednesday | Comprehension Speed Sprint | 15 min |
| Thursday | Free reading with pacer at push pace | 20 min |
| Friday | Peripheral Vision + Timed Reading | 15 min |
| Weekend | Pleasure reading applying all techniques | 30+ min |

## Comprehension Strategies

### Pre-Reading (2-5 minutes)
- **Survey**: Title, headings, bold text, images, summary
- **Question**: Turn each heading into a question ("The Causes of..." becomes "What are the causes of...?")
- **Purpose**: Define what you need from this material

### Active Reading
- **Annotate**: Brief margin notes (1-3 words) capturing key ideas
- **Connect**: Link new information to what you already know
- **Predict**: Anticipate what comes next based on structure
- **Question**: Ask "so what?" and "why?" as you read

### Post-Reading (5-10 minutes)
- **Recall**: Close the book and write/say everything you remember
- **Review**: Check your recall against the text
- **Summarize**: Write a 3-5 sentence summary
- **Teach**: Explain the material to someone (or to yourself aloud)

### The SQ3R Method (For Study Reading)
1. **Survey**: Preview the chapter
2. **Question**: Generate questions from headings
3. **Read**: Read to answer your questions
4. **Recite**: Close the book, answer your questions from memory
5. **Review**: Check answers, re-read weak areas

## Note-Taking Integration

### Speed Reading + Note Taking

The challenge: stopping to take notes kills reading speed. Solutions:

**Method 1: Marginal Marks**
- Quick symbols while reading: Star (important), ? (unclear), ! (surprising), -> (connects to something)
- Go back and process marks after finishing a section

**Method 2: The Pause Method**
- Read a full section without stopping
- Pause at the end, write 2-3 key points from memory
- This tests comprehension and creates spaced retrieval

**Method 3: Progressive Summarization**
- First pass: Read at speed, bold/highlight key sentences
- Second pass: Of highlighted material, bold the essential phrases
- Third pass: Write your own summary from the bolded material

## Retention Methods

### Spaced Repetition
- Review notes at expanding intervals: 1 day, 3 days, 7 days, 14 days, 30 days
- Use flashcard apps (Anki) for factual information
- The skipping curve is real: without review, you lose ~70% within 24 hours

### The Feynman Technique
1. Read the material
2. Explain it in simple language as if teaching a child
3. Identify gaps in your explanation
4. Go back and fill those gaps
5. Simplify and use analogies

### Connection Mapping
After reading, create a visual map connecting:
- Main concepts to supporting details
- New information to things you already knew
- Ideas from this source to ideas from other sources

## Adapting Speed by Material Type

| Material Type | Recommended Approach | Target Speed |
|--------------|---------------------|-------------|
| Pleasure fiction | Full speed, pacer, minimal notes | Maximum comfortable |
| News/articles | Preview + speed read body | 1.5-2x normal |
| Textbooks | SQ3R, slower with notes | 0.8-1x normal |
| Technical/scientific | Slow, careful, re-read as needed | 0.5-0.8x normal |
| Email/correspondence | Skim for action items | Maximum speed |
| Contracts/legal | Very slow, word-by-word | 0.5x normal |

**Key principle**: Speed reading is a tool, not a mandate. Adjust your speed to the material and purpose.
