# Product Description

## When to Use

Use this skill when the user's request maps to one of these specific scenarios:

- User asks to write, rewrite, or improve a product description for an e-commerce product page on their own website, a marketplace, or a social commerce platform
- User wants to translate a product spec sheet or manufacturer data sheet into customer-facing copy that drives purchase decisions
- User has an existing product description that is underperforming (low conversion rate, high bounce from product page) and wants a rewrite
- User needs descriptions for a product catalog upload -- multiple products in a consistent format, often in bulk
- User is launching a new product and needs the listing copy ready before going live
- User wants to optimize an existing description for search visibility without sacrificing conversion quality
- User needs to adapt a description to a new platform (e.g., moving from their own site to a marketplace) with different character limits, formatting rules, and buyer intent

**Do NOT use this skill when:**
- User needs a full-page sales letter or long-form conversion page -- use `landing-page-copy` instead, which handles above-the-fold hero copy, objection sections, and multi-section persuasion architecture
- User needs paid ad copy for Google Shopping, Meta product ads, or sponsored listings -- use `paid-ad-copy`, which handles character limits, ad fatigue rotation, and bidding-aligned messaging
- User is planning a full product launch campaign across channels -- use `campaign-planning`, which coordinates messaging hierarchy across awareness, consideration, and conversion touchpoints
- User needs a technical data sheet, compliance document, or product specification document for B2B procurement -- use a technical writing skill, which handles structured specification formats, regulatory language, and procurement-ready tables
- User needs email copy to announce a new product -- that is a product launch email sequence, which has its own narrative arc, subject line optimization, and send-timing logic

## Process

### Step 1: Gather Mandatory Product Intelligence

Never write a single word of copy before you have the information below. Ask directly if any item is missing -- guessing on price point or target buyer will produce unusable copy.

- **Product name and category:** The exact name as it will appear in the listing, plus the category hierarchy (e.g., "Kitchen > Coffee & Tea > French Presses"). Category determines the vocabulary buyers use and the comparison set they have in mind.
- **Target buyer:** Not a demographic segment -- a buying scenario. "Who is holding this product, where are they, and what problem just got solved?" produces more useful answers than "women 25-45." Probe for the primary use case and any secondary use cases.
- **Key features:** Ask for 3-7 features, then rank them by buyer relevance, not by what the product team is proudest of. The engineering team's favorite innovation is often not the buyer's primary motivation.
- **Materials, dimensions, compatibility, and certifications:** Exact numbers matter. "Lightweight" is useless; "weighs 4.2 oz" is copy. Certifications (FDA, CE, OEKO-TEX, UL Listed) are proof points that justify premium pricing.
- **Price point and competitive positioning:** Collect the actual price and ask the user to characterize positioning (budget, value, mid-range, premium, luxury). Also ask: what does the buyer compare this to? The answer shapes the copy's persuasion angle.
- **Platform:** Platform determines title format, character limits, keyword placement rules, HTML rendering capability, and bullet point conventions. Amazon, Shopify, Etsy, Walmart Marketplace, and Target Plus all have different structural requirements.
- **SEO keywords:** If provided, integrate them. If not provided, derive them from the product category, use case, and buyer language -- then confirm with the user before finalizing. Use the buyer's vocabulary, not the manufacturer's vocabulary.
- **Brand voice and tone:** Ask if there are voice guidelines or existing descriptions that represent the brand's style. Matching established tone prevents copy that sounds inconsistent across a product catalog.

### Step 2: Build the Features-Benefits-Proof (FBP) Matrix

Before writing a single line of copy, construct a mapping table that drives every claim in the description. This is the intellectual work of the process -- the writing itself is secondary.

- **Feature:** The factual, verifiable attribute of the product. State it in plain technical language first (e.g., "18/8 stainless steel interior," "160 lumens output," "rated IP67 waterproof").
- **Benefit:** The transformation the buyer experiences because the feature exists. Move through two levels: the functional benefit (what it does) and the emotional benefit (how it feels). "18/8 stainless steel interior" → functional: "no metallic taste" → emotional: "tastes exactly like you made it, not like the container."
- **Proof:** The specific, verifiable evidence that the benefit is real. Proof types include: materials certifications, independent test results, lab measurements, third-party ratings, usage statistics, comparison claims backed by specification data, and review excerpts. Every benefit claim without proof is a marketing assertion that experienced buyers discount.
- **Priority ranking:** Rank each FBP row by importance to the target buyer's primary decision. The top 1-2 benefits go into the opening paragraph. The next 3-5 become bullet points. Lower-priority features move to the specifications table.
- **Claim strength assessment:** Flag any benefit that is superlative ("most durable," "longest lasting") and either find proof for it or downgrade the language to a specific, verifiable claim. "Outlasts standard AA batteries by 3x in our testing" is stronger and more credible than "longest battery life."

### Step 3: Write the SEO-Optimized Product Title

The title is the highest-value piece of copy on the page -- it determines click-through from search results, search ranking, and the buyer's first impression. Structure varies by platform.

- **For brand websites (Shopify, WooCommerce, custom):** Format as `[Brand] [Product Name] [Key Differentiator] [Size/Variant]`. Keep under 60 characters for full display in Google search results. Front-load the primary keyword in the first 3 words.
- **For Amazon:** Follow Amazon's preferred format: `[Brand] + [Product] + [Key Feature] + [Size] + [Variant] + [Pack Count if applicable]`. Amazon titles can run to 200 characters but display truncates at ~80 in search. The critical keywords must appear in the first 80 characters. Do not use ALL CAPS, promotional language ("Best Seller," "Sale"), or non-informational punctuation.
- **For Etsy:** Lead with the most searched descriptive phrase, include material, style, and occasion. Etsy buyers search conversationally ("personalized leather wallet men"). Use natural language over formal structure.
- **For Walmart Marketplace:** Mirror Amazon structure. Walmart's algorithm weights title keywords heavily and penalizes keyword stuffing with de-ranking. Stick to 1-2 primary keywords in the title.
- **Never include:** Price, promotional language ("Free Shipping," "Buy Now"), subjective quality claims ("Amazing," "Best"), or seller name in product titles on any major marketplace.

### Step 4: Write the Hook Headline (On-Page)

This is the H1 or display headline that appears on the product page above the description, distinct from the SEO title. It is optimized for human reading, not search crawlers.

- State the primary benefit as an outcome, not a feature: "Sleep through the heat" beats "Cooling mattress topper."
- Write for the buyer's best possible day, not the average day: "Carry everything you need without checking a bag" beats "Fits 40L of gear."
- Keep it to 5-12 words. Any longer and the hook becomes a sentence that competes with the opening paragraph.
- Avoid questions unless they are extremely specific ("Still losing signal in the basement?") -- generic questions ("Ready to experience quality?") are ignored by experienced online shoppers.
- Test two formulations: one benefit-led, one problem-solved. The problem-solved version often converts better for pain-point products (sleep aids, pain relief, organizational tools). The benefit-led version works better for aspirational products (fitness gear, travel accessories, luxury goods).

### Step 5: Write the Opening Paragraph

The opening paragraph carries the heaviest persuasive load in the description. In mobile layouts, it may be the only prose the buyer reads before making a decision.

- Open with the primary use scenario, not a company story or product origin. Place the buyer in the moment of use in the first sentence.
- Deliver the primary benefit by sentence two, supported by the primary feature and proof.
- Use sensory language for physical products -- texture, weight, sound, temperature, smell are all legitimate copy elements that digital product pages lack by default. "Silky-smooth against bare skin," "The satisfying click of the magnetic closure," "Cool ceramic surface in your hand" compensate for the buyer's inability to touch the product.
- Integrate the primary SEO keyword naturally in the first paragraph. Do not force it -- rewrite the sentence until the keyword fits conversationally.
- Length: 3-4 sentences maximum. If you need more space, the bullet section is where it belongs.
- Voice should match the buyer's self-image. A buyer purchasing a premium kitchen knife does not want to be addressed like a beginner cook. A first-time homeowner buying a basic power drill wants reassurance, not technical authority.

### Step 6: Write the Benefit Bullet Points

Bullets are the most-read section of most product descriptions because buyers scan before they read. Structure every bullet to work as a standalone persuasion unit.

- **Format:** `**[Benefit phrase]** -- [Supporting feature + proof/specification]`. The bold benefit phrase must be intelligible without reading the rest of the bullet. A buyer who only reads the bold text should understand what the product does for them.
- Write 4-6 bullets per description. Fewer than 4 leaves benefits uncovered. More than 6 dilutes attention and starts to read as a feature list rather than a benefit statement.
- Each bullet must address a different buyer concern or use case. Do not write two bullets that both address the same fear (e.g., two bullets about durability). Cover the widest range of decision factors: performance, durability, convenience, compatibility, safety, value.
- The first bullet must address the primary purchase motivator -- the #1 reason this buyer type chooses this product over alternatives.
- Include at least one bullet that handles a common objection or hesitation. "Works with all standard 1/4-inch drill bits -- no adapters needed" handles a compatibility fear before the buyer has to ask.
- Avoid starting every bullet with "You'll" or "Get" -- vary the opening structure. Start with the outcome, the scenario, or the sensory detail, then support with the feature.

### Step 7: Write the Closing Statement and Call to Action

The closing line of the description body converts the persuaded reader into a buyer.

- Identify who the product is for -- a brief, specific characterization that lets the right buyer self-select: "Designed for the home barista who refuses to compromise on extraction."
- Address urgency or availability if honest and relevant (limited colorways, seasonal product, made-to-order). Never manufacture false urgency.
- On the brand's own website, include a direct call to action in the closing sentence or immediately below: "Add to cart and have it delivered by [day]." On marketplaces, the platform provides the CTA -- omit it from the copy to avoid policy violations (Amazon, for example, prohibits directing buyers off-platform).
- Keep it to 1-2 sentences. The closing is punctuation, not a second opening.

### Step 8: Finalize Specifications Table, Social Proof Placement, and Keyword Audit

The final structural pass ensures the technical and conversion infrastructure is complete.

- **Specifications table:** Include all measurable, comparable attributes. Buyers in product comparison mode are looking for specific numbers to adjudicate between options. Missing dimensions, missing compatibility specs, or missing material details cause abandonment. Use consistent units (both imperial and metric for any product sold internationally).
- **Social proof placement:** Specify exactly where reviews, ratings, and usage statistics should appear relative to the copy. Star rating below the title, review excerpt after the bullet points, usage stat ("47,000 sold") near the CTA. These placements are not copywriting -- they are conversion architecture notes for the page designer or store developer.
- **Keyword audit:** Read the final copy and verify that the primary keyword appears in the title, opening paragraph, and at least one bullet. Verify that secondary keywords appear at least once each. Read the copy aloud -- if any keyword sounds forced, rewrite the surrounding sentence. Keyword density above 2-3% for any single phrase creates a stuffing penalty risk on search engines and reads as unnatural to humans.

## Rules

1. **Never write copy before completing the FBP matrix.** Features-Benefits-Proof is the structural foundation -- copy written before this mapping is done will drift toward feature lists or unsupported claims. The matrix takes 5 minutes and prevents 30 minutes of revision.

2. **Lead with the buyer's outcome, not the product's existence.** The opening sentence must put the buyer in a use scenario or describe their life with this product. "Meet the [Product]" and "Introducing our new..." are the two weakest possible openings -- they signal marketing speak and lose scanner-readers immediately.

3. **Every superlative requires a source.** "Most durable," "longest-lasting," "softest," "best quality" are legally and ethically problematic without substantiation. Replace with specific, verifiable claims: "survives 10-foot drops in independent ASTM F1805 testing," "holds an edge 3x longer than standard 420 stainless steel." If proof cannot be found, remove the superlative.

4. **Match copy sophistication to buyer knowledge level.** A description for a 12V power tool sold to professional contractors must use the vocabulary a contractor uses ("brushless motor for extended runtime and reduced maintenance" not "advanced motor technology"). A description for the same tool sold to homeowners must translate: "runs cooler and lasts longer than standard motors -- no carbon brushes to replace." Using the wrong register loses the buyer's trust.

5. **Price positioning governs the emotional register.** Budget copy ($0-$25 range) emphasizes practicality, savings, and no-risk reliability. Value/mid-range copy ($25-$100) balances quality and practicality. Premium copy ($100-$500) leads with experience, quality of materials, and long-term value. Luxury copy ($500+) drops utilitarian language almost entirely and writes about identity, craft, and exclusivity. Violating this register is the single fastest way to make a premium product feel cheap or a budget product feel aspirational without substance.

6. **Specifications live in the table, not in the prose.** A description that reads "The 18/8 grade 304 stainless steel with double-wall vacuum sealed construction measuring 10.5 inches in height and 3.2 inches in diameter with a lid thread diameter of 2.1 inches..." has stopped being a description and started being a spec sheet. Buyers stop reading. Put numbers in the table; put transformations in the prose.

7. **Bullets must stand alone as benefit statements.** Read only the bold text of every bullet. If the bold text does not communicate a clear buyer benefit, rewrite it. "Double-wall insulation" fails. "Ice stays ice, all day" passes. "No more lukewarm coffee by 9am" passes. "Engineered with proprietary ThermaLock technology" fails.

8. **Platform character limits are hard constraints, not guidelines.** Amazon product titles above 200 characters are auto-truncated in ways that destroy keyword structure. Amazon bullet points above 500 characters are truncated on mobile. Google Shopping titles above 70 characters lose most of their display advantage. Etsy titles above 140 characters lose search value. Build the output for the platform's limits from the start -- retrofitting a 300-character title to 80 characters usually requires a complete rewrite.

9. **Keywords must be confirmed against buyer search behavior, not assumed.** Manufacturer vocabulary and buyer search vocabulary diverge significantly. Buyers search "water bottle that keeps drinks cold" -- manufacturers call it "vacuum insulated hydration vessel." If the user provides no keywords, derive them from buyer-facing language in the product category, common review text vocabulary, and the target buyer's likely search phrasing. Always show the user the keywords you're targeting before finalizing copy.

10. **For variant products, write one canonical description that covers all variants, then add variant-specific copy in designated variant fields.** Writing a full description per color variant or size wastes crawl budget, splits review aggregation on many platforms, and creates maintenance nightmares when pricing or features change. The base description covers the product; variant fields handle color names, size-specific dimensions, and compatibility notes.

11. **Sensory language is not optional for physical products.** Touch, weight, texture, temperature, sound, and smell cannot be communicated by the product image. Copy must compensate. "Cool, smooth ceramic in your palm," "The satisfying snap of the lid sealing," "150gsm fabric that drapes without clinging" give the buyer a physical experience they cannot get from a photo. Omitting sensory language is the most common reason premium physical products underperform mid-range alternatives with richer copy.

12. **Social proof placement is structural, not decorative.** A 4.7-star average displayed prominently converts. The same rating buried at the bottom converts poorly. Review excerpts must address the buyer's primary fear or objection -- a glowing review about shipping speed does not help a buyer deciding whether the product is durable. Identify the #1 purchase fear, find a review that directly addresses it, and place that excerpt immediately after the benefit bullets.

## Edge Cases

### Technical Products (Electronics, Tools, Scientific Equipment)

Buyers in technical categories compare specifications directly and will abandon descriptions that do not provide them. Treat the specifications table as primary conversion content, not supplementary documentation.

- Include all comparative specifications: not just "long battery life" but "24-hour battery life at 50% brightness; 8 hours at 100% brightness." Buyers know the category norms and will assess your number against them.
- Use both the technical term and the plain-language translation in the bullets: "Brushless motor (runs cooler, lasts longer, zero maintenance) vs. standard brushed motor." The parenthetical translation serves less technical buyers without condescending to technical ones.
- Compatibility specifications are critical and commonly omitted. State exactly what the product works with and, if it is frequently asked about, what it does NOT work with. "Compatible with all Android devices running Android 8.0 and above; not compatible with iOS" prevents returns and negative reviews.
- For products where the buyer may be comparing across brands on a retailer platform, do not ignore competitor specs -- write bullets that address the dimensions where your product leads, which implicitly frames the comparison without making claims about competitors (which most marketplaces prohibit).

### Handmade, Artisan, and Small-Batch Products

The differentiation for handmade products is the story, the maker, and the irreplicability -- not efficiency or specification uniformity.

- Lead with what human hands and non-industrial process produce that machines cannot: variation that makes each piece unique, attention to material quality at the individual item level, relationships between the maker and material.
- Sensory and material language carries disproportionate weight: grain direction in wood, hand of the fabric, temperature of the clay, color variation in natural dyes. These details signal authenticity to buyers who are specifically choosing artisan over mass-produced.
- If the product has natural variation (handmade ceramics, natural stone, hand-dyed textiles), state this explicitly and frame it as a feature: "Each piece is unique -- minor variations in glaze pattern are the signature of the firing process, not defects." Failure to disclose variation leads to disappointed buyers and return disputes.
- Production timeline matters for buyers making gift or event purchases. State production and shipping time in the description for made-to-order products, not just at checkout.
- Etsy specifically rewards rich narrative descriptions in its search algorithm. On that platform, a 400-600 word description outperforms a 150-word description when the longer version integrates search terms naturally.

### Commodity and Undifferentiated Products

When the product itself offers no meaningful differentiation from dozens of identical alternatives, the description must shift persuasion to the purchase experience, the brand's reliability signal, or bundle/value construction.

- Emphasize the buying risk reduction: hassle-free returns, satisfaction guarantees, responsive customer service, and transparent quality standards. For commodities, the buyer is choosing who to trust, not what to buy.
- Highlight any differentiator in the purchase experience: faster shipping, better packaging that makes it gift-ready, bulk pricing tiers, subscription discounts.
- If the product has certifications that competitors may lack (organic, BPA-free, cruelty-free, recycled content), lead with those -- they are genuine differentiators even on commodity items.
- Do not invent differentiation that does not exist. Overselling a commodity product produces the highest return rates and most damaging reviews of any product category.

### Products with Multiple Variants (Size, Color, Material, Configuration)

Variant handling is where most product description systems break down into duplicate content and inconsistent messaging.

- Write one base description that is completely accurate for all variants. Do not write "available in blue" if some variants are not blue. Use inclusive language: "available in [X] colors" or "in sizes from [small] to [large]."
- For size variants: include a sizing table or sizing guidance in the base description. "Fits true to size; order your usual size" or "Runs 1 size small -- size up if between sizes" prevents the #1 cause of soft goods returns.
- For color variants: use rich color names and descriptions in the variant-specific field, not the base description. "Ocean Blue: a deep teal with slight grey undertones; pairs with natural materials and white interiors" belongs in the variant field, not the main description.
- For material variants (e.g., stainless steel vs. matte plastic vs. titanium): create separate FBP matrices for each material variant, since the benefits and proofs differ. A single description trying to cover all materials produces copy that is accurate for none.
- On Amazon, use parent-child listing architecture -- one parent listing with variant child ASINs that inherit the base description. Do not create separate parent listings per variant.

### Subscription or Consumable Products

Consumable and subscription products require dual-register copy: justify the initial purchase AND the ongoing relationship.

- The description must serve two buyers simultaneously: the first-time buyer evaluating whether to try the product, and the returning buyer considering whether to subscribe or reorder. The opening paragraph serves the first-time buyer; the bullet section should include at least one bullet addressing the subscription or reorder value.
- State the per-use or per-serving economics explicitly for consumables: "At $0.28 per serving, less than a gas station coffee" converts budget-conscious buyers better than any quality claim.
- Address commitment anxiety directly for subscriptions: "Pause or cancel anytime from your account dashboard -- no phone calls, no penalties." Buyers who fear subscription traps will not convert even if the product is excellent.
- For subscription products, include the savings calculation in the copy: "Subscribe and save 20% -- [annual cost] vs. [12 × single purchase price]." Make the buyer's financial decision trivially easy.
- Highlight repurchase triggers: "Most customers reorder every 6-8 weeks" both sets expectations and implicitly validates that this is a product people choose to keep buying.

### International or Multi-Region Products

Products sold across regions require copy adjustments beyond simple unit conversion.

- Include both imperial and metric measurements in every specification. This is non-negotiable for products sold in any market where metric is standard. A UK or European buyer who sees only inches and ounces will not mentally convert -- they will leave.
- Voltage and plug compatibility must be explicitly stated for electronics: "110-240V universal input; compatible with UK, EU, US, and Australian outlets with appropriate adapter."
- Regulatory compliance claims must be accurate for the region. CE marking for EU, FCC for US, RoHS for environmental compliance -- these are proof points for international technical buyers and legal requirements for sale in those markets.
- Date, sizing, and color vocabulary vary by region. UK sizing differs from US sizing for footwear and apparel. "Fall" is a season name not universally understood. Write for the primary market and add regional callouts only where necessary.
